# Scenario Design Reference

How to construct legal scenario questions for memo and analysis tasks.

---

## What makes a strong scenario

A strong legal scenario has three properties:

1. **No clean answer**. The candidate cannot recite a rule and be done. There is a genuine
   tension between competing interests, and the right answer requires prioritising one.

2. **Commercial stakes are clear**. The candidate understands what is at risk for the organisation
   if they give the wrong advice, not just what the legal position is.

3. **Novelty is real**. Either the law is genuinely unsettled, or the facts are unusual enough
   that standard doctrine does not map cleanly. This tests reasoning under uncertainty, not recall.

---

## Scenario archetypes

### Archetype A: The mission integrity tension
The organisation must choose between its commercial interests and its core mission.

Template:
> [Organisation] has been asked by [client] to [do something]. Doing so would [commercial benefit].
> However, [doing it threatens the organisation's independence / creates a conflict / requires
> suppressing findings]. Advise.

Example (mission integrity / classification):
> A G7 government has classified the organisation's evaluation findings as a national security matter.
> The CEO wants to publish. Advise.

Strong because: advising the CEO "you cannot publish" is the right answer but the uncomfortable
one. Tests whether the candidate will be honest vs. find a workaround.

### Archetype B: The novel IP question
An IP ownership question where the technology is new enough that settled doctrine doesn't apply cleanly.

Template:
> [Party A] created [type of output] using [tool / process]. [Party B] claims ownership because
> [argument]. [Party A] wants to [use the output in a new way]. Advise.

Example (novel IP / monitoring tool):
> The organisation's evaluation platform generates behavioural logs. The client claims these are
> the client's IP. The organisation wants to use aggregated anonymised data to train a safety
> classifier. Advise.

Strong because: AI-generated output ownership is genuinely unsettled. Tests honesty about
legal uncertainty and ability to reason by analogy.

### Archetype C: The conflict of interest problem
Concurrent engagements that create a risk of information contamination.

Template:
> [Organisation] is engaged by [Client A] and [Client B] simultaneously. [Client A] alleges that
> [information / personnel / methodology] from their engagement has contaminated the [Client B]
> engagement. They are threatening [action]. Advise.

Strong because: requires both immediate response (de-escalation, letter drafting) and structural
fix (information barriers). Tests whether the candidate thinks in both short and long time horizons.

### Archetype D: The regulatory classification
A regulatory body or government client applies a classification or restriction that cuts against
the organisation's core interests.

Template:
> [Regulator / Government] has [classified / restricted / required modification of] [output / action].
> The contract contains [clause]. [CEO / leadership] wants to [proceed regardless / challenge it /
> find a middle path]. Advise.

Strong because: there is usually no purely legal solution. The right advice combines legal
analysis with stakeholder management, process design, and honest counsel that leadership may
not want to hear.

### Archetype E: The first-mover structural question
The organisation has no precedent for the structure it needs to build.

Template:
> [Organisation] needs to [build a legal structure / implement a policy / create a framework]
> for [novel activity]. There is no market standard. Advise on how to build it and what
> the key risks are.

Example: "The organisation needs to design an information barrier protocol for concurrent evaluations
of competing clients. There is no industry standard. Build it."

Strong because: tests whether the candidate can build from principles rather than copy a template.

## Memo format to encourage

For candidate prep, recommend this structure:

```
MEMORANDUM

To:      [CEO / COO / Relevant stakeholder]
From:    Head of Legal
Date:    [Date]
Re:      [One-line summary]

1. Issue
   One sentence.

2. Key facts
   Only the legally material facts. Not a narrative summary.

3. Analysis
   The legal reasoning. State uncertainties where they exist.
   Conclude on each sub-issue before moving to the next.

4. Conclusion
   One clear recommendation. Not "it depends."

5. Next steps / recommended actions
   What should the organisation do in the next 7 days?
```

Strong candidates: conclusions in 3-4 paragraphs with clear structure.
Weak candidates: narrative essays that avoid committing to a recommendation.

## Scenario difficulty calibration

| Level | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| **Junior** | One issue, English law settled, clear answer with room for nuance. Tests whether they can apply a rule correctly. |
| **Mid** | Two competing interests, some legal uncertainty, requires prioritisation. Tests whether they can advise under ambiguity. |
| **Senior** | Novel facts, unsettled law, commercial + mission stakes both in play, requires recommending against leadership's preferred outcome. Tests whether they can be a genuine counsellor, not just an analyst. |
| **Partner / Head of Legal** | Multi-party, multi-jurisdiction, genuine legal novelty, no market standard, and requires designing a structural solution rather than a transactional answer. |

Head of Legal / GC roles typically sit at **Senior / Partner** calibration.

---

## Red lines: what to avoid in scenario design

- **Scenarios with obvious answers**: "Client has breached the NDA. Advise." Too simple for senior roles.
- **Scenarios requiring specific UK/German/US law recall**. The test is judgment, not a bar exam. If the scenario requires knowing a specific statute number, redesign it.
- **Scenarios where the legally correct answer is also the commercially comfortable one**. Always include at least one scenario where the right advice is uncomfortable.
- **Hypotheticals that are implausible in the organisation's actual context**. Test scenarios should feel like things the candidate would genuinely encounter.
