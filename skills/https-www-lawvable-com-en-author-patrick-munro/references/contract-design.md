# Contract Design Reference

How to write realistic contracts with well-embedded problems for legal assessment purposes.

---

## Core principle

The best assessment contracts read like real contracts drafted by a competent but adversarial
counterparty. Problems should be **plausibly bad**. Not obviously wrong, not egregiously
drafted. A candidate who spots a problem because the clause is clumsily written has not
demonstrated anything. A candidate who spots a problem because they understand the commercial
stakes of clean-looking language: that candidate can think.

---

## Problem embedding patterns

### Pattern 1: The creeping definition
Bury a commercial poison in a defined term that applies throughout the contract.

Example:
> "Confidential Information" shall include .. and **any outputs, findings, reports, or conclusions
> generated by Service Provider in connection with the Services.**

This looks like a standard confidentiality definition. The problem is that it captures the
service provider's own work product and treats it as the client's confidential information,
meaning the service provider cannot use or publish their own analysis.

Use when testing: IP awareness, confidentiality scope, publication rights.

### Pattern 2: The absolute right hidden in a conditional
Grant an unconditional veto inside a clause that appears to be setting up a reasonable process.

Example:
> Client shall review the draft report within 30 days. **Where Client withholds consent, Service Provider
> acknowledges that Client's determination shall be final and binding.**

The first sentence looks like a review process. The second sentence converts it into a permanent,
unconditional veto with no time limit, no good faith requirement, no deemed consent mechanism.

Use when testing: publication rights, independence clauses, negotiation instinct.

### Pattern 3: The overbroad assignment
Draft an IP assignment that captures background IP by using "materials created in connection with."

Example:
> All work product, deliverables, reports, **and materials created by Service Provider in
> connection with the Services** shall be assigned to Client.

Standard work-for-hire language. The problem is "materials created in connection with",
which sweeps in pre-existing methodology, tools, templates, and software that the service
provider uses across all clients.

Use when testing: IP ownership, work-for-hire vs. licensing, background IP protection.

### Pattern 4: The licence-back launder
Pair an overbroad assignment with a licence-back that appears to solve the problem but doesn't.

Example (immediately after overbroad assignment):
> Service Provider hereby grants Client a non-exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual licence to
> use Service Provider's pre-existing tools to the extent necessary to make use of the Work Product.

This appears to protect the service provider. It doesn't. You cannot licence back what you
never intended to assign. The assignment should be narrowed, not papered over with a licence-back.

Use when testing: whether candidate spots the real fix vs. the superficial fix.

### Pattern 5: The missing chain of custody
Include a white-box access clause with no destruction obligation, no access log, no liability
allocation for post-engagement leaks.

Example:
> Client shall provide Service Provider with access to the Model weights for the evaluation
> period. Service Provider shall implement **reasonable security measures** in connection
> with its handling of the Model.

"Reasonable security measures" is undefined. No destruction obligation. No access log. No
timeline for return/deletion. The service provider has no documented evidence of having
discharged its obligations if a leak later occurs.

Use when testing: due diligence instinct, chain of custody, risk allocation.

### Pattern 6: Asymmetric liability cap without a publication carve-out
Cap the service provider's liability at fees paid, with no corresponding client cap and no
express protection for publishing accurate findings.

Example:
> Service Provider's total aggregate liability shall not exceed the fees paid in the
> preceding 12 months.

The problem: fees paid may be a small fraction of the commercial exposure from publishing
accurate safety findings that damage the client's business. More importantly, there is no
clause confirming that Service Provider is not liable for exercising a contractual right to
publish, leaving it exposed to a claim that accurate publication constitutes breach.

Use when testing: liability structuring, indemnification thinking, publication protection.

### Pattern 7: The survival cliff
Set a confidentiality survival period that is either too long (trapping the service provider in
obligations that outlive the commercial context) or too short (failing to protect the client's
genuinely sensitive information).

For AI/tech contracts: 7 years is too long; 2-3 years is reasonable for most information;
model weights might warrant 3 years but 7 is absurd.

## Problem severity calibration

| Severity | What makes it this level |
|---|---|
| CRITICAL | If a competent lawyer accepts this clause, the organisation's core mission or business model is materially threatened. |
| HIGH | Significant commercial or legal exposure that should be caught and negotiated. The organisation can survive this clause but will be worse off. |
| MEDIUM | Suboptimal. Good lawyers improve it. Not catastrophic if accepted. |

For a 3-hour test, target: 2 critical, 2–3 high, 1–2 medium, 1 missing clause bonus.

## The missing clause

Every strong contract exercise should include one bonus question: "What is missing entirely?"

Strong missing clause candidates:
- **Information barriers / conflict of interest**. How does the service provider manage concurrent engagements with competing clients?
- **Governing law inconsistency**. Parties are in different jurisdictions but governing law clause is silent on how to apply it
- **Anti-assignment**. No restriction on client assigning the agreement to an acquirer
- **Data processing agreement**. GDPR/UK GDPR compliance where personal data may be processed
- **Force majeure**. Missing or inadequate, particularly for AI/tech contracts where compute access and regulatory change matter
- **Step-in rights**. Missing in contracts where continuity of service is critical to the client

## Jurisdiction-specific notes

### English law
- Implied duties of good faith are weaker than in civil law systems. Always push for express good faith obligations where needed
- Unfair Contract Terms Act 1977 / Consumer Rights Act 2015 apply to B2B contracts in limited ways
- IP assignment under English law requires a signed written instrument (CDPA 1988 s.90(3) for copyright)
- Non-compete / restraint of trade clauses must be reasonable in scope, area, and duration

### German law
- AGB (standard terms) control is strict: §§ 305–310 BGB. Surprising or unreasonable clauses in standard terms are void.
- IP in software: UrhG §§ 69a–69g. Work-for-hire doctrine differs from English law.
- Employment law is substantially more protective. Use employment law modules separately

### New York law
- UCC Article 2 applies to goods transactions; service contracts are common law
- Non-disclosure via trade secret law: Defend Trade Secrets Act (federal) + NY state law
- Work-for-hire under US Copyright Act § 101 is narrow. Assignment clauses are standard
- Inevitable disclosure doctrine: accepted in some states, rejected in others (NY is mixed)
