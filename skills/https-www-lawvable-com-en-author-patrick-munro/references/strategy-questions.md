# Strategy Question Templates

Strategy and function-building questions calibrated by role type.
These test seniority and commercial judgment, not legal doctrine.

---

## When to use a strategy question

Always include one strategy question in a senior (Head of Legal / GC) assessment.
It is the single best discriminator between lawyers who think commercially and those
who think only doctrinally.

---

## Template library by role type

### First legal hire / Head of Legal (0→1 build)

**Classic framing:**
> "You are [Organisation]'s first legal hire. It is Day 90. What have you built, what have
> you deliberately not built yet, and what keeps you up at night?"

**Alternative:**
> "You have been in role for 6 months. Leadership wants to know what the legal function
> has done for the organisation. How do you answer that question. And what do you measure?"

**What strong answers include:**
- Specific choices made, not a list of things they plan to do
- Something they deliberately did NOT build, with a reason
- An honest "what keeps me up at night" that identifies real risk, not performative concern
- Evidence they understand the difference between infrastructure (contract templates, policies)
  and judgment (being in the room before decisions are made)

**What weak answers look like:**
- "I would implement a contract management system". Tool decision, not strategy
- Lists of things they'd theoretically like to build without any prioritisation rationale
- No acknowledgment of what they *can't* do yet at this stage

---

### Scaling legal function (10→50+ lawyers)

**Framing:**
> "[Organisation] has grown from 12 to 45 lawyers in 18 months. The legal function has not
> kept pace. You are brought in as General Counsel. What do you do in the first 100 days?"

**What strong answers include:**
- Rapid assessment of where legal decisions are currently being made without legal input
- Decision on what to handle in-house vs. what to manage via panel firms
- Governance: what goes to the GC, what goes to the CLO, what can be delegated
- Identification of the two or three areas of highest legal risk (not a comprehensive audit)

---

### Mission-driven organisation (nonprofit / PBC / NGO)

**Framing:**
> "[Organisation] has a dual mandate. Commercial sustainability and mission integrity.
> Identify the three areas where these are most likely to come into conflict, and explain
> how the legal function should manage that tension."

**What strong answers include:**
- Specific, credible conflict areas (not generic "mission drift" language)
- Structural solutions (contract provisions, governance mechanisms) not just process
- Acknowledgment that legal cannot resolve value conflicts. It can only make them visible

---

### Regulated entity (financial services / healthcare / AI governance)

**Framing:**
> "The regulator has signalled that it will bring enforcement action in [area] within the
> next 12 months. The business wants to continue current practices pending outcome.
> How do you advise?"

**What strong answers include:**
- Clear distinction between legal advice (what the law says) and business advice (what they should do)
- Risk quantification. Fine, reputational, operational
- Escalation path if leadership rejects the advice
- Documentation of the advice given

---

### Technology company / AI lab

**Framing:**
> "[Organisation] is about to launch a product in three jurisdictions simultaneously.
> The legal team has not been involved in the product design. You have 30 days before launch.
> What do you do?"

**What strong answers include:**
- Triage: what can still be changed vs. what is locked
- Jurisdiction-specific red lines vs. jurisdiction-specific preferences
- The conversation you need to have with the CEO about launch timing
- How you prevent this from happening again (legal embedded in product design cycles)

## Checklist criteria for strategy questions

Use these as marking criteria:

1. **Decisions made, not plans stated**. Strong answers are in past tense ("I built X") or
   present tense ("I am currently managing Y"), not future tense ("I would implement Z").

2. **Deliberate non-choices**. The candidate must show they understand opportunity cost.
   What they chose NOT to build (and why) is as revealing as what they built.

3. **Honest risk identification**: "what keeps me up at night" should name specific, credible
   risks. Not generic concerns like "regulatory change" or "resource constraints."

4. **Commercial awareness**. The legal function exists to enable the business, not to police it.
   Strong candidates articulate what they built in terms of what it enabled, not just what risk it reduced.

5. **Tone of counsel, not analyst**. The strategy answer should feel like advice from someone
   who has thought hard about this specific organisation's situation, not a framework applied generically.
