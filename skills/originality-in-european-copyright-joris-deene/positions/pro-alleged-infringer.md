# Position 2 — Pro-alleged-infringer

> How to argue that an object does **not** qualify as a copyright-protected work, or is insufficiently original.

> **Version 1.0** — based on fifteen CJEU judgments: Infopaq (general formula), Painer (photography), BSA (software/GUI), FAPL (sports events), Football Dataco (databases), SAS Institute (software-scope), Levola Hengelo (work concept), Renckhoff (photography confirmation), Cofemel (consolidated test + applied art), Brompton (technical-function exclusion), Funke Medien (functional texts), Sony/Datel (reproducibility test for software), Mio/Konektra (unique-aspect requirement; pre-existing works as attack instrument for applied art), Călinescu/FNSA ("expertise immaterial"; mere transcription not a work; technical conventions exclude originality; public-domain safeguard) and Nintendo / PC Box (per-component analysis for multimedia works; lex specialis boundary); supplemented with **horizontal attack lines based on the EU statutory floor** (art. 1(3) Dir. 2009/24/EC, art. 3(1) Dir. 96/9/EC, art. 6 Dir. 2006/116/EC and art. 14 Dir. (EU) 2019/790 DSM) — four attack lines for the alleged infringer: threshold maintenance of "author's own intellectual creation", art. 14 DSM anti-reviving against claims on museum reproductions, anti-monopolisation of public-domain substrate (Călinescu point 68), and formal rebuttal basis against threshold-circumvention. Follows the two-step test.

## Strategic structure

From the perspective of the alleged infringer there are **two independent attack lines**, each sufficient on its own to defeat the copyright defence:

1. **Attack the work concept** (Levola Hengelo) — show that the object is not sufficiently precisely and objectively identifiable
2. **Attack originality** (Football Dataco + exclusion grounds) — show that the object is not the author's own intellectual creation

Strategically, the first attack (work concept) is often **more powerful** when available: success there means the object is **by definition** excluded from copyright protection, regardless of how much creative effort the "author" invested in it. The Levola attack is a **knock-out attack**: one successful attack at step 1 makes the opponent's entire further argumentation superfluous. The second attack (originality) is more factual and discretionary and is assessed more nuancedly by the court.

## Attack line 1 — Work concept (based on Levola Hengelo, C-310/17)

### The Levola attack

When the opposing party asserts a claim on an object that is **not objectively identifiable**, the claim can be cut off at step 1, **before** the originality question even arises.

> Levola Hengelo point 40: the subject matter must be capable of being **expressed in a precise and objective manner**.
>
> Levola Hengelo point 41: this is required so that (i) **authorities and economic operators** can clearly and precisely identify what is protected, and (ii) any **legal-certainty-undermining subjectivity** is eliminated.

### Strong examples of work-concept attack

| Object | Status after Levola | Operational argument |
|---|---|---|
| **Taste** of a foodstuff | No work (Levola dictum) | Direct application of the operative part |
| **Scent** (perfume, aroma) | Almost certainly no work | Levola logic by analogy
| **Visual "ambience"** or "atmosphere" | No work | Not concretely identifiable; subjective impression |
| **Style as such** (genre, aesthetic) | No work | General characteristic, not a concrete object |
| **"Concept"** without concrete elaboration | No work | Idea, not form of expression (Levola point 39, idea/expression dichotomy) |
| **"Overall impression"** without concrete components | No work | Subjective impression, no objective object |
| **Type of experience** (a kind of interactive event) | No work | Too abstract |
| **Mouthfeel / texture** (as Levola itself contended) | No work | Levola dictum expressly covers such elements |
| **"The feel" of a brand** | No work | Subjective, not objectifiable |

### Operational test for the defence

Ask the opposing party to **concretely describe**:

1. **What exactly is the work** on which you rely?
2. **How can a third party** identify this work without subjective assessment?
3. **What form of expression** has this work taken?
4. **How can authorities** (customs, judge) and **competitors** establish what is protected?
5. **How does** your work objectively differ from other objects in the same category?

If the opposing party can only answer with terms such as "the taste of", "the scent of", "the atmosphere of", "the style of", "the overall impression of", "the feel of" without concrete anchoring, the step 1 test fails.

### What the Levola attack does **not** cut off

Important to remember: the Levola attack excludes only **subjective / sensory objects**. Works that are objectively identifiable but **banal or conventional** are not attacked via Levola but via the originality test (Football Dataco + exclusion grounds).

For example:

- A **standard text** is objectively identifiable (Levola succeeds) but possibly not original (Football Dataco attack)
- A **technical drawing of a standardised component** is objectively identifiable but functionally dictated (BSA attack)
- An **alphabetical list** is objectively identifiable but without creative choices (sweat-of-the-brow attack, Football Dataco point 42)

The Levola attack is therefore **complementary** to the originality attack, not a substitute.

### Anticipating attempts to circumvent Levola

A strong opposing party will try to "objectify" sensory objects via:

- **Recipe as text**: can be a work as text (if original), but the **taste produced by the recipe** remains excluded
- **Visual plating** of a dish: can be a work as sculpture or image, but not as taste experience
- **Photograph of a perfume bottle**: can be a work as photograph, but not the scent itself
- **Chemical composition**: can be patentable but yields no copyright on the sensory experience

The defence must **sharply distinguish** between the objectively identifiable **medium** (which may attract copyright) and the **sensory experience** (which cannot be protected).

### Evidentiary leverage for the defence

- **Point out variability of experience**: age, preferences, habits, surroundings and context affect perception
- **Scientific impossibility**: at the current state of science the object cannot be identified by technical means
- **Comparative examples**: different persons identify the object differently
- **Unclear boundary of the claim**: the opposing party cannot indicate where its claim begins and ends

### Citation form

> CJEU 13 November 2018, C-310/17, *Levola Hengelo*, ECLI:EU:C:2018:899, paragraph 40 (objective identifiability required) and/or paragraph 42 (taste subjective and variable) and/or operative part (taste is not a work).

## Attack line 2 — Originality

### Starting point

The EU criterion (CJEU 16 July 2009, C-5/08, *Infopaq International*, paragraph 37) requires that the subject matter "is original in the sense that it is its author's own intellectual creation". Both components — "own" **and** "intellectual creation" — must be satisfied.

### Sub-line A — Not "own": derivation or slavish following of existing material

- Show that the object is **derived** from pre-existing material
- Show that the "choices" made already existed in earlier works or in the state of the art
- Show comparable objects which the original maker could not ignore

### Sub-line B — No "intellectual creation": dictated or banal

This is the centre of gravity of the defence. Possible arguments:

1. **Functionally or technically dictated** — the form is merely the necessary consequence of the function to be performed. No room for creative choices. **Authority**: BSA (C-393/09) paragraphs 48-49 — components dictated solely by their technical function do not satisfy the originality criterion.
2. **Dictated by external rules (rules of the game, norms, regulations)** — competitions, rule-governed events and standard-bound outcomes are not works. **Authority**: FAPL (C-403/08) paragraph 98 — rules of the game leave no room for creative freedom in the copyright sense; *a contrario* Painer paragraph 89.
3. **Genre- or convention-bound** — the "choices" are the customary execution within the relevant genre; all practitioners in the field would work similarly
4. **Banal or trivial** — the choices made are so obvious that no creative effort can be established

### General scheme: the Painer/FAPL mirror

For each alleged work the following analytical scheme applies:

- Painer paragraph 89 (positive): **did the author have scope for free and creative choices?** If yes → work
- FAPL paragraph 98 + BSA paragraphs 48-49 (negative): **was such scope absent, because external factors (technical function, rules of the game, conventions, norms) dictated the expression?** If yes → no work

The same test in two formulations. A defence that does not expressly invoke one of them misses half of the argumentative force.

### Sub-line C — Components versus whole

An effective technique: **deconstruct** the object into its components and show that:

- The individual components (words, colours, shapes, technical elements) are themselves **not protected** (Infopaq paragraph 46)
- The **combination** of those components is banal or obvious
- Or: what was taken concerns only loose, unprotected components — not the possibly protected combination

### Sub-line D — Idea/expression dichotomy

Copyright does **not** protect ideas, concepts, methods or styles — only their concrete expression. Often a contestation argument is in reality an attempt to monopolise an idea or method via copyright.

### Sub-line E — Limited scope of protection

Even if minimally original: for objects with limited creative scope (technical domain, strongly genre-bound) the scope of protection is correspondingly narrow. Reproduction of general elements remains permitted; only quasi-identical taking can constitute infringement.

## Attack line 3 — Horizontal attack lines based on the EU statutory floor ⭐⭐⭐

In addition to the Cofemel two-step test and the sector-specific attack lines, the alleged infringer has **horizontal attack lines** that rest directly on primary EU law. These attack lines are particularly relevant against rightholders who try to lower the threshold by relying on "investment", "craftsmanship", "expertise" or "market value" without actually meeting the Cofemel two-step test.

Full doctrinal elaboration: `methodology/01-originality-test.md`, section "The EU harmonisation framework for originality".

### Attack line F — The statutory threshold remains "author's own intellectual creation" and not less

The *"no other criteria"* clause in art. 1(3) Dir. 2009/24, art. 3(1) Dir. 96/9 and art. 6 Dir. 2006/116 excludes **additional requirements** (no artistic value, no stamp of personality). But the clause likewise does not allow **lowered thresholds**: the EU test remains the cumulative requirement that there be an **author's own intellectual creation** — free creative choices, personal touch (Painer paragraphs 87-94) and a unique aspect (Mio/Konektra operative part 2).

**When usable**:

| Rightholder argument | Attack line |
|---|---|
| *"We made significant investments"* | Football Dataco operative part 2 + paragraphs 38-39 — sweat of the brow is **excluded**; substantial effort ≠ creative choice |
| *"Our expertise and craftsmanship are considerable"* | Călinescu paragraph 54 in fine — *"the expertise involved in creating the subject matter is immaterial"* |
| *"Market success / professional recognition proves originality"* | Mio/Konektra operative part 2 in fine — professional recognition is **not decisive**; subjective perception ≠ objective creative addition |
| *"Everyone finds this a beautiful object"* | Cofemel paragraph 35 + art. 6 Dir. 2006/116 — aesthetic criterion impermissible, in both directions (no heightened nor lowered threshold via aesthetic assessment) |
| *"This is unique of its kind"* | Mio/Konektra paragraph 65 — no presumption of creative nature of choices; uniqueness does not suffice without proof of free creative choices |

### Attack line G — Art. 14 DSM Directive (anti-reviving) against claims on museum reproductions

For reproductions of works of visual art in the public domain (museum digitisation, high-resolution scans, photographic reproduction of iconic public-domain works), art. 14 Dir. (EU) 2019/790 offers a **particularly powerful attack line**: the regime is an **anti-reviving regime** that expressly keeps the public-domain work free. Reproduction acts create **no** new copyright **unless** the reproduction itself meets the EU originality test — and the burden of proof for that creative addition lies with the rightholder.

**When usable**:

- Opposing party is a museum, archive or heritage institution claiming copyright on digitised reproductions of public-domain works
- Opposing party is a publisher/photographer commercially exploiting mere mechanical reproductions (facsimile, professional scan) of public-domain works
- Opposing party carried out a high-resolution museum digitisation according to technical standards without its own creative choices

**Pleading material**:

> "Article 14 of Directive (EU) 2019/790 (DSM) provides *expressis verbis* that *'when the term of protection of a work of visual art has expired, any material resulting from an act of reproduction of that work is not subject to copyright or related rights, unless the material resulting from that act of reproduction is original in the sense that it is the author's own intellectual creation.'* The statutory presumption is that the work remains free; the burden of proving an author's own intellectual creation in the reproduction lies with the opposing party."

**Anticipating counter-arguments**: If the rightholder asserts that their reproduction was "creative" because of lighting, perspective, or composition — demand that they positively prove the Painer formulas (free creative choices + personal touch + unique aspect Mio/Konektra). Technically correct museum digitisation according to conservation standards does **not** suffice — those choices are technically dictated, not free creative choices.

### Attack line H — Anti-monopolisation of public-domain substrate (Călinescu paragraph 68)

When a rightholder claims copyright on a derivative work that builds on a **public-domain work** (critical edition, free adaptation, restoration, photographic reproduction), the alleged infringer can rely on the **public-domain safeguard** of Călinescu paragraph 68. Full copyright on the derivative work confers **no** exclusive right on the original public-domain work.

**Pleading material**:

> "Recognition of copyright on a derivative work does not confer on the derivative author a monopolistic right on the original work which is in the public domain. Our client remains entitled to rely on the original work in the public domain as a basis for its own independent derivative creation (Călinescu/FNSA, C-649/23, paragraph 68; confirmed by art. 14 DSM Directive for reproductions of works of visual art). The burden of proof for infringement requires the claimant to **specifically** identify the creative elements taken from the protected derivative work — not the public-domain substrate elements."

### Attack line I — Threshold enforcement through primary texts

Against rightholder claims that try to circumvent the threshold via "creative-height" substitutes, the **common "no other criteria" clause** in the three sectoral directives offers a formal rebuttal basis. The English versions of all three provisions consistently use *"the author's own intellectual creation"* — exactly the same formula as in Infopaq paragraph 37. This shows that the EU legislature expressly intended one harmonised threshold, neither more nor less.

**When usable**: against rightholders who try to obtain a lowered threshold via derivative criteria such as "originality in the sense of novelty", "deviation from the state of the art", or "individual character" (design-law criterion improperly transposed to copyright). The Cofemel two-step test remains the test; alternative threshold formulations not derived from Cofemel/Brompton/Mio-Konektra/Călinescu lack legal basis.

## Photography (based on Painer, C-145/10)

### Realistic expectations

Painer is strategically a **difficult judgment for the defence**: the Court expressly rejects two classical defence lines that national courts used to rely on:

- "Portrait photographs have limited creative possibilities, hence weaker protection" — Painer paragraph 93 (freedom "not necessarily minor or even non-existent")
- "Photographs have less extensive protection in any event" — Painer paragraphs 97-98 (no gradation according to creative freedom)

Anyone still using these arguments is in fact deploying an argument that has been *expressly rejected by the CJEU*. That undermines the credibility of the remaining position. Avoid it.

### Which attack lines do work

The only successful attack line runs via the **a contrario reasoning from Painer paragraph 89** (referring to FAPL): where there was no scope for free creative choices, there is no work. Strategically that means: show that in the three stages of Painer paragraph 91 *no* genuine free creative choices were made.

### Show absence of choice per stage

**Preparation stage**:
- No staged background, no pose direction, no light control
- Subject coincidentally encountered, photographer had no influence
- Passport-photo-booth protocol or identical setup for all shots

**Shooting stage**:
- Camera setup automatic or programmed
- Speed-flash, surveillance, security camera — no human intervention
- Fully automatic settings
- For sports and spot-news photography: merely recording an event without creative direction *[nb: this is treacherous; reportage photography also involves creative choices in moment, angle, framing. Use cautiously.]*

**Development / post-processing stage**:
- No or standard conversion, no substantial editing
- Fully automatic batch processing

### Strong categories for the defence

> *Indicative; the assessment must be made for each concrete photograph.*

- **Surveillance and CCTV photos**: no human creative choice
- **Speed-camera / traffic-flash photographs**: fully automatic
- **Passport photos from a booth**: standardised protocol
- **Reproduction photographs of 2D artworks**: deliberately no creative input; fidelity is the aim — typical case of "no free creative choice"
- **Microscopic / scientific standard images**: technically dictated protocol
- **Product photographs under a fixed standard protocol** (white background, frontal shot, fixed lighting setup): no free choice where the protocol is imposed

### Idea-expression distinction for photographs

A separate attack line that does not coincide with Painer but functions by analogous application: not everything in a photograph is protectable. The **subject** itself (a person, a building, an event) is not a work. What may be protected is the **specific expression** by this photographer of that subject. Hence rephotographing the same subject by another person, with their own choices, does not infringe the copyright on the first photograph (but may infringe on other grounds — image rights, etc.).

### Procedural attack line — per-photograph burden of pleading (Renckhoff paragraph 14)

Renckhoff (C-161/17) paragraph 14 expressly anchors the **procedural requirement** that the national court must verify "**in each individual case**" whether the photograph meets the Painer test:

> "[A] photograph may be protected by copyright, provided, **which it is for the national court to determine in each case**, that it is the intellectual creation of the author reflecting his personality and expressing his free and creative choices in the production of that photograph […]."

Operational use for the defence:

- **Demand a per-photograph statement of reasons**: a photographer claiming copyright on multiple photographs must indicate **per photograph** which specific creative choices were made in the three Painer stages
- **Parry general references**: claims based on "my style", "my technique" or "my signature" do not suffice — those do not qualify a specific photograph
- **In the absence of such reasoning**: seek dismissal of the claim for insufficient substantiation of originality

This is particularly effective in files where a photographer asserts a large bundle of photographs without per-item reasoning — a frequent pattern in mass-debt-collection actions on online photo infringements.

### Citation form

> CJEU 1 December 2011, C-145/10, *Painer*, ECLI:EU:C:2011:798, paragraph 89 *a contrario* (no work where no free creative choices) or paragraph 91 (three stages — argue that in none was a real choice made); CJEU 7 August 2018, C-161/17, *Renckhoff*, ECLI:EU:C:2018:634, paragraph 14 (procedural requirement of per-case assessment).

## Software and GUI (based on BSA, C-393/09 + SAS Institute, C-406/10 + Sony/Datel, C-159/23)

### Strategic starting position

BSA is **strategically strong for the defence** in every GUI/software case. The judgment provides three connected attack lines:

1. **Track exclusion** — certain elements fall under no copyright protection at all
2. **Technical-function exclusion** — components dictated solely by their technical function do not meet the threshold
3. **Idea/expression merger** — where the execution possibilities are so limited that idea and expression coincide

### Attack line 1 — Track 1 / track 2 / no protection distinction

First determine the correct legal basis. The claimant often chooses the most favourable basis — often wrongly:

- A claim invoking protection of **functionality, algorithms, programming language or file formats** fails. BSA paragraph 49 + art. 1(2) Directive 91/250 + (later) SAS Institute (C-406/10).
- A claim seeking GUI protection under the **Software Directive** fails — BSA operative part question 1.
- A claim seeking GUI protection under InfoSoc must prove the author's own intellectual creation.

Force the claimant to identify the precise legal basis and submit it to the right test.

### Attack line 2 — Technical-function exclusion (BSA paragraphs 48-50)

This is the **strongest attack line** for the defence. For each allegedly protected element:

1. Identify the **technical / functional function** performed by the element
2. Show that the expression **is dictated by that function**:
   - Standard form in the sector (Material Design, iOS HIG, Microsoft Fluent)
   - Universal conventions (magnifying-glass icon for search, X for close)
   - Technical standards or norms
   - Compatibility or accessibility requirements (WCAG)
3. Show that the **execution possibilities are so limited** that idea and expression coincide (BSA paragraph 49)

Concrete examples where this attack line works:

- A standard login form (username field + password field + button)
- A standard hamburger menu in a mobile app
- A standard checkout flow (cart → address → payment → confirmation)
- A standard tabular dashboard with sort and filter options
- A standard search-with-autocomplete pattern

### Attack line 3 — Building blocks vs. composition (Infopaq + BSA)

Where a claim asserts that the GUI as a whole is original, but only isolated, standard elements have been taken:

- Point out that copyright protects the **arrangement and specific configuration** (BSA paragraph 48), not the loose building blocks
- If only loose, conventional elements have been taken, there is **no taking of the bearer of originality**
- Analogous to the Infopaq doctrine for text: loose words are not protected, only their specific arrangement

### Attack line 4 — Independent creation

As with other categories of work: even if the opposing party proves originality of its GUI, it can be argued that the own GUI came into being independently (version control with date stamps, own design history, own wireframes prior to knowledge of the older work).

### Attack line 5 — Look & feel as concept (idea, not expression)

The doctrine rejects that "look & feel" as a general concept would be protectable (no specific CJEU judgment; follows from BSA paragraphs 49-50 and art. 1(2) Directive 91/250). Concretely:

- "Our app has a minimalistic design" — not protectable (style/idea)
- "Our app has a swipe-to-delete interaction" — not protectable (interaction idea)
- "Our app uses blue accents throughout" — not protectable (colour concept)

What **is** protectable is the concrete execution. Attack: show that only the concept was taken, not the specific execution.

### Evidentiary leverage for the defence

- **Anteriority**: older GUIs with comparable components/layouts
- **Design conventions and standards**: Material Design Guidelines, Apple Human Interface Guidelines, Nielsen Norman Group publications
- **Functional analysis**: UX expert opinion that the chosen form was functionally dictated
- **Documentation of own development**: version control, design files, briefings — for independent creation
- **Comparative screenshots**: showing that the common elements are sector-wide customary

### The SAS Institute attack lines (the "Murphy defence")

SAS Institute (C-406/10) is **the key judgment for the defence in software cases**. The judgment expressly legitimises what often happens in practice: a competitor develops a replacement product by observation and testing, without source-code access.

#### Attack line 1 — Functionality exclusion

> SAS operative part 1 + paragraph 39: **neither the functionality of a computer program, nor the programming language and the format of data files** constitute a form of expression of that program and are not protected under the Software Directive.

Operationally:

- "What my client has reproduced is only the functionality of the program — what the program can do, not how it was written."
- "Under SAS operative part 1, functionality is not a form of expression and not protected under track 1."
- "Using the same programming language or file format does not infringe the program."

#### Attack line 2 — Anti-monopolisation policy

> SAS paragraph 40: protecting functionality would amount to **monopolising ideas** to the detriment of technological progress and industrial development.

Operationally: use this as a **policy back-up** for the substantive legal argumentation. The Court has expressly stated that **free competition and innovation** are core values of EU copyright. A claim that *de facto* amounts to a functionality monopoly stands in direct conflict with these values.

#### Attack line 3 — Similar or identical programs permitted

> SAS paragraph 41: copyright "leaves other authors **the desired latitude to create similar or even identical programs provided that they refrain from copying**."

Operationally: this sentence is exceptionally powerful for the defence. Even a program producing exactly the same results as the first program is **not infringing**, unless **plagiarism** takes place — i.e., taking the specific form of expression (source code, object code).

#### Attack line 4 — Reverse-engineering legitimisation

> SAS paragraph 61 + operative part 2: copyright is **not infringed** where the lawful acquirer of a licence did not have access to the source code and limited itself to **studying, observing and testing** the program in order to reproduce the functionality in a second program.

The four key conditions:

1. **Lawful licence** for the program — has your client lawfully acquired a copy?
2. **No access to source code** — can you prove that your client never had access?
3. **No decompilation** — can you prove that no decompilation took place?
4. **No infringement of other exclusive rights** — can you prove that only observation/testing took place?

If all four are satisfied: **immunity under SAS operative part 2**.

#### Attack line 5 — Contractual prohibition on observation void

> SAS paragraph 53: any contractual provision contrary to art. 5(3) Software Directive (right to observe, study and test) is **void**.

Operationally: even if the licence contract contains a clause prohibiting reverse engineering, that clause is unenforceable. The defence can act on the basis of the lawful right to observe, study and test.

#### Evidentiary leverage for the defence

- **Licence agreement**: showing that your client lawfully acquired a copy
- **Internal documentation**: showing that no source code was ever accessed (organisational compartmentalisation, NDAs, technical lock-out)
- **Development history**: own design documents, architectural schemas, code commits with time stamps
- **Test logs**: showing that only observation and testing took place
- **Expert opinion**: showing that the functional results could be achieved by independent design

#### When the SAS defence FAILS

Be realistic about the limits of the defence:

- **Source-code access**: if your client did have access (employee mobility, leaked code, ex-employer materials) — SAS does not protect
- **Decompilation outside art. 6**: unauthorised decompilation is a separate infringement (cross-reference: Top System C-13/20, footnote mention in `methodology/03-software.md`)
- **Copying of expression**: if (parts of) source code or object code were actually copied, regardless of how — SAS does not protect
- **Other legal grounds**: trade-secret infringement, unfair commercial practices, contract breach remain possible

#### Anticipation on the "manual-flank attack"

A sophisticated rightholder will try to circumvent the functionality exclusion by basing the claim on the **manuals, training documentation or marketing materials** instead of the program itself. Counter-arguments:

- These materials are separate works that must independently meet the originality test
- Functional content of the manuals (parameter descriptions, command syntax) is itself often functionally dictated — Funke Medien attack applicable
- Mere taking of factual information from the manuals (parameter names, error codes) does not constitute infringement (idea/expression distinction)

### The Sony/Datel attack lines (the "reproducibility test")

Sony/Datel (C-159/23, 17 October 2024) is the **most recent and most powerful** software-defence judgment. The Court establishes a categorical limit on the scope of protection under the Software Directive: only what can be reproduced in a computer program is a form of expression.

#### Attack line 1 — The reproducibility test (Sony/Datel paragraph 37) ⭐

> Sony/Datel paragraph 38: "**the protection guaranteed by Directive 2009/24 is limited to the intellectual creation as it is reflected in the text of the source code and object code and, therefore, to the *literal expression* of the computer program in those codes** […]."

Operational use:

- **Identify what is allegedly taken**: source code, object code, or something else?
- **If something else**: argue that this element cannot be "reproduced" in the sense of the Software Directive and therefore falls outside its scope of protection
- **Concrete examples of non-reproducible elements**:
  - Runtime variables in working memory (Sony/Datel paragraph 51)
  - Behaviour of the program at runtime
  - Output of the program
  - Functionality (already excluded under SAS Institute)

#### Attack line 2 — Literal-expression boundary (Sony/Datel paragraph 38)

> Sony/Datel paragraph 38: protection covers only what is "reflected" in source or object code. Elements that the program produces *during execution* but that do not themselves form part of source or object code, fall outside that protection.

Operational use:

- For runtime modifications (cheating tools, trainers, mods that operate exclusively on variables in working memory): argue that there is no form of expression of the program in the Software Directive sense
- The fact that the modification has **effects** on the perception of the program does not transform the modification into a form of expression of the program

#### Attack line 3 — Anti-monopolisation (Sony/Datel paragraphs 36, 48)

> Sony/Datel paragraph 36: "[T]he protection of computer programs by copyright is not intended to ensure that the rightholder benefits from the rapid progress of computer technology."
>
> "Moreover, the competitors of the author of a computer program are free, once they establish through independent analysis which ideas, rules or principles are being used, to create their own implementation of them in order to create compatible products. **They may, moreover, build on the identical idea, but may not use the same expression as that of other protected programs**." (paragraph 48)

This dovetails with SAS paragraphs 39-40, BSA paragraphs 48-49 and Brompton paragraph 27 for the general anti-monopolisation principle:

- **Technological progress requires competition**: copyright must not create monopolies that obstruct independent development
- **Compatible products are legitimate**: developing a compatible product is a legitimate business activity
- **The same idea may be used**: only the form of expression may not be taken
- **Cross-reference 1989 Commission proposal**: the Court expressly refers to the recitals of the Software Directive as authoritative source

#### Attack line 4 — Clean-room evidence

Document and present a **clean-room reverse engineering trajectory**:

- **Independent analysis of ideas, rules, principles** (Sony/Datel paragraph 48) — permitted
- **No access to source code**: witness statements, NDAs, organisational compartmentalisation
- **No decompilation outside art. 6**: evidence that no object-code analysis took place, or that this took place only under the strict conditions of art. 6 (interoperability)
- **Own design**: design documents, architectural schemas, code commits with time stamps showing the own development
- **Observing and testing legitimised** (SAS paragraph 61): combined with Sony/Datel this strengthens the position

#### Attack line 5 — Runtime modification as categorical exclusion

For products that operate only at runtime (cheating tools, game trainers, memory editors, plugins, hooks, browser extensions):

- **No modification on disk**: the protected program remains untouched on its carrier
- **Operation only via working memory**: the tool acts only on variables during execution — exactly the Datel scenario (paragraphs 50-51)
- **The program must run simultaneously**: the tool cannot function without the protected program — points to a complementary (not substitute) function
- **Cross-reference Sony/Datel operative part**: "in so far as that content does not allow such a computer program to be reproduced or to be created at a later stage"

#### Anticipation on counter-arguments by the rightholder

The rightholder will try to circumvent Sony/Datel. Counter-arguments:

| Rightholder counter-argument | Defence |
|---|---|
| "We seek protection under InfoSoc, not under the Software Directive" | Sony/Datel paragraph 29 makes clear that the Court does not pronounce on InfoSoc; but to the extent the claim concerns non-code elements (GUI, audio, documentation), this is a separate work-concept test which must succeed on its own |
| "The tool causes indirect reproduction in working memory during execution" | Sony/Datel paragraph 51: variables in working memory are **not** a form of expression; and in any case the program itself is already lawfully loaded into working memory by the end-user (cf. art. 5(1) Software Directive, lawful use) |
| "We have TPM that is circumvented" | This is a separate claim under art. 7(1)(c) Software Directive / art. 6 InfoSoc — separate from the scope-of-application question. Defence here requires its own analysis (was there a TPM, is it circumvented, etc.) |
| "EULA prohibits runtime modification" | A contractual action is something different from copyright infringement. Moreover, EULA provisions undermining the statutory exceptions (art. 5(3), art. 6) are void (SAS paragraph 53 + art. 8 Software Directive) |
| "Sony/Datel concerned a specific factual configuration" | The judgment formulates a general criterion (the reproducibility test), not merely a factual assessment. Paragraph 37 is a statement of principle |

#### When the Sony/Datel defence FAILS

Be realistic about the limits of the defence:

- **Actual reproduction**: if the own software does contain (parts of) source or object code (embedded, in a loader, in an installer) — Sony/Datel does not protect
- **Decompilation outside art. 6**: unauthorised decompilation is a separate infringement, even if the end product does not reproduce code (cross-reference: Top System C-13/20, footnote mention in `methodology/03-software.md`; exceptions to exploitation rights fall outside the skill scope)
- **TPM circumvention**: art. 7(1)(c) Software Directive is an independent cause of action that does not require the tool itself to constitute infringement
- **Copyright on non-code elements**: GUI taking, documentation taking, audio taking — Sony/Datel offers no defence
- **Other legal grounds**: unfair commercial practices, parasitic acts, database right, breach of contract remain possible

### Citation form

> CJEU 22 December 2010, C-393/09, *BSA*, ECLI:EU:C:2010:816, paragraph 48 (technical-function exclusion) and/or paragraph 49 (idea/expression merger with limited execution possibilities).
>
> CJEU 2 May 2012, C-406/10, *SAS Institute*, ECLI:EU:C:2012:259, paragraph 39 + operative part 1 (functionality/programming language/file format not a form of expression), paragraph 40 (anti-monopolisation), paragraph 41 (similar programs permitted), paragraph 61 + operative part 2 (reverse engineering legitimised), paragraph 53 (contractual prohibition void).
>
> CJEU 17 October 2024, C-159/23, *Sony Computer Entertainment Europe / Datel*, ECLI:EU:C:2024:887, paragraph 37 (reproducibility test), paragraph 38 (literal-expression boundary), paragraph 48 (anti-monopolisation, clean-room reverse engineering), paragraph 51 + operative part (runtime variables not a form of expression).

## Sports events and rule-governed events (based on FAPL, C-403/08 and C-429/08)

### Strategic starting position

FAPL offers a **categorical exclusion**: the event itself is not a work. For the defence this is an exceptionally powerful weapon when the opposing party seeks copyright protection for:

- A **sports event** as such (FAPL paragraph 98)
- A **chess match, card game or other rule-governed competition** (by analogy)
- **Results, scores, statistics** (facts, not works — confirmed by case-law on facts)
- An **organised event** whose course is determined by external rules

### The FAPL attack line

**Three steps**:

1. **Identify the event** for which protection is sought
2. **Show that its course is dictated by external rules** (rules of the game, competition rules, technical norms, legal procedure)
3. **Conclude**: no scope for creative freedom → no author's own intellectual creation → no work (FAPL paragraph 98, confirmed in 99)

### Crucial demarcation: event vs. production

Beware: FAPL paragraph 149 expressly recognises that the **production surrounding** the event (camera choices, direction, opening videos, anthems, pre-recorded clips, graphic overlays) **can** be copyright-protected. The defence must therefore distinguish carefully:

- **What was taken?**
  - Only the event itself (the match) → FAPL attack line works
  - Also elements of the production (commentary, editing, graphics) → those may be protected
- **Narrow the infringement claim**: argue that at most the non-protectable part (the match) was taken

### Strong application domains for the defence

> *Indicative; the assessment must be made for each concrete situation.*

- **Live sports reporting without own production**: rendering match events by third parties — to the extent limited to facts/events, no infringement
- **Sports data and statistics**: scoreboards, final standings, player statistics — facts, no work
- **Re-broadcasting of only the gameplay** without taking commentary, graphics, intros
- **Chess game notation**: standard notation system for moves, factual reproduction of a rule-dictated game
- **Automatic recordings**: surveillance, speed measurements, sensor logs without human creative input

### Citation form

> CJEU 4 October 2011, joined cases C-403/08 and C-429/08, *FAPL*, ECLI:EU:C:2011:631, paragraph 98 (rules-of-the-game exclusion).

## Databases (based on Football Dataco, C-604/10)

### Strategic starting position

Football Dataco offers the defence **three powerful attack lines** against copyright claims on databases:

1. **Sweat-of-the-brow attack** — effort alone does not suffice (Football Dataco paragraph 42, operative part 1 third indent)
2. **Technically/rule-dictated structure** — where the structure is dictated by external rules, no originality (Football Dataco paragraph 39, drawing together BSA and FAPL)
3. **Creation versus structuring** — effort in generating data is irrelevant for copyright (Football Dataco paragraphs 32-36, operative part 1 first indent)

### First attack — Copyright / sui generis distinction

For the defence it is crucial in each database claim to **sharply distinguish** between art. 3 (copyright) and art. 7 (sui generis):

| What the opposing party argues | Under which right does it belong | What the defence answers |
|---|---|---|
| "We did much work to collect the data" | Sui generis (if substantial) | Not relevant for copyright — Football Dataco operative part 1 first indent |
| "We have invested millions in the database" | Sui generis (if substantial) | Not relevant for copyright — Football Dataco paragraph 42 |
| "Our database is unique by its size" | Sui generis (quantitatively substantial) | Not relevant for copyright; and quantity without originality fails art. 3 too |
| "We used only standardised categories" | (No protection) | Confirms no originality |
| "Our editorial team developed its own creative selection criteria" | Copyright (if demonstrable) | Here the test must be made concrete |

If the opposing party invokes **only** art. 3 without also invoking art. 7, the defence should investigate whether the claim is in fact a sui generis claim placed in the wrong track.

### Second attack — Sweat of the brow excluded ⭐

Football Dataco contains the **definitive EU rejection** of sweat of the brow:

> "[...] the fact that the setting up of the database required, irrespective of the creation of the data which it contains, **significant labour and skill of its author** […] **cannot as such justify the protection of it by copyright** under Directive 96/9, **if that labour and that skill do not express any originality in the selection or arrangement of that data**." (paragraph 42)

Standard attack line:

1. Identify the elements relied on by the opposing party (effort, expertise, time, costs, editorial care)
2. Point out that **none** of these elements suffices without originality
3. Cite Football Dataco paragraph 42 + operative part 1 third indent
4. Demand that the opposing party point to **concrete free creative choices** — not abstract "creativity" but specific decisions where alternatives existed

### Third attack — Technically/rule-dictated structure

Football Dataco paragraph 39 draws BSA and FAPL together into **a single general exclusion ground**:

> "By contrast, that criterion is not satisfied **when the setting up of the database is dictated by technical considerations, rules or constraints which leave no room for creative freedom**."

Three alternative grounds to argue:

| Ground | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Technical considerations** | Database architectures that automatically follow from data types; table normalisations; CSV/XML/JSON structures |
| **Rules** | Legal requirements (e.g., commercial register, statutory company registers); international conventions (UDC, NACE, ICD); rules of the game (match calendars) |
| **Constraints** | Hardware constraints; format requirements of third parties; legal restrictions on data use |

For each of these grounds: **show the external factor and thereby the absence of creative scope**.

### Fourth attack — Creation of data versus structuring it

Football Dataco paragraphs 32-36 draw a sharp distinction between:

- **Creation of the data itself** — not relevant for art. 3 (potentially for sui generis or for separate copyright on the individual works)
- **Structure of the database** — selection and arrangement; here the art. 3 test arises

The defence must ensure that the discussion focuses on the **structure**, not on the generated content:

> "The opposing party claims to have done much work to determine the match data, collect the addresses, perform the measurements — but these are activities in *creating* the data, not in *structuring* it. Under Football Dataco operative part 1 first indent, this effort is irrelevant for the copyright protectability of the database itself."

### Strong example cases for the defence

> *Categories of databases where the art. 3 claim is weak:*

- **Telephone directories, address books**: alphabetical ordering is conventional; no original structure
- **Match calendars**: dictated by rules and organisational constraints (Football Dataco itself)
- **Official registers and statutorily prescribed databases**: structure prescribed by regulation
- **Product catalogues based on EAN or ISBN codes**: classification follows from external standards
- **Market data with chronological or numerical ordering**: quotes, sports results, statistics
- **Collections according to international norms**: ISO classifications, NACE codes, UDC system
- **Automatically generated databases**: drawn up by algorithms without human creative input in the structure

### Evidentiary leverage for the defence

- **Examples of similar databases** with the same or similar structure (shows that the structure is conventional)
- **Reference to applicable norms or rules** dictating the structure
- **Technical documentation** showing that the structure automatically follows from the data architecture
- **Indications that the opposing party used standard software or standard templates**
- **Comparison with the first version of the database**: if the structure did not substantially evolve, this attests to conventional choices

### Anticipation on cumulative strategy

A strong opposing party will claim **both** art. 3 and art. 7. The defence must then **attack both claims separately**:

- Against art. 3: focus on the sweat-of-the-brow exclusion and rule-dictated structure
- Against art. 7: focus on the absence of **substantial** investment or on the fact that the investment was directed at **data acquisition** rather than at the database itself (cf. Fixtures Marketing 2004)

Loss on one track does not automatically signal success on the other — a strong defence pleads both.

### Citation form

> CJEU 1 March 2012, C-604/10, *Football Dataco*, ECLI:EU:C:2012:115, paragraph 39 (technical considerations/rules/constraints exclude originality), paragraph 42 (sweat of the brow excluded), and/or operative part 1 third indent (significant labour as such no ground for protection).

## Applied art, designs and industrial design (based on Cofemel, C-683/17 + Mio/Konektra, C-580/23 + C-795/23)

### Strategic starting position

Cofemel is for the defence one of the **most useful judgments**, particularly against rightholders who try to elevate the copyright test for applied art by demanding "aesthetic merit", "artistic value" or "designer status". The judgment makes clear that:

- The copyright test for applied art is **identical** to that for other works (Cofemel paragraph 35)
- **No** additional aesthetic criterion is permitted (Cofemel paragraphs 53-54)
- Design registration **does not** automatically entail copyright protection (Cofemel paragraph 52)

### The five Cofemel attack lines

#### Attack line 1 — No additional aesthetic criterion ⭐

> Cofemel operative part: a model may not "give rise to designs which, beyond their practical purpose, produce a specific and aesthetically significant visual effect", since this is "not such as to justify those designs being classified as 'works' within the meaning of Directive 2001/29".
>
> Cofemel paragraph 35: the copyright test is **uniform** for all categories of works.

Operationally — when the opposing party invokes the aesthetic, artistic or designer value:

- **Point out that this is not a legal criterion**: aesthetic value is irrelevant; what counts is the author's own intellectual creation
- **Demand that the test be made concrete**: which free creative choices were made? What is the personal touch?
- **Reject the implicit additional criterion**: the opposing party is in reality trying to apply an aesthetic test that the Court has rejected

#### Attack line 2 — Aesthetic effect is subjective (Cofemel paragraph 53)

> Cofemel paragraph 53: the aesthetic effect "is the result of an intrinsically subjective sensation of beauty experienced by each individual called upon to look at that design".

Operationally:

- The aesthetic effect is by definition **subjective**, hence cannot be a legal criterion
- Different observers experience different effects — no objective foothold
- The opposing party cannot deduce protection from its own (positive) subjective experience
- Cross-reference Levola Hengelo paragraph 41: legal certainty requires objective identifiability

#### Attack line 3 — Functional constraints (Cofemel paragraph 31 + Football Dataco exclusion)

> Cofemel paragraph 31: "[A] subject matter satisfying the condition of originality may be eligible for copyright protection, **even if its realisation has been dictated by technical considerations, provided that its being so dictated has not prevented the author from reflecting his personality in that subject matter, as an expression of free and creative choices**."

Operationally:

- Show that the form was substantially dictated by technical / functional / ergonomic requirements
- Show that the design choices follow logically from the use function
- Demonstrate that "alternative" forms would impair the function
- Cross-reference Brompton paragraphs 26-31 (technical-function exclusion in applied art)

#### Attack line 4 — Genre- and convention-bound elements

The "creative choices" often follow genre conventions:

- **Industrial-design conventions**: established proportions, typologies of "chair", "table", "lamp"
- **Industry-specific conventions**: design language of a sector (cars, electronics, furniture)
- **Fashion trends**: following a seasonal trend is not a creative choice; it is conformity
- **Styles** as such (minimalist, Scandinavian, retro): are ideas, not works

#### Attack line 5 — Design right ≠ copyright (Cofemel paragraph 52)

> Cofemel paragraph 52: "[S]uch a cumulation can be envisaged only in certain situations."

Operationally:

- The fact that a design is **registered** as a design does **not** mean that it is automatically copyright-protected
- The opposing party must **separately** show, for copyright, that the two-step test is met
- Design law's "novelty + individual character" is **no substitute** for copyright's "author's own intellectual creation"

In practice:

- Ask the opposing party to **separately** substantiate why the design is also a work
- Point to the fundamentally different objectives (Cofemel paragraph 50): design law protects industrial investment, copyright protects creative works
- Where in doubt: argue that only design law is available (subject to relevant registrations or unregistered Community design protection)

### Strong examples of applied-art claims that are weak

> *Categories where the copyright claim typically does not succeed:*

- **Standard clothing**: basic T-shirts, basic jeans, basic sweaters — form predominantly conventional or functional
- **Fashion-trend-driven designs**: follow a seasonal trend; the choices are not "free" but trend-dictated
- **Industrial packaging**: form predominantly dictated by product protection and logistics
- **Functional furniture**: form predominantly dictated by ergonomics
- **Mass-production articles**: no individual creative input
- **Product lines following a platform style**: stylistically determined predominantly by the corporate identity

### Evidentiary leverage for the defence

#### Anteriority and state of the art
- **Older designs** with comparable elements — from other collections, other Member States, from the past
- **Sector publications** describing the customary solutions
- **Design history** showing that the elements have existed for a long time
- **Trend reports** documenting the seasonal trend

#### Functional analysis
- **Ergonomic studies** for furniture and clothing
- **Technical norms** for industrial products
- **Expert opinions** by designers explaining the functional dictates

#### Convention analysis
- **Comparative screenshots/photographs** of competitors with comparable elements
- **Fashion and design magazines** documenting the trends
- **Purchasing catalogues** of comparable products

#### Design-law status
- **Design registrations of the opposing party** — show that the opposing party itself sought design protection (typically not copyright)
- **Expired design rights** — show that design protection has ended; a copyright claim as extension is suspect

### Anticipation on the Cofemel circumvention strategy

A sophisticated opposing party will try to circumvent Cofemel by:

- **Framing aesthetic effect as free creative choices**: parry by demanding that the specific choices and the alternatives be shown
- **Invoking artistic added value**: parry with Cofemel paragraph 35 (uniform threshold, no artistic quality required)
- **Invoking designer status**: parry with the objective test — copyright status does not depend on the maker's reputation
- **Invoking design registration**: parry with Cofemel paragraph 52 (cumulation only in certain situations; separate substantiation required)

### Citation form

> CJEU 12 September 2019, C-683/17, *Cofemel*, ECLI:EU:C:2019:721, operative part (aesthetic effect insufficient), paragraph 31 (technical considerations/rules/constraints exclude originality), paragraph 35 (uniform threshold), paragraph 52 (cumulation only in certain situations), and/or paragraph 53 (aesthetic effect is subjective).

### Physical useful articles — the Brompton attack lines (C-833/18)

For physical useful articles with a technical function, Brompton gives the defence five targeted attack lines.

#### Attack line 1 — Form determined solely by technical function (Brompton paragraph 33) ⭐

> Brompton paragraph 33: "Where the appearance of a product is dictated solely by its technical function, the product cannot be eligible for copyright protection."

Operationally — show that all essential elements are explainable as functional:

- Every form choice is derivable from a technical requirement
- For every "creative choice" there is a functional justification
- The claimed elements belong to the obvious execution of the technical principle

#### Attack line 2 — Idea/expression merger (Brompton paragraphs 27, 31)

> Brompton paragraph 31: "[T]hat cannot be the case where [...] technical considerations [...] **have not left room for creative freedom or where they have left room only so limited that the idea and its expression become indissociable**."

Operationally:

- Show that the "alternative" forms were not practically realisable
- Show that the author's choice was the natural / obvious execution of the technical solution
- Invoke the **anti-monopolisation** consideration (Brompton paragraph 27): copyright must not be used to monopolise ideas, **to the detriment of technological progress and industrial development**

Cross-references: BSA paragraphs 48-49 (idea/expression merger) + SAS Institute paragraphs 33 and 40 (anti-monopolisation).

#### Attack line 3 — Expired patent as anti-monopolisation argument (Brompton paragraph 36) ⭐

> Brompton paragraph 36: "The existence of an earlier, now expired, patent [...] [must] be taken into consideration only in so far as [this element] make[s] it possible to reveal what was taken into consideration in choosing the shape of the product concerned."

Where the opposing party previously enjoyed patent protection, the copyright argument can be seen as an attempt to prolong that monopoly after patent expiry.

Operationally:

- Ask whether the form was primarily chosen to give effect to the patent
- Ask whether the creative considerations stood apart from the technical dictates
- Show that the design coincides with the patentable invention
- Invoke the **anti-monopolisation** consideration (Brompton paragraph 27)

This argument is particularly powerful: it allows the defence to **shift the burden** for the rightholder. An expired patent on the product creates a strong presumption that the form was technically dictated.

#### Attack line 4 — Effectiveness of the form (Brompton paragraph 36)

> Brompton paragraph 36: "[T]he effectiveness of the appearance of the product in achieving the same technical result [must] be taken into consideration only in so far as [this element] makes it possible to reveal what was taken into consideration."

Where the chosen form is **particularly effective** in achieving the technical result, this suggests that the form was dictated by technical considerations:

- Ask experts whether the form is the most efficient execution
- Show that alternative forms would have been less effective
- Point to the correlation between form choices and technical optimisation

#### Attack line 5 — Time of assessment (Brompton paragraph 37)

> Brompton paragraph 37: "[I]t is for the referring court to take account of all the relevant aspects of the present case, **as they existed when that subject matter was designed**, irrespective of the factors external to and subsequent to the creation of the product."

Operationally:

- Point to the **historical context** of the design: which alternatives were then technically possible?
- Point to the **state of the art** at design time
- Prevent the rightholder from using posterior developments to enhance the creative choices

#### Important nuance — no automatic exclusion

The rightholder may rely on Brompton paragraph 26: technical dictates do **not** automatically exclude copyright. The defence must therefore not only show that there were technical dictates, but that those dictates **removed all room for free creative choices**.

#### Strong examples of useful-article claims that are weak after Brompton

- **Products with expired patent**: the copyright claim raises the suspicion of patent extension
- **Standardised industrial products**: form predominantly dictated by norms and function
- **Functional furniture** with standard forms
- **Tools** with technically optimised forms
- **Means of transport** whose form is primarily aerodynamically / ergonomically determined

#### Evidentiary leverage for the defence — useful articles

- **Technical analysis**: expert opinion on the functional explanation of each form choice
- **Patent texts and claims**: historical documents describing the technical dictates
- **State-of-the-art documentation**: trade manuals, norms, industry standards
- **Market analysis**: showing that alternative forms were not practical at the time of design
- **Cost analysis**: showing that the chosen form was the most efficient
- **Comparison with DOCERAM (design law)**: design law already has a function exclusion; show any prior rejection by EUIPO

### The Mio/Konektra attack lines (C-580/23 + C-795/23) ⭐⭐⭐

Mio/Konektra (4 December 2025) is for the defence the most **operationally powerful** core judgment in applied-art doctrine. It concerns on the one hand dining tables (Mio/Asplund) and on the other hand the modular furniture system USM Haller (USM/Konektra). The Court gives three principled answers, of which operative parts 1 and 2 serve the defence in this skill (operative part 3 addresses infringement and falls outside scope).

**Scope of application**: all claims regarding copyright protection of furniture, lighting, industrial design, tableware, fashion accessories, designer products, packaging-with-design-elements, decorative useful articles.

#### Attack line 1 — "Free choices without unique aspect" (operative part 2) ⭐⭐⭐

This is the **most substantial doctrinal addition** of Mio/Konektra. The central defence: even if the author was not bound by constraints, his choices must **reflect the personality by giving the subject matter a unique aspect**.

> "**Choices dictated by various — particularly technical — constraints which bound that author during the creation of that subject matter are not free and creative, and nor are choices which, while free, do not bear the imprint of the author's personality by giving that subject matter a unique appearance**." (Mio/Konektra operative part 2)

Points of attack:

- **Trend-driven execution**: the object follows a current design trend ("mid-century modern", "Scandinavian", "Bauhaus revival"). The choices are free but give the object no unique aspect — the object is a **conventional expression** of the trend
- **Conventional combinations**: the combination of design elements is generic for the product category (four legs + flat tabletop, backrest + seat + legs, cylinder + handle)
- **Functional variants**: all choices fall within what the target audience expects for the function type
- **Sector standardisation**: comparison with related products of competitors shows that the choices made are **not unique**
- **"Generic aspect" evidence**: question the rightholder to point concretely to which aspect of the object is unique — force specificity

#### Attack line 2 — Pre-existing similar works (paragraph 80) ⭐⭐⭐

> "[T]he creation by a different author of subject matter that is similar or identical to given subject matter, before the creation of the latter, **may constitute a relevant indication of the low degree, or even lack, of originality of the given subject matter**." (Mio/Konektra paragraph 80)

This is a **powerful new attack instrument**. Copyright imposes no novelty requirement, but pre-existing works may be an indication of **low or absent originality**.

Evidentiary leverage for pre-existing material:

- **EUIPO, WIPO and equivalent registers** for designs older than the claimed work
- **Catalogues, annual reports, trade publications** from the years before the work
- **Museum collections and design collections** with dating
- **Online design archives** (Pinterest, designboom, dezeen) with dating
- **Auction-house catalogues** (Christie's design auctions, Phillips, Wright) as dated references
- **Trade literature** on the design history of the product category
- **Statements from design historians** as experts
- **Earlier copyright proceedings** on similar products

Cross-reference: the Mio case turned expressly on the relationship between the "Palais Royal" tables and earlier registered designs in the European design register. Mio argued that the tables were "simple variations on older known models" — an argumentative route that the Court recognises as legitimate (paragraph 80).

**Important limitation**: the Court acknowledges in paragraph 80 in fine that "the possibility that two authors independently make similar or even identical creative choices cannot be entirely excluded". Pre-existing material is therefore an **indication**, not an automatic exclusion. But in **applied art** specifically, the presence of technical constraints is a factor that limits creative scope — which raises the likelihood of actual independent creation.

#### Attack line 3 — No presumption of creative nature (paragraph 65) ⭐

> "[U]nder the law of copyright, **the creative nature of the choices made by the author of the subject matter cannot be presumed**. Thus, the court before which the question of the originality of a utilitarian object is brought must **seek out and identify the creative choices in the shape of that subject matter** in order to be able to declare it protected by copyright." (Mio/Konektra paragraph 65)

Operationally: force the rightholder to deliver positive evidence. Points of attack:

- **Element-by-element analysis**: dissect the object into components and ask for each: is this functional? Is this conventional? Is this creative?
- **Demand concrete specification**: let the rightholder point precisely to which choices would be creative — often this remains abstract on follow-up ("the elegant line", "the characteristic composition")
- **Negative evidence**: show that the choices made are either constraint-driven, or conventional/banal, or a mere execution of a trend
- **Reject implicit burden-shifting**: refuse the implicit presumption that a polished design is *ipso facto* original

#### Attack line 4 — Author's intentions = ideas (paragraphs 74-75) ⭐

> "**The author's intentions lie within the realm of ideas. They can therefore be protected only in so far as the author has expressed them in the work concerned.**" (Mio/Konektra paragraph 74)

Points of attack when the rightholder leans heavily on design narrative:

- **Shift focus to objective form**: contest any argument that rests solely on the creation process, design rationale or "author's intent"
- **Show that the intent is not visible**: if the rightholder claims to have wanted to achieve a specific aesthetic effect, show that this effect is not identifiable in the end result
- **Refute post-hoc rationale**: design statements are often polished retrospectively; show any inconsistencies with contemporaneous documentation (presentations, catalogue texts, early marketing)
- **Cross-reference paragraph 75**: the Court states expressly that the court "[may not] base its assessment exclusively on those elements"

#### Attack line 5 — Aesthetic effect ≠ work (Mio/Konektra paragraphs 67-68 + Cofemel) ⭐

> "**The fact that a design generates, beyond its practical purpose, a specific and aesthetically or artistically significant visual effect is not, in itself, such as to justify that design being classified as a 'work', within the meaning of Directive 2001/29**." (Mio/Konektra paragraph 68, cross-ref Cofemel paragraph 55)

Points of attack when the rightholder leans heavily on aesthetic merit:

- **"Beautiful" is not a legal concept**: copyright protects originality, not beauty
- **Design status is not proof of originality**: "design classic", "iconic", "timeless" — none of these qualifications is legally decisive
- **Commercial success is irrelevant**: sales figures, market share and demand are not indicators of originality
- **Press attention and critical praise are not decisive**: professional recognition is incidental (Mio/Konektra operative part 2 in fine)

#### Attack line 6 — Rebut non-decisive factors (operative part 2 in fine) ⭐

Mio/Konektra gives an operational list of factors that "may, where appropriate, be taken into consideration, but [...] are **in any event neither necessary nor decisive**":

| Factor invoked by rightholder | Counter-argument |
|---|---|
| "The work was exhibited at MoMA / Pompidou / Stedelijk" | Not decisive (operative part 2); museums are not the legal yardstick |
| "The designer won the Compasso d'Oro / Red Dot / iF Design Award" | Professional recognition is incidental, not a substitute for the originality assessment |
| "The work is recognised in trade literature as iconic" | Not decisive; show objective creative choices "in the form" |
| "The designer has a unique design language and vision" | Intentions and vision are ideas (paragraph 74); relevant only to the extent expressed in the work |
| "The work is inspired by a specific art movement" | Sources of inspiration are not decisive; paragraph 79: only the "inspired work" itself counts |

#### Attack line 7 — Variant of an existing work by another (paragraph 79)

Where the work is in fact a variant or further development of an earlier work by **another author**:

> "[W]here the authors are different, that subject matter must then be regarded as an inspired work, that is to say, as a work that does not reproduce the creative elements of another work as such, but draws on them in some other way. That new work may nevertheless also enjoy that protection as such, **provided that the requirements** laid down by the case-law cited in paragraph 48 above are met." (Mio/Konektra paragraph 79)

Points of attack:

- **Identify the original work**: show that the claimed work is inspired by an earlier work of another author
- **Apply the two-step test to the delta**: only the **new** elements added by the "inspired" author can be original — not the whole
- **Show insufficient creative addition**: often a variant is merely a minimal intervention on an existing design; the delta rarely meets the originality threshold independently

#### Strong examples of applied-art claims that are weak after Mio/Konektra

- **Trend-driven furniture** in a current style (Scandinavian, mid-century revival, japandi)
- **Generic form variations** within a fixed sector typology (dining tables, sofas, armchairs)
- **Modular systems with functionally dictated elements** whose arrangement is trend-driven or conventional (cf. USM Haller, although there still to be assessed in concreto)
- **Designer products positioned on name rather than on form** — where the rightholder leans heavily on designer reputation but is non-specific about creative choices
- **Products with substantial earlier design repertoire**: the Mio tables (Cord series) against Asplund's Palais Royal tables — both heavily inspired by tables in the EU design register
- **Designs following an older commercially successful model** (typical "variant" strategy)

#### Evidentiary leverage for the defence — applied art Mio/Konektra test

- **Design-historical context**: trade literature on the evolution of the product category
- **Pre-existing design corpus**: design registers, old catalogues, museum collections, trade publications with dating
- **Sector conventions**: standard typologies, norms, customs in the sector
- **Trend analysis**: trade literature documenting the trends within which the claimed work falls
- **Comparative material**: photographs and specifications of similar products of competitors from before the claimed work
- **Expert reports** of design historians and sector experts
- **Demand specification from rightholder**: force concrete identification of creative choices "in the form" — often this exposes the generic nature

### Citation form

> CJEU 11 June 2020, C-833/18, *Brompton*, ECLI:EU:C:2020:461, paragraph 26 (technical dictates ≠ automatic exclusion but possible), paragraph 27 (anti-monopolisation, idea/expression merger), paragraph 31 (scope so limited that idea coincides with expression), paragraph 33 (solely technical → no copyright), paragraph 35 (alternative forms not decisive), and/or paragraph 36 (expired patent and effectiveness as indicia).
>
> CJEU 4 December 2025, joined cases C-580/23 and C-795/23, *Mio AB e.a. / Galleri Mikael & Thomas Asplund AB and USM U. Schärer Söhne AG / konektra GmbH e.a.*, ECLI:EU:C:2025:941, operative part 2 (free choices without unique aspect are not creative; non-decisive factors), paragraph 65 (no presumption of creative nature of choices), paragraph 68 (aesthetic effect in itself insufficient), paragraph 74 (author's intentions are ideas), paragraph 79 (inspired work must independently meet the two-step test), and/or paragraph 80 (pre-existing similar works as indication of low or absent originality).

## Text and fragments (based on Infopaq, C-5/08 + Funke Medien, C-469/17)

### Strategic starting position

Textual works form a heterogeneous category. Literary works practically automatically meet the originality threshold, but **purely functional texts** (status reports, standard forms, technical specifications, statistical overviews, meeting minutes, press releases with predominantly factual content) often fall outside protection on idea/expression merger. Funke Medien (C-469/17) is for the defence *the* core judgment: it provides an EU basis to reject protection for functional texts.

### The Funke Medien attack lines ⭐

#### Attack line 1 — Idea/expression merger (Funke Medien paragraph 24)

> Funke Medien paragraph 24: "If [...] reports [...] constitute **purely informative documents, the content of which is essentially determined by the information which they contain, so that such information and the expression of those reports become indissociable and that those reports are thus entirely characterised by their technical function, precluding all originality**, [...] in drafting those reports, **it was impossible for the author to express his or her creativity in an original manner and to achieve a result which is that author's own intellectual creation**."

The test requires cumulative demonstration of three criteria:

| Criterion | Operational meaning | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Purely informative character** | The text serves only as information carrier | Show that the text has no argumentative, analytical or persuasive function |
| **2. Content entirely determined by information** | No design scope alongside factual content | Show that word choice was dictated by technical / conventional / professional requirements |
| **3. Entirely technical function** | The text serves an operational purpose | Show that the text fulfils a specific administrative, technical, military or registration function |

Where cumulatively satisfied: idea and expression coincide → **no work**.

#### Attack line 2 — Sweat of the brow excluded (Funke Medien paragraph 23)

> Funke Medien paragraph 23: "[T]he mere intellectual effort and skill of creating those reports are not relevant in that regard."

Parry typical arguments by the opposing party:

- "The drafting required **substantial workload**" → irrelevant
- "The author is an **expert** in this field" → irrelevant
- "The report required **careful editing**" → irrelevant
- "Making the text required **much time and resources**" → irrelevant
- "Writing required **specialised knowledge**" → irrelevant

Only free creative choices in the **textual form** count, not the effort or expertise required to produce the text.

#### Attack line 3 — No per-fragment reasoning (Funke Medien paragraphs 22, 25)

> Funke Medien paragraph 22: "It is for the national court to determine whether [...] reports, **or certain elements thereof**, may be regarded as 'works' [...]."
>
> Funke Medien paragraph 25: "[Reports] [...] can be protected by copyright only if those reports are an intellectual creation of their author which reflect the author's personality and are expressed by free and creative choices made by that author in drafting those reports, **which must be ascertained by the national court in each case**."

Operational use:

- **Demand per-text reasoning**: a rightholder claiming multiple texts must indicate per text which free creative choices were made
- **Demand per-fragment reasoning** for partial takings: fragments too must be separately reasoned
- **Parry general references**: claims based on "style", "expertise" or "the copyright on the work as a whole" do not suffice
- **In the absence of reasoning**: dismissal of the claim may be sought

### Strong examples of textual works where the copyright claim is weak

#### Based on Funke Medien paragraph 24

- **Status reports with fixed structure** (such as the UdP reports themselves)
- **Standard forms** and standardised reports
- **Statistical overviews** and numerical documents
- **Technical specifications** whose content is determined by technical requirements
- **Meeting minutes** that merely record facts (without argumentative summary)
- **Inventories and catalogues** without original selection or arrangement
- **Routine administrative correspondence** (standard messages, acknowledgements of receipt)
- **Product descriptions** with purely factual features
- **Scientific measurement data and raw data**
- **Press releases** with predominantly factual content (no opinion elements)

#### Short fragments and titles

- **Banal or obvious formulations** do not meet the threshold
- **Descriptive titles** ("Annual Report 2024", "Minutes Board of Directors 15/03/2025") do not meet the threshold
- **Standard sentences** from business correspondence ("Thank you for your attention", "Yours sincerely") do not meet the threshold
- **Functional headings** ("Conditions", "Conclusion", "Summary") do not meet the threshold

### Evidentiary leverage for the defence — textual works

- **Comparison with other texts in the same sector**: showing that all comparable texts use the same or very similar form
- **Templates and template documents**: showing that the text is based on a mandatory or customary template
- **Prescribed structures**: regulations, norms or conventions dictating textual structure (such as norms for scientific articles, ISO standards for technical documentation, regulations for administrative reports)
- **Anteriority**: earlier texts with comparable wording
- **Functional analysis**: expert opinion on why the chosen wording was determined by external requirements
- **Negative plagiarism detection**: paradoxically useful — if a plagiarism scan shows that the text overlaps with other standard texts, this supports the argument that the text is not an original creation

### Critical editions and derivative works on public-domain works — the Călinescu attack lines (C-649/23) ⭐

For developed attack lines against critical editions, philological reconstructions, translations, adaptations and arrangements, see the module `methodology/07-derivative-works.md`. Călinescu/FNSA (19 March 2026) is for the defence **challenging** because the Court takes a broad stance, but there are five principal attack lines.

#### Attack line 1 — "Expertise immaterial" (paragraph 54) ⭐⭐⭐

Călinescu expressly states that **philological expertise and academic skill in themselves** do not imply originality. Points of attack where the rightholder leans heavily on the editor/translator/adapter's academic status:

- **Shift focus to concrete creative choices "in the form"**: contest any argumentation resting solely on subject-matter knowledge, expertise or academic reputation
- **Place burden on rightholder**: force the opposing party to identify concrete creative choices — which specific corrections, additions, arrangements were made, and why are they creative in the copyright sense?
- **Cross-reference Mio/Konektra paragraph 65**: no presumption of creative nature of choices

#### Attack line 2 — Mere transcription or facsimile (paragraph 57 a contrario)

Călinescu paragraph 57 makes clear that the Slușanschi edition "is not a simple transcription of the Latin manuscript [...] nor of a facsimile of that manuscript". A contrario: a **mere transcription or facsimile** does **not** qualify as a work. Points of attack:

- **Show mechanical character**: the edition reproduces the manuscript exactly (diplomatic edition), without substantive interpretative choices
- **No reconstruction**: the edition adds nothing to the manuscript; no corrections, no completions, no variant choices
- **No substantial apparatus**: only trivial footnotes (dating, location) without own analytical contribution
- **Comparison with digital facsimiles**: in the age of Open Access and digitised manuscripts, a mere readable transcription is rarely sufficient — available facsimiles show the same text

#### Attack line 3 — Scientific conventions and standards (paragraph 59)

Călinescu paragraph 59 excludes originality "where the redaction [...] was dictated solely by technical considerations, rules or constraints without any creative freedom". Points of attack:

- **Philological edition conventions**: the choices strictly follow customary edition practice (CTS standards, Loeb Classical Library conventions, Migne standard for patristic texts, EETS standard for Middle English)
- **Lemmatological standards**: choices among variant readings follow objective criteria (lectio difficilior, lectio brevior potior)
- **Apparatus formats**: standard formats dictate the structure of the critical apparatus
- **Edition handbooks**: binding guidelines of the publisher or scientific community

#### Attack line 4 — Public-domain safeguard (paragraph 68) ⭐⭐

Călinescu paragraph 68 is for the defence an **important anchor**:

> "[T]he recognition of copyright in the critical edition of a literary work in the public domain does not bring that work into the private domain and cannot confer on the author of that critical edition an exclusive right over that pre-existing work."

Points of attack on the infringement question (note: this skill addresses strictly the work concept + originality, not infringement — but paragraph 68 borders both):

- **The reproduced parts belong to the public domain**: show that the material taken is the original work itself (Latin text, old translation, classical composition), not the specific creative additions of the editor
- **Own alternative reconstruction**: a third party may produce its own critical edition of the same public-domain work, without infringing editor X's protection
- **Distinguish paragraph 68 from paragraph 64**: anti-splitting (paragraph 64) prohibits splitting for the **qualification as work**; paragraph 68 limits the **scope** of protection to the specific creative additions

#### Attack line 5 — Limits on the anti-splitting doctrine (paragraph 64)

The anti-splitting doctrine (paragraph 64) is in principle negative for the defence — it prohibits splitting the critical edition into protected and unprotected parts for the work qualification. But there are three limits the defence can invoke:

1. **The doctrine touches only works that "have meaning only as a whole"**: in an edition whose parts have meaning independently of each other (e.g., a stand-alone introductory study + critical apparatus + text, each with its own meaning), individual components can still be assessed separately
2. **The doctrine applies to qualification, not infringement**: for the infringement question Infopaq-Călinescu paragraph 67 remains applicable — partial reproduction is infringement only if the reproduced parts contain creative elements. Reproduction of the **original text** without the specific additions of the editor is no infringement (cross-ref paragraph 68)
3. **The doctrine does not relieve the rightholder of the two-step test**: the **rightholder must show** that the work as a whole is original and objectively identifiable (paragraph 65). The defence can continue to contest that the whole is original

### Strong examples of critical editions/derivative works where the copyright claim is weak

- **Diplomatic editions** without substantial creative additions
- **Literal facsimile editions** with only a brief introduction
- **Digital re-editions** of older editions without own editorial intervention
- **Machine-generated translations** (before editorial revision)
- **Standard transliterations** from non-Latin scripts following conventional tables
- **Re-editions** with minimal editorial changes (only modern spelling)
- **Scientific editions** whose choices are entirely dictated by edition handbooks

### Citation form for derivative works

> CJEU 19 March 2026, C-649/23, *Institutul de Istorie și Teorie Literară „G. Călinescu" e.a. / HK e.a.*, ECLI:EU:C:2026:213, paragraph 54 in fine ("expertise immaterial"), paragraph 57 (mere transcription/facsimile no work), paragraph 59 (technical considerations exclude originality), paragraph 64 in fine (limits on anti-splitting to works having meaning only as a whole), and/or paragraph 68 (public-domain safeguard: no exclusive right on the original work).

### Citation form

> CJEU 29 July 2019, C-469/17, *Funke Medien NRW*, ECLI:EU:C:2019:623, paragraph 23 (sweat of the brow excluded; intellectual effort and skill immaterial), paragraph 24 (three cumulative criteria for idea/expression merger: purely informative character, content entirely determined by information, entirely technical function), and/or paragraph 25 (case-by-case assessment by national court).

## Multimedia works (based on Nintendo, C-355/12)

### Realistic expectations

Nintendo / PC Box (23 January 2014, ECLI:EU:C:2014:25) is for the defence **challenging** where the work is a typical multimedia work (video game, mobile app with multimedia, AR/VR work): the complex-matter doctrine is rightholder-friendly, no heightened threshold applies, and holistic protection prevents individual components from being artificially isolated. But there are five attack lines.

### Attack line 1 — No "complex matter" — merely computer program

Not every software product is a multimedia work. For products whose graphic/auditory component is **minimal or trivial**, the defence can argue that Nintendo paragraph 23 does not apply:

- Mere CLI tool with decorative banner
- Server-side software without UI
- API without substantial multimedia dimension
- Database application whose UI reproduces standard elements
- Build tools, compilers, code generators

In such cases **only Directive 2009/24 applies** (see also `03-software.md`) and the attack lines on the Software Directive (SAS Institute, Sony/Datel, BSA) apply.

### Attack line 2 — No "own creative value" of components

Nintendo paragraph 23 requires that graphic and sound elements have an **own creative value** "that cannot be reduced to that coding". Points of attack:

- **Standard UI elements**: buttons, menus, dialogue boxes follow platform conventions (iOS/Android/Windows)
- **Generic gameplay mechanics**: jump-and-run, point-and-click, drag-and-drop are ideas, not expression (cross-ref SAS Institute paragraphs 39-40)
- **Functional sound effects**: beeps, alerts, feedback sounds are often purely functional
- **Algorithmically generated content**: where content is entirely derived from user input or random generators, the creative creation is absent (cross-ref Painer formula)
- **Components with solely technical function** (cross-ref BSA paragraph 48, Brompton)

### Attack line 3 — Per-component analysis possible

Although the Nintendo doctrine is holistic, it does **not** preclude individual components being assessed separately:

- **For a work whose components have independent meaning** (e.g., a mobile app whose text, image, sound can be used separately): per-component test remains possible
- **Pre-existing material**: identify which components already existed or were made by third parties (stock graphics, royalty-free audio, open-source code)
- **Distinguish "own work" and "compiled work"**: in predominantly compiled works the creative addition of the compiler decreases (cross-ref Mio/Konektra paragraph 80: pre-existing similar works as indication)
- **Limits on the anti-splitting doctrine** (Călinescu paragraph 64): the doctrine applies only to works having meaning only as a whole — not to works whose components have independent meaning

### Attack line 4 — Use the lex specialis boundary for software aspects

For the **purely computer-program components** Directive 2009/24 applies with its specific regime — including favourable exceptions for the defence:

- **Reverse engineering** (art. 5 Dir. 2009/24)
- **Decompilation for interoperability** (art. 6 Dir. 2009/24)
- **Runtime modifications** (Sony/Datel: variables in working memory ≠ form of expression)
- **Functionality, programming language and file formats** not a form of expression (SAS Institute)
- **GUI not under Dir. 2009/24** but under Dir. 2001/29 (BSA) — can work for or against depending on the case

Strategy: identify which components fall under Dir. 2009/24 and deploy the attack lines from `03-software.md`.

### Attack line 5 — Idea/expression merger for gameplay and design

Gameplay mechanics, narrative clichés, design choices and user-interface patterns can qualify as **ideas** (cross-ref SAS Institute paragraph 40, BSA paragraph 49, Funke Medien paragraph 24):

- **Gameplay mechanic as idea**: "a platformer in which you jump over obstacles" is an idea, not expression
- **Narrative cliché**: "hero rescues princess from castle" is an idea, not expression
- **UI pattern**: hamburger menu, swipe-to-delete, pull-to-refresh are conventions
- **Design patterns**: "flat design", "skeuomorphism" are styles/ideas

Show where the reproduced elements sit at the idea level rather than the expression level.

### Strong examples of multimedia works where the copyright claim is weak

- **App with standard UI** and content delivered solely by API
- **CLI tool** with decorative banner or trivial ASCII art
- **Software** whose graphical output is entirely determined by user input
- **Generic "skin" or "theme"** for an existing platform
- **Web work** with merely standard HTML/CSS templates
- **NFT** containing only a hash without substantive digital content
- **AI output** where the user merely provided a prompt without substantive post-production

### Evidentiary leverage for the defence — multimedia works

- **Anteriority per component**: showing that graphic assets, audio tracks or code components already existed
- **Convention and standard analysis**: expert opinion on which elements follow platform conventions or genre conventions
- **Reverse-engineering documentation**: showing that the attacking party performed clean-room reverse engineering (cross-ref SAS Institute paragraphs 50-61)
- **Independent creation**: parallel development without access to the original
- **Component mapping**: detailed mapping of which components fall under which regime (Dir. 2009/24 vs Dir. 2001/29)

### Citation form for multimedia works

> CJEU 23 January 2014, C-355/12, *Nintendo Co. Ltd e.a. / PC Box Srl and 9Net Srl*, ECLI:EU:C:2014:25, paragraph 23 (required "own creative value" of components; lex specialis boundary Dir. 2009/24 / Dir. 2001/29 — both elements offer footholds for the defence).

## Evidence and factual material

- **Anteriority**: showing pre-existing comparable material
- **State of the art**: handbooks, norm documents, trade literature describing the customary solution
- **Functional analysis**: expert opinion on why the chosen form was the only or the obvious one to perform the function
- **Comparison with third parties**: other objects that made the same "choices" — evidence that it was not a free creative choice
- **Genre typing**: showing that the elements belong to the standard pattern of the genre

## Anticipation matrix — typical opposing-party arguments and rebuttal

| Rightholder argument | Rebuttal |
|---|---|
| "It is the author's own intellectual creation" | Contest with functional/genre-bound analysis, anteriority, banality |
| "There are alternatives that were not chosen" | Show that those alternatives have drawbacks (technical, economic, ergonomic) that exclude them in practice |
| "Short fragments are also protected" | Correct, but this specific fragment contains only banal components or factual information |
| "There is a personal stamp" | The "personal stamp" requirement is no longer permitted in EU law as an autonomous test — the test remains "author's own intellectual creation" (Infopaq + Cofemel) |
| "It is a high-quality design" | Aesthetic value is not a criterion — what counts is creative choice, not quality |

## Points of attention

- **Examine the work concept first**: if that falls, the rest is superfluous — strategically stronger
- **Concretise functionality**: vague references to "customary practice" do not convince; deliver concrete comparison points
- **No artistic quality judgments**: avoid arguments along the lines of "it is not beautiful" or "it is not art" — irrelevant in EU law
- **Prove on the facts**: the legal yardstick is clear; the battle is fought on the facts. Invest in expert reports, technical documentation, anteriority overviews
- **Limited scope of protection as fall-back**: if originality cannot be excluded, then minimise the scope of protection
