# Position 1 — Pro-rightsholder

> How to argue that a concrete object IS a copyright-protected work.

> **Version 1.0** — based on fifteen CJEU judgments: Infopaq (general formula), Painer (photography), BSA (software/GUI), FAPL (sporting events), Football Dataco (databases), SAS Institute (software scope), Levola Hengelo (concept of work), Renckhoff (confirmation photography), Cofemel (consolidation test + applied art), Brompton (technical-function exclusion), Funke Medien (functional texts), Sony/Datel (reproducibility test for software), Mio/Konektra (unique-aspect requirement; non-decisive factors for applied art), Călinescu/FNSA (critical editions and derivative works; "skill irrelevant"; anti-severability rule; public-domain safeguard) and Nintendo / PC Box (multimedia works / complex-matter doctrine; lex specialis boundary Dir. 2009/24 / Dir. 2001/29); supplemented with **horizontal lines of argument based on the EU statutory floor** (art. 1(3) Dir. 2009/24/EC, art. 3(1) Dir. 96/9/EC, art. 6 Dir. 2006/116/EC and art. 14 Dir. (EU) 2019/790 DSM) — four lines of argument for the pro-rightsholder: "no additional requirements", the English-version formula identity, art. 14 DSM reverse reading for reproductions of public-domain works, and sectoral lex specialis effect. Follows the two-step test.

## Strategic structure

The argumentation for the rightsholder proceeds along **two consecutive lines of attack**, both necessary and cumulative:

1. **Step 1 — Concept of work** (Levola Hengelo): establish that the object is sufficiently precisely and objectively identifiable
2. **Step 2 — Originality** (Football Dataco): establish that the object is the author's own intellectual creation (free creative choices + personal touch, without an exclusion ground)

If step 1 fails, step 2 is never reached. The concept of work is therefore a **conditio sine qua non** that must be addressed first.

## Step 1 — Concept of work (based on Levola Hengelo, C-310/17)

### The Levola formula (paragraph 40)

> "Accordingly, for there to be a 'work' as referred to in Directive 2001/29, **the subject matter protected by copyright must be expressed in a manner which makes it identifiable with sufficient precision and objectivity, even though that expression is not necessarily in permanent form**."

### The three elements to establish

1. **Form of expression**: the object must be expressed in a form (not merely exist as an idea in someone's head)
2. **Sufficient precision**: the expression must be precise enough for the content of the work to be concretely identified
3. **Objectivity**: identification must not depend on subjective assessment

### Strong examples where step 1 typically succeeds easily

The following categories of works are expressly recognised by Levola paragraph 42 as "precise and objective forms of expression": **literary, pictorial, cinematographic or musical works**. By analogy: photographs, sculptures, software (source code/object code), architectural plans, technical drawings.

For **non-permanent works** (dance, performance art, theatre, improvisation, oral delivery): thoroughly document the performance (video, audio, witness statements) to substantiate objective identifiability.

### Strategic pitfalls to avoid

- **Describe the work itself, not the experience of it**: a musical work is identified through score or recording, not through "how it sounds to the listener"
- **Avoid subjective descriptions**: a work that can only be described through "impression", "atmosphere", "style" or "experience" (without an objective anchor) is not a work in the EU-law sense
- **For sensorially oriented products** (perfume, foodstuff): argue **not** via the taste/smell (Levola operative part) but via objective fixation (recipe as text, composition as formula, packaging as design) — for the sensory elements themselves no copyright protection is possible

### Evidence for step 1

- **Reproduction of the work itself**: manuscript, score, code, image, physical object
- **Fixation in a medium**: tape, video, photograph, copy
- **Descriptive elements that objectify**: technical specifications, dimensions, colour codes, length, structure
- **Comparison with other works in the same category**: shows that this type of object is objectively delimitable for third parties

### Examples — beyond the borderline

| Category | Objectively identifiable? | Note |
|---|---|---|
| Text | ✅ yes | Directly through reading |
| Painting | ✅ yes | Directly through looking |
| Film / video | ✅ yes | Through playback |
| Music (score or recording) | ✅ yes | Through listening |
| Software (source/object code) | ✅ yes | Through inspection |
| Choreography (notated or recorded) | ✅ yes | Through reconstruction |
| Improvisation during performance | ✅ yes (paragraph 40 non-permanence) | At the moment of performance |
| Live performance art | ✅ yes (provided documentation) | Idem |
| **Taste** | ❌ no | Levola operative part |
| **Smell** | ❌ almost certainly no | Logic of Levola |
| **"Style"** or "look" as such | ❌ no | Too abstract |
| **"Overall impression"** without concrete components | ❌ no | Subjective |

### Citation form

> CJEU 13 November 2018, C-310/17, *Levola Hengelo*, ECLI:EU:C:2018:899, paragraph 40 (objective identifiability) and/or paragraph 35 (two cumulative conditions).

## Step 2 — The central argument on originality

### Starting point

The EU originality criterion is **one single formula** (CJEU 16 July 2009, C-5/08, *Infopaq International*, paragraph 37): "subject-matter which is original in the sense that it is its author's own intellectual creation". This formula applies to all categories of works, not only to the three categories pre-harmonized sectorally (computer programs, databases, photographs).

### Operational approach: identify the creative choices

The core of the argumentation consists of identifying and making plausible the **specific creative choices** of the author (Infopaq paragraph 45):

- Which **choices** did the author make in form, presentation, language, composition, arrangement?
- Which **sequence** did they give to the components?
- Which **combination** is unique to this object?

For press articles specifically (Infopaq paragraph 44): originality lies "in the form, the manner of presentation of the subject and the linguistic expression". *Mutatis mutandis* for other categories of works — sector-specific refinement follows.

### Anticipate the banality/functionality defence

The other party will assert that the object is banal, conventional or technically dictated. The standard response:

- Point to concrete **alternatives** that the author did **not** choose — that proves creative freedom
- Establish that the choices made were **not necessary** to fulfil the function
- Point to the **specific combination**: even where individual components are banal, their original combination may carry originality

### Components versus the whole

A common pitfall: the other party focuses on individual components ("words are not protected", "that colour is not protected", "that basic shape is not original"). That is in itself correct (Infopaq paragraph 46) but **not decisive**. The rightsholder must consistently point to the **whole** or to the **specific combination** — that is where the author's own intellectual creation manifests itself (Infopaq paragraph 45).

### Short fragment or limited size? Not a defence

If the other party argues that the appropriated or allegedly protected object is too short (a fragment, a title, a short phrase), Infopaq expressly holds that even short fragments can be protected (paragraphs 47-48), provided they themselves contain the expression of the author's own intellectual creation. **Quality, not quantity**.

## Step 3 — Horizontal lines of argument based on the EU statutory floor ⭐⭐⭐

In addition to the Cofemel two-step test and the sector-specific argumentation, the pro-rightsholder has **horizontal lines of argument** resting directly on primary EU law. These lines of argument are particularly relevant where the other party invokes a **heightened national threshold** or attempts to impose additional requirements.

Full doctrinal treatment: `methodology/01-originality-test.md`, section "The EU harmonization framework for originality".

### Argument line A — "No additional requirements may be applied"

The statutory floor for the originality test is set out in **three sectoral directive provisions** that each contain the **common clause** that no other criteria may be applied:

- **Art. 1(3) Dir. 2009/24/EC** (computer programs): *"No other criteria shall be applied to determine its eligibility for protection."*
- **Art. 3(1) Dir. 96/9/EC** (databases): *"No other criteria shall be applied to determine their eligibility for that protection."*
- **Art. 6 Dir. 2006/116/EC** (photographs): *"No other criteria shall be applied to determine their eligibility for protection."*

CJEU case law (Infopaq, Cofemel paragraph 29) has **horizontally extended** this clause to the general concept of work under art. 2(a) Dir. 2001/29.

**When usable**:

| Counter-argument by the other party | Rebuttal |
|---|---|
| *"This design has no artistic value"* | Cofemel paragraph 35 — aesthetic criterion not permissible; rests on art. 6 Dir. 2006/116 "no other criteria" and horizontal Cofemel doctrine |
| *"This object does not bear the stamp of personality"* | Pre-Infopaq national criteria overtaken by reference to the EU formula; rests on the common clause |
| *"This photographic work has no artistic merit"* | Art. 6 Dir. 2006/116 — no artistic-merit test; Painer paragraphs 87-94 |
| *"Member State X applies a heightened threshold"* | Cofemel paragraph 29 + the three sectoral clauses — Member States cannot impose additional requirements |
| *"The court must first decide whether it is 'art'"* | Levola paragraph 41 + Cofemel paragraph 32 — the concept of work is an autonomous EU concept; no subjective-aesthetic judicial assessment |

### Argument line B — The English versions of the three directives consistently use the same formula

The Dutch and certain other language versions of art. 1(3) Dir. 2009/24 and art. 6 Dir. 2006/116 speak of "the author's own creation" without the word "intellectual". The **English versions** however consistently use "the author's own intellectual creation" — the same formula as in Infopaq paragraph 37. This shows that the linguistic variation is doctrinally not relevant and cannot be invoked to raise the threshold.

### Argument line C — Art. 14 DSM Directive (reverse reading) for reproductions of public-domain works

For reproductions of works of visual art in the public domain (museum digitisation, restoration photography, photographic reproduction of iconic public-domain works) art. 14 Dir. (EU) 2019/790 offers a **reverse-reading argument**: although the main rule is that reproductions of public-domain works remain free, an autonomous copyright **in the reproduction** does arise where the reproduction itself constitutes the author's own intellectual creation.

**When usable**:

- The client is a photographer who has made a photographic reproduction of a public-domain painting or sculpture with substantial creative choices (own lighting, perspective, recontextualisation, artistic interpretation)
- The client is a restorer/editor who has produced a scientific re-issue with apparatus of a public-domain work of visual art (potentially a critical edition — cross-ref Călinescu)
- The client is a museum institution that has performed more than technical digitisation (e.g. interpretative recontextualisation, comparative study, own composition)

**Argument**:

> "Art. 14 Directive (EU) 2019/790 (DSM) expressly provides that material resulting from an act of reproduction **is** subject to copyright or related rights **where** the material resulting from that act of reproduction is original in the sense that it is the author's own intellectual creation."

**Burden of proof**: the pro-rightsholder must positively establish the creative addition — apply the Painer formulas (cross-ref section *Photography* below). The claim must be limited to the **delta** (the creative addition), without extending to the original public-domain work (cross-ref Călinescu paragraph 68 public-domain safeguard).

### Argument line D — Sectoral lex specialis effect

For specific categories of works, the pro-rightsholder can rely **directly on the sectoral directive** instead of on art. 2(a) Dir. 2001/29:

| Category of work | Lex specialis | Argumentation advantage |
|---|---|---|
| Computer programs | Art. 1(3) Dir. 2009/24 | Explicit idea/expression dichotomy in art. 1(2); form of expression covers both source and object code (Top System paragraph 36) |
| Databases | Art. 3(1) Dir. 96/9 | Specific "selection or arrangement" formula + sui generis right as alternative basis (art. 7) |
| Photographs | Art. 6 Dir. 2006/116 | No artistic-value test permissible; Painer three operational formulas |
| Works of visual art in the public domain — reproductions | Art. 14 Dir. 2019/790 | Reverse reading (see argument line C above) |

## Photography (based on Painer, C-145/10)

### Starting point for a photo argument

The Painer test (paragraph 94/operative part question 4): a photograph receives copyright protection where it is the author's intellectual creation **reflecting his personality and expressing his free and creative choices in the production of that photograph**.

Operationally: work through the **three phases of paragraph 91** and concretise in each what the photographer has chosen. For each choice made: show that alternatives existed which were not chosen — that proves freedom of choice (Painer paragraph 89).

### Checklist of creative choices per phase

**Preparatory phase**:
- Background, location, set — determined by whom or what?
- Pose, posture, expression of the subject — directed by the photographer?
- Lighting, light source, mood lighting — captured naturally or chosen?
- Clothing, props, styling — determined by the photographer?
- Time — deliberate choice (golden hour, blue hour, weather conditions)?

**Shooting phase**:
- Camera positioning, tripod, height
- Lens choice, focal length, perspective
- Aperture (depth of field), shutter speed (movement), ISO (film grain/noise)
- Framing, composition, golden ratio, leading lines
- Angle of incidence, high/low viewpoint, frontal/profile
- Moment of release — "decisive moment"

**Development / post-processing phase**:
- RAW conversion, white balance, exposure correction
- Colour grading, look, palette
- Crop, recomposition
- Retouching
- Conversion to black and white, contrast adjustment
- Specific software and technique choices

### Key arguments

1. **Fill in the three phases**: identify per phase which choices have been made. It suffices that in *one* phase real free creative choices have been made (Painer paragraph 91 seems cumulative on its face, but doctrine accepts choices in one phase as sufficient for the threshold).
2. **Point to alternatives**: show other photographs of the same subject by other photographers — different choices prove that the choice was free.
3. **Personal touch**: identify recurring stylistic elements of this photographer — a signature (Painer paragraph 92).
4. **No "weaker" protection for portrait photographs**: Painer paragraphs 93 and 98 expressly reject this. Arguments such as "it's only a portrait photograph" or "limited expressive scope in realistic photographs" are not sustainable.
5. **Nothing requires artistic merit**: recital 17 of Directive 93/98 expressly excludes criteria such as "merit" or "purpose".

### Confirming case law — Renckhoff (C-161/17) paragraph 14 and Cofemel (C-683/17) paragraph 30

The Painer formula has been expressly reaffirmed twice by the CJEU for photographs:

**Renckhoff** paragraph 14:

> "[A] photograph may be protected by copyright, provided, **which it is for the national court to determine in each case**, that it is the intellectual creation of the author reflecting his personality and expressing his free and creative choices in the production of that photograph (see, to that effect, judgment of 1 December 2011, Painer, C-145/10, EU:C:2011:798, paragraph 94)."

Operational value for the rightsholder:

- **Generalisation of Painer to photographs in general**: Painer concerned a portrait photograph; Renckhoff concerns a travel photograph. Both fall under the same formula — usable in cases on landscape photographs, travel photographs, product photographs with staging, and journalistic photographs.
- **Threefold authoritative source**: Painer paragraphs 88/89/94 + Renckhoff paragraph 14 + Cofemel paragraph 30 together form a coherent CJEU line on copyright protection of photographs.
- **Anticipation of pleading burden**: Renckhoff paragraph 14 requires a per-photograph assessment. The rightsholder must **for each concrete photograph** document which free creative choices have been made in the three Painer phases — general references to "style" or "technique" are insufficient.

### Citation form

> CJEU 1 December 2011, C-145/10, *Painer*, ECLI:EU:C:2011:798, paragraph 89 (free and creative choices) and/or paragraph 91 (three phases) and/or paragraph 92 ("personal touch"); confirmed in CJEU 7 August 2018, C-161/17, *Renckhoff*, ECLI:EU:C:2018:634, paragraph 14, and CJEU 12 September 2019, C-683/17, *Cofemel*, ECLI:EU:C:2019:721, paragraph 30.

## Software and GUI (based on BSA, C-393/09 + SAS Institute, C-406/10 + Sony/Datel, C-159/23)

### Starting point for a software argument

First determine which **track** the element falls under:

- **Source code, object code, preparatory design material** → track 1, Software Directive (91/250 / 2009/24). Threshold low: only "author's own creation".
- **GUI, screen displays, icons, layout, visual elements, animations** → track 2, InfoSoc (2001/29). Threshold: author's own intellectual creation (Infopaq, Painer formulas).
- **Documentation, manuals** → track 2, assessed as a textual work.

### For source code / object code (track 1)

- The originality threshold is low. In practice it suffices that the code is not taken from a third source and displays concrete choices (variable naming, structure, algorithm implementation, optimisations).
- Evidence:
  - Version control (git history) shows authorship and evolution
  - Comparison with open-source equivalents — shows own creation
  - Code analysis: non-trivial architecture, own design patterns

### For GUI / visual software elements (track 2)

Test under BSA paragraph 48 — **arrangement and specific configuration of all components**.

#### Step 1 — inventory the GUI components

- Layout: positioning of windows, panels, regions
- Components: buttons, menus, form fields
- Visual identity: colour scheme, typography, icon style, depth treatment
- Animations and interaction patterns
- Decorative elements

#### Step 2 — identify creative choices per component

Apply Painer formulas (free creative choices, personal touch) to each component:
- Where did the author have real alternatives?
- Which choices are not dictated by technical function?

#### Step 3 — argue the arrangement as a whole

Crucial: even if individual building blocks are conventional (e.g. a rectangular search bar), the **composition, arrangement and configuration of the whole** may be original. This is an Infopaq selection/arrangement/combination test (Infopaq paragraph 45) applied to a GUI.

#### Key arguments

1. **Showing alternatives**: other GUIs for the same function making different choices — proves the freedom of choice (as in Painer)
2. **Personal touch**: recurring stylistic elements of the same designer or design system
3. **Not merely functional**: for each appropriated element, show that the specific expression appropriated is not dictated by function
4. **Combination of individually banal elements**: Infopaq teaches that even fragments and combinations of banal elements can be original

#### Evidence

- Versions, design files, Figma files with version history
- Briefs from the client and design rationale
- Comparative analyses with other GUIs in the sector
- Awards, design recognitions (not required, but supportive)
- Statements from UX/UI designers on choices and alternatives

### What the SAS Institute doctrine means for the pro-rightsholder

SAS Institute (C-406/10) is for the **rightsholder a double-edged** judgment. On the one hand it limits the scope of protection (functionality, programming language, file formats are not an expression of the program under track 1). On the other hand it **opens track 2** for programming languages and file formats as autonomous works.

#### What the rightsholder can NO LONGER claim under track 1

- The **functionality** of the program as such
- The **programming language** as an idea
- The **file format** as technical specification
- The fact that a competing program produces the same results

#### What the rightsholder CAN claim

- **Track 1 (Software Directive)** — literal source code or object code, code structure and organisation (provided not function-dictated), preparatory design material
- **Track 2 (InfoSoc)**:
  - **GUI** (BSA): arrangement and configuration, provided author's own intellectual creation
  - **Programming language as a work** (SAS paragraph 45): rare but possible — think of a developed specification language with own syntax choices
  - **File format as a work** (SAS paragraph 45): possible where the specific structure of the format is the author's own intellectual creation
  - **Manual** (SAS paragraph 67 + operative part 3): the selection, arrangement and combination of keywords, commands, options, examples

#### Strategic shift: from program to manual

Where the other party has reverse-engineered without access to source code or decompilation (SAS paragraph 61), a **claim on the program itself can hardly succeed**. The rightsholder must then **pivot to other subject-matter**:

1. **Manual of the program** — operative part 3: has the other party appropriated elements from the manual into its own program or its own manual?
2. **GUI of the program** — has the other party appropriated elements of the visual interface?
3. **Specific source-code elements** — can it be shown that there was source-code access (by whatever route)?

#### Evidence of source-code access or decompilation

SAS paragraph 43 is an **important gateway**: as soon as it is shown that the other party has had access to source or object code and used it to create elements, there is partial reproduction under art. 4(a). Evidence:

- Forensic IT examination (comment similarities, variable-name patterns, code structure)
- Documents on the other party's development cycle
- Statements from former employees
- Indirect indications (speed of development, complexity of result)

#### Anticipation on the reverse-engineering defence

The other party may invoke SAS paragraph 61 ("I had no access to source code, I only observed and tested"). The rightsholder must then establish:

- **That reverse engineering went beyond the licence** (although this argument has been limited since SAS paragraph 53/operative part 2: licence conditions prohibiting observation are null and void)
- **That the other party did have access to source code** (forensic evidence)
- **That the other party did decompile** without satisfying the strict art. 6 conditions
- **That there has been substantial manual appropriation** (operative part 3)

### Anticipation on the Sony/Datel attack (the reproducibility test)

Sony/Datel (C-159/23, 17 October 2024) increases the burden of proof on the rightsholder in claims on software elements that do not involve classical code reproduction (cheating tools, plugins, hooks, runtime modders, browser extensions, AI agents). The Court formulated a **reproducibility test**: an element is "expression" only where it enables the program to be **reproduced or subsequently produced** (paragraph 37). Runtime variables in working memory do not meet this and are therefore not protected under track 1.

The other party will invoke Sony/Datel to assert that its product merely facilitates "optimal use" of the protected program, without reproduction. The rightsholder can rebut this defence along one or more of the following lines:

#### Line 1 — Establish that reproduction does take place

Sony/Datel requires that the other party realises **no** reproduction of source or object code. Concretely:

- **Embedded source code**: does the other party's tool contain pieces of source or object code from the protected program? Forensic code analysis can prove this
- **Reproduction during installation or operation**: does the other party's software require an internal copy of (parts of) the protected program to function? A dynamic library copy, a memory dump, a patcher that modifies object code on disk — any of these makes the Sony/Datel reasoning inapplicable
- **Decompilation**: has the other party decompiled the object code to build its tool? Decompilation outside the strict art. 6 conditions is a separate infringement, independent of what the final tool does (BSA, SAS paragraphs 36-38)
- **Cross-reference Sony/Datel paragraph 50**: the Court proceeds on the basis that the Datel software effects "no alteration of source or object code, or reproduction of it". Diverge from this factual premise if it is incorrect in your case

#### Line 2 — Pivot to track 2 (InfoSoc)

Sony/Datel limits only the scope of **track 1** (Software Directive). Other works around the program remain protectable under track 2:

- **GUI appropriation**: where the other party reproduces visual elements of the protected program (icons, layout, themes), this is an InfoSoc infringement independent of Sony/Datel
- **Documentation and manuals** (SAS operative part 3): appropriation of keywords, examples, commands in its own documentation is a separate infringement
- **Audiovisual components** (relevant for games): music, video, character art are not "computer programs" and fall under different protection. Cross-reference: Nintendo (C-355/12) on hybrid categories
- **Game content as compilation**: a game can be a compilation of works (script, graphics, audio, program) — separate tests apply to each

#### Line 3 — Invoke non-copyright grounds

Sony/Datel does not preclude other legal grounds. Concrete alternatives:

- **Contractual restrictions (EULA)**: breach of terms of use prohibiting runtime modification gives rise to contractual liability
- **Anti-circumvention of TPM** (art. 6 InfoSoc, art. 7(1)(c) Software Directive): where the other party's tool circumvents technical protection measures (DRM, anti-cheat systems, integrity checks), this is a separate infringement independent of the scope question
- **Unfair commercial practices**: commercialising tools that undermine the game experience for other players may be wrongful vis-à-vis the publisher
- **Free-riding on another's effort / parasitism**: a classic ground in national tort/unfair-competition law where IP protection is lacking
- **Sui generis database right**: for games with large content databases (level data, character stats) this is an independent protection title

#### Line 4 — Anti-circumvention as an independent action

For products such as Datel's Action Replay, an **anti-circumvention claim** (art. 7(1)(c) Software Directive and/or art. 6 InfoSoc) is often the strongest route:

- The Software Directive protects **technical measures protecting a computer program** against **circumvention, distribution, sale, import or export**
- It suffices that the device/software is "intended solely to facilitate the unauthorised removal or circumvention of any technical device"
- This is an **independent** cause of action that does not depend on the Sony/Datel scope question

#### Multimedia works — the Nintendo outer boundary (C-355/12) ⭐

For developed argumentation on multimedia works (videogames, AR/VR, interactive installations, NFT works, AI multimedia): see the module `methodology/08-multimedia-works.md` and the section *Multimedia works* below. Nintendo / PC Box (23 January 2014) is **particularly favourable** for the rightsholder where the work is hybrid (software + graphic + sound + interactivity):

- **Full regime of Directive 2001/29**: the multimedia work falls within the full set of exclusive rights (reproduction, communication, distribution) and not within the more limited regime of Directive 2009/24
- **Own creative value of components** (paragraph 23): the digital encoding does not strip creative value from graphic, sound or other components
- **Holistic protection**: components are protected "together with the entire work" — no artificial severance between software portion (Dir. 2009/24) and non-software portion (Dir. 2001/29)
- **Anti-severability anchor** (cross-ref Călinescu paragraph 64): for multimedia works that have meaning only as a whole

## Sporting events and media productions (based on FAPL, C-403/08 and C-429/08)

### Strategic starting position

FAPL is for the rightsholder a **double-edged** judgment:

- **Negative**: the sporting event itself is not a work (FAPL paragraph 98). A rightsholder relying solely on rights to the "match" fails.
- **Positive**: all elements of the **media production around** the event are potentially protectable (FAPL paragraph 149). A good argument shifts the centre of gravity from the match to the production.

### Operationally applying the FAPL split

For each sports broadcast or media production, identify **what is** and **what is not** protectable:

#### Protectable (author's own intellectual creation possible)

- **Opening sequence, intro, leader, jingle**
- **Anthem, theme music, background score**
- **Pre-recorded clips**: highlights, atmosphere shots, interviews
- **Graphic visuals**: scoreboards, lower thirds, statistic overlays, on-screen logos
- **Direction and camera choices**: framing, switching between cameras, slow motion, instant replays
- **Commentary**: linguistic elaboration, narrative choices, mood
- **Editing**: programme structure, integration of studio and live footage

For each of these elements, the general Painer test applies (free creative choices, personal touch).

#### Not protectable (rule-dictated, FAPL paragraph 98)

- The course of the match itself
- Results, statistics, final scores (facts)
- Rules of the game (methods, ideas)

### Key arguments for the rightsholder

1. **Identify the specific protectable components** of the media production (as in FAPL paragraph 149)
2. **Argue for each component** the free creative choices (Painer formulas)
3. **Avoid claiming "the match report" globally**: split it into protectable and non-protectable parts
4. **At the national level**: combine with any **sui generis right for organisers of sporting events** where available — this is not copyright but a specific economic right that does protect the broadcast

### Evidence

- Storyboards, director's notes, broadcast plans
- Statements from directors on choices (camera positions, switching, editing)
- Comparison with other sports broadcasts — shows the creative latitude
- Awards, recognitions for sports direction
- Original music and graphic design files with version control

### Citation form

> CJEU 4 October 2011, joined cases C-403/08 and C-429/08, *FAPL*, ECLI:EU:C:2011:631, paragraph 149 (media production protectable) and — where defence claims the event itself — paragraph 98 (match itself not a work, but production is).

## Databases (based on Football Dataco, C-604/10)

### Strategic starting position

For the rightsholder, Football Dataco offers two parallel protection tracks that are independent but **cumulative** (art. 7(4) Directive 96/9):

- **Copyright** (art. 3): originality in selection or arrangement — protects the **structure**
- **Sui generis** (art. 7): substantial investment — protects the **contents**

A strong argument for a strong database claims **both rights side by side**: copyright for the structural creativity, sui generis right for the investment in data obtaining/verification/presentation.

### The argument for copyright protection

The key element is **free and creative choices in selection or arrangement** (Football Dataco paragraph 38, based on Painer). Concretely:

#### For selection

- Which data were chosen for inclusion, and which excluded?
- Which criteria did the maker apply, and were alternative criteria available?
- Was the selection editorial-curatorial (own choices) or merely mechanical (all available data)?

#### For arrangement

- What ordering scheme was applied?
- Is this scheme unique or generic (alphabetical, chronological, numerical)?
- How is the categorisation built up? Which hierarchies, which cross-links?
- For digital databases: how is the navigation structure built up?

### Strong example cases

- **Anthologies** in which the compiler makes a personal selection from a larger corpus
- **Exhibition catalogues** with thematic rather than strictly alphabetical arrangement
- **Online platforms** with an original navigation structure and own categorisation system
- **Specialised editorial databases** where selection itself constitutes the added value (e.g. "essential precedents" in an area of law)
- **Scientific databases** with their own developed classification system (rather than an existing nomenclature)

### Evidence

- **Editorial guidelines** explaining the selection criteria
- **Documentation of considered alternatives** for the structure
- **Version control** of the classification system
- **Expert consultations** on the architecture of the database
- **Comparison with competing databases** to illustrate own choices
- **Statements from the editors/architects** of the database

### Anticipation on the sweat-of-the-brow defence

The other party may argue that the claim is based merely on labour (sweat of the brow), which since Football Dataco is no longer a basis. The pro-rightsholder must therefore **sharply distinguish**:

- **For copyright** (art. 3): substantive creative choices in the structure, not the amount of work
- **For sui generis** (art. 7): substantial investment, here relevant

The labour thus shifts to the sui generis track, while for copyright **attention must focus entirely on the creative choices**.

### Citation form

> CJEU 1 March 2012, C-604/10, *Football Dataco*, ECLI:EU:C:2012:115, paragraph 38 (originality through free creative choices in selection/arrangement) and/or operative part 1 (complete test).

## Applied art, designs and industrial design (based on Cofemel, C-683/17 + Mio/Konektra, C-580/23 + C-795/23)

### Strategic starting position

For the rightsholder, Cofemel presents a **mixed picture**:

- **Positive**: the prohibition of an additional aesthetic criterion means that applied art **may not be subjected to stricter conditions** than other works (paragraph 35: "no less than that enjoyed by any work falling within the scope of that directive")
- **Negative**: an "aesthetically marked visual effect" is **not sufficient** to qualify as a work (operative part); the full two-step test must be applied

The winning strategy is therefore: **do not rely on aesthetic arguments**, but fully substantiate the two-step test — concept of work (Levola) + originality (Painer/Football Dataco).

### Step 1 — Concept of work for applied art

Document the **objective identifiability**:

- **Drawings and designs**: digital or paper sketches that precisely depict the object
- **Technical specifications**: dimensions, materials, colour codes, finishes
- **Prototypes and samples**: physical realisations
- **3D models and CAD files**: digital depictions
- **Photographs**: documentation of the final object
- **Design rationale**: statements from the designer on the choices made

A clothing model is identified through patterns, cut features, seams and placements — **not** through "appearance" or "style". A furniture piece is identified through dimensions and design — not through "the atmosphere it evokes".

### Step 2 — Originality for applied art

#### Positive argumentation (Painer formula + Cofemel paragraph 30)

Identify **free creative choices**:

- What alternatives did the designer have? Which choices did they make?
- Which specific elements reflect the author's personality?
- Which original combinations of form, colour, material and proportion have been made?

Concrete examples of free creative choices in applied art:

| Category of work | Possible free creative choices |
|---|---|
| Clothing model | Cut lines, seam placement, material combinations, colour palette, specific detailing |
| Design furniture | Design, proportions, material choice, construction method, decorative elements |
| Jewellery | Composition, shape of elements, material choice, surface treatment |
| Lamps | Housing shape, light distribution as a design choice, material combination |
| Packaging | Design, graphic composition, typography choices, material choice |
| Industrial products | Specific design not dictated by function |

#### Anticipation on the exclusion grounds (Cofemel paragraph 31)

- **Technical considerations**: identify which elements are **not** dictated by function (which shape was free?)
- **External rules**: identify which elements fall **outside** genre conventions
- **Constraints**: document that despite constraints the designer had creative latitude

### Strong example cases

- **Iconic design furniture** with distinctive design (think of classics in museum collections)
- **Author designs in haute couture** with distinctive creative elements
- **Jewellery** with original design choices (composition, material combination)
- **Sculpture in functional context** (vases, lamps)
- **Original typefaces**
- **Unique industrial forms** without functional necessity

### Cumulation strategy

**Claim parallel design right and copyright** where possible:

- **For design right**: establish novelty and individual character (registered design or unregistered Community design of 3 years)
- **For copyright**: fully apply the two-step test
- **Do not rely on design registration** as evidence of originality for copyright (Cofemel paragraph 52)

#### When design right alone suffices

For **standard products** or products with predominantly functional design, design right is usually the appropriate track:

- Functional furniture with standard forms
- Basic clothing models
- Packaging with standard graphic elements
- Industrial products with technical design requirements

#### When copyright offers added value

Copyright offers added value where:

- The design displays **free creative choices** that transcend function
- The **term** of protection is relevant (life + 70 years versus 25 years for registered design)
- Design right is **expired or unavailable** (e.g. too old designs)
- Additional **moral rights** are desired (paternity, integrity)

### Evidence for the rightsholder

- **Design history**: design sketches, variants, rejected alternatives
- **Designer statements**: on the choices made and the design rationale
- **Mood boards and inspirational material**: documents the creative process
- **Comparison with sector standards**: shows where the design departs from the conventional
- **Recognition in the sector**: awards, publications (supportive, not decisive)
- **Design registrations**: evidence of the creation and publication date

### Citation form

> CJEU 12 September 2019, C-683/17, *Cofemel*, ECLI:EU:C:2019:721, paragraph 29 (two-step test), paragraph 30 (Painer formula), paragraph 35 (uniform scope of protection), and/or paragraphs 43-47 (cumulation copyright + design right).

### Physical utilitarian articles — the Brompton test (C-833/18)

For physical utilitarian articles with a technical function (furniture, tools, means of transport, packaging, industrial products) a **refined version** of the BSA doctrine applies: technical dictates do **not automatically** exclude copyright.

#### The Brompton strategy for the rightsholder

1. **Document all free creative choices** of the designer — especially those falling outside strict technical dictates
2. **Show the design rationale** through design drawings, variants and rejected alternatives
3. **Point to alternative forms** as an indication of freedom of choice (Brompton paragraph 35) — but also substantiate the **concrete creative choices**, since alternatives are not decisive
4. **Anticipate the expired-patent argument**: show that the design was not solely technically determined but also aesthetically / personally
5. **Focus on the design moment** (Brompton paragraph 37): document the designer's considerations at the moment of creation, irrespective of later developments

#### Concrete argument

> "The utilitarian article concerned is admittedly functional, but the specific design does not flow exclusively from technical dictates. At the design moment the author had the choice between several implementations that would have achieved the technical result equally. The chosen design — [enumerate specific elements: lines, proportions, material choices, degree of curvature, placement of components] — reflects the author's personality through free and creative choices (Brompton paragraph 26)."

#### Evidence for utilitarian articles

- **Design sketches and variants**: show that real choices were made
- **Comparison with other solutions on the market**: show that other designs achieved comparable technical results with different forms
- **Designer statements**: on considerations at the design moment
- **Awards, museum collections, design recognitions**: supportive evidence of creative value (not decisive but strengthening)
- **Patent analysis**: show that the patent protected the technical principle, while the copyright claim concerns the specific design choices

#### Anticipation on typical Brompton attacks

| Counter-argument | Rebuttal |
|---|---|
| "The form is technically dictated" (Brompton paragraph 33) | Show creative latitude within the technical constraints; refer to paragraph 26 (technical dictates ≠ automatic exclusion) |
| "Idea and expression merge" (Brompton paragraph 31) | Show that several implementations were possible; refer to existing alternative market solutions |
| "The expired patent suggests technical dictates" (Brompton paragraph 36) | Show that the patent protected the method, not the specific design; document the separate creative choices |
| "Alternatives do not sufficiently prove" (Brompton paragraph 35) | Confirm that alternatives are only one element; combine with design history and designer statements |

### Anticipation on the Mio/Konektra attacks (the "unique aspect" test) ⭐

Mio/Konektra (joined cases C-580/23 and C-795/23, 4 December 2025) is for the pro-rightsholder double-edged:

- **Positive**: operative part 1 definitively excludes any rule-exception relationship between design right and copyright. No heightened requirements apply to applied art
- **Negative**: operative part 2 adds a second exclusion ground (free choices without a unique aspect) and excludes any presumption of creative nature (paragraph 65)

The rightsholder must rebut four main attacks:

#### Attack 1 — "The choices are free, but do not give a unique aspect" (operative part 2)

Rebuttal: identify concrete design elements that **specifically distinguish** the work from conventional implementations in the sector. Point to:

- **Specific proportions** typical of this work
- **Unexpected combinations** of design elements
- **Detail choices** giving the work an own identity (finishes, surface treatment, connection methods, material combinations)
- **Connection with a recognisable design language** of the author (consistency with other work by the same author)

Avoid the pitfall of "this is beautiful and recognisable" — Mio/Konektra paragraph 68 expressly states that **a striking aesthetic effect on its own is insufficient**.

#### Attack 2 — "Pre-existing similar works show low or no originality" (paragraph 80)

The other party will adduce older registered designs or well-known designs from the sector heritage as an indication that the work has no originality. Rebuttals:

- **Show the specific differences**: avoid generalities ("it is different"); precisely identify the creative choices that distinguish the work from the pre-existing material
- **Invoke independent creation**: document that the author was not aware of the cited examples (design books, witness statements, development documentation)
- **Qualify the pre-existing material**: are the cited designs really similar, or merely the same category/function? Categorical similarity is not formal similarity
- **Cross-reference paragraph 80 in fine**: the Court expressly recognises that "the possibility that two authors will independently make similar or even identical creative choices cannot be entirely excluded"

#### Attack 3 — "No presumption of creative nature" (paragraph 65)

The other party will require the rightsholder to produce positive evidence. Rebuttals — produce positive evidence:

- **Identify creative choices "in the form"** (paragraph 65): not "the author chose", but "the work shows choice X resulting in form Y"
- **Evidence in the work itself**: surface-level analysis of the specific design elements
- **Comparison with a conventional implementation**: a design of the same category but without the creative choices shows what the delta is

#### Attack 4 — "Intentions and stories are ideas" (paragraph 74)

Avoid building the argument entirely on the design process or design rationale. Mio/Konektra paragraph 74 expressly states that intentions are ideas and are protected only insofar as expressed in the work.

Permitted (paragraph 75):

- Design statements as an **aid** in making the creative choices in the work visible
- Design rationale as **background** to identifying choices
- Briefs and mood boards as **context** to illustrate alternatives

Not permitted: relying for originality exclusively on testimony, narrative or "story behind the design" without connection to the objective form.

#### Non-decisive factors — to be mentioned, not as core

Mio/Konektra operative part 2 in fine expressly states that the following factors "may, where appropriate, be taken into account, but [...] in any event neither necessary nor decisive":

- Author's intentions, sources of inspiration
- Use of pre-existing forms
- Possibility of independent creation
- Recognition in professional circles, museums, exhibitions, awards

Mention these elements **incidentally** but **do not build the argument** around design awards or museum acquisitions. Professional recognition is supportive evidence, not an autonomous basis.

### Citation form

> CJEU 11 June 2020, C-833/18, *Brompton*, ECLI:EU:C:2020:461, paragraph 26 (technical dictates ≠ automatic exclusion), paragraph 34 (operational test: author has expressed creative ability in original manner), and/or operative part (original work despite partly technically dictated form).
>
> CJEU 4 December 2025, joined cases C-580/23 and C-795/23, *Mio AB and Others / Galleri Mikael & Thomas Asplund AB and USM U. Schärer Söhne AG / konektra GmbH and Others*, ECLI:EU:C:2025:941, operative part 1 (no heightened threshold for applied art), paragraph 78 (use of pre-existing forms does not exclude originality; the arrangement can carry it), and/or paragraph 79 (variant of own work: protection as long as creative elements remain).

## Text and fragments (based on Infopaq, C-5/08 + Funke Medien, C-469/17)

### Strategic starting position

Textual works are a heterogeneous category. Literary works (novels, poems, essays, columns) almost automatically meet the originality threshold; **purely functional texts** (status reports, standard forms, technical specifications) usually do not. The intermediate category — reports with argumentative elements, policy notes, legal opinions, marketing texts, journalistic articles — requires a case-by-case analysis based on **Infopaq** and **Funke Medien**.

### The Infopaq formula as starting point

> Infopaq (C-5/08) paragraphs 45-47: the originality of a textual work flows from the **selection, sequence and combination of the words** through which the author has expressed their creativity in an original manner.

#### Operational application for the rightsholder

For each of the three pillars, document which free creative choices the author has made:

| Pillar | Burden of proof |
|---|---|
| **Selection of words** | Show that the author has chosen specific words from the available lexical pool — synonyms rejected, register choices, deliberate avoidance or use of jargon |
| **Sequence of words** | Show the textual structure — sentence structure, paragraph order, rhetorical build-up, chapter division |
| **Combination of words** | Show stylistic elements — imagery, rhythm, alliteration, wordplay, lexical combinations characteristic of the author's style |

### Funke Medien as a defensive anchor for functional texts

For texts falling outside the literary category, Funke Medien (C-469/17) is the crucial test. The rightsholder must **anticipate** the Funke Medien attack lines.

#### The Funke Medien strategy for the rightsholder

1. **Point to non-purely-informative character**: show that the text does more than merely communicate facts — argumentation, analysis, synthesis, evaluation, recommendations
2. **Point to expressive room**: show that the same information could have been presented in other wording, structure or build-up — present concrete alternative formulations
3. **Identify the creative elements**: identify per section or fragment which free creative choices were made
4. **Request fragmentary assessment**: Funke Medien paragraph 22 permits **certain fragments** to be works even where the text as a whole is not protected — choose the most creative passages
5. **Do not invoke sweat of the brow**: significant research effort or expertise is not a valid argument (Funke Medien paragraph 23). Focus on the **creative expressive choices**.

#### Strong examples of administrative texts that may receive protection

- **Policy notes** with argumentative structure, analysis and recommendations
- **Legal opinions** with reasoned argument, professionally constructed argumentation
- **Research reports** with conclusions, analysis and interpretation
- **Scientific articles** where the linguistic framing of results is original
- **Marketing texts** with persuasive elements, original phrasing, creative tone
- **Journalistic articles** with argumentative / analytical / opinion elements
- **Strategic plans** with vision, narrative structure and original phrasing

#### Evidence for the rightsholder

- **Comparison with other texts on the same subject**: shows that different authors phrase the same information in different ways
- **Earlier drafts and versions**: show that the final text is the result of deliberate expressive choices
- **Identification of signature style elements**: recurring stylistic features of the author
- **Citation or plagiarism detection reports**: show that the textual expression is unique

### Short fragments and titles — Infopaq threshold

> Infopaq paragraph 47: fragments of **only 11 words** may be protected, provided they constitute the expression of the author's own intellectual creation.

Operationally for the rightsholder:

- For short fragments (titles, slogans, opening sentences): the burden of proof is heightened, but protection is **possible**
- Substantiate why this specific word combination is the expression of free creative choices
- Point to alternative formulations the author could have chosen
- Compare with banal or obvious formulations to show the creative difference

### Anticipation on typical Funke Medien attacks

| Counter-argument | Rebuttal |
|---|---|
| "The text is purely informative" (Funke Medien paragraph 24, criterion 1) | Point to argumentative, analytical or persuasive elements falling outside pure information transfer |
| "Content entirely determined by information" (criterion 2) | Show concrete alternative formulations, structures and word choices that could have conveyed the same information |
| "Purely technical function" (criterion 3) | Point to functions of the text beyond mere information transfer (persuading, motivating, recommending, analysing) |
| "Standard structure excludes originality" | Show free creative choices **within** the standard structure — choices of substance, words, arrangement within the template fields |
| "No significant difference with other reports" | Point to signature style elements characteristic of the author |

### Critical editions and derivative works on public-domain works (Călinescu, C-649/23) ⭐

For critical editions, philological reconstructions, translations, adaptations and arrangements of public-domain works: see the developed module `methodology/07-derivative-works.md`. Călinescu/FNSA (19 March 2026) is **particularly favourable** for the rightsholder:

- **No heightened threshold** for derivative works (Călinescu paragraph 50 + operative part)
- **Anti-severability rule** (paragraph 64): the other party cannot artificially split the work into "public-domain parts" and "creative parts" where the work has meaning only as a whole
- **Broad originality factors** (paragraphs 54-56): selection/sequence/combination of words + grammatical/lexical/literary/stylistic choices + composition/structure/form/arrangement + knowledge of period/author/interpretation of intention
- **Commentary and apparatus count** (paragraph 58)
- **Uniform scope of protection** (paragraph 66): derivative works are not "more weakly" protected
- **Partial reproduction suffices for infringement** (paragraph 67 cross-ref Infopaq paragraph 39)

Strategic considerations:

- **"Skill irrelevant"** (paragraph 54): avoid building the argument heavily on philological expertise or academic reputation of the author. Focus on the **concrete creative choices** in the final result — which corrections, additions, arrangements, commentary were specifically made?
- **Not merely transcription/facsimile**: show that the edition is not a mechanical reproduction of the manuscript, but the result of substantive interpretative and editorial choices (Slușanschi edition as reference model — paragraph 57)
- **No exclusive right over the original work** (paragraph 68): protection covers only the delta added by the author. Be concrete about what that delta is; avoid suggestions that copyright over the critical edition covers the rights to the original work

#### Operational approach for the pro-rightsholder

1. **Step 1 (concept of work)**: show that the edition as a whole is a sufficiently precisely and objectively identifiable object — paragraph 63 makes this straightforward: the entire edition is regarded as one work
2. **Step 2 (originality)**: identify concrete creative choices in:
   - **Textual choices**: specific corrections of corrupt passages, choice between variant readings, lexical decisions
   - **Compositional choices**: structure, chapter division, layout of original + apparatus
   - **Apparatus and commentary**: substantive scholarly explanations, not merely mechanical footnotes
3. **Anticipate the skill defence**: prove creative choices "in the form", not through the academic status of the editor
4. **Defend the anti-severability rule** (paragraph 64): if the other party asserts that only the commentary is protected (not the reconstructed text), invoke paragraph 64 to show that the work has meaning only as a whole

### Citation form

> CJEU 16 July 2009, C-5/08, *Infopaq International*, ECLI:EU:C:2009:465, paragraphs 45-47 (selection, sequence, combination of words) and paragraph 47 (11-word threshold for fragments); CJEU 29 July 2019, C-469/17, *Funke Medien NRW*, ECLI:EU:C:2019:623, paragraph 19 (cumulative two-step test), paragraph 22 (case-by-case assessment, including fragmentary), and/or paragraph 25 (summary).
>
> CJEU 19 March 2026, C-649/23, *Institutul de Istorie și Teorie Literară „G. Călinescu" and Others / HK and Others*, ECLI:EU:C:2026:213, paragraph 54 (selection/sequence/combination of words + "skill irrelevant"), paragraph 58 (commentary and critical apparatus as intellectual creation), paragraph 64 (anti-severability rule), paragraph 66 (uniform scope of protection), and/or paragraph 67 (partial reproduction may be infringement).

## Multimedia works (based on Nintendo, C-355/12)

### Starting point for a multimedia argument

Nintendo / PC Box (23 January 2014, ECLI:EU:C:2014:25) holds that **multimedia works** (videogames, mobile apps with multimedia, AR/VR works, interactive installations, NFT works with digital content, AI-generated hybrid works) constitute **complex matter** to which Directive 2001/29 applies as a whole. For the rightsholder this is **particularly favourable**:

1. **Full regime of Directive 2001/29** for the multimedia work as a whole
2. **Lex specialis of Directive 2009/24** applies only to the pure computer-program portion — not to the graphic, sound, narrative or interactive components
3. **Own creative value** of components (paragraph 23): the digital encoding does not strip creative value from graphic or sound elements
4. **Holistic protection**: components are protected "together with the entire work" insofar as they contribute to originality
5. **Partial reproduction suffices for infringement** (paragraph 22): parts that "contain the originality of the entire work" are protected
6. **Anti-severability anchor** (cross-ref Călinescu paragraph 64): for multimedia works that have meaning only as a whole

### Operational approach for the pro-rightsholder

#### Step 1 — Concept of work

- Show that the work is a **multimedia work** within the meaning of Nintendo paragraph 23 (complex matter: not only a computer program but also graphic/sound/narrative/interactive components)
- Show objective identifiability of the work as a whole (Levola)

#### Step 2 — Originality

Identify concrete creative choices in each of the components **and** in their composition:

| Component | Creative choices to identify |
|---|---|
| **Graphic** | Character design, colour palette, art direction, animation style, UI design, level design (visual) |
| **Sound** | Musical composition, sound effects design, voice acting, audio mixing, dynamic audio response |
| **Narrative** | Storyline, dialogues, character development, branching, lore/universe elaboration |
| **Interactive** | Gameplay mechanics (specific implementation, not as a general idea), UX flow, control response, level pacing |
| **Composition** | How all components are combined and tuned to each other |

#### Anti-Software-Directive defence

Where the other party argues that the work is a computer program and therefore falls under the more limited regime of Directive 2009/24:

- Invoke Nintendo paragraph 23: "complex matter comprising not only a computer program but also graphic and sound elements"
- Show the **own creative value** of the non-software components
- Show that the work as a whole falls under Directive 2001/29 (full regime)

#### Anti-severability strategy

Where the other party seeks to split the work artificially ("only the source code is yours, not the graphics/sound"):

- Invoke Nintendo paragraph 23 in fine: components are protected "together with the entire work"
- Invoke Călinescu paragraph 64 (anti-severability rule): multimedia works have meaning "only as a whole"
- Show that the components together form the work — not as a sum of separate elements

### Strong examples of multimedia works

- **Videogames** with substantial narrative, visual and musical dimension (core case Nintendo)
- **Mobile apps** with multimedia content (educational apps, creative apps, entertainment apps)
- **VR/AR experiences** with visual, auditory and interactive components
- **Interactive installation art** (digital art with sensory input)
- **NFT works** with substantial digital content (not merely a hash)
- **Interactive e-learning modules**
- **Web works** with substantial creative dimension beyond pure HTML/CSS/JS

### Evidence for the pro-rightsholder

- **Concept art and design documents**: show creative choices in the visual dimension
- **Sound design documents**: show creative choices in the auditory dimension
- **Game/UX design documents**: show creative choices in the interactive/narrative dimension
- **Development credits**: show multidisciplinarity of the team (programmers + artists + sound designers + writers)
- **Comparison with other multimedia works**: show distinguishing features

### Citation form

> CJEU 23 January 2014, C-355/12, *Nintendo Co. Ltd and Others / PC Box Srl and 9Net Srl*, ECLI:EU:C:2014:25, paragraph 21 (confirmation of Infopaq paragraph 35), paragraph 22 (confirmation of Infopaq paragraph 38: partial reproduction), and/or paragraph 23 (complex-matter doctrine for multimedia works; lex specialis boundary Dir. 2009/24 / Dir. 2001/29).

## Evidence and factual material

Beyond the legal argument, factual substantiation is crucial. Provide:

- **Context of creation**: when, by whom, in what circumstances developed
- **Documentation of creative choices**: sketches, variants, drafts, decision moments
- **Comparison with alternatives**: show what the author did not choose
- **Uniqueness within the genre**: testimony, professional literature, prizes, reviews
- **Personal stamp**: recurring stylistic elements, signature

## Anticipation matrix — typical counter-arguments and rebuttal

| Counter-argument | Rebuttal |
|---|---|
| "It is purely functional/technically dictated" | Show alternatives not chosen, and the non-functional dimensions |
| "It consists of banal elements" | Point to the specific combination (Infopaq paragraph 45) |
| "It is too short/too small" | Qualitative test; even fragments can be original (Infopaq paragraphs 47-48) |
| "It is too similar to existing work" | Show the specific deviations and own choices |
| "It is merely information/facts" | Protection applies to form, not to subject (Infopaq paragraph 44) |
| "It is not 'art'" | No aesthetic requirement (Cofemel paragraph 35) |

## Points of attention

- **No exaggeration**: avoid presenting trivial or plainly functional elements as original; that undermines the credibility of the strong arguments
- **Be concrete**: abstract references to "creative choices" without examples are weak
- **Sectoral refinement first**: apply sector-specific judgments first (photography/software/database/applied art), then the general Infopaq formula
- **Do not forget the concept of work**: for objects on the borderline of the concept of work (taste, smell, fleeting live performance), first establish objective identifiability
