# Methodology — Derivative Works (critical editions and related)

> **Version 1.0** — based on Călinescu / FNSA (C-649/23, EU:C:2026:213, 19 March 2026) as the key judgment for critical editions of public-domain works, with cross-references to Infopaq (C-5/08), Cofemel (C-683/17), Funke Medien (C-469/17) and Levola Hengelo (C-310/17); supplemented with the verbatim text of art. 14 Directive (EU) 2019/790 (DSM) as anti-reviving regime for reproductions of public-domain works of visual art. To be supplemented as further CJEU case law on derivative works (translations, adaptations, arrangements) is integrated.

> **Positioning**: this module addresses **derivative works** as an independent category alongside the primary categories of works in modules 02-06. A derivative work is a new creation built upon an **existing work** — typically a critical edition, translation, adaptation, arrangement or reconstruction. The basis in international copyright is **art. 2(3) Berne Convention**: "Translations, adaptations, arrangements of music and other alterations of a literary or artistic work shall be protected as original works without prejudice to the copyright in the original work." EU law recognises this category implicitly (Directive 2001/29) and explicitly (art. 5 Directive 2006/116 for critical and scientific publications).

## Position within the two-step test

Derivative works follow the standard Cofemel two-step test (paragraph 29): concept of work + originality. Călinescu (C-649/23) expressly confirms that **no heightened threshold** applies to derivative works (Călinescu paragraph 50 + operative part). The test proceeds in two steps:

- **Step 1 — Concept of work**: the derivative work must be sufficiently precisely and objectively identifiable (Levola Hengelo, C-310/17, paragraph 40)
- **Step 2 — Originality**: the derivative work must be the author's own intellectual creation, in the form of free and creative choices reflecting their personality (Infopaq paragraph 37; Painer 88-92; Cofemel paragraphs 30-31; Mio/Konektra paragraph 65)

The core point for derivative works: originality must flow from the **delta** that the author of the derivative work adds to the existing work — not from the existing work itself.

## Critical editions — the Călinescu doctrine ⭐⭐⭐

### What is a critical edition?

A critical edition is a scholarly edition that **reconstructs** an existing work (often in an ancient language, only partially preserved, or available in multiple manuscript variants) into a readable and reliable form. The editor:

- Consults the manuscript or the manuscript variants
- Makes corrections, additions or substitutions where the text is unclear, damaged or corrupt
- Adds a critical apparatus (variant readings, conjectures, references)
- Provides the text with commentary, notes, explanations or introductory studies
- Makes grammatical, lexical, literary and stylistic choices in identifying the "correct" text

Typical domain: classical philology (Latin, Greek), biblical scholarship, medieval manuscripts, early modern literary works, historical-critical editions of composers or poets.

### The Călinescu core formula (paragraph 54) ⭐

> "In order to determine whether a literary object such as the Slușanschi critical edition is original, it is necessary to ascertain [...] **whether, when drafting it, its author was able to make free and creative choices capable of conveying to the reader the originality of that subject matter, the originality of which arises, not from the words themselves considered in isolation, but from the choice, sequence and combination of the words by which the author expressed his creativity in an original manner and achieved a result which is an intellectual creation, whereas the skill in creating that subject matter is not relevant in that regard**." (Calinescu paragraph 54)

The formula has three operational components:

| Component | Content | Operational consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **(a) Free and creative choices** | Painer-Cofemel formula | The editor must have had room for creative choices |
| **(b) Not from the words themselves, but from selection/sequence/combination** | Infopaq doctrine applied | Originality in structure and composition, not in isolated text fragments |
| **(c) Skill irrelevant** | Sweat-of-the-brow exclusion | Philological expertise = evidence of possibility, not autonomous ground |

### Supplementary assessment factors (paragraphs 55-56)

The Court lists four dimensions along which originality can be assessed:

#### Paragraph 55 — *linguistic and interpretative choices*

> "[T]he grammatical, lexical, literary and stylistic choices made by the author of a critical edition may probably be dictated, or at the very least influenced, by his or her **years of experience, philological expertise, knowledge and understanding of the period in which the original work concerned was drawn up** and of the historical period covered by that original work, his or her knowledge of the author of that original work, style and linguistic expression, and by his or her interpretation of what he or she perceives as being that author's intention." (Calinescu paragraph 55)

#### Paragraph 56 — *form and compositional choices*

> "[T]he condition of originality may also be assessed in the light of **the composition of the critical edition in question, the structuring of the edition concerned, the form given to it and the arrangement of the original text in relation to the comments and critical apparatus**." (Calinescu paragraph 56)

### What does NOT suffice as a critical edition

| Example | Status |
|---|---|
| Mere transcription of a manuscript | Not a work (Călinescu paragraph 57 a contrario) |
| Facsimile reproduction of the manuscript | Not a work (Călinescu paragraph 57 a contrario) |
| Mechanical diplomatic edition (exact character-for-character reproduction) | Not a work (paragraph 59: dictated by scholarly convention without creative freedom) |
| Mere revision of spelling/punctuation to modern conventions | Question mark — depending on the scope of creative choices |
| Translation without choices (machine or dictation translation) | Not a work (no creative choices) |

### What DOES qualify as a critical edition work

| Component | Source | Operational role |
|---|---|---|
| Corrections of corrupt passages based on interpretation | Călinescu paragraphs 57-58 | Substantively creative |
| Additions to reconstruct lost parts | Călinescu paragraph 67 | Creative addition |
| Choice between variant readings | Călinescu paragraph 55 | Lexical/literary |
| Arrangement of original + apparatus + commentary | Călinescu paragraph 56 | Compositional |
| Introductory study or explanations | Călinescu paragraph 58 | Author's own intellectual creation |

### The anti-severability rule (paragraph 64) ⭐⭐⭐

Călinescu formulates a fundamentally important rule with direct operational significance for critical editions:

> "**It is not necessary to draw a distinction between the parts corresponding to the original work, which may have been the subject of textual amendments, and the comments, critical notes or explanations accompanying them in order, where appropriate, to identify those parts which are capable of falling within the scope of copyright protection. Such an approach would run the risk of breaking up a work which is only meaningful as a whole**, in particular where such comments, notes or explanations supplement or relate to a specific part of the text of the original work which they are commenting on or restoring." (Calinescu paragraph 64)

Operational consequences for critical editions:

- The **entire critical edition** is assessed as one work
- No artificial severance between "original text" (public domain) and "creative additions" (copyright-protected)
- The court looks at the whole to determine whether there are free creative choices
- **Boundary**: the doctrine applies to the qualification as a work. For the **infringement question** the Infopaq doctrine remains applicable (partial reproduction can be infringement where the reproduced parts contain creative elements — paragraph 67)

For the broader reach of the anti-severability rule in other categories of works: see `01-originality-test.md`, section *The anti-severability rule (Călinescu paragraph 64)*.

### The public-domain safeguard (paragraph 68) ⭐⭐

Paragraph 68 is a principled limitation on copyright in critical editions:

> "[T]he recognition of copyright in the critical edition of a literary work in the public domain does not bring that work into the private domain and cannot confer on the author of that critical edition an exclusive right over that pre-existing work." (Calinescu paragraph 68)

Concrete consequences:

| Permitted by third parties | Not permitted by third parties |
|---|---|
| Make own critical edition of the same public-domain work | Take another person's edition verbatim |
| Reproduce the original work itself | Reproduce the editor's specific expression (corrections, arrangement, commentary) |
| Make new translation/adaptation | Copy editor X's delta without own creative addition |

This makes it possible for **several editions** of the same public-domain work to coexist, each with its own copyright protection. The original work remains in the public domain.

## Relationship with art. 5 Directive 2006/116 (neighbouring right for critical publications)

An important doctrinal distinction implicit in Călinescu: in addition to full copyright protection under the concept of work (art. 2(a) Directive 2001/29), Member States may **optionally** grant a neighbouring right for critical and scientific publications of public-domain works (art. 5 Directive 2006/116, recital 19).

| Regime | Basis | Requirement | Term | Character |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Full copyright** | Art. 2(a) Dir. 2001/29 + art. 2(3) Berne Convention | Work status (two-step test) | Author + 70 years (Dir. 2006/116 art. 1) | Mandatory EU harmonization |
| **Neighbouring right** | Art. 5 Dir. 2006/116 | No work status required | Max 30 years from publication | Optional national law |

Concrete consequences:

- A critical edition that does **not** meet the Cofemel two-step test may nonetheless be protected under a national neighbouring right — where the Member State has chosen to implement art. 5 Directive 2006/116

> **Scope demarcation**: this skill addresses only the full concept of work + originality under Directive 2001/29. The neighbouring right under art. 5 Directive 2006/116 (insofar as a Member State introduces it) falls outside scope.

## Art. 14 DSM Directive — anti-reviving regime for reproductions of public-domain works of visual art ⭐⭐

### Verbatim text of art. 14 Directive (EU) 2019/790 (DSM)

> "Member States shall provide that, when the term of protection of a work of visual art has expired, any material resulting from an act of reproduction of that work is not subject to copyright or related rights, unless the material resulting from that act of reproduction is original in the sense that it is the author's own intellectual creation."

### Doctrinal operation — anti-reviving regime

Art. 14 DSM is an **anti-reviving regime**: once the term of protection of a work of visual art has expired and the work is therefore in the public domain, subsequent acts of reproduction (digitisation, scanning, photographic reproduction) **cannot** give rise to new copyright or related rights over the original work. The statutory presumption is that the work remains free. **Exception**: where the reproduction itself meets the EU originality test — the author's own intellectual creation by the reproducing author — an autonomous copyright does arise **in the reproduction**, not in the original work.

### Operational table — when art. 14 does or does not afford protection

| Reproduction | Status under art. 14 DSM |
|---|---|
| Mere mechanical scan / facsimile of a public-domain painting | No new copyright — anti-reviving applies |
| High-resolution museum digitisation following technical standards (calibration, neutral lighting, conservation precision) | No new copyright — choices are technically dictated, no free creative choices |
| Photographic reproduction with substantially creative choices (own lighting, deliberate perspective choice, own composition, recontextualisation) | May constitute a work — author's own intellectual creation within the meaning of the Painer formula (cross-ref `02-photography.md`) |
| Free artistic adaptation of a public-domain work (paraphrase, citation-collage, transformative interpretation) | May constitute a work — author's own intellectual creation. The free adaptation is doctrinally a derivative work (Berne Convention art. 2(3)) |
| Critical edition or scholarly re-issue of a visual work with substantial apparatus (commentary, comparative study, reconstruction) | May constitute a work — cross-ref Călinescu (C-649/23) and the treatment in this module |

### Connection with the Călinescu public-domain safeguard (paragraph 68)

Art. 14 DSM and Călinescu paragraph 68 together form the **public-domain protection framework** in EU law:

| Provision / Judgment | Scope | Operation |
|---|---|---|
| **Art. 14 Dir. 2019/790 (DSM)** | Works of visual art — photographic / digital reproductions | Statutory anti-reviving regime. Reproduction creates **no** new copyright unless author's own intellectual creation |
| **Călinescu C-649/23 paragraph 68** | All categories of works — derivative works on public-domain works | Doctrinal safeguard. Recognition of copyright on a derivative work confers **no** exclusive right over the original work |

Both instruments ensure that the original public-domain work **remains free** while creative additions to that work can be protected. Both follow the logic: the public domain prevails unless there is an autonomous creative addition.

### Operational consequences

**For the pro-rightsholder** (defending copyright on reproduction of a public-domain work):

- Positively establish which **free creative choices** have been made in the reproduction (cross-ref Painer formulas in `02-photography.md`)
- Show that it is **not** a mere faithful reproduction (Painer 88-92; as opposed to mere documentation)
- Limit the claim to the **delta** — the creative addition — without extending to the original work (Călinescu paragraph 68)
- Evidence: creative process, expert evidence on creative choices, comparison with "neutral" reproductions

**For the alleged infringer** (contesting copyright on the reproduction):

- Art. 14 DSM **incorporates a statutory presumption against protection**: anti-reviving applies unless the reproduction is the author's own intellectual creation. The **burden of proof** for the creative addition lies with the rightsholder
- Show that the reproduction is purely mechanical / technical — no room for free creative choices
- Refer to the objective of the regime: **access to cultural heritage in the public domain** (recital 53 DSM Directive)
- In case of doubt: invoke the "no other criteria" clause in the other three directive provisions — an institution that relies only on its **expertise** or **investment** does not meet the originality threshold (cross-ref Football Dataco paragraphs 38-39, Călinescu paragraph 54 "skill irrelevant")

## Other derivative works — forward-looking

Călinescu is the first CJEU judgment in this category. The Cofemel-Călinescu doctrine can be applied by analogy to other derivative works, with the following forward-looking notes (subject to future case law):

### Translations

- Berne Convention art. 2(3) and 12 expressly recognise translations as protectable derivative works
- A translation qualifies as a work where the translator can make free creative choices in lexical/syntactic/stylistic register
- Mechanical or literal translations (e.g. machine translations, or translations dictated solely by technical constraints) do not meet the threshold
- *CJEU case law specifically on translations: still to be integrated*

### Adaptations

- Novel-to-film, theatre-to-novel, abridgements, paraphrases
- Berne Convention art. 2(3) recognises adaptations
- The adapter must make free creative choices in the transposition
- Protection is "without prejudice to the rights of the author of the original work" (art. 2(3) Berne Convention)
- *CJEU case law specifically on adaptations: still to be integrated*

### Musical arrangements

- Berne Convention art. 2(3) recognises musical arrangements
- An arrangement of a public-domain work may be a work where the arranger makes creative choices in instrumentation, harmonisation, rhythm
- *CJEU case law specifically on musical arrangements: still to be integrated*

### Re-editions and restoration of visual works

- Restoration of paintings, sculptures or buildings: typically dictated by conservation objectives, limited creative choices
- The Brompton-Cofemel-Mio/Konektra line on technical dictates may be relevant here
- *CJEU case law specifically on restoration: still to be integrated*

## Operational checklist — derivative works

### For the rightsholder (establishing copyright)

- [ ] Show that the work is a **derivative work** within the meaning of art. 2(3) Berne Convention or comparable
- [ ] Identify the **delta** that the author has added to the existing work
- [ ] Step 1 (concept of work): show objective identifiability (Levola)
- [ ] Step 2 (originality): show free creative choices in selection/sequence/combination (Călinescu paragraph 54)
- [ ] For critical editions: show that it is not a mere transcription or facsimile (Călinescu paragraph 57 a contrario)
- [ ] Show that the work is **not** exclusively dictated by technical conventions or scholarly standards (Călinescu paragraph 59)
- [ ] Avoid relying heavily on skill/expertise/academic reputation (Călinescu paragraph 54: "skill irrelevant")
- [ ] If appropriate, invoke the anti-severability rule (paragraph 64) to resist severance
- [ ] Limitation: copyright applies only to the delta, not to the original work (Călinescu paragraph 68)

### For the alleged infringer (contesting copyright)

- [ ] Examine whether the work is a mere transcription or facsimile (Călinescu paragraph 57 a contrario)
- [ ] Examine whether the "creative" choices are in fact dictated by scholarly/philological conventions (Călinescu paragraph 59)
- [ ] Rebut expertise and skill arguments (Călinescu paragraph 54)
- [ ] Ask the rightsholder to identify positively which specific creative choices were made (cross-ref Mio/Konektra paragraph 65)
- [ ] Show that the reproduced parts correspond to the **original work** (public domain) and not to the editor's creative additions (Călinescu paragraph 68)
- [ ] Own alternative reconstruction: paragraph 68 does not preclude a third party from making a **different** critical edition of the same public-domain work
- [ ] Limit on the anti-severability rule: for parts that have independent meaning, it remains possible to challenge individual components
