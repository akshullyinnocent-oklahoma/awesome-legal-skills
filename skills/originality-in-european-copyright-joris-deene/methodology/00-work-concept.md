# Methodology — Concept of Work (step 1 of the two-step test)

> **Version 1.0** — Full treatment based on Levola Hengelo (C-310/17, EU:C:2018:899, 13 November 2018), with cross-references to Cofemel (C-683/17), Brompton (C-833/18), Funke Medien (C-469/17), Mio/Konektra (C-580/23 + C-795/23), Călinescu/FNSA (C-649/23) and Nintendo / PC Box (C-355/12) as confirmatory case law.

## Position within the two-step test

Before Levola Hengelo, the EU originality test was usually presented as a single whole. With Levola, the test is explicitly **split into two consecutive steps**:

| Step | Question | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **Step 1 — Concept of work** | Can this object be qualified as a "work" within the meaning of Directive 2001/29 at all? | **Levola Hengelo** (C-310/17) — this module |
| **Step 2 — Originality** | Is this work original (the author's own intellectual creation)? | Football Dataco (C-604/10) — module `01-originality-test.md` |

The two steps are **cumulative**. An object that is objectively identifiable but not original is not a work. An object that might be original but is not objectively identifiable is likewise not a work.

## The Levola formula

The core of the concept-of-work test is set out in **Levola Hengelo paragraph 40**:

> "Accordingly, for there to be a 'work' as referred to in Directive 2001/29, **the subject matter protected by copyright must be expressed in a manner which makes it identifiable with sufficient precision and objectivity, even though that expression is not necessarily in permanent form**."

The test consists of three elements:

1. **Form of expression**: the object must be expressed in a form (not merely exist as an idea)
2. **Sufficient precision and objectivity**: the expression must be precise enough for the content of the work to be concretely identified
3. **Not necessarily permanent**: improvisation, dance, performance art and oral delivery can be works, even if they are not fixed

## The two underlying rationales (Levola paragraph 41)

The Court explains **why** objective identifiability is required — two rationales that can be operationally invoked:

### Rationale 1 — Identification by third parties

> "That is because, first, **the authorities responsible for ensuring that the exclusive rights inherent in copyright are protected must be able to identify, clearly and precisely, the subject matter so protected**. The same is true for individuals, in particular economic operators, who must be able to identify, clearly and precisely, what is the subject matter of protection which third parties, especially competitors, enjoy."

- Courts must be able to determine whether there is a protected work
- Collecting societies must be able to identify what falls within their management
- Customs authorities must be able to determine whether imported goods are infringing
- Competitors must be able to know what they may not reproduce
- Users must be able to know when their acts are permissible

### Rationale 2 — Legal certainty by excluding subjectivity

> "Secondly, **the need to ensure that there is no element of subjectivity –– given that it is detrimental to legal certainty –– in the process of identifying the protected subject matter** means that the latter must be capable of being expressed in a precise and objective manner."

- A copyright claim must not depend on the subjective impression of an individual
- Different observers must be able to ascertain whether they are looking at the same object
- The delimitation of the subject-matter of protection must be intersubjectively verifiable

## What is and is not objectively identifiable?

### Objectively identifiable (Levola paragraph 42 with explicit examples)

> "Unlike, for example, **a literary, pictorial, cinematographic or musical work**, which is a precise and objective form of expression [...]"

| Category of work | Anchor | Comment |
|---|---|---|
| Literary work | Text | Directly identifiable by reading |
| Painting | Visual composition | Directly identifiable by looking |
| Film | Audiovisual recording | Identifiable by viewing |
| Musical work | Score or recording | Identifiable by listening |
| Sculpture | Three-dimensional form | Directly identifiable |
| Photograph | Image | Directly identifiable |
| Architecture | Plan + building | Identifiable through the plan |
| Choreography | Notation or recording | Identifiable by reconstruction |
| Software | Source/object code | Identifiable by inspection |
| Theatrical work | Script + performance | Identifiable through the text |
| Live performance / dance | Performance | Identifiable at the moment of performance (paragraph 40, non-permanence) |
| Improvisation (e.g. jazz) | Performance | Idem |

### Not objectively identifiable (excluded from the concept of work)

| Object | Source | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| **Taste** of a foodstuff | Levola operative part + paragraphs 42-44 | Subjective, dependent on personal factors and context |
| **Smell** (perfume, aroma) | Levola paragraph 24 (implicit confirmation) | Idem |
| **Visual atmosphere or ambience** as such | Logic of Levola | Not concretely identifiable |
| **Style** as such (genre, aesthetic) | Logic of Levola | A general feature, not a concrete object |
| **Concept** without concrete elaboration | Levola paragraph 39 + SAS Institute | Idea, not a form of expression |
| **Overall impression** without concrete components | Logic of Levola | Subjective impression, not an objective object |
| **Type of experience** (e.g. an interactive-event "formula") | Logic of Levola | Too abstract |
| **Mouthfeel / texture** (as argued by Levola itself in the proceedings) | Levola operative part covers these elements explicitly | Sensory and subjective |

## Important nuances

### Non-permanent forms of expression are permitted

Levola paragraph 40 expressly does not include a requirement that a work be permanently fixed. This is relevant for:

- **Performance art** — one-off performances can be works
- **Improvisation** (jazz, modern dance) — works at the moment of performance
- **Land art and site-specific work** — temporary installations
- **Oral delivery** and speeches
- **Theatre** without a published script

The condition is that the work is **objectively identifiable during the performance**.

### It is an autonomous EU concept (Levola paragraph 33)

The concept of work is an **autonomous EU concept** which is uniformly interpreted by the CJEU. Member States cannot, by means of a broader national interpretation, grant copyright protection to objects that are not works in the EU-law sense (Levola paragraph 45 + operative part).

Important consequences:

- Earlier national case law granting broad protection (e.g. perfumes as protected works) is overtaken
- National concepts of "work" must be interpreted in the EU-law sense

### Cumulation with other IP rights remains possible

An object that is not a "work" within the EU-law sense may still receive protection through:

- **Patent law** (for inventions) — e.g. the process patent on Heksenkaas (Levola paragraph 15)
- **Trade mark law** (for distinctive signs, including taste/smell where capable of graphic representation — somewhat broader since the abolition of the graphic-representation requirement)
- **Design law** (for the appearance of a product)
- **Sui generis database right** (for the contents of a database, alongside/instead of copyright — Football Dataco)
- **Trade secret** (for recipes, formulae, know-how)
- **Unfair competition / slavish imitation** (subsidiary, subject to strict criteria)

## Operational checklist — step 1 of the two-step test

### For the rightsholder: establishing that a work exists

- [ ] **Identify the concrete object**: not merely the idea, but the concrete expression
- [ ] **Describe the form of expression**: text, image, sound, code, shape, performance
- [ ] **Establish objective identifiability**: how can a third party identify this object without subjective assessment?
- [ ] **Document the anchor**: manuscript, score, code, photograph, video, physical object, recording of the performance
- [ ] **Anticipate the Levola attack**: avoid subjective descriptions ("the style of", "the atmosphere of", "the taste of")
- [ ] **For non-permanent works**: thoroughly document the performance (video, witnesses, description)

### For the defence: establishing that there is no work

- [ ] **Identify what the other party is claiming**: have the other party **concretely** describe what the subject-matter of protection is
- [ ] **Point to subjective elements**: does identification depend on personal assessment?
- [ ] **Point to variability**: is the object the same for different observers, at different moments?
- [ ] **Argue via Levola paragraph 41**: could a judge, a customs officer or a competitor objectively determine what is protected?
- [ ] **Identify which exclusion category applies**: taste, smell, atmosphere, style, concept, overall impression

## Cofemel, Brompton, Funke Medien, Mio/Konektra, Călinescu — building on Levola

Subsequent case law expressly takes up the Levola concept-of-work formula:

- **Cofemel paragraph 32** (C-683/17): literally repeats the Levola formula and applies it to fashion designs
- **Brompton** (C-833/18): confirms that utilitarian articles (consumer goods, folding bicycles) also fall under the Levola test
- **Funke Medien** (C-469/17): confirms for functional/administrative texts (cross-reference to Levola in Funke Medien paragraph 19)
- **Mio/Konektra paragraph 72** (joined cases C-580/23 + C-795/23): expressly repeats the two Cofemel rationales (identification by authorities and third parties + exclusion of legal-certainty-undermining subjectivity) for applied art. Express reference to Cofemel paragraphs 32-33
- **Călinescu / FNSA paragraph 62** (C-649/23): confirms for derivative works/critical editions that the concept of work "necessarily entails the existence of an expression which makes it possible to identify the work with sufficient precision and objectivity". Cross-reference to Levola Hengelo paragraph 40. Paragraph 63 holds that a critical edition can qualify as sufficiently identifiable **as a whole** — connected to the anti-severability rule in paragraph 64

For this skill, this means that the concept-of-work test forms a **uniform starting point in all sectors** — for photographs, software, sporting events, databases, applied art, text, derivative works and all other categories of works.

## Taste, smell and sensory experiences — definitively excluded

Levola is **the** authority for the exclusion of taste and (implicitly) smell:

| Object | Status after Levola | Alternative protection |
|---|---|---|
| Taste of a foodstuff | NOT protected (operative part) | Patent (process), trade secret, trade mark law (very limited) |
| Smell of a perfume | Almost certainly NOT protected | Patent (composition), trade secret, trade mark law |
| Texture, mouthfeel | NOT protected (Levola logic) | Limited; trade secret |
| Recipe (as idea/process) | NOT protected | Patent, trade secret |
| **Recipe (as text)** | **DOES qualify** as a writing, if original | — |
| **Written description of a taste** | DOES qualify as a text, if original | — |

Important distinction: the **expression** of a culinary creation (the recipe text, the image of the dish, a sculptural plating) can be a work. The **taste experience** itself cannot.

- **Levola Hengelo** (C-310/17, EU:C:2018:899) — formulates the two-step test and objective identifiability
- **Cofemel** (C-683/17, EU:C:2019:721) — confirmation of the two-step test and repetition of the Levola formula for applied art (paragraph 32)
- **Brompton** (C-833/18, EU:C:2020:461) — confirmation of Levola for utilitarian articles
- **Funke Medien** (C-469/17, EU:C:2019:623) — confirmation of Levola for functional texts
- **Mio/Konektra** (C-580/23 + C-795/23, EU:C:2025:941) — confirmation of the Levola formula for applied art (paragraph 72); repetition of the two rationales of objective identifiability
- **Călinescu / FNSA** (C-649/23, EU:C:2026:213) — confirmation of the Levola formula for derivative works / critical editions (paragraph 62); a work can be sufficiently precisely and objectively identifiable **as a whole** (paragraph 63); cross-reference to the anti-severability rule in paragraph 64
- **Nintendo / PC Box** (C-355/12, EU:C:2014:25) — confirmation of the Infopaq concept-of-work formula for multimedia works (paragraph 21); partial reproduction of parts which "contain the originality of the entire work" (paragraph 22, cross-reference to Infopaq paragraph 38); doctrinal precursor of the Călinescu anti-severability rule in a multimedia context (paragraph 23)
