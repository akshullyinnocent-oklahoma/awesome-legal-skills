# Methodology — Text and Fragments

> **Version 1.0** — based on Infopaq (C-5/08) as the base judgment for textual works and Funke Medien (C-469/17) as the key judgment for functional/administrative texts, with cross-references to Painer, BSA, SAS Institute, Football Dataco, Levola Hengelo and Călinescu/FNSA (C-649/23, for critical editions — developed in `07-derivative-works.md`).

## Strategic positioning

Textual works — from literary works to administrative reports, journalistic articles, policy notes, legal texts, marketing texts, technical manuals and software documentation — constitute a **heterogeneous** category. The EU two-step test is uniformly applied, but the outcome varies sharply depending on the type of text.

Two extremes:

- **Literary works** (novels, poems, essays, columns) → almost automatically a work thanks to the abundance of free creative choices in selection, arrangement and combination of words
- **Purely functional texts** (status reports, standard forms, technical specifications) → generally NOT a work because of idea/expression merger (Funke Medien paragraph 24)

Between these extremes lies a spectrum of **partially functional textual works** (reports with argumentative elements, legal opinions, press releases with opinion elements, marketing texts with persuasive components) where the outcome of the two-step test must be determined case by case.

## The Infopaq base formula for textual works

The Infopaq judgment (C-5/08, ECLI:EU:C:2009:465) provides the base formula for the originality of textual works:

> Infopaq paragraphs 45-47: the originality of a textual work flows from the **selection, sequence and combination of the words** through which the author has expressed their creativity in an original manner.

### Three pillars of textual originality

| Pillar | Operational substance |
|---|---|
| **Selection of words** | Which words have been chosen from the available lexical pool? Synonyms, register differences, professional jargon vs. colloquial language? |
| **Sequence of words** | How is the text structured? Sentence construction, paragraph order, rhetorical build-up? |
| **Combination of words** | How do the words play off each other? Imagery, rhythm, stylistic devices, lexical combinations? |

For each of these three pillars the author must have been able to make **free creative choices**. As the text is more functionally dictated (by technical requirements, conventions, imposed structure), the room for creative choices diminishes.

## The Funke Medien test for functional texts ⭐

Funke Medien (C-469/17, ECLI:EU:C:2019:623) is the definitive key to the copyright status of functional/administrative texts. The Grand Chamber judgment of 29 July 2019 concerned military situation reports of the German Federal Armed Forces ("Afghanistan Papers"), but the test is much more broadly usable.

### The Funke Medien formula (paragraph 24)

> Funke Medien paragraph 24: "If military status reports [...] constitute **purely informative documents, the content of which is essentially determined by the information which they contain, so that such information and the expression of those reports become indissociable and that those reports are thus entirely characterised by their technical function, precluding all originality**, [...] in drafting those reports, **it was impossible for the author to express his or her creativity in an original manner and to achieve a result which is that author's own intellectual creation**."

### The three cumulative criteria for idea/expression merger

| Criterion | Operational substance | Test |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Purely informative character** | The text serves only as an information carrier — no literary, narrative, persuasive or analytical function | Does the text have any function other than information transfer? |
| **2. Content entirely determined by information** | There is no expressive room alongside the factual content | Could the text have been written differently with the same information? |
| **3. Purely technical function** | The text serves a specific operational purpose (administrative, military, technical) | Does the text serve a function outside the text itself? |

Where these are cumulatively met: idea and expression coincide → **no work**.

### Sweat of the brow expressly excluded (paragraph 23)

> Funke Medien paragraph 23: "[T]he mere intellectual effort and skill of creating those reports are not relevant in that regard."

Cross-reference Football Dataco paragraph 33. This means operationally:

- Workload, required expertise or diligence in drafting are **not relevant**
- Significant research effort does not count
- Professional competence of the drafter does not count
- Only **free creative choices in textual form** count

### No categorical exclusion — case-by-case assessment (paragraph 25)

Funke Medien does **not categorically exclude** administrative texts. Paragraph 25 requires a case-by-case test. Whether or not protection applies depends on the concrete form of the specific text.

Moreover, paragraph 22 expressly permits **certain fragments** to be works, even where the text as a whole is not protected. A fragmentary approach is thus possible.

## Operational typology of textual works

### Category 1 — Almost automatic work

- Literary works (novels, novellas, poems, essays)
- Columns, opinion pieces, reviews
- Song lyrics
- Personal correspondence with expressive content
- Advertising texts with creative elements (slogans excepted — see below)

### Category 2 — Possibly a work, case-by-case test

- Policy notes with argumentative structure
- Legal opinions with reasoning
- Reports with conclusions and recommendations
- Research reports with analysis
- Scientific articles (linguistic framing of results)
- Marketing texts with persuasive components
- Journalistic articles (depending on argumentative / analytical content)
- Press releases (depending on opinion elements)
- Corporate brochures and annual reports (depending on form)

### Category 3 — Generally NOT a work (Funke Medien merger)

- Status reports with fixed structure (such as the situation reports themselves)
- Standard forms and standardised reports
- Statistical overviews and figure documents
- Technical specifications whose content is determined by technical requirements
- Meeting minutes that record only facts
- Inventories and catalogues (without original selection / arrangement)
- Routine administrative correspondence
- Scientific measurement data and raw data
- Product descriptions with purely factual features
- Press releases with predominantly factual content

### Special category — Short fragments and titles

Infopaq paragraph 47 teaches that **fragments of 11 words** can be protected, provided they constitute the expression of the author's own intellectual creation. Short texts (titles, slogans, opening sentences) are therefore not categorically excluded from protection, but the **burden of proof** for originality is heightened:

- The shorter the fragment, the harder to demonstrate free creative choices in selection/sequence/combination
- Banal or obvious formulations do not meet the threshold
- Descriptive titles (e.g. "Annual Report 2024") do not meet the threshold
- Advertising slogans with creative word combinations may qualify as works

## Principal judgments in this module

- **Infopaq** (C-5/08) — base formula "selection, sequence and combination of words" for textual works
- **Funke Medien** (C-469/17) — **key judgment for functional texts**: idea/expression merger, three cumulative criteria, sweat of the brow excluded, case-by-case assessment
- **Painer** (C-145/10) — threefold originality formula (personality, free creative choices, author's own intellectual creation) — applicable to all categories of works
- **Football Dataco** (C-604/10) — sweat of the brow excluded (confirmed in Funke Medien paragraph 23)
- **SAS Institute** (C-406/10) — idea/expression dichotomy (confirmed in Funke Medien paragraph 24)
- **Levola Hengelo** (C-310/17) — concept of work / step 1 (confirmed in Funke Medien paragraphs 18, 20)
