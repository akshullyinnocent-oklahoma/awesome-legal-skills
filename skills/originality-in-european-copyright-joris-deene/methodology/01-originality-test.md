# Methodology — Originality Test (EU Law)

> **Version 1.0** — based on Infopaq (C-5/08), Painer (C-145/10), BSA (C-393/09), FAPL (C-403/08 & C-429/08), Football Dataco (C-604/10), SAS Institute (C-406/10), Levola Hengelo (C-310/17), Cofemel (C-683/17), Brompton (C-833/18), Funke Medien (C-469/17), Sony/Datel (C-159/23), Mio/Konektra (joined cases C-580/23 + C-795/23), Călinescu/FNSA (C-649/23) and Nintendo / PC Box (C-355/12), plus the EU **statutory harmonization framework for originality** which rests on art. 1(3) Directive 2009/24/EC, art. 3(1) Directive 96/9/EC, art. 6 Directive 2006/116/EC and art. 14 Directive (EU) 2019/790 (DSM).

> **Positioning**: this module addresses **step 2** of the two-step test (originality). **Step 1** (concept of work / objective identifiability based on Levola Hengelo) is set out in `00-work-concept.md`. The two steps are cumulative: an object that fails the concept-of-work test never reaches the originality test. **Cofemel (C-683/17) paragraph 29** consolidates the entire two-step test in a single paragraph and has been the standard formulation since 2019. **Brompton (C-833/18)** refines the BSA doctrine on technical function for physical utilitarian articles. **Funke Medien (C-469/17)** is the first explicit CJEU application of the two-step test to functional/administrative texts. **Mio/Konektra (C-580/23 + C-795/23)** adds the "unique aspect" requirement to the Painer formulas, excludes any presumption of creative nature and provides a list of non-decisive factors. **Călinescu/FNSA (C-649/23)** adds three generally relevant refinements: "skill irrelevant" (paragraph 54, sweat of the brow excluded for expertise); the **anti-severability rule** (paragraph 64, no artificial severance of works that "have meaning only as a whole"); and the **public-domain safeguard** (paragraph 68, derivative works on public-domain works). Sector-specific treatment of critical editions and derivative works in `07-derivative-works.md`. **Nintendo / PC Box (C-355/12)** opens the complex-matter doctrine for multimedia works (paragraph 23): hybrid digital works fall as a whole under Directive 2001/29 and not under Directive 2009/24; doctrinal precursor to the Călinescu anti-severability rule in a multimedia context. Sector-specific treatment in `08-multimedia-works.md`.

## The EU core formula

Since Infopaq, the test for copyright protection in EU law has been autonomously harmonized. An object is **original** when it is the **author's own intellectual creation** (Infopaq, paragraph 37).

This formula applies to **all categories of works** within the scope of the InfoSoc Directive, and not only to the three categories for which the EU legislator had already harmonized in advance (computer programs, databases, photographs — Infopaq paragraph 35). The Court derives this generalisation from the general scheme of the Berne Convention (Infopaq paragraph 34) and from the harmonization objective of the directive (Infopaq paragraph 36).

## The EU harmonization framework for originality ⭐⭐⭐

The EU originality test rests on a **statutory floor** consisting of four directive provisions plus the horizontally harmonizing CJEU case law that builds upon those provisions. **The Infopaq formula "author's own intellectual creation" is not a judicial creation ex nihilo** but a generalisation to the general concept of work of the formulas that the EU legislator had already laid down sectorally for computer programs, databases and photographs before Infopaq.

### Overview table

| Provision | Category of work | Originality formula | Lex specialis vis-à-vis art. 2(a) Dir. 2001/29? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Art. 1(3) Dir. 2009/24/EC** (formerly art. 1(3) Dir. 91/250/EEC) | Computer programs | "author's own intellectual creation" + "no other criteria" | Yes (lex specialis for computer programs; Nintendo C-355/12 draws the outer boundary — see `08-multimedia-works.md`) |
| **Art. 3(1) Dir. 96/9/EC** | Databases (copyright layer, Chapter II) | "author's own intellectual creation" by "selection or arrangement" + "no other criteria" | Yes (lex specialis for the structural layer of databases; cross-ref `04-databases.md`) |
| **Art. 6 Dir. 2006/116/EC** (formerly art. 6 Dir. 93/98/EEC) | Photographs | "author's own intellectual creation" + "no other criteria" + optional protection of "other photographs" | Yes (lex specialis for photographs; cross-ref `02-photography.md`) |
| **Art. 14 Dir. (EU) 2019/790 (DSM)** | Reproductions of works of visual art in the public domain | "author's own intellectual creation" as exception to exclusion rule | Specific anti-reviving regime; cross-ref `07-derivative-works.md` |
| **Art. 2(a) Dir. 2001/29/EC (InfoSoc)** | All works | *No explicit originality provision*; harmonized via CJEU case law (Infopaq, Painer, Cofemel) on "author's own intellectual creation" | General regime |

### Verbatim texts of the four sectoral directive provisions

#### Art. 1(3) Directive 2009/24/EC (computer programs)

> "A computer program shall be protected if it is original in the sense that it is the author's own intellectual creation. No other criteria shall be applied to determine its eligibility for protection."

#### Art. 3(1) Directive 96/9/EC (databases)

> "In accordance with this Directive, databases which, by reason of the selection or arrangement of their contents, constitute the author's own intellectual creation shall be protected as such by copyright. No other criteria shall be applied to determine their eligibility for that protection."

#### Art. 6 Directive 2006/116/EC (photographs)

> "Photographs which are original in the sense that they are the author's own intellectual creation shall be protected in accordance with Article 1. No other criteria shall be applied to determine their eligibility for protection. Member States may provide for the protection of other photographs."

#### Art. 14 Directive (EU) 2019/790 (DSM — works of visual art in the public domain)

> "Member States shall provide that, when the term of protection of a work of visual art has expired, any material resulting from an act of reproduction of that work is not subject to copyright or related rights, unless the material resulting from that act of reproduction is original in the sense that it is the author's own intellectual creation."

### Doctrinal operation of the four provisions

#### 1. The common "no other criteria" clause ⭐⭐⭐

Three of the four provisions (art. 1(3) Dir. 2009/24, art. 3(1) Dir. 96/9, art. 6 Dir. 2006/116) contain the **common clause** that **no other criteria** may be applied to determine whether the work qualifies for protection. This clause constitutes the **doctrinal floor** for the Cofemel rule (C-683/17, paragraph 29) that Member States may not impose **additional requirements** — for example an aesthetic criterion, artistic value or a subjective-qualitative assessment. CJEU case law has horizontally extended this clause to the general concept of work under art. 2(a) Dir. 2001/29.

Practical consequence: a Member State (or a national court) that would seek to apply a heightened originality threshold for a specific category of work — a "personal stamp" criterion, an "artistic value" criterion, a "uniqueness" criterion — acts contrary to the "no other criteria" clause of the relevant directive and to the Cofemel rule. This is the doctrinal basis for Mio/Konektra operative part 1 (no rule-exception relationship between design and copyright for applied art).

#### 2. The variation between the formulas

The EU legislator has formulated the originality formula with slight variation across the four provisions:

| Provision | English formula | Variation |
|---|---|---|
| Art. 1(3) Dir. 2009/24 | "the author's own intellectual creation" | Bare formula |
| Art. 6 Dir. 2006/116 | "the author's own intellectual creation" | Bare formula |
| Art. 3(1) Dir. 96/9 | "the author's own intellectual creation" by reason of the "selection or arrangement" of the contents | Bare formula + selection/arrangement specification |
| Art. 14 Dir. 2019/790 | "the author's own intellectual creation" | Bare formula |
| Infopaq paragraph 37 (CJEU) | "the author's own intellectual creation" | **Harmonized formula** |

The English versions of all four provisions use exactly **the same formula**: "the author's own intellectual creation". Minor textual variation appears between language versions of the directives (e.g. some language versions omit the word "intellectual" in the older Software and Term directives), but this variation has been doctrinally overcome through the horizontally harmonizing effect of Infopaq: in all cases the consolidated Cofemel formula "author's own intellectual creation" applies, and linguistic variation between official language versions cannot be used as a standalone argument for a divergent threshold.

#### 3. Specificity of art. 3(1) Dir. 96/9 — selection or arrangement

Art. 3(1) Dir. 96/9 adds to the basic formula a **specific component**: the "author's own intellectual creation" must arise from the **"selection or arrangement" of the contents**. This is the database equivalent of the Infopaq formula "selection, sequence and combination of words" (Infopaq paragraph 45). The Football Dataco judgment (C-604/10, operative part 1) confirms that the selection or arrangement must contain creative choices that go beyond purely technical, rule-dictated or mechanical ordering — sweat of the brow excluded. Cross-reference: `04-databases.md`.

#### 4. Specificity of art. 6 Dir. 2006/116 — optional protection of "other photographs"

Art. 6 Dir. 2006/116 contains a third sentence: "Member States may provide for the protection of other photographs." This is an **optional possibility** for Member States to create protection regimes for photographs that do not meet the EU originality threshold — typically a neighbouring right for "ordinary" photographs (simple photographs without an author's own intellectual creation). Compare the structure with art. 5 Dir. 2006/116 (optional protection of critical and scientific publications — cross-ref `07-derivative-works.md`).

#### 5. Art. 14 Dir. 2019/790 — anti-reviving regime for public-domain works

Art. 14 DSM is an **anti-reviving regime**: once the term of protection of a work of visual art has expired (and the work has thus entered the public domain), subsequent reproduction acts (digitisation, scanning, photographic reproduction) **cannot** give rise to new copyright or related rights over the original work. Unless — and this is where the EU originality test comes into play — the reproduction itself constitutes the reproducing author's own intellectual creation.

Operational application:

| Reproduction | Status under art. 14 DSM |
|---|---|
| Mere mechanical scan / facsimile of a public-domain painting | No new copyright; remains in the public domain |
| High-resolution museum digitisation following technical standards (non-creative choices) | No new copyright |
| Photographic reproduction with substantially creative choices (lighting, perspective, composition — Painer formula) | May constitute a work — author's own intellectual creation |
| Free adaptation, artistic interpretation or recontextualisation | May constitute a work |

This logic aligns doctrinally with the Călinescu paragraph 68 public-domain safeguard: recognition of copyright on the derivative act does **not** return the original work to private ownership. Further treatment in `07-derivative-works.md`.

### Connection between the statutory floor and CJEU case law

CJEU case law (Infopaq, Painer, Cofemel, Brompton, Mio/Konektra, Călinescu, etc.) has developed this statutory floor doctrinally:

| CJEU doctrine | Statutory basis |
|---|---|
| Two-step test (concept of work + originality) | Cofemel paragraph 29 ← rests on art. 2(a) Dir. 2001/29 read in conjunction with the three sectoral provisions |
| "No additional requirements" (no aesthetic criterion, no artistic value) | Cofemel paragraphs 29, 35 ← rests on the "no other criteria" clause in art. 1(3) Dir. 2009/24, art. 3(1) Dir. 96/9, art. 6 Dir. 2006/116 |
| "Free and creative choices" / "reflecting personality" / "personal touch" | Painer paragraphs 88-92 ← development of "author's own intellectual creation" |
| Sweat of the brow excluded | Football Dataco paragraphs 38-39, Funke Medien paragraph 23, Călinescu paragraph 54 ← rests on the formulas that consistently require "creative" and "intellectual" elements |
| Anti-severability rule | Călinescu paragraph 64 ← no statutory floor; entirely doctrinal |
| Public-domain safeguard | Călinescu paragraph 68 ← reinforced by art. 14 Dir. 2019/790 |

## The two components of the test

### Component 1 — "Own" (own / propre)

The object must **originate from the author**. It may not be derivative, and must display sufficient **individual input** to be attributable to a specific author.

Negative indicators:
- Slavishly copied from existing material
- Mechanical result without human creative intervention
- Mere reproduction of a pre-existing pattern or model

### Component 2 — "Intellectual creation"

The object must be the product of **mental effort** — not merely mechanical, technically dictated or the automatic consequence of applying rules.

Negative indicators:
- Form entirely dictated by technical function or physical necessity
- The object is the necessary result of pre-established rules (genre, convention, technical standard)
- No room for any creative choice

## Operational test: selection, arrangement, combination (Infopaq paragraph 45)

In Infopaq, the Court formulates the operational test for the first time: originality manifests itself in **the selection, sequence and combination** of the elements in an original manner. Applied to press articles (Infopaq paragraph 44), that originality lies concretely "in the form, the manner of presentation of the subject and the linguistic expression" — not in the subject itself, the facts or the individual words (Infopaq paragraph 46).

The test is **qualitative**, not quantitative: even short fragments can be original (Infopaq paragraphs 47-48), provided they themselves contain the expression of the author's own intellectual creation.

## Painer refinement: three operational formulas

The Painer judgment (C-145/10) concretises the Infopaq test with **three interrelated formulas** that recur in all subsequent CJEU case law. These formulas are primarily formulated for photographs but apply to all categories of works:

### Formula 1 — Free and creative choices (Painer paragraph 89)

A work is the author's own intellectual creation when the author has been able to express their creative abilities by making **free and creative choices**. This is the operational core: the test asks whether the author had room to choose, and whether they actually used that room creatively.

*A contrario* benchmark (Painer paragraph 89, referring to FAPL): where the form or content is entirely dictated by external rules or technical necessity (e.g. sporting events governed by the rules of the game), there is no room for free creative choices and therefore no work.

### Formula 2 — Reflecting personality (Painer paragraph 88)

An intellectual creation is the author's own creation when it constitutes the **expression of the author's personality**. This formula bridges the objective criterion (author's own intellectual creation) and the subjective element (personal expression). It comes from recital 17 of Directive 93/98 (photographs) but applies as general doctrine.

### Formula 3 — Personal touch (Painer paragraph 92)

Through their choices, the author imprints a **"personal touch"** on the work. Operational synonym for the "personal stamp" used in national legal traditions.

### Coherence of the three formulas

The three formulas are three expressions of the same underlying concept:

> **Free and creative choices** → make **the author's personality** visible → result in a work bearing a **personal touch**.

In argumentation they may be used independently or cumulatively.

## Mio/Konektra refinement: the "unique aspect" requirement ⭐⭐⭐

Mio/Konektra (joined cases C-580/23 and C-795/23, 4 December 2025) refines the Painer formulas on four doctrinal points applicable to all categories of works (even though the judgment was handed down in an applied-art context).

### Refinement 1 — A second exclusion ground: free choices without a unique aspect

> "Choices dictated by various constraints, particularly technical, which bound that author during the creation of that subject matter are not free and creative, and nor are choices which, while free, do not bear the imprint of the author's personality by giving that subject matter a unique appearance." (Mio/Konektra operative part 2)

The Painer formula "free creative choices reflecting the author's personality" acquires a two-sided exclusion ground:

| Exclusion ground | Existing doctrine | Mio/Konektra refinement |
|---|---|---|
| **(i) Dictated by constraints** | BSA, FAPL, Brompton, Cofemel | Confirmed; technical, ergonomic, safety constraints or sectoral norms/conventions (paragraph 62) |
| **(ii) Free but no unique aspect** ⭐ NEW | — | Operative part 2: choices that are indeed free but do not reflect personality through a unique aspect |

**Important nuance**: this is **not a heightening** of the originality threshold. Mio/Konektra operative part 1 + paragraph 57 expressly state that no heightened threshold applies to applied art, and the "unique aspect" criterion is an operational clarification of what the Painer formula always implied. Free choices are not automatically creative choices.

### Refinement 2 — No presumption of creative nature of choices (paragraph 65)

> "[U]nder the law of copyright, **the creative nature of the choices made by the author of the subject matter cannot be presumed**. Thus, the court before which the question of the originality of a utilitarian object is brought must **seek out and identify the creative choices in the shape of that subject matter** in order to be able to declare it protected by copyright, it being specified that **even where the author has made choices that are not dictated by technical or other constraints, it cannot be presumed that those choices are creative**, for the purposes of copyright." (Mio/Konektra paragraph 65, cross-ref Brompton paragraph 32)

Operational consequence: the burden of proof lies with the rightsholder. The court must positively identify the creative choices — they are not presumed from the mere existence of a freedom to choose.

### Refinement 3 — Intentions of the author = ideas (paragraphs 74-75) ⭐

> "[I]t is not ideas that are protected, but only their expression [...]. **However, the author's intentions lie within the realm of ideas. They can therefore be protected only in so far as the author has expressed them in the work concerned**." (Mio/Konektra paragraph 74)

The court may take account of the creative process and the author's intentions, "provided that those elements have been expressed in the object itself, although the court may not base its assessment **exclusively on those elements**" (paragraph 75).

This explicitly applies the idea/expression dichotomy (BSA, SAS Institute, Levola Hengelo paragraphs 41) to the **author's mental state**. Practical consequences:

- Design statements, design rationales, briefings, mood boards = **aids**, not autonomous grounds of proof
- The focus must be on the **objective form** of the object
- A "story behind the design" is insufficient if the creative choices are not visible in the final result

### Refinement 4 — Non-decisive factors (operative part 2 in fine)

Mio/Konektra provides an operational list of factors that the trial court "may, where appropriate, take into account, but which are **neither necessary nor decisive**":

1. **Author's intentions** during the creative process (see refinement 3)
2. **Sources of inspiration** (paragraph 79: variant of own work vs. inspired work — different regimes)
3. **Use of pre-existing forms** (paragraph 78: does not exclude originality; the arrangement can carry it)
4. **Possibility of independent creation** of similar objects
5. **Recognition in professional circles** / museums / exhibitions / awards

For each: may provide an indication, but cannot be decisive. Only the **identification of creative choices in the form** of the object is decisive.

### Refinement 5 — Pre-existing similar works (paragraph 80)

> "[E]ven if the law of copyright does not lay down a **requirement of novelty**, **the creation by a different author of subject matter that is similar or identical to given subject matter, before the creation of the latter, may constitute a relevant indication of the low degree, or even lack, of originality of the given subject matter**." (Mio/Konektra paragraph 80)

Copyright imposes no novelty requirement — which distinguishes it from design law — but pre-existing works can be an **evidential indication** that the claimed originality is low or absent. Counter-measure: independent creation of similar objects remains possible (particularly in applied art with technical constraints — paragraph 80 in fine).

### Coherence with the Painer formulas

The Mio/Konektra refinements do not undermine the Painer formulas but **sharpen** them:

> **Painer-Mio/Konektra formula (consolidated)**:
>
> An object is original when the author (a) had room for free creative choices, (b) exercised those choices without being dictated by constraints, (c) those choices give the object **a unique aspect** which (d) is identifiable **in the form** of the object itself and (e) reflects the author's personality. **Burden of proof on the rightsholder**; **no presumption of creative nature**; **intentions and non-decisive factors** as auxiliary indicators.

## Călinescu refinement: anti-severability rule and skill exclusion ⭐⭐⭐

Călinescu / FNSA (C-649/23, 19 March 2026) adds three generally relevant refinements to the originality test, handed down in the context of a critical edition but with broader scope. Sector-specific treatment of critical editions and derivative works in `07-derivative-works.md`.

### Refinement 1 — "Skill irrelevant" (paragraph 54) ⭐

> "[I]n order to determine whether a literary object such as the Slușanschi critical edition is original, it is necessary to ascertain [...] **whether, when drafting it, its author was able to make free and creative choices capable of conveying to the reader the originality of that subject matter, the originality of which arises, not from the words themselves considered in isolation, but from the choice, sequence and combination of the words by which the author expressed his creativity in an original manner and achieved a result which is an intellectual creation, whereas the skill in creating that subject matter is not relevant in that regard**." (Calinescu paragraph 54)

Călinescu explicitly integrates the sweat-of-the-brow exclusion (Football Dataco, Funke Medien) into the operational originality test for textual works. The judgment establishes that **expertise, professional knowledge and skill in themselves** do not imply originality — they may serve as background to demonstrate the possibility of creative choices, but are not autonomous grounds of proof.

In conjunction with Mio/Konektra paragraph 65 (no presumption of creative nature of choices), a consolidated burden-of-proof doctrine emerges:

| Factor | Evidential status |
|---|---|
| **Free creative choices in the form** | Decisive; must be positively identified |
| **Skill / expertise** | Auxiliary; not an autonomous ground (Călinescu paragraph 54) |
| **Author's intentions, creative process** | Auxiliary, provided expressed in the work (Mio/Konektra paragraphs 74-75) |
| **Pre-existing material** | Indication against originality (Mio/Konektra paragraph 80) |
| **Recognition in professional circles, awards** | Not decisive (Mio/Konektra operative part 2) |

### Refinement 2 — The anti-severability rule (paragraph 64) ⭐⭐⭐

> "**It is not necessary to draw a distinction between the parts corresponding to the original work, which may have been the subject of textual amendments, and the comments, critical notes or explanations accompanying them in order, where appropriate, to identify those parts which are capable of falling within the scope of copyright protection. Such an approach would run the risk of breaking up a work which is only meaningful as a whole**." (Calinescu paragraph 64)

Although formulated for critical editions, this doctrine has **broader scope**. It prohibits the trial court from artificially severing a work into "creative" and "non-creative" parts for qualification as a work where the work "**has meaning only as a whole**".

Applications beyond critical editions:

| Type of work | Anti-severability application |
|---|---|
| **Compilations** (anthologies, encyclopaedias) | Selection + arrangement + contributions may together form one work |
| **Multimedia works** | Text + image + sound + interactivity not to be severed artificially |
| **Applied art with functional elements** | Functional + aesthetic part may together form one work (cross-ref Brompton + Cofemel + Mio/Konektra) |
| **Software architectures** | Code + data + interface may together form one work (provided non-functional) |
| **Compositions with existing material** (collages, sampling works) | Original material + incorporated existing material: not to be automatically severed |

**Important boundary 1**: the anti-severability rule applies to the **qualification** as a work. It does **not** exempt the rightsholder from the Cofemel two-step test for the whole — the work **as a whole** must be original and objectively identifiable (Călinescu paragraph 65).

**Important boundary 2**: for the **infringement question**, the Infopaq doctrine remains applicable (components are protected where they contain elements that are the expression of the author's own intellectual creation — Infopaq paragraph 39). A partial reproduction may constitute infringement if it contains the creative elements (Călinescu paragraph 67 cross-ref Infopaq paragraph 39).

**Important boundary 3**: the doctrine applies only to works that "have meaning only as a whole". For works whose parts have independent meaning (e.g. a compilation whose individual contributions stand entirely on their own), it remains possible to assess individual components separately.

### Refinement 3 — Public-domain safeguard (paragraph 68) ⭐⭐

> "[T]he recognition of copyright in the critical edition of a literary work in the public domain does **not** bring that work into the private domain and cannot confer on the author of that critical edition an exclusive right over that pre-existing work." (Calinescu paragraph 68)

A generally relevant principle for all derivative works on public-domain works (critical editions, translations, adaptations, restorations): protection of the derivative work covers **only the delta** that the author adds — never the original work itself. For the infringement question: only the appropriation of the **specific creative additions** of the derivative-work author constitutes infringement; the appropriation of the original work (which is in the public domain) does not.

Applies by analogy to derivative works on non-public-domain works: copyright on a translation, adaptation or arrangement extends to the derivative author's own creative additions, without prejudice to the rights of the author of the original work (Berne Convention art. 2(3): "without prejudice to the rights of the author of the original work").

## Uniform scope of protection (Painer paragraphs 97-98)

Once the threshold is met, the **scope of protection is uniform** across all categories of works. Painer explicitly rejects the doctrine that certain categories of works (in casu portrait photographs) would receive narrower protection due to limited expressive scope. An argument such as "it's only a [photograph/utilitarian article/banal text], so protection is weak" is not legally sustainable under EU law.

## BSA doctrine: technical-function exclusion and idea/expression merger

The BSA judgment (C-393/09) introduces a **general exclusion ground** that pervades all subsequent case law: elements whose expression is **dictated solely by their technical function** do not meet the originality criterion (BSA paragraph 48).

### Operational formulation

> "where the expression of those components is dictated by their technical function, the criterion of originality is not met, **since the different methods of implementing an idea are so limited that the idea and the expression become indissociable**" (BSA paragraph 49).

In such a situation "the components of a graphic user interface do not permit the author to express his creativity in an original manner and achieve a result which is an intellectual creation of that author" (BSA paragraph 50).

### Idea/expression dichotomy and merger doctrine

This doctrine is the EU expression of two related principles:

1. **Idea/expression dichotomy**: copyright protects only the expression, not the underlying ideas, methods, functionalities, principles. For software this is expressly enshrined in art. 1(2) Dir. 91/250/2009/24; for other categories of works it follows from the general doctrine of copyright.
2. **Merger doctrine**: where an idea can be expressed in only one (or very few) ways, idea and expression merge — protection of the expression would amount to protection of the idea. The expression is then not protectable.

### General applicability

Although BSA deals with GUIs, the doctrine applies generally. Confirmations and extensions:

- **Cofemel** (C-683/17) — applied art and fashion items: same test, no additional aesthetic requirement but technical-function exclusion applies
- **Brompton** (C-833/18) — utilitarian articles (folding bicycle): technically dictated form excludes copyright protection, with direct reference to BSA
- **Funke Medien** (C-469/17) — **functional/administrative texts** (military situation reports): idea/expression merger expressly applied to texts whose content is "entirely determined by the information contained therein" and which have a "purely technical function". Cross-reference BSA paragraphs 48-50 (Funke Medien paragraph 24). See further `06-text-and-fragments.md`.
- **Sony/Datel** (C-159/23) — **runtime modifications of computer programs**: the Court refines the BSA/SAS Institute line for software by formulating a **reproducibility test** (paragraph 37). Elements that allow users to "use the program more optimally" without being able to reproduce or subsequently produce the program are not an expression. Runtime variables in working memory fall outside the scope. Cross-reference BSA paragraphs 41-42 (GUI) and SAS Institute paragraphs 39-40 (functionality, programming language, file format). See further `03-software.md`.
- **Mio/Konektra** (C-580/23 + C-795/23) — **applied art**: confirms and refines the Brompton doctrine (Mio/Konektra paragraphs 63-65: idea/expression merger where expression is technically dictated; "the creative nature of choices is not to be presumed"). Adds two generally relevant elements: (i) the "unique aspect" requirement for free choices (operative part 2); (ii) pre-existing similar works as an indication of low or absent originality (paragraph 80). See further `05-applied-art.md`.

### Brompton refinement: nuancing the technical-function exclusion ⭐

Brompton (C-833/18, EU:C:2020:461) refines the BSA doctrine for physical utilitarian articles. **Technical dictates do not automatically exclude copyright** — three scenarios apply:

| Scenario | Copyright? | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Form **exclusively** dictated by technical function | **No** copyright | Brompton paragraph 33 |
| Form partly technically dictated, room so limited that **idea merges with expression** | **No** copyright | Brompton paragraph 31 (BSA merger) |
| Form partly technically dictated, **but** room for free creative choices reflecting the **author's personality** | Copyright **possible** | Brompton paragraph 26 ⭐ |

> Brompton paragraph 26: "It follows that a subject matter satisfying the condition of originality **may be eligible for copyright protection, even if its realisation has been dictated by technical considerations, provided that its being so dictated has not prevented the author from reflecting his personality in that subject matter, as an expression of free and creative choices**."

The four criteria for assessment (Brompton paragraphs 35-37):

| Criterion | Relevance to the assessment |
|---|---|
| Existence of alternative forms | Indication of freedom of choice; **not decisive** |
| Effectiveness of the form | To be considered insofar as it reveals what considerations were made |
| Will of the alleged infringer | **Irrelevant** |
| Expired patent | To be considered insofar as it reveals the choice made by the author |

Time of assessment: design moment, not later (Brompton paragraph 37).

Distinction with DOCERAM (design law): for design law (art. 8 Regulation 6/2002), the test is "exclusively technical function", with alternatives not decisive; for copyright the originality test applies **in addition**, with alternatives providing indications but the core test remaining whether the author made creative choices.

## FAPL doctrine: rule-dictated events are not works

The FAPL judgment (joined cases C-403/08 and C-429/08) introduces a **second general exclusion ground** complementary to the BSA doctrine: events whose course is dictated by external rules cannot constitute an "author's own intellectual creation".

### Operational formulation

> "However, sporting events cannot be regarded as intellectual creations classifiable as works within the meaning of the Copyright Directive. That applies in particular to football matches, **which are subject to rules of the game, leaving no room for creative freedom for the purposes of copyright**" (FAPL paragraph 98).

### The *a contrario* mirror with Painer

FAPL paragraph 98 is explicitly the **a contrario benchmark** to which Painer paragraph 89 refers. Both judgments deliver the same doctrine in opposite formulations:

- **Painer paragraph 89 (positive)**: a work presupposes that the author was able to make **free and creative choices**
- **FAPL paragraph 98 (negative)**: where **there is no room for creative freedom** (e.g. by rules of the game), there is no work

In practice both apply simultaneously: for each alleged work, the test asks whether there was real creative latitude (Painer) or whether external rules eliminated that latitude (FAPL).

### Scope of application

The FAPL doctrine covers:

- **Sporting events** themselves (FAPL paragraph 98 expressly) — not the TV product surrounding them
- Other **rule-dictated events**: chess games, card games, organised competitions
- **Automatically generated output** without human creative input (by analogy)
- **Natural phenomena** of which a human is not the creator (by analogy)

Important boundary: it is consistently **the course** or **the event itself** that is not protectable. The **production around it** (camera choices, direction, commentary, editing, graphics) may well be a work (FAPL paragraph 149).

### National sui generis protection

FAPL paragraph 100 acknowledges that sporting events are "unique and in that respect original". Member States may grant comparable protection through specific national schemes.

## Two related negative exclusion grounds

BSA and FAPL together deliver the **two main negative exclusion grounds** of the EU originality test:

| Judgment | What does it exclude? | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **BSA** (C-393/09, paragraphs 48-49) | Elements whose expression is dictated by **technical function** | Idea/expression merger |
| **FAPL** (C-403/08, paragraph 98) | Events whose course is dictated by **rules of the game or other external rules** | No room for creative freedom |

In summary: **where the expression is dictated by external factors (technical, functional, rule-bound, conventional) without room for free creative choices, there is no work**. This principle is later consolidated in Brompton (C-833/18) for utilitarian articles.

## The unified test — Football Dataco (C-604/10)

With Football Dataco, the Court for the first time brings **all four originality formulas explicitly together** in one coherent test. Paragraphs 37-39 have become **the** operational standard formula for subsequent case law (Cofemel, Brompton, Funke Medien).

### Cross-references consolidated

Football Dataco paragraph 37 explicitly refers to the entire line of case law for the originality criterion:

> "[...] **the notion of the author's own intellectual creation refers to the criterion of originality** (see, to that effect, Case C-5/08 *Infopaq International* [2009] ECR I-6569, paragraphs 35, 37 and 38; Case C-393/09 *Bezpečnostní softwarová asociace* [2010] ECR I-13971 paragraph 45; Joined Cases C-403/08 and C-429/08 *Football Association Premier League and Others* [2011] ECR I-9083, paragraph 97; and Case C-145/10 *Painer* [2011] ECR I-12533, paragraph 87)."

### The unified positive test (Football Dataco paragraph 38)

> "As regards the setting up of a database, **that criterion of originality is satisfied when, through the selection or arrangement of the data which it contains, its author expresses his creative ability in an original manner by making free and creative choices** [...] **and thus stamps his 'personal touch'** [...]"

This is the Painer formula (paragraphs 89 + 92), now explicitly stated as the general EU standard.

### The unified negative test (Football Dataco paragraph 39)

> "By contrast, that criterion is not satisfied **when the setting up of the database is dictated by technical considerations, rules or constraints which leave no room for creative freedom** (see, by analogy, *Bezpečnostní softwarová asociace*, paragraphs 48 and 49, and *Football Association Premier League and Others*, paragraph 98)."

This brings BSA (technical considerations) and FAPL (rules) **together in one formulation**, with the third category "constraints" as a general catch-all.

### Practical significance

When a practitioner must cite **one** judgment that summarises the entire doctrine, Football Dataco (especially paragraphs 37-39) is a strong candidate. The judgment:

- Cites all four earlier key judgments expressly
- Brings the positive and negative tests together
- Definitively excludes "sweat of the brow" (see below)
- Confirms full EU harmonization

## Effort, skill and investment are irrelevant — exclusion of "sweat of the brow"

Football Dataco contains a third, general EU exclusion:

> "[...] the fact that the setting up of the database required, irrespective of the creation of the data which it contains, **significant labour and skill of its author** [...] **cannot as such justify the protection of it by copyright** under Directive 96/9, **if that labour and that skill do not express any originality in the selection or arrangement of that data**." (Football Dataco paragraph 42, operative part 1 third indent)

Although formally stated for databases, this principle applies generally to the EU originality criterion:

| What is **not** sufficient | What **is** required |
|---|---|
| Significant labour | Free creative choices |
| Skilful execution | Personal touch |
| Time and cost | Author's own intellectual creation |
| Editorial diligence | Originality in selection or form |
| Investment, expertise | Creative spirit |

This is the **EU rejection of the Anglo-American sweat-of-the-brow doctrine** (as previously found in the UK CDPA 1988). Effort is a factual element that can indicate originality, but never suffices on its own.

## Idea/expression dichotomy — SAS Institute (C-406/10)

The SAS Institute judgment confirms and operationalises the **idea/expression dichotomy** as a general EU principle, with international anchoring:

- Art. 9(2) TRIPs: "expressions and not [...] ideas, procedures, methods of operation or mathematical concepts as such"
- Art. 2 WIPO Copyright Treaty: same
- Recital 14 Dir. 91/250/2009/24 (Software Directive): the ideas and principles underlying logic, algorithms and programming languages are not protected

### Operational formulation (SAS paragraphs 31-34)

> SAS paragraph 31: "[...] **the ideas and principles which underlie any element of a computer program, including those which underlie its interfaces, are not protected**."
>
> SAS paragraph 33: "[B]oth Article 2 of the WIPO Copyright Treaty and Article 9(2) of the TRIPS Agreement [provide] that copyright protection extends to expressions and not to ideas, procedures, methods of operation or mathematical concepts as such."

### Policy consideration: anti-monopolisation (SAS paragraph 40) ⭐

> SAS paragraph 40: "[T]o accept that the functionality of a computer program can be protected by copyright would amount to making it possible to **monopolise ideas, to the detriment of technological progress and industrial development**."

Although pronounced in a software context, this policy consideration applies generally: EU copyright seeks a **balance between protection and free competition/innovation**. Protection of ideas (irrespective of their specific form of expression) would amount to a monopoly incompatible with the objectives of EU law.

### Legitimisation of similar independent works (SAS paragraph 41)

> SAS paragraph 41: "[T]he main advantage of protecting computer programs by copyright is that such protection covers only the individual expression of the work and thus **leaves other authors the desired latitude to create similar or even identical programs provided that they refrain from copying**."

A practical principle for all categories of works: **similar or even identical independent works are permitted**, provided that there is no appropriation of the specific expression of the protected work.

### Practical distinction: protectable / not protectable (general)

| Level | Status | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Idea** | NOT protected | The concept of a love story; the principle of a feature; a method |
| **Procedure / method of operation** | NOT protected | Recipe, algorithm, production steps |
| **Mathematical concept** | NOT protected | Formulas, statistical methods |
| **Functionality** | NOT protected | What a program can do, what a work achieves |
| **Style as such** | NOT protected | Genre, painting style, musical style |
| **Specific form of expression** | DOES qualify, if original | Concrete text, concrete painting, concrete code |

## The consolidation — Cofemel (C-683/17) ⭐⭐⭐

With Cofemel, the Court brings the entire doctrine together in **one paragraph** (paragraph 29) into **the** standard formulation of the two-step test:

> (paragraph 29) — "The concept of 'work' [...] constitutes [...] an autonomous concept of EU law which must be interpreted and applied uniformly, **requiring two cumulative conditions to be satisfied**. First, that concept entails that there exist an original subject matter, in the sense of being the author's own intellectual creation. Second, classification as a work is reserved to the elements that are the expression of such creation [...]."

In the three following paragraphs Painer (paragraph 30), Football Dataco (paragraph 31) and Levola Hengelo (paragraph 32) are again incorporated into this unified formulation.

### Practical consequence

When a practitioner must cite **one single paragraph** that summarises the entire doctrine, since 2019 **Cofemel paragraph 29** is the logical choice.

- Football Dataco paragraphs 37-39 remains the choice for the **originality** test separately
- Levola Hengelo paragraph 40 remains the choice for the **concept of work** separately
- **Cofemel paragraph 29** is the choice for the **two-step test as a whole**

### The prohibition on an additional aesthetic criterion (Cofemel operative part)

Cofemel categorically prohibits Member States from imposing additional criteria on applied art (or other categories of works):

> Operative part: Article 2(a) of Directive 2001/29 "must be interpreted as precluding national legislation from conferring protection, under copyright, to designs such as the clothing designs at issue in the main proceedings, **on the ground that, over and above their practical purpose, they generate a specific, aesthetically significant visual effect**."

This is an **equalisation rule** with dual effect:

- No stricter threshold for applied art (no "artistic quality", no "artistic radiance")
- No more lenient threshold for applied art (the full two-step test remains required)

### Aesthetic effect is not an objective criterion (Cofemel paragraphs 53-54)

> Cofemel paragraph 53: "[T]he aesthetic effect that may be produced by a design is the product of an **intrinsically subjective sensation of beauty** experienced by each individual who may look at that design. Consequently, that subjective effect does not, in itself, permit a subject matter to be characterised as existing and identifiable with sufficient precision and objectivity [...]."

Important refinement: the Court shifts the assessment from the **result** (aesthetic effect) to the **creative process** (free creative choices by the author). A beautiful object without free creative choices is not a work; an ugly object with free creative choices may be.

## Four related negative exclusion grounds

Since SAS Institute, EU law has **four related negative exclusion grounds** for the originality test, the first three of which were already brought together in Football Dataco:

| Exclusion ground | Source judgment | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Technical function dictates expression** | BSA (C-393/09) paragraphs 48-49 | Idea/expression merger; merger doctrine |
| **Rules dictate outcome** | FAPL (C-403/08) paragraph 98 | No room for creative freedom |
| **Effort without originality** | Football Dataco (C-604/10) paragraph 42 | Sweat of the brow excluded |
| **Idea / functionality as such** | SAS Institute (C-406/10) paragraph 39 + paragraphs 31-34 | Idea/expression dichotomy |

The first three are brought together in the Football Dataco formula (paragraphs 38-39). SAS Institute adds a **fourth, fundamental exclusion** that pervades all categories of works: the idea itself is **never** protected, no matter how original, complex or labour-intensive it is.

## Distinction work — components

A fundamental distinction from Infopaq (paragraphs 45-46): the **components** of a work (words, colours, notes, basic shapes) are not protected as such. Only their **specific selection, arrangement and combination** can carry originality.

This distinction is operationally crucial:

- An argument about originality must focus on the **combination/arrangement**, not on the individual components
- A defence may seek to demonstrate that the appropriated material consists only of unprotected loose components — not the original combination

## What the test does NOT require

Although these elements are expressly excluded only in later case law, it follows from Infopaq that the test is neutral as regards:

- **Aesthetic merit** or artistic value
- **Novelty** in the design-law sense
- **Investment** or effort as such (skill and labour without creative choices is insufficient)
- **Purpose** or destination of the work (utilitarian vs. artistic)
- **Length** or size (Infopaq paragraphs 47-48)

> Express confirmation in **Cofemel (C-683/17) paragraphs 54-55** (aesthetic effect on its own insufficient), **Football Dataco (C-604/10) paragraphs 38-39** (sweat of the brow excluded), **Funke Medien (C-469/17) paragraph 23** (sweat of the brow excluded) and **Infopaq (C-5/08) paragraphs 47-48** (length irrelevant).

## What the test DOES require

- **An intellectual creation** originating from the author (Infopaq paragraph 34)
- **Free creative choices**, not dictated by external necessity (technical, functional, conventional)
- **An original combination** of components (Infopaq paragraph 45)
- **An expression** that carries the author's own intellectual creation — even in fragments (Infopaq paragraphs 47-48)

## Burden of proof and factual assessment

The test is a **question of law of EU origin**, but the **application** to the concrete object remains a factual assessment by the national court (Infopaq paragraph 48: "which it is for the referring court to determine"). The trial court assesses the facts, subject to legal review on the test itself.
