# Methodology — Photography

> **Version 1.0** — based on Painer (C-145/10, ECLI:EU:C:2011:798) as the key judgment, with confirmation by Renckhoff (C-161/17, paragraph 14) and Cofemel (C-683/17, paragraph 30); supplemented with the verbatim text of art. 6 Directive 2006/116/EC (originality test for photographs + optional protection of "other photographs"). For the EU harmonization framework for originality as a whole: see `01-originality-test.md`.

## Legal basis

For photographs, EU law provides two successive protection regimes:

1. **Art. 6 Directive 93/98/EEC** (term of protection) — harmonized originality test for "photographic works" since 1993. Codified in art. 6 Directive 2006/116/EC.
2. **Art. 2(a) Directive 2001/29/EC** (InfoSoc) — general reproduction right for "works", including photographs that meet the originality threshold.

### Verbatim text of art. 6 Directive 2006/116/EC

> "Photographs which are original in the sense that they are the author's own intellectual creation shall be protected in accordance with Article 1. No other criteria shall be applied to determine their eligibility for protection. **Member States may provide for the protection of other photographs.**"

Three operational components:

| Component | Content |
|---|---|
| **"Author's own intellectual creation"** | Originality test — identical to the Infopaq formula (Painer paragraphs 87-94 confirm the identity) |
| **"No other criteria"** | Member States cannot apply an additional threshold — no "artistic value", no "personal stamp" as additional requirement. Doctrinal basis for the Cofemel rule (overarching treatment in `01-originality-test.md`) |
| **"Other photographs"** | Optional possibility for Member States to introduce a threshold-free neighbouring regime for photographs that do not meet the EU originality threshold (typically "simple photographs") |

Since Painer it has been clear that the Infopaq test (author's own intellectual creation) and the art. 6 test (Directive 93/98/2006/116) are **identical** — one uniform EU test, concretised through the three Painer formulas.

## The three operational formulas from Painer

### 1. Free and creative choices (paragraph 89)

A work is the author's own intellectual creation when the author has been able to express their creative abilities by making **free and creative choices**. *A contrario*: where there is no room for free creative choices (FAPL judgment, sporting events governed by rules of the game), there is no work.

### 2. Reflecting personality (paragraph 88)

An intellectual creation is the author's own creation when it constitutes the **expression of the author's personality**. This formula bridges the objective criterion (author's own intellectual creation) and the subjective element (personal expression).

### 3. Personal touch (paragraph 92)

Through their choices, the author imprints a **"personal touch"** on the work. Operational synonym for the "personal stamp" used in national legal traditions.

These three formulas reinforce each other and each express a different angle on the same EU concept of originality.

## The Painer checklist for photographs (paragraph 91)

For each photograph, free creative choices can be identified in **three phases**:

### Preparatory phase

- Staging / mise en scène
- Pose of the subject
- Lighting
- Location and background
- Costuming, props, set
- Direction of the subject (in portrait)
- Time of shooting

### Shooting phase

- Camera settings (aperture, shutter speed, ISO)
- Lens choice (focal length, type)
- Framing
- Angle of incidence / camera position
- Created atmosphere / mood
- Composition
- Moment of release (decisive moment)

### Development / post-processing phase

- Development technique (analogue: darkroom processing; digital: RAW conversion)
- Exposure and colour correction
- Retouching and editing
- Software choices
- Crop in post-production
- Conversion to black and white, contrast adjustment

This list is **not exhaustive** but illustrative. For other types of photographs (landscape, product photography, reportage, macro) the same phases apply mutatis mutandis.

## No "weaker" protection for photographs

Painer **explicitly rejects** two lines of defence that had appeared in national case law:

- "Portrait photographs have limited expressive scope, so weaker protection" — Painer paragraph 93: scope is "not necessarily limited or even non-existent"
- "Photographs receive less extensive protection than other works" — Painer paragraph 97: nothing in EU law supports gradation of protection by creative scope; paragraph 98: protection of a portrait photograph is "no less" than that of other works

**Implication**: once the originality threshold is met, the scope of protection is uniform. An argument such as "it's only a snapshot" or "portrait photographs are standard work" is not legally sustainable under EU law.

## Distinction original / non-original for photographs

EU law recognises **non-original photographs** as a category. Art. 6 Directive 93/98/2006/116 expressly states: "Member States may provide for the protection of other photographs." This means:

- Photographs that are original (free creative choices, personal touch) → **photographic works** with full copyright protection
- Photographs that are not original (purely reproductive, technically dictated, automatic) → fall outside EU copyright, but may receive sui generis protection through national law (e.g. "neighbouring right on photographs" in some Member States)

## Categories of photographs and their typical assessment

> *Indicative, not an automatic classification. The Painer test must be applied concretely to each photograph.*

| Category | Typical assessment | Note |
|---|---|---|
| Staged portrait photograph | Original (three phases full of choices) | Painer judgment itself |
| Reportage photograph (press photograph) | Generally original (framing, moment, angle) | Choice of moment is key |
| Fashion photography | Original (full creative direction) | |
| Landscape photograph | Original provided creative choices (composition, light, moment) | Beautiful subject ≠ original |
| Product / catalogue photograph | Variable — choices in light, background, angle possible; standard protocol problematic | White-background protocol may qualify as non-original |
| Snapshot / amateur photograph | Variable — threshold is low but not zero | Free creative choices still required |
| Surveillance / CCTV photograph | Generally not original | No human choice |
| Speed / flash photograph | Not original | Automated |
| Identity photograph (passport-photo booth) | Not original | Standardised |
| Reproduction photograph of 2D art | Not original | Deliberately no creative input — fidelity is the goal |
| Microscopic / scientific photograph | Variable — may be original where choices in framing and technique | Often technically dictated |
| Drone / aerial photograph | Variable — choices in path, angle, moment may be original | |

## The concept-of-work test (step 1) for photographs

For photographs, the concept of work in the sense of **objective identifiability** (Levola test, step 1) is in principle **not a problem**: a photograph is by definition an objectively fixed expression. The Painer test therefore focuses entirely on step 2 (originality).

Exception: for fleeting images or live streams of which no stable expression exists, the concept-of-work question may indeed arise again. That falls outside the direct Painer doctrine.

## Confirmation in later case law — Renckhoff and Cofemel

The Painer formula for photographs has been expressly reaffirmed twice by the CJEU, with some operationally important nuances.

### Renckhoff (C-161/17) paragraph 14 — confirmation plus procedural requirement

> "As a preliminary point, it must be recalled that a photograph may be protected by copyright, provided, **which it is for the national court to determine in each case**, that it is the intellectual creation of the author reflecting his personality and expressing his free and creative choices in the production of that photograph."

Two elements that Renckhoff paragraph 14 adds beyond a pure Painer citation:

1. **Generalisation to photographs in general**: Painer concerned a portrait photograph; Renckhoff concerns a travel photograph of Córdoba. Both fall under the same formula. This supports arguments regarding landscape photographs, travel photographs, product photographs with staging, and journalistic photographs.
2. **Procedural requirement of case-by-case assessment**: "**which it is for the national court to determine in each case**". Photographs are **not** automatically protected; the two-step test must be substantiated for each concrete work. This anchors the **burden of pleading on the photographer** — general references to their "style" or "technique" are insufficient; for each photograph the creative choices in the three Painer phases must be argued.

### Cofemel (C-683/17) paragraph 30 — definitive consolidation

In Cofemel the Court expressly refers to both Painer (paragraphs 88, 89 and 94) and Renckhoff (paragraph 14) for the standard formulation:

> "[I]f a subject matter is to be capable of being regarded as original, it is both necessary and sufficient that the subject matter reflects the personality of its author, as an expression of his free and creative choices."

Since 2019 this has been **the** standard formula, including for photographs.

### Scope demarcation — Renckhoff for the rest

> *The core subject of Renckhoff (art. 3(1) Directive 2001/29 — communication to the public, "new public" doctrine) falls **outside** the scope of this skill, which addresses only the concept of work and originality. For the right of communication to the public, separate analytical frameworks apply (Svensson, GS Media et al.).*
