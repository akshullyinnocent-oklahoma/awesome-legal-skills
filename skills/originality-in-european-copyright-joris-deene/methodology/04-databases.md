# Databases — Originality Test (based on Football Dataco, C-604/10)

> **Version 1.0** — based on Football Dataco (C-604/10, EU:C:2012:115, 1 March 2012), with cross-references to Fixtures Marketing (C-46/02, C-338/02, C-444/02), Ryanair (C-30/14) and CV-Online Latvia (C-762/19); supplemented with the verbatim text of art. 3(1) Directive 96/9/EC. For the EU harmonization framework for originality as a whole: see `01-originality-test.md`.

## Legal framework

Directive 96/9/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council of 11 March 1996 on the legal protection of databases (OJ L 77/20).

Key provisions:

- **Art. 1(2)**: definition of "database" — *"a collection of independent works, data or other materials arranged in a systematic or methodical way and individually accessible by electronic or other means"*
- **Art. 3(1)**: copyright protection — verbatim:

> "In accordance with this Directive, databases which, by reason of the selection or arrangement of their contents, constitute the author's own intellectual creation shall be protected as such by copyright. No other criteria shall be applied to determine their eligibility for that protection."

Three operational components:

| Component | Content |
|---|---|
| **"Selection or arrangement"** | The database equivalent of the Infopaq paragraph 45 formula "selection, sequence and combination". Copyright protects the **structure** of the database — not the contents, not the data, not the works included in it |
| **"Author's own intellectual creation"** | Formula identical to Infopaq paragraph 37. Football Dataco confirms that this is the harmonized EU formula for databases |
| **"No other criteria"** | Member States cannot apply an additional threshold. Football Dataco operative part 2 expressly excludes national "significant labour" criteria. Doctrinal basis for the Cofemel rule (overarching treatment in `01-originality-test.md`) |

- **Art. 3(2)**: limitation of protection — *"shall not extend to their contents and shall be without prejudice to any rights subsisting in those contents themselves"*
- **Art. 7**: sui generis right for the maker of a database which shows that there has been **substantial investment** in obtaining, verifying or presenting the contents
- **Art. 7(4)**: independence of the sui generis right from copyright
- **Art. 14(2)**: transitional provision for databases protected by copyright in a Member State on 27 March 1996 under different conditions

## Two-track system of database protection

| Protection track | Condition | Subject-matter | Term | Holder |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Copyright** (art. 3) | Originality in selection or arrangement | **Structure** of the database | Copyright term (life + 70 years) | Author (natural person) |
| **Sui generis** (art. 7) | Substantial investment in obtaining, verifying or presenting | **Contents** of the database | 15 years (renewable on substantial modification) | Maker (may be a legal person) |

The two rights are **cumulative** and **independent** (art. 7(4)). A database may be simultaneously protected by both, by one of the two, or by neither.

## The originality test for databases

### The concept of work (step 1)

For databases, no general Levola test applies. The concept of work is defined in the directive itself in art. 1(2): a **collection** of works, data or other independent materials, **arranged in a systematic or methodical way**, and **individually accessible**.

Three cumulative conditions:

1. **Collection**: multiple elements — not a single work
2. **Systematic or methodical arrangement**: a structure, not a random accumulation
3. **Individual accessibility**: each element must be retrievable or queryable as such, independently of the others

The Court has accepted in Fixtures Marketing (2004) and confirmed in Football Dataco paragraph 26 that **football fixture lists** constitute a database in this sense. Other examples from the case law:

- Telephone directories
- Collections of scientific articles
- Bibliographic databases
- Online job vacancy databases
- Antiquarian catalogues
- Collections of hyperlinks

### The originality test (step 2) — Football Dataco formula

The test consists of a **positive** and a **negative** side, consolidated in Football Dataco paragraphs 37-39.

#### Positive side (Football Dataco paragraph 38, based on Painer)

The originality criterion is **met** where the author, through the **selection or arrangement** of the data:

> "[...] expresses his creative ability in an original manner by making **free and creative choices** [...] and thus stamps his 'personal touch' [...]"

This is literally the Painer formula (paragraphs 89 and 92), applied to the structure of a database.

#### Negative side (Football Dataco paragraph 39, based on BSA + FAPL)

The originality criterion is **not met** where the compilation of the database:

> "[...] technical considerations, rules or constraints leave no room for creative freedom."

Three alternative exclusion grounds:

1. **Technical considerations** (reference to BSA paragraphs 48-49) — where the structure is dictated by technical necessities
2. **Rules** (reference to FAPL paragraph 98) — where external rules (rules of the game, standards, conventions) dictate the structure
3. **Constraints** — where organisational, legal or factual constraints remove the room for creative choice

## Three exclusions for the copyright protection of databases

Football Dataco formulates three additional exclusions (operative part 1):

### Exclusion 1 — Creation of data is not relevant

> "[...] the intellectual effort and skill of creating that data are not relevant in order to assess the eligibility of that database for protection by that right"

**Only the structure matters, not the contents.** Effort in generating data counts under the sui generis right (art. 7), not under copyright (art. 3).

#### Examples where this is at issue:

| Effort in data collection | Effort in selection/arrangement |
|---|---|
| Statisticians collect sports results | Their **categorisation** in tables |
| Researchers collect scientific data | Their **thematic arrangement** in a database |
| Telecom companies collect telephone data | Their **alphabetical ordering** in a directory |

For copyright, only the right-hand column counts. For the sui generis right, the left-hand column counts (provided substantial).

### Exclusion 2 — Adding significant content is not relevant

> "[...] it is irrelevant, for that purpose, whether or not the selection or arrangement of that data includes the addition of important significance to that data"

In Football Dataco the **addition of the match date to a data item** was specifically assessed. The Court holds: irrelevant — what matters is the originality of the selection/arrangement, not what is added to the data.

### Exclusion 3 — Sweat of the brow expressly excluded ⭐

> "[...] the significant labour and skill required for setting up that database cannot as such justify such a protection if they do not express any originality in the selection or arrangement of the data which that database contains"

This is the **EU rejection of the Anglo-American sweat-of-the-brow doctrine**. Labour, costs, expertise, time, editorial diligence: all irrelevant for copyright on databases (but relevant for the sui generis right).

## Full EU harmonization

Football Dataco operative part 2 confirms: Member States cannot apply **any other** originality criterion than that of art. 3(1) Directive 96/9.

> "[...] Article 3(1) of the directive **precludes national legislation which grants databases [...] copyright protection under conditions which are different to that of originality** laid down in Article 3(1) of the directive." (paragraph 50)

Practical consequences:

- Traditional "sweat of the brow" protection for databases is no longer permissible for databases falling within the definition of art. 1(2) (subject to transitional protection for pre-1996 databases)
- All Member States must apply the EU harmonized criterion

A database that falls neither under art. 3 (copyright) nor under art. 7 (sui generis) cannot be **protected by alternative national IP rights** (see Ryanair v PR Aviation, C-30/14). Contractual protection remains possible.

## Strategic application — concrete categories of databases

### Category A — Strong position for copyright protection

> *Examples of databases where original choices in selection/arrangement are **plausible**:*

- **Anthologies and literary compilations** — the choice of works and their thematic arrangement reflect the taste and vision of the compiler
- **Exhibition catalogues with substantive arrangement** — not merely alphabetical but according to curatorial perspective
- **Specialised databases with substantive filtering** — e.g. a database of "essential precedents in an area of law", where the selection choice is editorial
- **Online platforms with interactive navigation structure and original categorisation**
- **Scientific databases with own classification schemes** — not via standard nomenclatures

In this category the rightsholder must document the **concrete creative choices**: editorial guidelines, category choices, alternatives that were rejected, expert consultations on the structure.

### Category B — Weak position for copyright protection (focus on sui generis)

> *Examples of databases where the structure is largely dictated:*

- **Fixture lists** — Football Dataco doctrine: structure follows from rules and organisational constraints
- **Telephone and address directories** — alphabetical ordering is conventional
- **Reference work overviews with standard classifications** — e.g. NACE codes, ICD classifications, legal taxonomies
- **Market data with chronological arrangement** — quotations, sports results, traffic data
- **Collections according to external standards** — ISO standards, EU classifications

For these databases, the **sui generis right** (art. 7) is usually the appropriate protection track — provided substantial investment can be demonstrated.

### Category C — Borderline cases (to be factually assessed)

- **Online job vacancy databases** — selection criteria may be original, arrangement by standard categories usually not
- **Product catalogues** — an original "cookbook-style" arrangement may be original, a classification based on EAN codes not
- **Bibliographic databases** — depending on whether an own classification system or a standard (e.g. UDC) is used

## Practical checklist for the originality test

> *For any database claim under art. 3:*

**First step — qualification as a database (art. 1(2))**
- [ ] Is there a collection of independent elements?
- [ ] Are those elements systematically or methodically arranged?
- [ ] Are they individually accessible?

**Second step — originality in selection (Football Dataco paragraph 38)**
- [ ] Which data were chosen, and which were not?
- [ ] Were there alternatives?
- [ ] Did the selection follow from editorial/curatorial decisions or from external rules?
- [ ] Does the selection display a "personal touch"?

**Third step — originality in arrangement (Football Dataco paragraph 38)**
- [ ] What ordering scheme was applied?
- [ ] Is this scheme based on standards or conventions, or on own choices?
- [ ] Is the hierarchy/categorisation/structure unique or generic?

**Fourth step — exclusions (Football Dataco paragraph 39 + operative part 1)**
- [ ] Is the structure **technically dictated**? (BSA exclusion)
- [ ] Does the structure follow from **external rules**? (FAPL exclusion)
- [ ] Is reliance placed solely on **labour and skill** without originality? (sweat-of-the-brow exclusion)
- [ ] Is reliance placed on the **creation of the data** instead of its structure? (irrelevant under art. 3)

**Fifth step — in case of doubt: cumulate with sui generis**
- [ ] Was there a **substantial investment** in obtaining, verifying or presenting the contents?
- [ ] If so: parallel sui generis claim under art. 7
