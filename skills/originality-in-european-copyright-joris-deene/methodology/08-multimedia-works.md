# Methodology — Multimedia Works (complex matter)

> **Version 1.0** — based on Nintendo / PC Box (C-355/12, EU:C:2014:25, 23 January 2014) as the key judgment for multimedia works / complex matter, with cross-references to Infopaq (C-5/08), BSA (C-393/09), SAS Institute (C-406/10), Sony/Datel (C-159/23) and Călinescu/FNSA (C-649/23). To be supplemented as further CJEU case law on multimedia works (NFT works, AR/VR works, AI multimedia, interactive installations) is integrated.

> **Positioning**: this module addresses **multimedia works** as an independent category alongside software (`03-software.md`) and the other primary categories of works. A multimedia work is a **hybrid digital work** that **does not consist solely of a computer program**, but also contains **graphic, sound, narrative or interactive elements** with their **own creative value** that cannot be reduced to the digital encoding. The basis in EU law is **Directive 2001/29/EC** (InfoSoc) for the multimedia work as a whole, with **Directive 2009/24/EC** (Software Directive) applying only to the pure computer-program portion as *lex specialis*.

## Position within the two-step test

Multimedia works follow the standard Cofemel two-step test (paragraph 29): concept of work + originality. Nintendo (C-355/12) expressly confirms that **no heightened threshold** applies to multimedia works; they are assessed under the standard Infopaq-Cofemel formula. The test proceeds in two steps:

- **Step 1 — Concept of work**: the multimedia work as a whole must be sufficiently precisely and objectively identifiable (Levola Hengelo, C-310/17, paragraph 40)
- **Step 2 — Originality**: the multimedia work must be the author's own intellectual creation (Infopaq paragraph 35; Painer 88-92; Cofemel paragraphs 30-31). Free creative choices must be identified in each of the components **and** in their composition

The core point for multimedia works: the work is assessed **as a whole**; **no artificial severance** between technical-functional components (computer program) and creative components (graphics, sound, narrative).

## The Nintendo doctrine ⭐⭐⭐ — complex matter

### What is a multimedia work?

A multimedia work is a hybrid digital work that unites different creative components in one whole:

- **Computer program** (executable, scripts, smart contract, etc.)
- **Graphic elements** (images, 3D models, animations, UI design, typography)
- **Sound elements** (music, sound effects, voice-over, spatial audio)
- **Narrative elements** (storyline, dialogues, level design)
- **Interactive elements** (gameplay mechanics, input/output response, branching)
- **Data structures** (game state, content database, asset library)

Typical domain: videogames, mobile applications with multimedia content, interactive installation art, VR/AR works, web works with a multimedia dimension, NFT works with digital content, AI-generated hybrid works.

### The Nintendo core formula (paragraph 23) ⭐

> "[V]ideogames, such as those at issue in the main proceedings, constitute complex matter comprising not only a computer program but also **graphic and sound elements, which, although encrypted in computer language, have a unique creative value which cannot be reduced to that encryption**. In so far as the parts of a videogame, in this case, the graphic and sound elements, are part of its originality, they are **protected, together with the entire work, by copyright in the context of the system established by Directive 2001/29**." (Nintendo paragraph 23)

The formula has four operational components:

| Component | Content | Operational consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **(a) Multimedia = complex matter** | Not only a computer program but also graphic + sound elements | The concept of work does not sever the technical-functional carrier from the creative-substantive elements |
| **(b) Own creative value** | Digital encoding does not strip creative value from components | The fact that creative elements are realised via computer language does not detract from their independent creative nature |
| **(c) Lex specialis only for the pure software portion** | Dir. 2009/24 covers **only** the computer-program portion | Full regime of Dir. 2001/29 for the multimedia work as a whole |
| **(d) Holistic protection** | Components contributing to originality are protected **together with the entire work** | Cross-ref Infopaq paragraph 39 (partial reproduction) and Călinescu paragraph 64 (anti-severability rule) |

### Lex specialis boundary Dir. 2009/24 / Dir. 2001/29

A fundamental distinction Nintendo draws between the two directives:

| Aspect | Directive 2009/24 (Software) | Directive 2001/29 (InfoSoc) |
|---|---|---|
| **Scope** | Exclusively computer programs (art. 1(1)) | Works in general, including multimedia works |
| **Lex specialis position** | Specific regime for one category of works | General regime; yields to Dir. 2009/24 within its scope |
| **Specific exceptions** | Reverse engineering (art. 5), decompilation for interoperability (art. 6) | Specific exceptions art. 5 Dir. 2001/29 (quotation, parody, education, etc.) |
| **Concept of work** | "Computer program" as a defined concept | "Work" as an open EU autonomous concept (Levola) |

**Operational consequence**: in any multimedia case, the practitioner must ask — which components fall under which regime?

- Pure computer-program components (source code, object code, program structure, data structures as expression) → Dir. 2009/24 (see `03-software.md`)
- Graphic, sound, narrative, interactive components with own creative value → Dir. 2001/29 (this module)
- The multimedia work as a whole → Dir. 2001/29 (Nintendo paragraph 23)

### Link with the Călinescu anti-severability rule (paragraph 64)

Nintendo paragraph 23 and Călinescu paragraph 64 together form a **holistic concept-of-work doctrine for hybrid works**:

| Element | Nintendo (multimedia) | Călinescu (critical edition) |
|---|---|---|
| **Hybrid character** | Computer program + graphic + sound | Original text + corrections + commentary/apparatus |
| **Anti-severability statement** | Components are protected "together with the entire work" insofar as they contribute to originality (paragraph 23) | Not necessary to distinguish between "parts corresponding to the original work" and commentaries; a work that has meaning only as a whole is not to be severed (paragraph 64) |
| **Scope of the statement** | Multimedia works specifically | Works that "have meaning only as a whole" — cross-sectoral |
| **Relationship to partial reproduction** | Cross-ref Infopaq paragraph 38 (Nintendo paragraph 22) | Cross-ref Infopaq paragraph 39 (Călinescu paragraph 67) |

For the general doctrine on the anti-severability rule in cross-sectoral perspective: see `01-originality-test.md`, section *Călinescu refinement: anti-severability rule and skill exclusion*.

### What does NOT qualify as a multimedia work

| Example | Status |
|---|---|
| Computer program without significant creative graphic/sound output | Not a multimedia work; falls under Dir. 2009/24 |
| Command-line tool with a decorative banner | Not a multimedia work; banner component possibly separately protected under Dir. 2001/29 |
| Database without its own multimedia dimension | Not a multimedia work; database regime (see `04-databases.md`) |
| Mere file-conversion tool | Not a multimedia work; software under Dir. 2009/24 |

### What DOES qualify as a multimedia work

| Category | Qualification |
|---|---|
| **Videogames** (core case Nintendo) | Complex matter under Dir. 2001/29 (Nintendo paragraph 23) |
| **Interactive mobile apps with multimedia content** | Complex matter under Dir. 2001/29 (by analogy) |
| **VR/AR works** | Complex matter under Dir. 2001/29 (by analogy) |
| **Interactive digital installation art** | Complex matter under Dir. 2001/29 (by analogy) |
| **NFT works with substantial digital content** | Complex matter for the content layer under Dir. 2001/29 (by analogy) |
| **Multimedia educational works** (e-learning with video, audio, interaction) | Complex matter under Dir. 2001/29 (by analogy) |

## The Nintendo confirmation of Infopaq

### Concept-of-work formula (paragraph 21)

Nintendo paragraph 21 expressly confirms the Infopaq formula for the concept of work under Directive 2001/29 — not specifically for multimedia, but as a general basis:

> "As for works such as computer programs, they are protected by copyright provided that they are original, that is that they are their author's own intellectual creation." (Nintendo paragraph 21, cross-ref Infopaq paragraph 35)

### Partial-reproduction doctrine (paragraph 22)

Nintendo paragraph 22 confirms that **parts of a work** are protected insofar as they "as such contain the originality of the entire work". This applies directly to multimedia works: a graphic sub-element, a music fragment, a UI component or a gameplay segment that contributes to the originality of the multimedia work is as such protected (cross-ref Infopaq paragraph 38).

> Operational effect: for a rightsholder claim on partial reproduction of a multimedia work it suffices that the reproduced sub-element "contains the originality of the entire work". Cross-references: Infopaq paragraph 39 (applied in Călinescu paragraph 67) for the infringement question in partial reproduction.

## Operational checklist — multimedia works

### For the rightsholder (establishing copyright)

- [ ] Show that the work is a **multimedia work** within the meaning of Nintendo paragraph 23 (complex matter: not only a computer program)
- [ ] Step 1 (concept of work): show objective identifiability of the work as a whole (Levola)
- [ ] Step 2 (originality): show free creative choices in each of the components — visual, auditory, narrative, interactive — and in their composition/combination
- [ ] Show that the creative components have an **own creative value** that cannot be reduced to the digital encoding (Nintendo paragraph 23)
- [ ] Refer to **holistic protection**: components together with the entire work (Nintendo paragraph 23 in fine)
- [ ] Resist the other party splitting the work artificially into a software portion (Dir. 2009/24) and a non-software portion
- [ ] Invoke **Călinescu anti-severability rule** paragraph 64 for works that "have meaning only as a whole"
- [ ] Partial reproduction: cross-ref Infopaq paragraphs 38-39 and Nintendo paragraph 22

### For the alleged infringer (contesting copyright)

- [ ] Examine whether the work is really **complex matter**, or merely a computer program with minimal graphic output
- [ ] Examine whether the graphic/sound elements have an **own creative value**, or whether they are only technical/functional components (banal UI, generic sound effects)
- [ ] Per-component analysis possible: argue that individual creative components do not meet the threshold (e.g. standard UI elements, generic gameplay mechanics as ideas under SAS Institute, technical dictates under Brompton)
- [ ] Take advantage of the lex specialis boundary for pure software components: Dir. 2009/24 with its specific regime (decompilation exception art. 6)
- [ ] Limit the anti-severability rule (Călinescu paragraph 64): for multimedia works whose components have independent meaning (e.g. a mobile app with separately usable multimedia content), per-component analysis may continue to apply
- [ ] Idea/expression merger: gameplay mechanics, narrative clichés and generic design choices may count as ideas (cross-ref SAS Institute, BSA, Funke Medien)
