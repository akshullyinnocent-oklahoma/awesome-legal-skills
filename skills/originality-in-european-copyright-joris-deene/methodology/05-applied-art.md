# Methodology — Applied Art, Designs and Industrial Design (based on Cofemel, C-683/17 and Brompton, C-833/18)

> **Version 1.0** — based on Cofemel (C-683/17, EU:C:2019:721, 12 September 2019), Brompton (C-833/18, EU:C:2020:461, 11 June 2020) and Mio/Konektra (joined cases C-580/23 and C-795/23, EU:C:2025:941, 4 December 2025), with cross-references to Levola Hengelo (C-310/17), Football Dataco (C-604/10), BSA (C-393/09), Painer (C-145/10) and Infopaq (C-5/08).

## Position within the two-step test

Cofemel is a sector-specific judgment for **applied art, designs and design works**. The judgment applies the same general two-step test as for all other categories of works:

- **Step 1 — Concept of work**: objective identifiability (Levola Hengelo)
- **Step 2 — Originality**: free creative choices + personal touch (Painer / Football Dataco)

Particularity of Cofemel: the judgment **expressly prohibits** any **additional aesthetic criterion** for applied art. Member States cannot apply a stricter threshold than the general EU two-step test.

## The Cofemel uniformity rule ⭐

> Cofemel paragraph 35: "[T]he extent of that protection **does not depend on the degree of creative freedom exercised by its author**, and that that protection is therefore **not inferior to that to which any work falling within the scope of that directive is entitled** [...]."
>
> Cofemel operative part: Article 2(a) of Directive 2001/29 "must be interpreted as precluding national legislation from conferring protection, under copyright, to designs such as the clothing designs at issue in the main proceedings, **on the ground that, over and above their practical purpose, they generate a specific, aesthetically significant visual effect**."

**Equalisation rule**: applied art may not be subjected to stricter conditions than other works (prohibition of additional aesthetic criterion), and conversely not to more lenient conditions either (the two-step test must be applied in full). The threshold is **the same** as for all other categories of works.

## What is NO LONGER permitted after Cofemel

Cofemel categorically rejects a number of national criteria that applied before 2019 in various Member States:

| Former criterion | Status after Cofemel | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **"Aesthetically marked visual effect"** | Not permissible | Operative part |
| **"Artistic creation"** or "work of art" | Not permissible (implicit through non-answer to question 2) | Cofemel structure |
| **"Artistic" quality or "artistic radiance"** | Not permissible | Cofemel structure + Painer paragraph 98 |
| **Specific aesthetic threshold** above the EU criterion | Not permissible | Paragraph 35 |
| **Recognition in cultural circles** | Not permissible | Paragraph 35 + Cofemel structure |

## The two-track system: design right ↔ copyright

Cofemel confirms and operationalises the two-track system for industrial design:

| Protection track | Conditions | Term of protection | Holder | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Registered design right** | Novelty + individual character | 5 × 5 = 25 years (maximum) | Design holder | Directive 98/71/EC; Regulation 6/2002 |
| **Unregistered Community design** | Idem | 3 years from first disclosure | Maker | Regulation 6/2002 |
| **Copyright** | Two-step test (concept of work + originality) | Life + 70 years | Author (natural person) | Directive 2001/29 + Cofemel |

### Cumulation is possible but not automatic

> Cofemel paragraph 43: "[T]he EU legislature opted for a **system in which the protection reserved for designs and the protection ensured by copyright are not mutually exclusive**."
>
> Cofemel paragraph 52: "[T]hat **concurrent protection can be envisaged only in certain situations**."

Practical consequences:

- A registered design cannot **automatically** also be protected by copyright — for copyright the two-step test must be **separately** met
- "Novelty + individual character" under design law is no substitute for "author's own intellectual creation" under copyright
- The cumulation must be substantiated **separately** for each claim

### Fundamentally different objectives (Cofemel paragraph 50)

| Aspect | Design right | Copyright |
|---|---|---|
| Objective | Investment in new, individualised, utilitarian mass products | Protection of works that qualify as such |
| Character | Industrial | Cultural/creative |
| Threshold | Novelty (objective) + individual character | Author's own intellectual creation (subjectively anchored in the author) |
| Term | Short (max. 25 years) | Long (life + 70 years) |
| Competition trade-off | Limited to avoid restricting competition too much | No specific trade-off — a work deserves protection if it is a work |

## The four negative exclusion grounds — applied to applied art

For applied art the general exclusions covered in the skill apply in full:

### Exclusion 1 — Technical function (BSA + Cofemel paragraph 31)

> Cofemel paragraph 31: "By contrast, where the realisation of a subject matter has been **dictated by technical considerations, rules or other constraints which have left no room for creative freedom**, that subject matter cannot be regarded as possessing the originality required for it to constitute a work [...]."

Applications for applied art:

- **Functionally dictated elements** of clothing: anatomical fit, ergonomics, usage requirements, comfort zones
- **Industrially dictated elements** of furniture: ergonomics, static strength, production processes
- **Technical standards** in fashion (safety, labelling) and design (CE marking)

### Exclusion 2 — External rules and conventions

- **Genre conventions** in fashion (collars on shirts, trouser legs, button closures)
- **Fashion trends** as such — following a seasonal trend is not a creative choice
- **Market standards** (sizes, colour palettes, material choices)

### Exclusion 3 — Sweat of the brow (Football Dataco)

- **Production costs** and **investment** in the design process do not produce originality
- **Number of prototypes** or **duration of the design process** are not relevant
- **Craftsmanship** without free creative choices does not meet the threshold

### Exclusion 4 — Idea, style, concept as such

- **"Look & feel"** of a brand is not a work
- **Design style** (e.g. minimalist, Scandinavian, retro) is an idea, not a work
- **Aesthetic effect** is not an objectively identifiable subject-matter (Cofemel paragraph 53)
- **Concept** without concrete elaboration is not a work

## Strategic application — categories of applied art

### Category A — Strong position (copyright protection plausible)

> *Categories where free creative choices and personal touch are often demonstrable:*

- **Iconic design furniture** with distinctive design (think of classics included in MoMA collections)
- **Author designs in haute couture** with distinctive creative elements
- **Jewellery** with original design choices
- **Sculpture in functional context** (vases, lamps) where the artistic element transcends function
- **Original typefaces** and fonts
- **Unique industrial forms** not dictated by function or convention

In this category the rightsholder must document the **concrete creative choices**: design sketches, design rationale, rejected alternatives, designer statements.

### Category B — Weak position (typically the domain of design right alone)

> *Categories where the copyright claim generally does not succeed:*

- **Standard clothing** (basic T-shirts, basic trousers, basic sweaters)
- **Fashion-trend-following designs** that follow seasonal trends
- **Industrial packaging** with functional requirements
- **Mass-production articles** with standard design
- **Functional furniture** without particular artistic added value

For this category, **design right** (registered or unregistered Community design) is the appropriate protection track.

### Category C — Borderline cases (to be factually assessed)

- **Design variations** on standard products — depending on whether the variations display free creative choices
- **Lighting fixtures** — depending on whether the design is free or technically dictated
- **Sporting goods** — depending on whether the form is functionally necessary
- **Packaging design** — depending on whether the design is editorial/graphic
- **Bags and leather goods** — depending on whether the components reflect specific creative choices

## Practical checklist for the two-step test in applied art

### Step 1 — Concept of work (Levola Hengelo)
- [ ] Is the design objectively identifiable through drawings, photographs, prototypes, specifications?
- [ ] Can third parties (judge, competitors) ascertain without subjective assessment what is protected?
- [ ] **Important pitfall**: do **not** argue through "aesthetic effect" or "beauty" (Cofemel paragraphs 53-54)

### Step 2 — Originality (Cofemel paragraphs 29 + 30 + 31)

**Positive elements (paragraph 30 — Painer formula):**
- [ ] Which free creative choices did the author make?
- [ ] Were there alternatives that would have satisfied the function/requirements equally well?
- [ ] Does the design reflect the author's personality?

**Negative elements (paragraph 31 — exclusion grounds):**
- [ ] Is the form dictated by **technical considerations**?
- [ ] Does the form follow from **external rules** (genre, convention, standards)?
- [ ] Does the form follow from **constraints** leaving no room for creative freedom?

**Cofemel-specific pitfall:**
- [ ] Does the party argue **solely** on the basis of an "aesthetically marked visual effect"? → insufficient (operative part)
- [ ] Does the claim depend on **artistic quality** or **artistic radiance**? → not relevant (paragraph 35)

### Cumulation consideration
- [ ] Is there **parallel design right** available (registered design or unregistered Community design)?
- [ ] If so: claim **separately** for both rights, with separate substantiation

## The Brompton refinement for physical utilitarian articles ⭐

Brompton (C-833/18) builds on Cofemel and provides sector-specific elaboration for **physical utilitarian articles with a technical function** (folding bicycles, furniture, tools, means of transport, packaging, industrial products).

### The core refinement (Brompton paragraph 26) ⭐⭐⭐

> Brompton paragraph 26: "It follows that a subject matter satisfying the condition of originality **may be eligible for copyright protection, even if its realisation has been dictated by technical considerations, provided that its being so dictated has not prevented the author from reflecting his personality in that subject matter, as an expression of free and creative choices**."

This is an important nuance of the BSA doctrine: **technical dictates do not automatically exclude copyright**. The test is whether the author had room **despite** those dictates for free creative choices and used that room to express their personality.

### The three scenarios for utilitarian articles

| Scenario | Originality | Copyright? |
|---|---|---|
| Form **exclusively** dictated by technical function (Brompton paragraph 33) | No free creative choices possible | **No** copyright |
| Form **partly** technically dictated, room so limited that idea merges with expression (Brompton paragraph 31, idea/expression merger) | Idea/expression merger | **No** copyright |
| Form **partly** technically dictated, **but** room for free creative choices reflecting the author's personality (Brompton paragraph 26) | Possibly original | Copyright **possible** |

### The four criteria for assessment (Brompton paragraphs 35-37)

Brompton expressly answers four criteria put before the Court by the referring national court:

| Criterion | Relevance | Source |
|---|---|---|
| 1. **Existence of alternative forms** | **Indication** of freedom of choice; **not decisive** | Paragraph 35 |
| 2. **Effectiveness of the form** in achieving the technical result | **To be considered** insofar as it reveals considerations | Paragraph 36 |
| 3. **Will of the alleged infringer** | **Irrelevant** | Paragraph 35 |
| 4. **Expired patent** | **To be considered** insofar as it reveals the maker's choice | Paragraph 36 |

### Difference with DOCERAM (design law)

Brompton expressly contrasts the copyright test with the design-law test from DOCERAM (C-395/16, EU:C:2018:172):

| Aspect | Design law (art. 8 Regulation 6/2002, DOCERAM) | Copyright (Brompton) |
|---|---|---|
| Test | "**Exclusively** dictated by technical function" | Author has expressed creative ability originally through free creative choices |
| Alternative forms | **Not decisive** | Indication of freedom of choice, but **not decisive** |
| Originality requirement | No (novelty + individual character) | Yes (two-step test) |
| Objective | Protect industrial investment | Protect creative creation |

### Anti-monopolisation doctrine ⭐

> Brompton paragraph 27: "[I]t follows in particular from Article 2 of the WIPO Copyright Treaty that **copyright protection does not extend to ideas. Protecting ideas by copyright would amount to making it possible to monopolise ideas, to the detriment, in particular, of technical progress and industrial development** [...]. Where the expression of those components is dictated by their technical function, **the different methods of implementing an idea are so limited that the idea and the expression become indissociable**."

Cross-references: BSA paragraphs 48-49 (idea/expression merger) + SAS Institute paragraphs 33 and 40 (anti-monopolisation).

Practical consequences:

- Copyright may **not** be used to extend expired patents to life + 70 years
- Copyright does **not** protect the technical solution as such
- Copyright **can** protect the specific design choice, provided there was room for free creative choices

### Time of assessment (Brompton paragraph 37)

> Brompton paragraph 37: account must be taken of all relevant elements "**as they existed when that subject matter was designed, irrespective of the factors external to and subsequent to the creation of the product**".

Practical consequences:

- **Subsequent trends**: later fashion trends do not affect originality at the time of design
- **Later alternatives**: the fact that alternative solutions have been developed **after** the design does not affect the test
- **Subsequent market developments**: irrelevant

## Additional operational checklist — Brompton application to utilitarian articles

### Step 1 — Concept of work
For physical utilitarian articles, the concept-of-work test generally proceeds smoothly. Brompton paragraph 28 implicitly confirms that such objects can be "identified with sufficient precision and objectivity".

### Step 2 — Originality for utilitarian articles

#### First filter: is the form exclusively technically dictated?
- [ ] Given the technical requirements, was there room for creative choices? (Brompton paragraph 33)
- [ ] Is the room so limited that idea and expression merge? (Brompton paragraph 31)

#### Second filter: did the author make free creative choices?
- [ ] Which elements fell outside the strict technical dictates?
- [ ] Which alternative implementations were available?
- [ ] Which choices did the author make?
- [ ] Do those choices reflect the author's personality?

#### Indicia to weigh (Brompton paragraphs 35-37)
- [ ] **Alternative forms**: indication of freedom of choice (not decisive)
- [ ] **Effectiveness** of the form: reveals considerations
- [ ] **Expired patent**: reveals what was considered
- [ ] **Will of the infringer**: irrelevant
- [ ] **Timing**: only the design moment counts, not subsequent developments

## The Mio/Konektra consolidation for applied art ⭐

Mio/Konektra (joined cases C-580/23 and C-795/23, 4 December 2025) is **the third key judgment** for applied art, alongside Cofemel and Brompton. The judgment concerns dining tables on the one hand ("Palais Royal" vs. "Cord") and on the other the modular furniture system **USM Haller** (USM) vs. spare parts with assembly service (Konektra). The Court answers three groups of questions; for this module operative parts 1 and 2 are decisive (operative part 3 concerns infringement and falls outside scope — see `case-law/mio-konektra-c-580-23-c-795-23.md`).

### Contribution 1 — No rule-exception relationship between design right and copyright (operative part 1) ⭐

The Court definitively answers the question left open after Cofemel: **do higher originality requirements apply to applied art than to other works?**

> "Directive 2001/29 [...] must be interpreted as meaning that **there is no relationship of rule and exception between design protection and copyright protection**, such that, when assessing the originality of subject matter of applied art, it is necessary to apply stricter requirements than are laid down for other types of works." (Mio/Konektra operative part 1)

**Three clarifications of Cofemel (paragraph 54)**:

1. Design subject-matter is in principle **not comparable** to works protected by Directive 2001/29
2. There is **no automatism** between design protection and copyright
3. The criteria **may not be confused**: novelty + individual character (design law) ≠ originality (copyright)

**Cumulation remains limited** (paragraph 55, cross-ref Cofemel paragraph 52): the author must "create a unique work reflecting his personality". Mio/Konektra therefore does **not lower the threshold** for applied art — it confirms only that **the same threshold** applies.

### Contribution 2 — The "unique aspect" requirement (operative part 2) ⭐⭐⭐

The most substantial doctrinal addition of Mio/Konektra is a second exclusion ground within the originality test, alongside the classical technical-function exclusion:

> "**Choices dictated by various — particularly technical — constraints which bound that author during the creation of that subject matter are not free and creative, and nor are choices which, while free, do not bear the imprint of the author's personality by giving that subject matter a unique appearance**." (Mio/Konektra operative part 2)

| Exclusion ground | Source | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **(i) Constraint-dictated choices** | BSA, Brompton, Cofemel, Mio/Konektra | Technical, ergonomic, safety constraints; sectoral norms/conventions |
| **(ii) Free choices without unique aspect** ⭐ | **Mio/Konektra operative part 2 — NEW** | Trend-driven variations; conventional combinations; generic arrangement |

The requirement implies that **free choices are not automatically creative choices** for the purposes of copyright. The court must positively establish that the choices give the object a unique aspect that reflects the author's personality.

### Contribution 3 — No presumption of creative nature (paragraph 65)

> "[U]nder the law of copyright, **the creative nature of the choices made by the author of the subject matter cannot be presumed**. Thus, the court before which the question of the originality of a utilitarian object is brought must **seek out and identify the creative choices in the shape of that subject matter** in order to be able to declare it protected by copyright, it being specified that **even where the author has made choices that are not dictated by technical or other constraints, it cannot be presumed that those choices are creative**, for the purposes of copyright." (Mio/Konektra paragraph 65, cross-ref Brompton paragraph 32)

Operationally: the rightsholder must **prove** that the choices were creative — not merely that choices were made. The focus lies on the **form** of the object, not on the design process or the author's intentions.

### Contribution 4 — Author's intentions = ideas (paragraphs 74-75) ⭐

> "[I]t is not ideas that are protected, but only their expression [...]. **However, the author's intentions lie within the realm of ideas. They can therefore be protected only in so far as the author has expressed them in the work concerned**." (Mio/Konektra paragraph 74)

> "Consequently, the court considering the question of the originality of that subject matter **may take into account the creative process and the author's intentions, provided that those elements are expressed in that subject matter itself, but it cannot, however, base its assessment decisively on those elements**." (Mio/Konektra paragraph 75)

This is a principled holding that explicitly applies the idea/expression dichotomy to **the author's mental state** during the creative process. Practical consequence: testimony, design statements, design rationales, briefings and mood boards are only **aids** for identifying creative choices in the object itself — not autonomous grounds for originality.

### Contribution 5 — Pre-existing similar works (paragraph 80) ⭐⭐

> "[E]ven if the law of copyright does not lay down a **requirement of novelty**, **the creation by a different author of subject matter that is similar or identical to given subject matter, before the creation of the latter, may constitute a relevant indication of the low degree, or even lack, of originality of the given subject matter**. The fact remains that, in the case of subject matter of applied art, where various constraints characterised by their technical function limit authors' freedom, **it cannot be completely ruled out that two authors may have made, independently, similar, or even identical, creative choices**." (Mio/Konektra paragraph 80)

Mio/Konektra creates a new evidential balance here:

- Copyright imposes no novelty requirement (settled case law, in contrast to design law)
- **However**: pre-existing similar works may be an indication of **low or absent** originality
- Counter-measure: independent creation remains possible, especially in applied art

This is a **powerful weapon** for the pro-alleged-infringer. See `positions/pro-alleged-infringer.md`, section *Applied art*.

### Contribution 6 — Variant of own work vs. inspired work (paragraph 79)

Mio/Konektra distinguishes two regimes where the author has been inspired by existing objects:

| Type | Author | Protection |
|---|---|---|
| **Variant of own work** | Same author as the original | Protected as long as creative elements of the original remain and reflect personality |
| **Inspired work** | Different author from the original | May be independently protected, provided the two-step test is independently met (paragraphs 48 + 79) |

Operationally: for variants of own work the burden of proof shifts simply — the original is by definition original. For inspired works, the **new** author must independently establish that their work meets the two-step test. Mere inspiration is not an original creation.

### Contribution 7 — Pre-existing forms (paragraph 78)

> "[T]he author's use of already available shapes does not, in itself, exclude originality. **Indeed, subject matter composed solely of available shapes may be original where its author has expressed his or her creative choices in the arrangement of those shapes**." (Mio/Konektra paragraph 78)

This confirms and applies the Infopaq doctrine (selection, sequence and combination of building blocks — Infopaq paragraph 45) to utilitarian articles. For the pro-rightsholder: even if individual design elements are banal, the arrangement can carry originality.

### Contribution 8 — Recognition in professional circles, exhibitions, museums (paragraph 81 + operative part 2)

> Circumstances such as recognition in professional circles "may, where appropriate, be taken into account, but are **in any event neither necessary nor decisive** for establishing the originality of the object for which protection is sought." (Mio/Konektra operative part 2)

Professional recognition, museum acquisition, design awards and critical acclaim may be mentioned **incidentally** but **cannot be decisive**. This is an important limitation on the type of evidence that designers and galleries typically present ("this object is included in the permanent collection of MoMA…").

### The consolidated Cofemel/Brompton/Mio-Konektra two-step test for applied art

**Step 1 — Concept of work** (Levola; Cofemel paragraphs 32-33; Mio/Konektra paragraph 72):

- Sufficiently precisely and objectively identifiable
- A utilitarian article almost always satisfies this (provided sufficient determination in form/configuration — cf. USM Haller paragraph 41)

**Step 2 — Originality** (Cofemel paragraph 29; Brompton; Mio/Konektra):

- **(a) Free creative choices reflecting personality** by giving the object **a unique aspect** (Mio/Konektra operative part 2)
- **(b) Not dictated by constraints**: technical, ergonomic, safety constraints or sectoral norms/conventions (Brompton; Mio/Konektra paragraph 62)
- **(c) Identification of creative choices "in the form"**, not solely on the basis of intentions or creative process (Mio/Konektra paragraphs 65, 74-75)
- **(d) No presumption of creative nature**: burden of proof on the rightsholder (Mio/Konektra paragraph 65)
- **(e) Aesthetic effect on its own insufficient** (Cofemel paragraphs 54-55; Mio/Konektra paragraphs 67-68)

**Non-decisive factors** (Mio/Konektra operative part 2 in fine):

- Author's intentions during the creative process
- Sources of inspiration
- Use of pre-existing forms
- Possibility of independent creation
- Recognition in professional circles / museums / exhibitions

**Possible indicia against originality** (Mio/Konektra paragraph 80):

- Pre-existing similar or identical objects by other authors
