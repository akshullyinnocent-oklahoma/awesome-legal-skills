## Status within this skill — CONFIRMING JUDGMENT, not core judgment

> **Important scope delimitation**: Renckhoff is **not a core judgment** for the work-concept or originality doctrine (this skill). The main subject matter of the judgment is **Article 3(1) of Directive 2001/29 — the right of communication to the public**, and more specifically the application of the "new public" doctrine to the re-posting of a previously freely accessible photograph on another website. That subject matter falls **outside** the scope of this skill (work concept + originality) and belongs in a separate skill on exploitation rights.
>
> Renckhoff is addressed in this skill **only** because of paragraph 14: a preliminary statement in which the Court — as a stepping stone to the actual communication question — reaffirms the Painer formula and expressly applies it to photographs generally, with an important procedural directive to the national court. Cofemel (C-683/17) since refers expressly to Renckhoff paragraph 14 as authority for the Painer formula (Cofemel paragraph 30).

## Subject matter and category

- **Main category of the judgment**: communication to the public (art. 3(1) Directive 2001/29) — *outside the scope of this skill*
- **Relevant for this skill**: **paragraph 14** — preliminary confirmation of the Painer formula for photography, plus the procedural requirement that the national court must verify **in each individual case** whether the photograph meets the originality test
- **Applicable legislation (for this skill)**: Directive 2001/29/EC (implicitly art. 2(a))
- **Relevance for the skill**: ★★ — *merely confirmatory; no new doctrine, but an authoritative restatement frequently cited*

## Facts in four lines

Dirk Renckhoff, a German photographer, had licensed a photograph of Córdoba to a travel portal. A pupil at a secondary school in North Rhine-Westphalia downloaded the photograph from that travel portal — where it was freely available without download restrictions — and used it to illustrate a school project, which the school placed on its own website (with source attribution to the travel portal). Renckhoff brought proceedings in the German courts for copyright infringement. The Bundesgerichtshof referred a question on the interpretation of "communication to the public" (art. 3(1) Directive 2001/29) — not on the copyright status of the photograph, which all parties assumed.

## Legal question (in the judgment)

> Must the posting on a website of a photograph that was previously freely accessible, with the copyright holder's consent, on another website, be classified as a **communication to the public** within the meaning of art. 3(1) Directive 2001/29?

That is **not** a work-concept or originality question, and falls outside the scope of this skill.

## Consideration relevant for this skill — paragraph 14

### Paragraph 14 (official EUR-Lex EN)

> "As a preliminary point, it must be recalled that **a photograph may be protected by copyright, provided, which it is for the national court to determine in each case, that it is the intellectual creation of the author reflecting his personality and expressing his free and creative choices in the production of that photograph** (see, to that effect, judgment of 1 December 2011, Painer, C‑145/10, EU:C:2011:798, paragraph 94)."

## What paragraph 14 adds beyond Painer

Renckhoff paragraph 14 is not a verbatim copy of Painer paragraph 94, but an **application** of the Painer formula to photography generally. Two operational elements are made explicit:

### Element 1 — Photographs generally can be works

> "**A photograph** may be protected by copyright, provided [...]"

In Painer the photograph was a **portrait photograph** (Eva-Maria Painer was a professional school photographer). Renckhoff generalises: travel photographs and other non-portrait photographs can also be works, provided they meet the same test. This supports arguments in cases on landscape photographs, travel photographs, product photographs with staging, journalistic photographs, etc.

### Element 2 — Procedural requirement: per-case assessment by national court ⭐

> "[W]hich it is for the national court to determine **in each case**."

This is operationally relevant: photographs are **not** automatically protected. The national court must determine **per concrete work** whether the two-step test is met. That has direct consequences for the **burden of pleading and proof** in infringement disputes — a photographer relying on copyright must be able to show concretely which free creative choices were made, for the specific photographs on which he relies.

After Renckhoff this procedural requirement has been applied repeatedly: a general reference to a photographer's "style" or "technique" does not suffice — there must be **per-photograph** reasoning on which specific creative choices were made in the three Painer stages (preparation, shooting, development/post-processing).

## What Renckhoff does NOT add to this skill

- **No new formulation** of work concept or originality — it follows Painer paragraph 94 and is itself followed by Cofemel paragraph 30
- **No sector-specific refinement** for a category of work outside photography
- **No exclusion ground** or new negative test
- **No work-concept-step-1 contribution** (objective identifiability)

## Operational value for the skill — limited but real

Renckhoff paragraph 14 is useful in two scenarios:

### For the photographer-rightholder

A **third confirmation** by the CJEU (after Painer and before Cofemel) that photographs are protectable under the Painer formula. Where a quote is needed that is:

- compact (a single paragraph)
- verbatim repetition of the Painer formula
- expressly speaks of **photographs generally** (not only portraits)
- **post-Cofemel confirmable** (Cofemel paragraph 30 refers expressly to Renckhoff paragraph 14)

Renckhoff paragraph 14 offers a convenient anchor.

### For the alleged infringer

The phrase "**which it is for the national court to determine in each case**" provides a defence argument against blanket claims:

- No automatic protection for all photographs of a photographer
- Require the rightholder to substantiate **per photograph** the two-step test
- Burden of pleading on the rightholder — insufficient reasoning may lead to dismissal

This connects to general burden-of-proof principles in national procedural law.

## Important demarcation

For anyone citing Renckhoff in a photography-copyright file: use the judgment for **paragraph 14** (originality + procedural requirement of per-case assessment), not for the broader right of communication. The "new public" doctrine of Renckhoff (paragraphs 24-46) is a distinct doctrine on exploitation rights and has its own analytical framework (Svensson line, GS Media test).
