## Subject matter and category

- **Primary category within skill**: multimedia works / complex matter — first CJEU application of the work-concept to hybrid digital works
- **Particular contributions** ⭐:
  - **Confirmation Infopaq paragraph 35** (paragraph 21): work-concept formula for computer programs and other works — "the author's own intellectual creation"
  - **Confirmation Infopaq paragraph 38** (paragraph 22): "parts of a work [...] are protected by copyright since, as such, they share the originality of the whole work"

## Facts in four lines

Nintendo markets two types of game consoles ("DS" portable and "Wii" home), with technological protection measures (a recognition system in the console + an encrypted code on the physical medium of the games) which block illegal copies of videogames — and also prevent non-Nintendo programs and multimedia content from being used on the consoles. PC Box sold "mod chips" and "game copiers" via a website hosted by 9Net; these devices circumvented the TPM and made possible both the use of illegal copies and of independent "homebrew" applications (MP3, films, video). Nintendo sued PC Box and 9Net before the Tribunale di Milano under the Italian transposition of Article 6 Directive 2001/29. The referring court raised two preliminary questions on (i) whether TPM protection may extend to console-interaction and (ii) the criteria for assessing the scope of protection against circumvention.

## Legal question (sub-question relevant to the skill)

Beyond the main TPM questions (out of scope) lies the preliminary question whether videogames **as such** are copyright-protected under Directive 2001/29 and whether the lex specialis of Directive 2009/24 excludes the application of Directive 2001/29 to videogames.

## Operative part (partial — relevant to the skill)

The operative part principally concerns the TPM question (out of scope). For the skill, only paragraph 23 of the judgment is doctrinally relevant: the **complex-matter doctrine for multimedia works** flows from the operation of paragraphs 21-23 of the judgment, read in conjunction with the Infopaq doctrine.

## Key reasoning within scope

### Paragraph 21 — *confirmation of Infopaq work-concept formula*

> "In that regard, first of all it must be noted that Directive 2001/29 concerns, as is apparent inter alia from Article 1(1) thereof, the legal protection of copyright and related rights, including, for authors, exclusive rights to their works. As for works such as computer programs, they are protected by copyright provided that they are original, that is that they are their author's own intellectual creation (see Case C-5/08 Infopaq International [2009] ECR I-6569, paragraph 35)."

### Paragraph 22 — *confirmation of Infopaq partial-reproduction doctrine*

> "As regards the parts of a work, it should be borne in mind that there is nothing in Directive 2001/29 indicating that those parts are to be treated any differently from the work as a whole. It follows that they are protected by copyright since, as such, they share the originality of the whole work (see Infopaq International, paragraph 38)."

### Paragraph 23 — *the complex-matter doctrine for multimedia works* ⭐⭐⭐

> "That finding is not weakened by the fact that Directive 2009/24 constitutes a lex specialis in relation to Directive 2001/29 (see Case C-128/11 UsedSoft [2012] ECR, paragraph 56). In accordance with Article 1(1) thereof, the protection offered by Directive 2009/24 is limited to computer programs. As is apparent from the order for reference, **videogames, such as those at issue in the main proceedings, constitute complex matter comprising not only a computer program but also graphic and sound elements, which, although encrypted in computer language, have a unique creative value which cannot be reduced to that encryption**. In so far as the parts of a videogame, in this case, the graphic and sound elements, are part of its originality, they are protected, together with the entire work, by copyright in the context of the system established by Directive 2001/29."

## Doctrinal significance for this skill

### 1. Confirmation of the Infopaq doctrine (paragraphs 21-22)

Nintendo adds no new originality test but confirms two Infopaq elements:

| Element | Source Nintendo | Cross-ref Infopaq |
|---|---|---|
| Work-concept formula "author's own intellectual creation" | paragraph 21 | Infopaq paragraph 35 |
| Partial reproduction of parts which "share the originality" | paragraph 22 | Infopaq paragraph 38 (later also Infopaq paragraph 39 applied in Calinescu paragraph 67) |

### 2. The complex-matter doctrine for multimedia works (paragraph 23) ⭐⭐⭐

The judgment explicitly formulates for the first time that **multimedia works (in casu videogames)** must be regarded as **complex matter** to which the full regime of Directive 2001/29 applies, and not as computer programs under the software Directive 2009/24.

| Component | Content | Operational consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **(a) Multimedia = complex matter** | Not only a computer program, but also graphic + sound elements | The work-concept does not split the carrier (technical) from the content (artistic/creative) |
| **(b) Unique creative value** | Graphic and sound elements have a "unique creative value which cannot be reduced to that encryption" | The fact that creative elements are realised via computer language does not deprive them of their autonomous creative character |
| **(c) Lex specialis only for the computer program component** | Directive 2009/24 covers **exclusively** the computer program component | The multimedia work as a whole is governed by the regime of Directive 2001/29 (full — including all exploitation rights and exceptions) |
| **(d) Sub-elements protected together with the whole** | In so far as graphic/sound elements contribute to the originality of the work: protected **together with the entire work** | Partial reproduction of a graphic or sound element may constitute infringement of the multimedia work (cross-ref Infopaq paragraph 39, Calinescu paragraph 67) |

### 3. Relationship with the software case-law (BSA, SAS Institute, Sony/Datel)

Nintendo refines the scope of the software directive. Whereas BSA, SAS Institute and Sony/Datel refine the scope **within** Directive 2009/24 (what is an "expression" of a computer program?), Nintendo draws the **outer boundary** of the software directive:

| Judgment | Contribution to scope of Dir 2009/24 |
|---|---|
| **BSA** (C-393/09) | GUI ≠ expression of a computer program (falls under Dir 2001/29) |
| **SAS Institute** (C-406/10) | Functionality, programming language, file formats ≠ expression |
| **Sony/Datel** (C-159/23) | Runtime variables in working memory ≠ expression |
| **Nintendo** (C-355/12) | Multimedia works (videogames with graphic/sound elements) ≠ mere computer program — fall under Dir 2001/29 as a whole |

### 4. Connection with the Calinescu anti-fragmentation doctrine (C-649/23)

Nintendo paragraph 23 is a **doctrinal forerunner** of the Calinescu anti-fragmentation doctrine (Calinescu paragraph 64). Both judgments reject an artificial splitting of a work into protectable and non-protectable components:

| Judgment | Anti-fragmentation statement |
|---|---|
| **Nintendo paragraph 23** | Multimedia works: graphic/sound elements are protected **together with the entire work** in so far as they contribute to the originality |
| **Calinescu paragraph 64** | Critical editions: not necessary to distinguish between "parts corresponding to the original work" and commentaries/apparatus — the work has meaning only as a whole |

Both judgments formulate a **holistic work-concept approach** for hybrid works in which different components together constitute the work. Nintendo does so in a multimedia context (technical + creative components), Calinescu in a derivative-work context (public-domain text + creative additions).

### 5. Application perspective for modern digital works

The complex-matter doctrine has remained highly current over time and offers the work-concept anchor for:

| Type of work | Application of complex-matter doctrine |
|---|---|
| **Videogames** (core case Nintendo) | Computer program + graphic + sound elements + narrative |
| **Interactive installations** | Code + visual/auditory components + sensory input |
| **VR/AR works** | Code + 3D models + spatial audio + interactive factors |
| **NFT works (digital-only)** | Code (smart contract) + visual/auditory content + metadata |
| **AI-generated multimedia** | Algorithm + parameters + output components |
| **Web works / hypertext** | HTML/CSS/JS + content + interactivity |
| **Mobile applications** | Computer program + UI + multimedia content |

In all these cases, by analogy: the work as a whole falls under Directive 2001/29 (full regime), with the software Directive 2009/24 applying only to the pure computer program component.

## Argumentative consequences

### For those seeking to establish copyright in a multimedia work (pro-rightholder)

Nintendo is **particularly favourable** for the rightholder:

1. **Full regime Directive 2001/29**: the multimedia work falls under the full set of exclusive rights (reproduction, communication, distribution) and not under the limited regime of Directive 2009/24
2. **Holistic work approach**: graphic, sound and other creative elements are protected **together** with the computer program — no artificial splitting
3. **Unique creative value of components**: digital encoding does not deprive the components of creative value
4. **Partial reproduction suffices for infringement**: paragraph 22 — parts which share the originality of the work are protected
5. **Anti-fragmentation anchor (cross-ref Calinescu paragraph 64)**: assess the work as a whole, do not split it artificially

Strategic approach:

- Step 1 (work-concept): identify the multimedia work as a whole
- Step 2 (originality): show free creative choices in each of the components (visual, auditory, narrative, interactive) and in their composition
- Counter the software directive defence: paragraph 23 makes clear that Dir 2009/24 covers only the computer program component

### For those seeking to contest copyright in a multimedia work (pro-alleged-infringer)

Nintendo is **challenging** for the defence, but there are lines of attack:

1. **"Unique creative value" must be demonstrated**: paragraph 23 requires that the graphic/sound elements have a **unique creative value**. For banal, generic or technically dictated components, the work does not meet the threshold
2. **Per-component analysis possible**: although the work is assessed as a whole, the defence may show that certain components offer no creative contribution on their own (e.g. standard UI elements, generic gameplay mechanics which count as ideas under SAS Institute)
3. **Exploit lex specialis boundary**: for pure software aspects (algorithms, data structures, program structure) Directive 2009/24 applies with its specific regime — including the decompilation exception in Articles 5-6 Directive 2009/24
4. **Insufficient multimedia character**: for a work which *de facto* is only a computer program with minimal graphic output (e.g. a command-line tool with a decorative banner), the defence may argue that it does not qualify as "complex matter" within the meaning of Nintendo
