## Subject matter and category

- **Main category**: **applied art / industrial design** — application of the two-step test to useful articles and clothing designs; relationship copyright / design right
- **Special contribution**:
  - **Definitive formulation of the two-step test** in a single paragraph (paragraph 29) — drawing together Infopaq, Painer, Football Dataco and Levola Hengelo
  - **Prohibition of an additional aesthetic criterion**: an "aesthetically significant visual effect" does not suffice for work qualification (operative part)
  - **Confirmation of cumulation** of copyright + design right under EU law (paragraphs 43-47)
  - **Aesthetic effect = subjective**: does not meet Levola's objectivity requirement (paragraphs 53-54)
  - **Uniform scope of protection** confirmed: no lower threshold for applied art (paragraph 35, cross-ref Painer paragraphs 97-99)
- **Applicable legislation**: Directive 2001/29/EC (art. 2(a)); Directive 98/71/EC (designs harmonisation, art. 17 cumulation); Regulation (EC) No 6/2002 (Community designs, art. 96(2) cumulation); art. 17(2) Charter of Fundamental Rights
- **Relevance for the skill**: ★★★★★ — *the judgment that consolidates the whole line in one coherent ruling; work concept and originality; builds directly on Levola; confirms cumulation copyright/design*

## Facts in four lines

G-Star Raw has exploited the marks G-STAR, G-STAR RAW e.a. since the 1990s, *inter alia* the ARC jeans model and the ROWDY sweatshirt/T-shirt model. Cofemel — a Portuguese clothing producer — marketed comparable clothing models under the TIFFOSI brand. G-Star brought copyright infringement proceedings in Portugal. Portuguese case-law and doctrine were divided on whether clothing models under Portuguese law (art. 2(1)(i) CDADC) could enjoy copyright protection on the basis of an "aesthetically significant visual effect", beyond their utilitarian purpose. The Supremo Tribunal de Justiça referred preliminary questions.

## Legal questions

1. **Does Directive 2001/29 preclude** national legislation granting designs copyright protection on the ground that they, beyond their utilitarian purpose, **produce an aesthetically significant visual effect of their own** (with originality as a fundamental criterion)?
2. Does Directive 2001/29 preclude national legislation granting copyright protection to designs **if upon a thorough assessment they qualify as an "artistic creation" or "work of art"** (taking into account dominant views in cultural circles)? (Not answered in view of the answer to question 1.)

## Operative part

> Article 2(a) of Directive 2001/29/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council of 22 May 2001 on the harmonisation of certain aspects of copyright and related rights in the information society must be interpreted as precluding national legislation from conferring protection, under copyright, to designs such as the clothing designs at issue in the main proceedings, **on the ground that, over and above their practical purpose, they generate a specific, aesthetically significant visual effect**.

## Key reasoning

### Paragraph 29 — *the definitive two-step test in a single paragraph* ⭐⭐⭐

> "The concept of 'work' [...] constitutes [...] an autonomous concept of EU law which must be interpreted and applied uniformly, **requiring two cumulative conditions to be satisfied**. First, that concept entails that there exist an original subject matter, in the sense of being the author's own intellectual creation. Second, classification as a work is reserved to the elements that are the expression of such creation [...]."

This is *the* standard formulation of the two-step test since 2019. In a single paragraph the Court brings together the doctrine of Infopaq (paragraphs 37 + 39) and Levola Hengelo (paragraphs 33 + 35-37).

### Paragraph 30 — *originality = Painer formula* (cross-ref)

> "[I]t is both necessary and sufficient that the subject matter **reflects the personality of its author, as an expression of his free and creative choices** [...]."

### Paragraph 31 — *Football Dataco exclusion* (cross-ref)

> "On the other hand, when the realisation of a subject matter has been dictated by technical considerations, rules or other constraints, **which have left no room for creative freedom**, that subject matter cannot be regarded as possessing the originality required for it to constitute a work [...]."

### Paragraph 32 — *Levola formula* (cross-ref)

> "[T]he concept of a 'work' [...] necessarily entails the existence of a subject matter that is **identifiable with sufficient precision and objectivity** [...]."

### Paragraph 33 — *Levola rationales* (cross-ref)

> "That is because, first, the authorities responsible for ensuring that the exclusive rights inherent in copyright are protected must be able to identify, clearly and precisely, the subject matter so protected. So also must third parties against whom the protection claimed by the author of that subject matter may be asserted. Second, the need to ensure that there is no element of subjectivity, which is detrimental to legal certainty, in the process of identifying that subject matter means that the latter must have been expressed in an objective manner (see, to that effect, judgment of 13 November 2018, Levola Hengelo, C‑310/17, EU:C:2018:899, paragraph 41)."

### Paragraph 35 — *uniform scope of protection for all categories of work*

> "[T]he extent of that protection **does not depend on the degree of creative freedom exercised by its author**, and that that protection is therefore not inferior to that to which any work falling within the scope of that directive is entitled [...]."

### Paragraph 40 — *design right and copyright are fundamentally different*

> "In so proceeding, the EU legislature considered that **subject matter protected as a design was not as general rule capable of being treated in the same way as subject matter constituting works** protected by Directive 2001/29."

### Paragraphs 43-47 — *cumulation* ⭐

> (paragraph 43) — "Against that background, the EU legislature opted for a system in which the **protection reserved for designs and the protection ensured by copyright are not mutually exclusive**."
>
> (paragraph 47) — "Accordingly, Directive 2001/29 preserves the existence and scope of the provisions in force relating to designs, including **the principle of 'cumulation'** referred to in paragraph 45 of the present judgment."

### Paragraph 50 — *fundamentally different objectives*

> "[T]he protection of designs, on the one hand, and copyright protection, on the other, **pursue fundamentally different objectives and are subject to distinct rules**. [...] the purpose of the protection of designs is to protect subject matter which, while being new and distinctive, is functional and liable to be mass-produced. [...] the protection attached to copyright, the duration of which is significantly greater, is reserved to subject matter that merits being classified as works."

### Paragraph 52 — *cumulation only in certain situations*

> "It follows that, although **the protection of designs and the protection associated with copyright may, under EU law, be granted cumulatively to the same subject matter**, **that concurrent protection can be envisaged only in certain situations**."

### Paragraph 53 — *aesthetic effect is subjective* ⭐

> "[T]he aesthetic effect that may be produced by a design is the product of an **intrinsically subjective sensation of beauty** experienced by each individual who may look at that design. Consequently, that subjective effect does not, in itself, permit a subject matter to be characterised as existing and identifiable with sufficient precision and objectivity [...]."

### Paragraph 54 — *aesthetic effect ≠ originality*

> "Second, it is admittedly the case that aesthetic considerations play a part in creative activity. **Nonetheless, the fact remains that the circumstance that a design may generate an aesthetic effect does not, in itself, make it possible to determine whether that design constitutes an intellectual creation** reflecting the freedom of choice and personality of its author, thereby meeting the requirement of originality set out in paragraphs 30 and 31 of the present judgment."

### Paragraph 55 — *conclusion: aesthetic effect alone does not suffice*

> "It follows that the circumstance that designs such as the clothing designs at issue in the main proceedings generate, over and above their practical purpose, **a specific and aesthetically significant visual effect** is **not such as to justify those designs being classified as 'works' within the meaning of Directive 2001/29**."

## Doctrinal significance

### 1. The definitive formulation of the two-step test

Cofemel paragraph 29 is *the* standard formulation of the EU two-step test. The judgment brings together in a single paragraph:

| Element | Source | Consolidated in Cofemel |
|---|---|---|
| Work concept = autonomous EU concept | Infopaq paragraphs 27-28; Levola paragraph 33 | Paragraph 29 sentence 1 |
| Two cumulative conditions | Levola paragraph 35 | Paragraph 29 |
| Condition 1: originality = author's own intellectual creation | Infopaq paragraph 37; Levola paragraph 36; FAPL paragraph 97 | Paragraph 29 sentence 2 + paragraph 30 (Painer formula) |
| Condition 2: expression + objective identifiability | Infopaq paragraph 39; Levola paragraph 40 | Paragraph 29 sentence 3 + paragraph 32 |

For the skill: where in a submission **one** judgment must be cited that summarises the entire doctrine, **Cofemel paragraph 29** is, since 2019, the logical choice. Football Dataco paragraphs 37-39 remains the choice for the **originality** test alone; Cofemel paragraph 29 is the choice for the **two-step test** as a whole.

### 2. The prohibition on additional aesthetic criteria

Before Cofemel many Member States (including Portugal in the Portuguese case-law at issue) applied **additional criteria** for copyright protection of designs and applied art: artistic merit, aesthetic appeal, artistic creation. Cofemel rejects this categorically:

- **No "aesthetically significant visual effect"** requirement (paragraphs 54-55, operative part)
- **No "artistic creation" requirement** (the second question is not answered, but the operative part implies that this additional requirement too is impermissible)
- **No "artistic" or "fine-art" quality** as threshold
- **Uniform threshold** for all categories of work: only the Infopaq/Painer criterion "author's own intellectual creation"

This is an **equality rule**: applied art may not be subject to stricter conditions than other works, but conversely may not be subject to laxer conditions either.

### 3. Aesthetic effect as decisive counter-evidence?

Cofemel paragraphs 53-54 supply an **operational distinction**:

- **Aesthetic effect**: subjective sensation of beauty in the observer — not sufficient for the work concept
- **Aesthetic considerations by the author**: may form part of creative activity, but on their own are not proof of originality

The assessment thus shifts from the **result** (aesthetic effect) to the **creation process** (free creative choices by the author). A beautiful object without free creative choices is no work; an ugly object with free creative choices may be one.

### 4. Cumulation of copyright + design right

Cofemel confirms and operationalises the **two-track system**:

| Track | Condition | Subject matter | Duration | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Design right** | Novelty + individual character | Form of a (utilitarian) product | 5 + 4×5 = 25 years | Directive 98/71 + Regulation 6/2002 |
| **Copyright** | Author's own intellectual creation (Infopaq/Painer/Levola/Cofemel) | Work within the meaning of Directive 2001/29 | Author's life + 70 years | Directive 2001/29 |

The rights are **cumulable** (Cofemel paragraphs 43-47) but **not automatically** — copyright requires a full two-step test, not merely design-law novelty and individual character (Cofemel paragraph 52).

### 5. No automatic spill-over design right → copyright

Important: a registered design can **not automatically** also be copyright-protected. The design-law "novelty + individual character" test is fundamentally different from the copyright "author's own intellectual creation" test. For cumulation **both regimes must separately be satisfied** (paragraph 52).

Equally important: the design-law function exclusion (art. 8 Regulation 6/2002 — form dictated solely by technical function) and the copyright function exclusion (BSA/Football Dataco) are **not identical**. For copyright the test is **creative freedom**; for design law the test is **technical necessity**.

### 6. Relationship with later case-law

- **Brompton** (C-833/18, EU:C:2020:461, 11 June 2020) — direct sequel to Cofemel; application to folding bicycles; technically dictated form excludes copyright even where alternative forms are possible (extension of BSA doctrine to physical products)
- **Top System** (C-13/20, 2021) — software decompilation
- **VG Bild-Kunst** (C-392/19, 2021) — autonomous work concept confirmed
- **Mio/Konektra** (C-580/23 + C-795/23, 4 December 2025) — applies Cofemel two-step test to furniture; adds unique-aspect requirement
- Recent national case-law applies Cofemel as standard in cases on applied art, design furniture, fashion and industrial design

### 7. How Cofemel + Brompton extend the BSA doctrine

| Category of work | Source judgment | Exclusion |
|---|---|---|
| Software (GUI) | BSA (C-393/09) | Technical function dictates expression |
| Sports events | FAPL (C-403/08) | Rules of the game dictate outcome |
| Databases (structure) | Football Dataco (C-604/10) | Effort without originality |
| Software (functionality) | SAS Institute (C-406/10) | Idea/functionality as such |
| **Applied art / clothing** | **Cofemel (C-683/17)** | **No additional aesthetic criterion; cumulation but no automatism** |
| Useful articles | Brompton (C-833/18) | Technically dictated form |

Cofemel is therefore both a **confirming** judgment (of the general doctrine) and a **sector-specific** judgment (for applied art).

## Argumentative consequences

### For those wishing to establish copyright protection of a design/applied art (pro-rightholder)

#### Step 1 — Work concept (Levola/Cofemel)

- **Document the form of expression**: drawings, designs, prototypes, photographs, technical specifications, 3D models
- **Show objective identifiability**: the design must be objectively delimitable by third parties (not merely via a subjective impression)
- **For clothing models**: document specific elements — seams, cut features, placements, materials, colour choices, accessories
- **For design furniture**: document form, materials, construction method, proportions
- **Avoid subjective descriptions**: "elegant appearance", "timeless beauty", "artistic effect" are not objective arguments

#### Step 2 — Originality (Painer + Football Dataco)

- **Identify the free creative choices**: which alternatives did the author have, which choices did he make?
- **Show the personal touch**: comparison with other designs in the same category shows own vision
- **Document the design process**: sketches, variants, rejected alternatives, design rationale
- **Avoid function-dictated arguments**: technical requirements of the clothing item (such as articulation points, freedom of movement) do not generate originality

#### Cumulation strategy

- **Parallel protection**: claim **both** design right (if registered or unregistered Community design) **and** copyright, with separate argumentation per track
- **For design right**: show novelty and individual character
- **For copyright**: fully run the two-step test
- **Do not rely on design registration** as evidence of originality for copyright (Cofemel paragraph 52)

#### Anticipating the Cofemel defence

The opposing party will almost certainly contend that the claim does not meet the two-step test and that an "aesthetically significant visual effect" does not suffice. The rightholder must therefore **not** argue via aesthetics, but via:

- **Free creative choices** (Painer formula)
- **Personal touch** of the author
- **Objectively identifiable result** (Levola formula)

### For those wishing to contest copyright protection of a design/applied art (pro-alleged-infringer)

#### The Cofemel attack lines

##### Attack line 1 — No additional aesthetic criterion

> Cofemel operative part: a "specific, aesthetically significant visual effect" does **not** suffice to qualify a design as a work.

Operationally:

- Where the opposing party argues that its design is "aesthetically distinctive" or "fashionably striking", this argument can be rejected as irrelevant
- The absence of free creative choices (Painer formula) is not compensated by aesthetic appeal

##### Attack line 2 — Aesthetic effect is subjective (paragraph 53)

- Require the opposing party to show **concretely** which specific elements reflect free creative choices
- Point to the subjective character of aesthetic judgment — different observers have different impressions
- Argue that a subjective aesthetic judgment is insufficient for the Levola objectivity test

##### Attack line 3 — Functional constraints (Football Dataco exclusion, repeated in Cofemel paragraph 31)

- For clothing models: anatomical constraints, use requirements, wearing comfort largely dictate the form
- For design furniture: ergonomic requirements, material constraints, production processes dictate the form
- For industrial products: technical norms, safety regulations, market conventions dictate the form

##### Attack line 4 — Genre- and convention-bound elements

- Standard elements of clothing (collars, sleeves, buttons, zippers) are conventional
- Standard proportions and standard forms belong to the public domain
- Fashion trends are general tendencies, not creative choices of an individual author

##### Attack line 5 — Design right ≠ copyright

- The fact that a design is **registered** as a design does **not** mean that it is automatically copyright-protected too (Cofemel paragraph 52)
- The opposing party must **separately** show, for copyright, that the two-step test is met
- Design-law "novelty + individual character" is no substitute for copyright "author's own intellectual creation"

#### Strong examples of applied art where the copyright claim is weak

- **Standard clothing** (basic T-shirts, basic jeans) whose form is predominantly function-dictated
- **Fashion-trend-driven designs** following the conventions of a specific season
- **Industrial products** with technically dictated forms
- **Decorative objects** without notable free creative choices
- **Functional furniture** with standard proportions and standard constructions

#### Evidentiary leverage for the defence

- **Anteriorities**: older designs with comparable elements
- **Sector publications**: showing that the "creative choices" are sector-wide customary
- **Functional analysis**: which elements are dictated by use/anatomy/ergonomics?
- **Trend reports**: showing that the design follows a general trend
- **Expert opinions**: designers confirming that the choices were conventional

#### Citation form

> CJEU 12 September 2019, C-683/17, *Cofemel*, ECLI:EU:C:2019:721, operative part (aesthetic effect insufficient), paragraph 53 (aesthetic effect is subjective) and/or paragraph 31 (technical considerations/rules/constraints exclude originality) and/or paragraph 52 (cumulation only in certain situations).
