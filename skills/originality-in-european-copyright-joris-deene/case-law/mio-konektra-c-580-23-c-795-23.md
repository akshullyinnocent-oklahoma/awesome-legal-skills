## Subject matter and category

- **Primary category**: applied art / utilitarian objects — definitive consolidation and refinement of the Cofemel/Brompton line
- **Particular contributions** ⭐:
  - **Operative part 1**: no relationship of rule and exception between design protection and copyright; **no heightened threshold for applied art** (paragraph 58 + operative part 1)

## Facts in four lines

**Case C-580/23 (Mio/Asplund)**: Asplund designs and manufactures indoor goods and furniture, including the "Palais Royal" series of dining tables. Mio (Swedish retail chain) sells dining tables in the "Cord" series. Asplund brought infringement proceedings against Mio in October 2021; Mio disputed originality (referring to earlier registered designs). The Patent- och marknadsdomstolen found for Asplund; on appeal, the Svea hovrätt referred questions to the CJEU.

**Case C-795/23 (USM/Konektra)**: USM (Swiss) has for decades marketed the modular furniture system **USM Haller** (round, shiny chrome tubes + spherical connectors + coloured metal panels). Konektra (German) initially sold only replacement parts without objection from USM, but from 2018 reorganised its online shop into a complete-system supplier with assembly service and assembly instructions. USM alleged infringement; the Landgericht Düsseldorf found for USM; the Oberlandesgericht Düsseldorf dismissed the copyright claim (no work under Cofemel/Brompton); the Bundesgerichtshof referred questions to the CJEU.

## Legal questions

### C-795/23, first question

Is there a relationship of rule and exception between design protection and copyright protection, such that stricter requirements as to originality apply to applied art?

### C-580/23 questions 1+2 and C-795/23 questions 2+3

How is the originality of subject matter of applied art to be assessed? Does it depend on (a) the creative process and the author's intentions, or (b) factors visible in the subject matter itself? What weight is to be given to (i) shapes that are already available, (ii) sources of inspiration, (iii) independent creation of similar subject matter, (iv) recognition in professional circles, exhibitions, museums?

### C-580/23 questions 3+4 (outside scope of skill)

Infringement assessment: recognisable reproduction of creative elements vs. same overall visual impression? Relevance of the degree of originality? Relevance of the existence of similar subject matter?

## Operative parts

### Operative part 1 (on the first question in C-795/23)

> Directive 2001/29/EC [...] must be interpreted as meaning that **there is no relationship of rule and exception between design protection and copyright protection**, such that, when assessing the originality of subject matter of applied art, it is necessary to apply stricter requirements than are laid down for other types of works.

### Operative part 2 (on questions 1+2 in C-580/23 and questions 2+3 in C-795/23) ⭐

> Article 2(a), Article 3(1) and Article 4(1) of Directive 2001/29 must be interpreted as meaning that **subject matter which reflects the personality of its author as an expression of his or her free and creative choices constitutes a work, for the purposes of those provisions**. **Choices dictated by various constraints, particularly technical, which bound that author during the creation of that subject matter are not free and creative, and nor are choices which, while free, do not bear the imprint of the author's personality by giving that subject matter a unique appearance**. **Circumstances such as the author's intentions during the creative process, his or her sources of inspiration and the use of shapes that are already available, the likelihood of a similar independent creation or the recognition of that subject matter in professional circles may be taken into account where appropriate, but are not, in any event, necessary or decisive for the purpose of establishing the originality of the subject matter for which protection is claimed**.

### Operative part 3 (on questions 3+4 in C-580/23) — *outside scope of skill, here only for cross-reference*

> Article 2(a), Article 3(1) and Article 4(1) of Directive 2001/29 must be interpreted as meaning that in order to establish a copyright infringement, it is necessary to determine whether creative elements of the protected work have been reproduced in a recognisable manner in the allegedly infringing subject matter. The same overall visual impression created by the subject matter in conflict and the degree of originality of the work concerned are irrelevant. The possibility of a similar creation cannot justify a refusal to grant protection.

## Key reasoning

### Paragraph 48 — *the two-step formula reaffirmed* (Cofemel consolidation)

> "[T]he concept of 'work' covered by Article 2(a) of Directive 2001/29 constitutes, as is clear from the Court's settled case-law, an autonomous concept of EU law which must be interpreted and applied uniformly and which requires two cumulative conditions to be satisfied. First, that concept entails that there exists original subject matter, in the sense of being the author's own intellectual creation. Second, classification as a work is reserved to the elements that are the expression of such creation (judgment of 12 September 2019, Cofemel, C‑683/17, EU:C:2019:721, paragraph 29 and the case-law cited)."

### Paragraph 49 — *the Painer-Brompton formula reaffirmed*

> "As regards the first of those conditions, it follows from the case-law that, in order for subject matter to be capable of being regarded as original, it is both necessary and sufficient that it reflects the personality of its author, as an expression of his or her free and creative choices. However, when the realisation of subject matter has been dictated by technical considerations, rules or other constraints, which have left no room for creative freedom, that subject matter cannot be regarded as possessing the originality required for it to constitute a work (judgment of 12 September 2019, Cofemel, C‑683/17, EU:C:2019:721, paragraphs 30 and 31 and the case-law cited)."

### Paragraph 50 — *decisive criterion*

> "Thus, the requirement that the author's personality be reflected in the subject matter for which protection is claimed, through the expression of that author's free and creative choices, is **decisive** for the concept of 'originality' and, consequently, for copyright protection under EU law."

### Paragraph 52 — *design protection vs. copyright: different objectives*

> "That distinction between the criteria for protection is explained by the fact that the protection of designs, on the one hand, and the protection afforded by copyright, on the other, **pursue different objectives and are subject to distinct rules**. The purpose of the protection of designs is to protect subject matter which, while being new and distinctive, is functional and liable to be mass-produced. Further, that protection is to apply for a limited time, but sufficient time to ensure a return on the investment necessary for the creation and production of that subject matter, without thereby excessively restricting competition. For its part, the protection attached to copyright, the duration of which is significantly greater, is reserved to subject matter that merits being classified as works (judgment of 12 September 2019, Cofemel, C‑683/17, EU:C:2019:721, paragraph 50)."

### Paragraph 54 — *three clarifications of Cofemel* ⭐

> "It follows that, **first**, subject matter protected as a design is not as a general rule capable of being treated in the same way as subject matter constituting works protected by Directive 2001/29 [...]. **Second**, there is **no automatic connection** between the grant of protection under the law on designs and protection under the law of copyright. **Third**, the conditions for such protection, namely novelty and individual character, on the one hand, and originality, on the other, **must not be confused**."

### Paragraph 55 — *cumulation limited to certain situations: the author creates a unique work*

> "[T]hat concurrent protection is limited to certain situations [...], since an author is deemed to create **a unique work bearing the imprint of his or her personality**, which, as such, is protected in accordance with Directive 2001/29."

### Paragraph 56 — *explicit denial: no rule-exception relationship*

> "However, there is **no relationship of rule and exception** between the protection reserved for designs and the protection ensured by copyright."

### Paragraph 57 — *consequence: a design may be a work if the two-step test is satisfied*

> "It must therefore be held that **a design may be classified as a 'work', for the purposes of Directive 2001/29, if it meets the two requirements set out in paragraph 48 above** [...] and that the originality of subject matter of applied art must be assessed according to **the same requirements** as those used to assess the originality of other types of subject matter."

### Paragraph 62 — *specific nature of applied art*

> "[T]hat assessment must be carried out taking into account the specific nature of the type of works concerned. Works of applied art differ from other categories of works by the fact that they are primarily **utilitarian objects**. Such objects are the result of the creators' know-how and choices, which may be dictated by **technical, ergonomic or safety constraints**, or may result from **standards or conventions adopted in the sector concerned**."

### Paragraph 64 — *idea/expression merger* (Brompton cross-ref)

> "It follows from the case-law that the criterion of originality cannot be met by the components of subject matter which are differentiated only by their technical function, since copyright protection does not extend to ideas. **Where the expression of those components is dictated by their technical function, the different methods of implementing an idea are so limited that the idea and its expression become indissociable** (see, to that effect, judgment of 11 June 2020, Brompton Bicycle, C‑833/18, EU:C:2020:461, paragraph 27 and the case-law cited)."

### Paragraph 65 — *no presumption of the creative nature of choices* ⭐

> "It follows that, under the law of copyright, **the creative nature of the choices made by the author of the subject matter cannot be presumed**. Thus, the court before which the question of the originality of a utilitarian object is brought must **seek out and identify the creative choices in the shape of that subject matter** in order to be able to declare it protected by copyright, it being specified that **even where the author has made choices that are not dictated by technical or other constraints, it cannot be presumed that those choices are creative**, for the purposes of copyright (see, to that effect, judgment of 11 June 2020, Brompton Bicycle, C‑833/18, EU:C:2020:461, paragraph 32)."

### Paragraph 66 — *components as independently protectable elements* (Infopaq cross-ref)

> "As regards the components of a utilitarian object, those components are subject to the same rules as the object as a whole. **The components of a work thus enjoy protection under Article 2(a) of Directive 2001/29 provided that they contain elements which are the original expression of the author of that work and, as such, they share the originality of the whole work** (see, to that effect, judgment of 16 July 2009, Infopaq International, C‑5/08, EU:C:2009:465, paragraphs 38 and 39)."

### Paragraphs 67-68 — *aesthetic effect not sufficient* (Cofemel cross-ref)

> **Paragraph 67** — "[W]hile it is true that artistic or aesthetic considerations play a part in creative activity, the fact that a design generates such an effect does not, in itself, make it possible to determine whether that design constitutes an intellectual creation reflecting the freedom of choice and personality of its author, thereby meeting the requirement of originality (judgment of 12 September 2019, Cofemel, C‑683/17, EU:C:2019:721, paragraph 54)."
>
> **Paragraph 68** — "**Thus, the fact that a design generates, beyond its practical purpose, a specific and aesthetically or artistically significant visual effect is not, in itself, such as to justify that design being classified as a 'work', within the meaning of Directive 2001/29** (see, to that effect, judgment of 12 September 2019, Cofemel, C‑683/17, EU:C:2019:721, paragraph 55)."

### Paragraph 71 — *linguistic argument: choices must be visible*

> "[T]he use of the words 'reflects' and 'expression' clearly indicates that **such choices and the author's personality must be visible in the subject matter for which protection is claimed**."

### Paragraph 72 — *objective identifiability reaffirmed* (step 1 requirement)

> "[T]he Court has stated that the concept of 'work', that is the subject of Directive 2001/29, entails the existence of subject matter that is **identifiable with sufficient precision and objectivity**. That is because, first, the authorities responsible for ensuring that the exclusive rights inherent in copyright are protected must be able to identify, clearly and precisely, the subject matter so protected. So also must third parties against whom the protection claimed by the author of that subject matter may be asserted. Second, **the need to exclude any element of subjectivity, which is detrimental to legal certainty**, in the process of identifying that subject matter means that the latter must have been expressed in an objective manner (see, to that effect, judgment of 12 September 2019, Cofemel, C‑683/17, EU:C:2019:721, paragraphs 32 and 33 and the case-law cited)."

### Paragraph 74 — *author's intentions = ideas* ⭐⭐⭐

> "That requirement for subject matter that is identifiable as a work is based on the fundamental principle of the law of copyright, according to which **it is not ideas that are protected, but only their expression** (see, to that effect, judgment of 11 June 2020, Brompton Bicycle, C‑833/18, EU:C:2020:461, paragraph 27). **However, the author's intentions lie within the realm of ideas. They can therefore be protected only in so far as the author has expressed them in the work concerned**."

### Paragraph 75 — *creative process and intentions may be considered, but not decisively*

> "Consequently, the court considering the question of the originality of that subject matter **may take into account the creative process and the author's intentions, provided that those elements are expressed in that subject matter itself, but it cannot, however, base its assessment decisively on those elements**."

### Paragraph 77 — *relevant elements at the time of design* (Brompton cross-ref)

> "[I]n accordance with settled case-law, in order to assess whether subject matter constitutes an original creation and is thus protected by copyright, it is for the court seised to **take account of all relevant aspects of the particular case as they existed when that subject matter was designed, irrespective of the factors external to and subsequent to the creation** (see, to that effect, judgment of 11 June 2020, Brompton Bicycle, C‑833/18, EU:C:2020:461, paragraph 37)."

### Paragraph 78 — *use of available shapes does not preclude originality*

> "[T]he author's use of already available shapes does not, in itself, exclude originality. Indeed, **subject matter composed solely of available shapes may be original where its author has expressed his or her creative choices in the arrangement of those shapes**."

### Paragraph 79 — *variant of one's own work vs. inspired work*

> "[A]s regards the situation in which the author of the subject matter has taken inspiration from existing subject matter, copyright protection will be limited to identifying the author's own creative elements. **Where the subject matter in question is a 'variant' of an existing work, created by the same author and original by definition, it may be eligible for copyright protection as long as the creative elements reproduced remain therein and constitute the imprint of that author's personality**. **By contrast, where the authors are different, that subject matter must then be regarded as an inspired work, that is to say, as a work that does not reproduce the creative elements of another work as such, but draws on them in some other way**. That new work may nevertheless also enjoy that protection as such, provided that the requirements laid down by the case-law cited in paragraph 48 above are met."

### Paragraph 80 — *prior similar works as indication of low or no originality* ⭐⭐⭐

> "[E]ven if the law of copyright does not lay down a requirement of novelty, **the creation by a different author of subject matter that is similar or identical to given subject matter, before the creation of the latter, may constitute a relevant indication of the low degree, or even lack, of originality of the given subject matter**. The fact remains that, in the case of subject matter of applied art, where various constraints characterised by their technical function limit authors' freedom, **it cannot be completely ruled out that two authors may have made, independently, similar, or even identical, creative choices**."

### Paragraph 81 — *recognition in professional circles, exhibitions, museums*

> "[C]ircumstances such as the display of subject matter in art exhibitions or museums and its recognition in professional circles, [...] which are external and subsequent to the creation of that subject matter, are not, in accordance with the case-law cited in paragraph 77 above, either necessary or decisive as such."

## Doctrinal significance for this skill

### 1. The definitive consolidation of the Cofemel/Brompton line

Mio/Konektra is not an entirely new doctrine but **the definitive consolidation and operational refinement** of the Cofemel-Brompton line for applied art. The three key judgments relate as follows:

| Judgment | Contribution |
|---|---|
| **Cofemel** (C-683/17, 2019) | Two-step formula paragraph 29; prohibition of an additional aesthetic criterion; cumulation copyright/design limited |
| **Brompton** (C-833/18, 2020) | Technical constraints do not automatically exclude copyright; alternatives not decisive; expired patent as indication |
| **Mio/Konektra** (C-580/23 + C-795/23, 2025) | No rule-exception relationship; "unique appearance" requirement; intentions = ideas; already available shapes; prior subject matter; recognition in professional circles not decisive |

### 2. The "unique appearance" requirement (operative part 2) ⭐

This is the **most substantial doctrinal addition** of Mio/Konektra. The Court formulates a **second exclusion ground** within the originality test, alongside the classical technical-function exclusion:

| Exclusion ground | Source | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **(i) Choices dictated by constraints** | Classical (BSA, Brompton, Cofemel) | Technical, ergonomic, safety constraints; sector standards/conventions |
| **(ii) Free choices without a unique appearance** ⭐ NEW | Mio/Konektra operative part 2 | Trend-based variations; conventional combinations; generic arrangement |

In concrete terms: it no longer suffices that the author has made "free choices" — those choices must also **bear the imprint of the author's personality by giving the subject matter a unique appearance**.

**Important nuance**: at first sight this looks like a raising of the originality threshold. But Mio/Konektra operative part 1 + paragraph 57 make explicit that **there is no heightened threshold for applied art**. The "unique appearance" requirement is therefore an **operational clarification** of what "free creative choices reflecting personality" (Painer/Cofemel) always already entailed. Free choices are not automatically creative choices.

### 3. The list of non-decisive factors (operative part 2 in fine)

Mio/Konektra gives an exhaustive list of factors which the trial court "may be taken into account where appropriate, but are not, in any event, **necessary or decisive**":

1. **Author's intentions** during the creative process
2. **Sources of inspiration**
3. **Use of shapes that are already available**
4. **Likelihood of independent creation** of similar subject matter
5. **Recognition in professional circles** (museums, exhibitions, awards)

For each of these factors: they **may** be indicative, but cannot be **determinative**. The determinative factor is exclusively the **objective identification of creative choices in the shape of the subject matter** (paragraphs 65, 71, 75).

### 4. Author's intentions = ideas (paragraph 74) ⭐

The proposition that "the author's intentions lie within the realm of ideas" and "can be protected only in so far as the author has expressed them in the work concerned" is principled and sharp. It builds on the idea/expression dichotomy (BSA paragraphs 41-42; SAS Institute; Sony/Datel paragraph 37) but applies it explicitly to the **mental state of the author** during the creative process.

Practical consequence: witness statements, design declarations, design rationales, briefings, moodboards can serve **only as aids** in identifying the creative choices in the subject matter itself. They do not constitute an independent ground of originality.

### 5. Prior similar subject matter (paragraph 80) ⭐⭐

Mio/Konektra creates a new **evidential** balance here:

- Copyright imposes **no novelty requirement** (settled case-law, in contrast to design law)
- **BUT**: pre-existing similar or identical subject matter may be a **relevant indication of the low degree or absence of originality**
- **COUNTER-MEASURE**: independent creation of similar subject matter remains possible, especially in applied art where technical constraints limit creative scope

This is an **important line of attack for the pro-alleged-infringer**: by showing prior design heritage, the originality of the claimed work can be undermined, not as a categorical exclusion but as an indication.

### 6. Variant vs. inspired work (paragraph 79)

Mio/Konektra distinguishes two regimes:

| Type | Author | Protection |
|---|---|---|
| **Variant** | Same author as original | Protected as long as creative elements remain present and reflect personality |
| **Inspired work** | Different author | May be independently protected on its own merits (two-step test) |

The difference is operationally relevant: for a variant of one's own work, the burden of proof shifts relatively easily (the original was by definition original; show that the variant preserves the elements). For an inspired work, the **new** author must independently demonstrate that his or her work meets the two-step test — mere inspiration is not in itself an original creation.

### 7. No rule-exception relationship between design protection and copyright (operative part 1)

Before Mio/Konektra there was uncertainty after Cofemel: does copyright impose heightened originality requirements for applied art compared to other works? The Bundesgerichtshof (in C-795/23) explicitly raised such a rule-exception relationship. The Court definitively answers: **NO**.

But beware of a common misunderstanding: this does **not** mean that cumulation of design and copyright protection becomes routine. Paragraph 55 expressly recalls: cumulation remains "limited to certain situations". The criterion is and remains: **a unique work bearing the imprint of the author's personality** (paragraph 55, with explicit cross-ref Cofemel paragraph 52). Mio/Konektra does not lower the threshold for applied art — it merely confirms that the threshold is **the same** as for other works.

## Relationship with Sony/Datel — software directive vs. InfoSoc

Mio/Konektra and Sony/Datel (C-159/23) were decided within close range (within one year) and belong to the most recent case-law of the First Chamber on the work-concept/originality. Key differences:

| | Sony/Datel | Mio/Konektra |
|---|---|---|
| Directive | 2009/24 (Software Directive) | 2001/29 (InfoSoc) |
| Sector | Software / runtime modification | Applied art / furniture |
| Issue | What is an "expression" of a program? | What are "free and creative choices"? |
| Core test | Reproducibility test | Unique appearance requirement |

Both judgments fit within the broader Levola-Cofemel-Brompton line, in which the Court continues to refine the meaning of "original" and "form of expression".

## Relationship with the infringement questions (operative part 3, outside scope)

For those who, despite this skill's scope delimitation, wish to address the infringement questions: operative part 3 lays down three rules (for cross-reference only):

1. **Infringement test**: recognisable reproduction of creative elements reflecting the author's personality (paragraph 86, cross-ref Pelham and Others C-476/17 paragraph 39 + Infopaq C-5/08 paragraph 47)
2. **Not the test**: same overall visual impression (= design law test) → **irrelevant** for copyright (paragraph 87)
3. **Scope of protection**: does **not** depend on the degree of originality (paragraph 88, cross-ref Cofemel paragraph 35)
4. **Independent creation**: possible but must be proved; the mere possibility does not justify a refusal of protection (paragraph 91)
5. **Common source of inspiration**: only the reproduction of "new" creative elements in a derivative work constitutes infringement; merely following a trend or current is not infringement (paragraph 90)

This doctrine belongs in a separate skill on copyright infringement and scope of protection.

## Argumentative consequences

### For those seeking to establish copyright protection (pro-rightholder)

Mio/Konektra does not in principle aggravate the burden of proof for applied art — the threshold remains that of Cofemel/Brompton — but the judgment sharpens the **evidential requirements**. Strategies:

1. **Identify the creative choices "in the shape"** (paragraph 65): avoid vague references to "the design process" or "creative intuition"; point to concrete design elements
2. **Show how those choices give a unique appearance** (operative part 2): point to specific combinations, proportions, details which distinguish the subject matter from a conventional execution
3. **Refute prior design material** (paragraph 80): where the opposing party points to prior similar works, show that they differ or that the own choices were made independently
4. **Do not rely on non-decisive factors** (operative part 2 in fine): museum acquisitions, awards, professional recognition may be mentioned but may not form the core of the argument
5. **For variants of own work** (paragraph 79): show the continuity of creative elements between original and variant
6. **For inspired works** (paragraph 79): independently apply the two-step test to the new work

### For those seeking to contest copyright protection (pro-alleged-infringer)

Mio/Konektra is **particularly powerful** for the defence. Six main lines of attack:

#### Line of attack 1 — "Free choices without unique appearance" (operative part 2) ⭐

The central defence: even where the author is not bound by constraints, the choices must **reflect the personality by giving the subject matter a unique appearance**. Points of attack:

- **Trend-based execution**: the subject matter follows a current design trend; the choices are "free" but give the subject matter no unique appearance
- **Conventional combinations**: the combination of design elements is generic for the sector
- **Functional variants**: all choices fall within what the target audience expects for the functional type

#### Line of attack 2 — Prior design (paragraph 80) ⭐⭐

Show that similar or identical subject matter was created **before** the claimed work by other authors. Mio/Konektra recognises this as **an indication of low or absent originality**.

Means of proof:

- BOIP, EUIPO and international design registers (pre-existing designs)
- Catalogues, annual reports, trade publications from years before the work
- Bouquet exhibitions, museum collections
- Online platforms (Pinterest, design archives) with dating
- Statements from design historians

Cross-reference: the Mio case explicitly turned on the "Palais Royal" tables and their relationship to earlier registered designs.

#### Line of attack 3 — No presumption of creativity (paragraph 65)

The author must **prove** that his or her choices were creative. It does not suffice to show that he had choices — he must show that those choices were creative within the meaning of copyright. Lines of attack:

- **Element-by-element analysis**: break the subject matter down into components and show, for each, that it is either functional/conventional or non-creative
- **Demand specification from the rightholder**: force him to point precisely to which choices would be creative — on probing this often remains abstract

#### Line of attack 4 — Aesthetic effect ≠ work (paragraph 68)

The rightholder's standard argument is often that the subject matter is "beautiful", "characteristic" or "iconic". Mio/Konektra paragraph 68 makes explicit that **a specific, aesthetically significant visual effect is not, in itself, sufficient**. This is directly usable:

- Professional recognition, press attention and commercial success do not prove originality (paragraph 81 + operative part 2)
- "Design classic" status is not a copyright qualification
- Aesthetic effect may even arise from technical or functional properties without creative choice

#### Line of attack 5 — Author's intentions not decisive (paragraphs 74-75)

Where the rightholder leans heavily on the intentions, design rationale or "story" behind the design: paragraph 74 explicitly holds that **intentions are ideas** and are protected only in so far as **expressed in the work**. Paragraph 75 forbids the court from basing its assessment **decisively** on those elements. Lines of attack:

- Shift the focus back to the **objective shape** of the subject matter
- Show that the claimed intention is **not visible** in the end result
- Distance design narrative (often constructed post hoc) from the actual creative process

#### Line of attack 6 — Variant of existing work (paragraph 79)

Where the work is a variant of an earlier work by a third party, it is an **inspired work** which must **independently** satisfy the two-step test. Line of attack:

- Identify the existing work by which the author was inspired
- Show that the "new" elements do not meet the originality requirement
- Apply the two-step test to the **delta** (what is new), not to the whole
