## Facts in six lines

Infopaq International A/S is a Danish company active in media monitoring. Through a data-capture process (scanning, OCR, text conversion) it extracts from newspaper and magazine articles fragments of eleven words around search terms chosen by clients. Danske Dagblades Forening, the professional association of Danish newspaper publishers, disputed that such a process could take place without the consent of the copyright holders. The dispute reached the Højesteret, which referred thirteen questions for a preliminary ruling, the first of which raised the central question: can a fragment of eleven consecutive words from a press article constitute a "reproduction in part" within the meaning of art. 2 of the InfoSoc Directive?

## Legal questions

1. Does the storing and printing of a fragment of eleven words from a press article constitute reproduction in part within the meaning of art. 2 of Directive 2001/29? (first question — relevant for originality)
2. Does this process meet the cumulative conditions of art. 5(1) for the exception for temporary acts of reproduction? (questions 2-12 — not relevant for the originality test)

## Operative part

1. An act of storing in a computer memory and of printing a fragment consisting of eleven words from a protected work may constitute reproduction in part within the meaning of art. 2 of Directive 2001/29 **if the elements thus reproduced are the expression of the author's own intellectual creation**. It is for the national court to verify this.
2. The printing of such a fragment does not meet the condition that the reproduction be transient (art. 5(1)) — not relevant for the originality test.

## Key reasoning — originality

### Paragraph 34 — literary and artistic works presuppose intellectual creations

> "It is, moreover, apparent from the general scheme of the Berne Convention, in particular Article 2(5) and (8), that the protection of certain subject-matters as artistic or literary works presupposes that they are intellectual creations."

### Paragraph 35 — vertical harmonisation through software, database and term directives

> "Similarly, under Articles 1(3) of Directive 91/250, 3(1) of Directive 96/9 and 6 of Directive 2006/116, works such as computer programs, databases or photographs are protected by copyright only if they are original in the sense that they are their author's own intellectual creation."

### Paragraph 37 — *the central formula*

> "copyright within the meaning of Article 2(a) of Directive 2001/29 is liable to apply only in relation to a subject-matter which is original in the sense that it is its author's own intellectual creation."

### Paragraph 38 — parts of a work fall under the same regime

> "As regards the parts of a work, it should be borne in mind that there is nothing in Directive 2001/29 or any other relevant directive indicating that those parts are to be treated any differently from the work as a whole. It follows that they are protected by copyright since, as such, they share the originality of the whole work."

### Paragraph 39 — protected parts must themselves embody the expression of originality

> "In the light of the considerations referred to in paragraph 37 of this judgment, the various parts of a work thus enjoy protection under Article 2(a) of Directive 2001/29, provided that they contain elements which are the expression of the intellectual creation of the author of the work."

### Paragraph 44 — *where originality lies in press articles*

> "As regards newspaper articles, their author's own intellectual creation, referred to in paragraph 37 of this judgment, is evidenced clearly from the form, the manner in which the subject is presented and the linguistic expression."

### Paragraph 45 — *the "choice, sequence and combination" formula*

> "Regarding the elements of such works covered by the protection, it should be observed that they consist of words which, considered in isolation, are not as such an intellectual creation of the author who employs them. It is only through the choice, sequence and combination of those words that the author may express his creativity in an original manner and achieve a result which is an intellectual creation."

### Paragraph 46 — individual words are not protected

> "Words as such do not, therefore, constitute elements covered by the protection."

### Paragraph 47 — broad interpretation: short fragments may also be original

> "That being so, given the requirement of a broad interpretation of the scope of the protection conferred by Article 2 of Directive 2001/29, the possibility may not be ruled out that certain isolated sentences, or even certain parts of sentences in the text in question, may be suitable for conveying to the reader the originality of a publication such as a newspaper article, by communicating to that reader an element which is, in itself, the expression of the intellectual creation of the author of that article. Such sentences or parts of sentences are, therefore, liable to come within the scope of the protection provided for in Article 2(a) of that directive."

### Paragraph 48 — *application to the eleven-word fragment*

> "the reproduction of an extract of a protected work which, like those at issue in the main proceedings, comprises 11 consecutive words thereof, is such as to constitute reproduction in part within the meaning of Article 2 of Directive 2001/29, if that extract contains an element of the work which, as such, expresses the author's own intellectual creation; it is for the national court to make this determination."

## Doctrinal significance

### The EU originality formula

Infopaq is the key judgment in which the Court for the first time explicitly formulates the **autonomous EU originality test** that previously existed only sectorally (software, databases, photographs). The core (paragraph 37): copyright within the meaning of art. 2(a) InfoSoc applies only to subject matter that is original "in the sense that it is its author's own intellectual creation" ("création intellectuelle propre à son auteur"). This formula henceforth becomes the **general EU protection threshold** for all categories of work under the InfoSoc Directive — a conclusion the Court will confirm and refine in later case-law (BSA, Painer, Football Association Premier League, SAS Institute, Cofemel, Brompton).

### Two components of the test

1. **"Own"** ("propre") — emanating from the author, not derived
2. **"Intellectual creation"** — fruit of mental effort, not merely mechanical or dictated by external rules

The Court grounds this on the overall scheme of the Berne Convention (paragraph 34: protection as literary and artistic work "presupposes that they are intellectual creations") and on existing vertical harmonisation through the Software, Database and Term directives (paragraph 35).

### How originality manifests itself: choice, sequence, combination

In paragraph 45 the operational test is formulated for the first time: originality manifests itself in **the choice, sequence and combination** of the elements in an original manner. For press articles (paragraph 44) that originality lies "in the form, the manner in which the subject is presented and the linguistic expression" — not in the subject matter itself.

### Threshold: short fragments may also be original

The judgment refuses to accept a quantitative lower limit. A fragment of eleven words *can* be original if it contains elements expressing the author's own intellectual creation (paragraphs 47-48). Individual words on their own are not protected (paragraph 46) — that is a **qualitative**, not quantitative limit.

### Work-element distinction

The Court draws an important distinction between the **work** as a whole (which is original) and its **elements** (words, colours, notes, form elements). Elements on their own are free; only their specific choice, sequence and combination can carry originality. This distinction is fundamental for later idea/expression case-law.

## Application in the two-step test

In the Cofemel doctrine (later case-law) the work concept is built up as a **two-step test**: (i) objective identifiability + (ii) originality. Infopaq in essence supplies the second step (originality criterion) and thereby the basic formula to which all later judgments will refer.

## Argumentative consequences

### For those wishing to establish originality (pro-rightholder)

- Show which specific **choices** the author made: in form, presentation of the subject, linguistic expression (for literary works) — mutatis mutandis for other categories of work
- Show that those choices were not dictated by external rules (genre, convention, technical function)
- Show that the combination/arrangement as a whole is a unique expression, not the mere sum of trivial parts
- Argue that a short fragment may also be protected if it reflects the originality of the whole

### For those wishing to contest originality (pro-defendant)

- Show that the elements as such (words, colours, basic shapes, technical elements) are not original and not protected — Infopaq paragraph 46 recognises that loose elements remain free
- Argue that the "choice, sequence and combination" is not original but banal/conventional/dictated by external rules
- For press articles specifically: not the subject matter or the facts are protected, only the specific form, presentation and linguistic expression (Infopaq paragraph 44)
- For short fragments: the fragment does not itself contain the expression of the author's own intellectual creation, but only loose building blocks (Infopaq paragraph 48)
