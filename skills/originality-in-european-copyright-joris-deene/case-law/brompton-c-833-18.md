## Subject matter and category

- **Main category**: **useful articles / physical products with technical function** — refinement of the BSA doctrine (technical-function exclusion) for industrial design
- **Special contribution**:
  - **Confirmation of the Cofemel two-step test** for useful articles (paragraph 22)
  - **Key refinement** (paragraph 26): a subject matter satisfying originality **may** be eligible for copyright protection, **even if its realisation has been dictated by technical considerations**, provided that those considerations did not prevent the author from expressing his personality through free and creative choices ⭐
  - **Idea/expression merger** expressly adopted (paragraph 27, BSA paragraphs 48-49 + SAS Institute paragraphs 33 and 40)
  - **Alternative forms not decisive** (paragraph 35): indicates possibility of choice, but not decisive for assessing the factors that influenced the creator's choice
  - **Intention of the alleged infringer is irrelevant** (paragraph 35)
  - **Expired patent and effectiveness of the form** are indicia weighed insofar as they reveal what was considered in choosing the form (paragraph 36)
  - **Time of assessment**: factors as they existed at design time, not posterior (paragraph 37)
  - **Difference from DOCERAM** (design law): for copyright the existence of alternatives is relevant; for design law (art. 8 Regulation 6/2002) not decisive
- **Applicable legislation**: Directive 2001/29/EC (art. 2-5); reference to art. 8 Regulation 6/2002 for distinction with design law
- **Relevance for the skill**: ★★★★★ — *the judgment that operationalises the BSA doctrine for physical useful articles; refines the mechanism of the technical-function exclusion; introduces the nuance that technical dictates do not automatically exclude copyright*

## Facts in four lines

Brompton Bicycle Ltd. has, since 1987, marketed the "Brompton bicycle", a folding bicycle with three positions (folded, open and intermediate). The folding bicycle was protected by a patent on the method; that patent has since expired. In 2017 Chedech/Get2Get marketed a visually highly similar folding bicycle with the same three positions. SI and Brompton sued Get2Get before the Tribunal de l'entreprise de Liège for copyright infringement. Get2Get defended itself by arguing that the appearance was necessary to achieve the technical result and could therefore be protected only by patent (expired) and not by copyright — while Brompton contended that the three positions could also have been achieved with other appearances. The Tribunal referred preliminary questions.

## Legal questions

1. Must Directive 2001/29 be interpreted as meaning that **copyright protection is excluded for works whose shape is necessary to achieve a technical result**?
2. Which criteria are relevant in that assessment?
   - Existence of **other possible appearances** for the same result
   - **Effectiveness** of the appearance
   - **Intention of the alleged infringer** to achieve that result
   - Existence of an **earlier, now expired patent** on the process

## Operative part

> Articles 2 to 5 of Directive 2001/29/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council of 22 May 2001 on the harmonisation of certain aspects of copyright and related rights in the information society must be interpreted as meaning that **the copyright protection provided for therein applies to a product whose shape is, at least in part, necessary to obtain a technical result**, where that product is **an original work resulting from intellectual creation**, in that, through that shape, **its author expresses his creative ability in an original manner by making free and creative choices in such a way that that shape reflects his personality**, which it is for the national court to verify, bearing in mind all the relevant aspects of the dispute in the main proceedings.

## Key reasoning

### Paragraph 22 — *the two-step test in two elements* (cross-ref Cofemel)

> "According to the Court's settled case-law, the concept of 'work' has two conditions. First, it entails an original subject matter which is the author's own intellectual creation and, second, it requires the expression of that creation (see, to that effect, judgment of 12 September 2019, Cofemel, C‑683/17, EU:C:2019:721, paragraphs 29 and 32 and the case-law cited)."

### Paragraph 23 — *originality = personality + free creative choices* (cross-ref Cofemel/Painer)

> "As regards the first of those conditions, it follows from the Court's settled case-law that, if a subject matter is to be capable of being regarded as original, it is both necessary and sufficient that **the subject matter reflects the personality of its author, as an expression of his free and creative choices** (judgment of 12 September 2019, Cofemel, C‑683/17, EU:C:2019:721, paragraph 30 and the case-law cited)."

### Paragraph 24 — *technical considerations leaving no room for creative freedom*

> "In that regard, it must be borne in mind that, according to settled case-law, where the realisation of a subject matter has been dictated by **technical considerations, rules or other constraints which have left no room for creative freedom**, that subject matter cannot be regarded as possessing the originality required for it to constitute a work and, consequently, to be eligible for the protection conferred by copyright (see, to that effect, judgment of 12 September 2019, Cofemel, C‑683/17, EU:C:2019:721, paragraph 31 and the case-law cited)."

### Paragraph 25 — *objective identifiability* (cross-ref Cofemel/Levola)

> "As regards the second condition referred to in paragraph 22 of the present judgment, the Court has stated that the concept of 'work' that is the subject of Directive 2001/29 necessarily entails the existence of **a subject matter that is identifiable with sufficient precision and objectivity** (judgment of 12 September 2019, Cofemel, C‑683/17, EU:C:2019:721, paragraph 32 and the case-law cited)."

### Paragraph 26 — *the core refinement: technical dictates ≠ automatic exclusion* ⭐⭐⭐

> "It follows that a subject matter satisfying the condition of originality **may be eligible for copyright protection, even if its realisation has been dictated by technical considerations, provided that its being so dictated has not prevented the author from reflecting his personality in that subject matter, as an expression of free and creative choices**."

### Paragraph 27 — *idea/expression merger and anti-monopolisation* (cross-ref BSA and SAS Institute)

> "In that regard, it should be noted that the criterion of originality cannot be met by the components of a subject matter which are differentiated only by their technical function. It follows in particular from Article 2 of the WIPO Copyright Treaty that **copyright protection does not extend to ideas. Protecting ideas by copyright would amount to making it possible to monopolise ideas, to the detriment, in particular, of technical progress and industrial development** (see, to that effect, judgment of 2 May 2012, SAS Institute, C‑406/10, EU:C:2012:259, paragraphs 33 and 40). Where the expression of those components is dictated by their technical function, **the different methods of implementing an idea are so limited that the idea and the expression become indissociable** (see, to that effect, judgment of 22 December 2010, Bezpečnostní softwarová asociace, C‑393/09, EU:C:2010:816, paragraphs 48 and 49)."

### Paragraph 28 — *work-concept-step-1 not contested*

> "It is therefore necessary to examine whether the folding bicycle at issue in the main proceedings is capable of constituting a work eligible for the protection provided for in Directive 2001/29, it being noted that the referring court's questions do not refer to the second condition mentioned in paragraph 22 of the present judgment, **because the bicycle appears to be identifiable with sufficient precision and objectivity**, but the first condition."

The Court thus implicitly confirms that physical useful articles almost always satisfy the Levola test of objective identifiability.

### Paragraph 31 — *limit: scope so limited that idea coincides with expression*

> "In that regard, as recalled in paragraphs 24, 26 and 27 of the present judgment, **that cannot be the case where the realisation of a subject matter has been dictated by technical considerations, rules or other constraints which have left no room for creative freedom or room so limited that the idea and its expression become indissociable**."

### Paragraph 32 — *multiple possible appearances ≠ automatic work qualification*

> "**Even though there remains a possibility of choice as to the shape of a subject matter, it cannot be concluded that the subject matter is necessarily covered by the concept of 'work' within the meaning of Directive 2001/29**. In order to determine whether the subject matter is actually covered, it is for the referring court to verify that the conditions referred to in paragraphs 22 to 27 of the present judgment are met."

Importantly: the existence of alternative shapes is **not sufficient** for copyright protection. The full originality test must be run.

### Paragraph 33 — *solely technically dictated = no copyright*

> "Where **the shape of the product is solely dictated by its technical function**, that product cannot be covered by copyright protection."

### Paragraph 34 — *operational test: did the author express creative ability originally?*

> "Therefore, in order to establish whether the product concerned falls within the scope of copyright protection, it is for the referring court to determine whether, **through that choice of the shape of the product, its author has expressed his creative ability in an original manner by making free and creative choices and has designed the product in such a way that it reflects his personality**."

### Paragraph 35 — *alternative shapes and intention of the infringer* ⭐

> "In that context, and in so far as only the originality of the product concerned needs to be assessed, even though the existence of other possible shapes which can achieve the same technical result makes it possible to establish that there is a possibility of choice, **it is not decisive in assessing the factors which influenced the choice made by the creator**. Likewise, the intention of the alleged infringer is irrelevant in such an assessment."

This is *the* core test from Brompton:
- Alternatives = **indication** of creative freedom
- But **not decisive** — the operative test remains whether the author made creative choices reflecting his personality
- The **intention** of the imitator is irrelevant

### Paragraph 36 — *expired patent and effectiveness*

> "**As regards the existence of an earlier, now expired, patent in the case in the main proceedings and the effectiveness of the shape in achieving the same technical result**, they should be taken into account only **in so far as those factors make it possible to reveal what was taken into consideration in choosing the shape of the product concerned**."

The expired patent and the effectiveness of the form are **indicia** that may reveal which factors influenced the maker's choice — but are not decisive in themselves.

### Paragraph 37 — *time of assessment: at design time*

> "In any event, it must be noted that, in order to assess whether the folding bicycle at issue in the main proceedings is an original creation and is thus protected by copyright, it is for the referring court to take account of all the relevant aspects of the present case, **as they existed when that subject matter was designed, irrespective of the factors external to and subsequent to the creation of the product**."

## Doctrinal significance

### 1. The difference with DOCERAM (design law)

Brompton expressly frames the copyright test **against** the design-law test from DOCERAM (C-395/16, EU:C:2018:172):

| Aspect | Design law (art. 8 Regulation 6/2002, DOCERAM) | Copyright (Brompton) |
|---|---|---|
| Test | "**Solely** dictated by technical function" | Author has expressed creative ability **originally** |
| Alternative shapes | **Not decisive** (DOCERAM paragraph 30) | Indication of creative freedom, but **not decisive** (Brompton paragraph 35) |
| Originality requirement | No (novelty + individual character) | Yes (two-step test) |
| Objective | Protect industrial investment | Protection of creative creation |

Both regimes apply a comparable nuance on alternatives (not decisive), but the **core test** is different: design law looks at whether function was the only factor; copyright looks at whether the author made creative choices to reflect his personality.

### 2. The three scenarios for useful articles

Brompton distinguishes three scenarios for the copyright status of useful articles:

| Scenario | Originality | Copyright? |
|---|---|---|
| Shape **solely** dictated by technical function (paragraph 33) | No free creative choices possible | **No** copyright |
| Shape **partly** technically dictated, scope so limited that idea coincides with expression (paragraph 31) | Idea/expression merger | **No** copyright |
| Shape **partly** technically dictated, **but** scope for free creative choices reflecting the author's personality (paragraph 26) | Possibly original | **Possible** copyright |

The middle category (scenario 2) is the **merger doctrine** from BSA, expressly confirmed in Brompton paragraph 27.

### 3. The role of the expired patent

Brompton gives an **operational interpretation** of a delicate question: how to deal with a product previously protected by patent?

> Paragraph 36: the expired patent and effectiveness must be weighed "**in so far as those factors make it possible to reveal what was taken into consideration in choosing the shape**".

This means in practice:

- **Against the rightholder**: the expired patent suggests that the form was primarily technically dictated, and the move to copyright extends the old monopoly
- **For the rightholder**: the expired patent does **not automatically exclude** copyright — there may still be scope for free creative choices within the patentable invention
- **Operationally**: the court examines the **historical considerations** of the designer. Was the form chosen to give effect to the patent? Or were there also aesthetic / creative considerations?

This is an important **anti-monopolisation** consideration: copyright must not be used to extend expired patents up to 70 years after the author's death.

### 4. Time of assessment

Brompton paragraph 37 introduces an important **temporal dimension**: originality is assessed at the **design moment**, **regardless** of what has occurred afterwards.

Practical consequences:

- **Posterior trends**: later fashion trends that overtake the design do not affect originality at the design moment
- **Later alternatives**: the fact that alternative solutions were developed **after** the design does not affect the test
- **Posterior market developments**: irrelevant

This supports both the rightholder (by offering protection despite later developments) and the defence (by excluding anachronistic analyses).

### 5. The four criteria of question 2 — answered

The referring court had submitted four specific criteria; Brompton answers them all:

| Criterion | Brompton answer | Location |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Existence of alternative shapes | **Relevant** for creative freedom, **not decisive** | Paragraph 35 |
| 2. Effectiveness of the form | **To be weighed** insofar as it reveals considerations | Paragraph 36 |
| 3. Intention of the alleged infringer | **Irrelevant** | Paragraph 35 |
| 4. Expired patent | **To be weighed** insofar as it reveals considerations | Paragraph 36 |

### 6. Relationship with Cofemel — sector-specific application

Brompton builds directly on Cofemel:

| Cofemel | Brompton |
|---|---|
| Applied art / clothing models | Physical useful articles / folding bicycle |
| Prohibition on additional aesthetic criterion | Refinement of technical-function exclusion |
| Cumulation copyright + design right | Cumulation copyright + patent (implicit) |
| No aesthetic threshold | No automatic exclusion on technical dictates |
| Paragraph 29: definitive two-step test | Paragraph 22: confirmation of two-step test |
| Paragraph 30: Painer formula | Paragraph 23: Painer formula |
| Paragraph 31: technical-considerations exclusion | Paragraphs 24, 26: refinement of that exclusion |
| Paragraph 32: Levola formula | Paragraph 25: Levola formula |

Brompton is thus an **operationalisation** of Cofemel for the specific question how to handle technical-function considerations in physical products.

### 7. The cumulation question copyright ↔ patent

Brompton implicitly touches on a sensitive question: can an expired patent be "extended" via copyright?

The Court does **not say expressly** that copyright is excluded after an expired patent — but the considerations in paragraph 36 plus the general anti-monopolisation consideration in paragraph 27 point in a **restrictive** direction:

- Copyright does not protect the **technical solution** (idea/functionality exclusion, SAS Institute)
- Copyright can protect the **specific design choice**, provided it does not amount to idea/expression merger
- The expired patent is a **historical indication** that the form was primarily technically dictated

In practice: the rightholder must be able to show that the **creative choices** were independent of or in addition to the technical dictates. An expired patent makes the burden of proof heavier.

## Argumentative consequences

### For those wishing to establish copyright protection of a useful article (pro-rightholder)

#### The Brompton strategy

1. **Document all free creative choices** of the designer — particularly those outside the strict technical dictates
2. **Show the design rationale**: design drawings, variants, rejected alternatives
3. **Point to alternative shapes** as indication of creative freedom (but additionally substantiate the concrete choices)
4. **Anticipate the expired-patent argument**: show that the design was determined not only technically but also aesthetically / personally
5. **Focus on the design moment**: document the considerations of the designer at the time of creation

#### Concrete argumentation

> "The Brompton bicycle — or the useful article in question — is admittedly functional, but the specific design (curved main frame, slender triangular rear frame, placement of components, position and curvature of the rear wheel) does not flow exclusively from technical dictates. At the design moment the author had a choice among several embodiments that would equally have achieved the technical result (three positions, balance on the ground). The chosen design reflects the author's personality through free and creative choices."

#### Strong example cases

- **Design furniture** whose form is functional but at the same time shows creative choices
- **Iconic lighting fixtures** with distinctive design
- **Means of transport** (bicycles, cars) with characteristic design signature
- **Tools and hand tools** with distinctive design beyond pure function
- **Industrial products** with design value

### For those wishing to contest copyright protection of a useful article (pro-alleged-infringer)

#### The Brompton attack lines

##### Attack line 1 — Solely technically dictated (paragraph 33)

Where the form is **solely** dictated by the technical function, copyright is excluded. Point of attack: show that all essential elements are explainable as functional.

##### Attack line 2 — Idea/expression merger (paragraphs 27, 31)

Where the scope for execution is so limited that idea and expression coincide, copyright is excluded — even where **theoretically** alternative shapes exist.

Operationally:

- Show that the "alternative" shapes were not practically realisable
- Show that the author's choice was the natural / obvious execution of the technical solution
- Invoke the anti-monopolisation consideration (paragraph 27): copyright must not be used to monopolise ideas

##### Attack line 3 — Expired patent as anti-monopolisation argument

Where the opposing party previously enjoyed patent protection, the copyright argument can be used to extend that monopoly after patent expiry. Brompton paragraph 36 gives the defence a handle:

- Ask whether the form was primarily chosen to give effect to the patent
- Ask whether the creative considerations were independent of the technical dictates
- Invoke the **anti-monopolisation** consideration from Brompton paragraph 27 + SAS Institute paragraphs 33 + 40

##### Attack line 4 — Effectiveness of the form

A shape that is **particularly effective** in achieving the technical result suggests that the form was dictated by technical considerations, not by creative choices (Brompton paragraph 36).

##### Attack line 5 — No alternative shapes (paragraph 35 reversed)

Where there were **no** alternative shapes achieving the same technical result, the indication of creative freedom is absent. This is not decisive in favour of the defence (by analogy with paragraph 35), but it strengthens the argument that there were no free creative choices.

#### Examples of useful articles where the copyright claim is weak

- **Functional furniture** with standard forms
- **Packaging** with standard dimensions and functional requirements
- **Tools** with technically dictated forms
- **Industrial machine parts** with functional forms
- **Sports articles** whose form is dictated by biomechanics or regulations
- **Products** whose form strongly overlaps with an expired patent

#### Evidentiary leverage for the defence

- **Technical analysis**: expert opinion on why the form was functionally dictated
- **Patent texts**: historical documents describing the technical dictates
- **Design history**: documentation that all "creative choices" already existed in the state of the art
- **Market analysis**: show that alternative forms were not practical
- **Cost analysis**: show that the chosen form was the most efficient
