## Subject matter and category

- **Main category**: **originality test for databases** (Directive 96/9/EC, art. 3)
- **Special contribution**:
  - Confirmation and **unified application** of the Infopaq / Painer / BSA / FAPL doctrine to databases
  - **Express exclusion of "sweat of the brow"** — significant labour and skill as such do not justify copyright protection
  - **Full harmonisation** of the originality criterion for databases — no national deviations allowed (subject to the transitional rule in art. 14(2))
- **Applicable legislation**: Directive 96/9/EC, in particular art. 1(2) (definition of database), art. 3(1) (copyright protection), art. 7 (sui generis right), art. 14(2) (transitional provision)
- **Relevance for the skill**: ★★★★★ — *the first judgment to expressly combine the four earlier originality formulas into a coherent test, and to definitively reject "sweat of the brow" as an EU originality standard*

## Facts in four lines

Football Dataco e.a. (organisers of the English and Scottish football leagues) relied on copyright and sui generis right in the annual match fixture lists. The lists are drawn up subject to "golden rules" (no three consecutive home or away matches, balance home/away, midweek matches spread out), supplemented with specific club requests and organisational constraints. Yahoo! e.a. used the fixture lists without payment for betting activities and commercial services. The UK court had accepted copyright protection under the British CDPA criterion (significant labour and skill — "sweat of the brow"), but referred the question of whether this is permissible under EU law.

## Legal questions

1. **What is the proper interpretation of "author's own intellectual creation" in art. 3(1) Directive 96/9?** Specifically:
   - (a) must intellectual effort and skill in **creating data** be excluded?
   - (b) does "selection or arrangement" extend to **adding substantial content** to a pre-existing data item (such as fixing a match date)?
   - (c) does "author's own intellectual creation" require **more than significant labour and skill** — and if so, what?
2. Does the directive preclude **national copyright protection of databases** under conditions other than art. 3(1)?

## Operative part

> **Operative part 1**: Article 3(1) of Directive 96/9 must be interpreted as meaning that a "database" within the meaning of art. 1(2) is protected by copyright **provided that the selection or arrangement of the data which it contains amounts to an original expression of the creative freedom of its author**; it is for the national court to verify this. Accordingly:
>
> - the intellectual effort and skill expended in **creating the data** are not relevant;
> - it is irrelevant whether the selection or arrangement involves an **addition of important significance** to that data;
> - the **significant labour and skill** required to set up the database cannot as such justify copyright protection if they do not express any originality in the selection or arrangement of that data.
>
> **Operative part 2**: Subject to the transitional provision of art. 14(2), Directive 96/9 precludes national legislation granting databases within the meaning of art. 1(2) copyright protection under conditions other than those in art. 3(1).

## Key reasoning — originality

### Paragraph 32 — *selection and arrangement = selection and ordering, not data creation*

> "In that context, the concepts of 'selection' and of 'arrangement' within the meaning of Article 3(1) of Directive 96/9 refer respectively to the selection and the arrangement of data, through which the author of the database gives the database its structure. **By contrast, those concepts do not extend to the creation of the data contained in that database.**"

### Paragraph 37 — *unified reference to Infopaq, BSA, FAPL and Painer* ⭐ *the coherent test*

> "Secondly, as is apparent from recital 16 of Directive 96/9, the notion of the author's own intellectual creation refers to the criterion of originality (see, to that effect, Case C-5/08 *Infopaq International* [2009] ECR I-6569, paragraphs 35, 37 and 38; Case C-393/09 *Bezpečnostní softwarová asociace* [2010] ECR I-13971, paragraph 45; Joined Cases C-403/08 and C-429/08 *Football Association Premier League and Others* [2011] ECR I-9083, paragraph 97; and Case C-145/10 *Painer* [2011] ECR I-12533, paragraph 87)."

### Paragraph 38 — *Painer formula applied to databases* (positive test)

> "As regards the setting up of a database, that criterion of originality is satisfied **when, through the selection or arrangement of the data which it contains, its author expresses his creative ability in an original manner by making free and creative choices** (see, by analogy, *Infopaq International*, paragraph 45; *Bezpečnostní softwarová asociace*, paragraph 50; and *Painer*, paragraph 89) and thus **stamps his 'personal touch'** (*Painer*, paragraph 92)."

### Paragraph 39 — *BSA + FAPL combined* (negative test) ⭐ *the consolidated exclusion*

> "By contrast, that criterion is not satisfied **when the setting up of the database is dictated by technical considerations, rules or constraints which leave no room for creative freedom** (see, by analogy, *Bezpečnostní softwarová asociace*, paragraphs 48 and 49, and *Football Association Premier League and Others*, paragraph 98)."

### Paragraph 40 — *the only criterion = originality*

> "As is apparent from both Article 3(1) and recital 16 of Directive 96/9, **no other criteria than that of originality is to be applied** to determine the eligibility of a database for the copyright protection provided for by that directive."

### Paragraph 42 — *sweat of the brow expressly excluded* ⭐ *end of the UK doctrine*

> "[...] the fact that the setting up of the database required, irrespective of the creation of the data which it contains, **significant labour and skill of its author** [...] **cannot as such justify the protection of it by copyright** under Directive 96/9, **if that labour and that skill do not express any originality in the selection or arrangement of that data**."

### Paragraph 45 — *positive formulation of the test*

> "[...] Article 3(1) of Directive 96/9 must be interpreted as meaning that a 'database' [...] is protected by the copyright [...] **provided that the selection or arrangement of the data which it contains amounts to an original expression of the creative freedom of its author**, which is a matter for the national court to determine."

### Paragraph 50 — *full harmonisation of the criterion*

> "[...] Article 3(1) of the directive **precludes national legislation which grants databases [...] copyright protection under conditions which are different to that of originality** laid down in Article 3(1) of the directive."

## Doctrinal significance

### 1. The first judgment to expressly unify the Painer, BSA, FAPL and Infopaq formulas

Before Football Dataco the four key formulas were cited separately. In Football Dataco the Court for the first time expressly brings them together in **one coherent test**:

| Element | Source in Football Dataco | Original doctrine |
|---|---|---|
| General formula "author's own intellectual creation" | paragraph 37 | Infopaq paragraphs 35, 37, 38 |
| Positive: free and creative choices | paragraph 38 | Painer paragraph 89; Infopaq paragraph 45; BSA paragraph 50 |
| Positive: personal touch | paragraph 38 | Painer paragraph 92 |
| Negative: technical considerations | paragraph 39 | BSA paragraphs 48-49 |
| Negative: rules removing creative freedom | paragraph 39 | FAPL paragraph 98 |

These paragraphs 37-39 have become *the* **operational standard formula** for later case-law. Cofemel (2019), Brompton (2020) and later judgments refer to this formula.

### 2. Definitive exclusion of "sweat of the brow"

The Court expressly rejects the British CDPA doctrine that significant labour and skill *per se* justify copyright protection (paragraphs 42, 46 third indent, operative part 1 third indent). This is a **hard limit**:

- Effort ≠ originality
- Investment, expertise, editorial care: relevant under the sui generis right (art. 7), not under copyright (art. 3)
- Skilled execution without free creative choices does not meet the threshold

Although the judgment is formally about databases, this exclusion applies **generally** to the EU originality criterion. It is the definitive EU rejection of the Anglo-American "sweat of the brow" tradition.

### 3. Two-track system for databases: sharp distinction

Football Dataco refines the sharp distinction between the two tracks of protection under the Database Directive:

| Protection track | Conditions | Subject matter | Location |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Copyright** (art. 3) | Original selection or arrangement — author's own intellectual creation | **Structure** of the database (not the content) | Chapter II Directive 96/9 |
| **Sui generis right** (art. 7) | Substantial qualitative or quantitative investment in obtaining, verification or presentation | **Content** of the database | Chapter III Directive 96/9 |

The two rights are **independent** (art. 7(4)): a database can be simultaneously protected by copyright and sui generis, or by either one, or by neither. For the fixture lists in Football Dataco the sui generis right had already been rejected by the UK court (in line with the earlier Fixtures Marketing judgments of 2004), so that only copyright was at issue.

### 4. Distinction between data creation vs. data structuring

For the originality test under art. 3 only **what the maker does with pre-existing data** (selection and ordering) is relevant. Not relevant:

- The effort to **create** the data (e.g., fixing match dates, collecting addresses, performing measurements) — paragraphs 32-36
- The **addition of substantive content** to pre-existing data items (e.g., fixing a match date) — paragraphs 41, 46 second indent
- The **quantity** of effort or investment — paragraph 42

Crucially: a database may indeed have required significant creative effort to **generate the content** (think of research work, data collection, statistical analysis), but where the **structure** itself (the way of selecting and arranging) shows no original choices, there is no copyright. Creative effort in data creation must where relevant be claimed under **another right** (e.g., copyright on the individual works included, or the sui generis right on the database itself).

### 5. Concrete application to fixture lists

The Court does not rule itself but refers to the national court (paragraphs 43-44). The direction, however, is clear:

> "In that respect, the procedures for creating those lists, as described by the referring court, **if they are not supplemented by elements reflecting originality in the selection or arrangement** of the data contained in those lists, **do not suffice for the database in question to be protected by the copyright** provided for in Article 3(1) of Directive 96/9." (paragraph 44)

The fixture-list creation as described (golden rules + organisational constraints + club requests + computer support) involves much labour and expertise, but the selection/arrangement itself is largely dictated by external rules. This falls under the FAPL exclusion (paragraph 39 + FAPL paragraph 98). The referring court will examine in the substantive case whether **on top of** that rule-bound basis any original creative choices were made.

### 6. Significance for the two-step test

Football Dataco implicitly confirms the two-step test:

- **Step 1 (work concept)** — a database within the meaning of art. 1(2) is a "work" within the meaning of copyright (paragraph 26, referencing Fixtures Marketing 2004)
- **Step 2 (originality)** — assessment against the originality criterion of art. 3(1) (paragraphs 37-42)

The work concept for databases (step 1) is defined in the directive itself in art. 1(2): "a collection of independent works, data or other materials arranged in a systematic or methodical way and individually accessible by electronic or other means." This directive-specific definition replaces, for databases, the general Levola test.

## Argumentative consequences

### For those wishing to establish copyright protection of a database (pro-rightholder)

1. **Focus on the structure, not the content**: argue about selection and arrangement of existing data, not the effort to create data
2. **Show free creative choices**:
   - Which selection criteria did the maker choose? Were there alternatives?
   - Which arrangement schemes? Were they substantive or merely mechanical?
   - Which categorisations, classifications, hierarchies?
   - Which navigation structure (for digital databases)?
3. **Show the "personal touch"**: comparison with other databases in the same domain shows that the maker made own choices
4. **Avoid the labour trap**: argumentation on "much work", "expertise", "quality control" yields nothing for copyright — channel this to the sui generis right
5. **Cumulation of copyright + sui generis**: a strong database may cumulate both rights; argue both separately

### For those wishing to contest copyright protection of a database (pro-alleged-infringer)

1. **Decomposition**: split the claim into (a) structure (copyright art. 3) and (b) content/investment (sui generis art. 7) — treat both separately
2. **Negative test art. 3** (three attack lines):
   - **Sweat-of-the-brow attack**: opposing party relies on effort, not on originality → Football Dataco paragraph 42, operative part 1 third indent
   - **Technically/rule-dictated structure**: the selection/arrangement automatically follows from technical norms, rules or constraints → Football Dataco paragraph 39 (BSA + FAPL combined)
   - **Creation vs. structuring**: opposing party argues about effort in generating data, not in structuring it → Football Dataco paragraphs 32-36, operative part 1 first indent
3. **Examples of structures that do not meet the threshold**:
   - Alphabetical, chronological, numerical ordering
   - Standard categories from technical norms (e.g., NACE codes, ICD classifications)
   - Schemas imposed by legislation or international conventions
   - Collections dictated by external rules (such as fixture lists)
4. **UK CDPA as historical case-law**: older UK case-law granting protection on the basis of "labour and skill" is **no longer compatible with EU law** (operative part 2)

### General doctrine for all categories of work (not only databases)

Although Football Dataco is formally about databases, the judgment confirms **generally applicable principles**:

- **Effort ≠ originality** — applies to all categories of work
- **Functionally/rule-dictated outcomes are not works** — applies to all categories of work (consolidated through paragraph 39)
- **Full EU harmonisation** of the originality criterion — no room for national variations

## Related case-law

- **Infopaq International** (C-5/08) — general "author's own intellectual creation" formula; foundation on which Football Dataco builds
- **BSA** (C-393/09) — technical-function exclusion; expressly cited in Football Dataco paragraph 39
- **FAPL** (C-403/08 & C-429/08) — rule-dictated-event exclusion; expressly cited in Football Dataco paragraph 39
- **Painer** (C-145/10) — free and creative choices + personal touch; expressly cited in Football Dataco paragraph 38
- **Fixtures Marketing** (C-46/02, C-338/02, C-444/02) (9 November 2004) — earlier judgments on sports fixture lists under the sui generis right; cited in Football Dataco for the finding that fixture lists are databases (art. 1(2)) but do not meet the sui generis investment test (art. 7)
- **Ryanair v. PR Aviation** (C-30/14, EU:C:2015:10) — confirms that databases falling neither under art. 3 nor under art. 7 are not protected by the directive but may be regulated by national contracts
- **CV-Online Latvia** (C-762/19, EU:C:2021:434) — further elaboration of the sui generis right
