## Subject matter and category

- **Main category**: sector-specific refinement of the originality concept — **photographs**, particularly portrait photographs
- **Sub-category**: introduction of the "free and creative choices" formula and the "personal touch" formula
- **Applicable directives**: Directive 93/98/EEC (term of protection), art. 6 — codified in Directive 2006/116/EC; Directive 2001/29/EC (InfoSoc), art. 2(a)
- **Relevance for the skill**: ★★★★★ — *key judgment on photographs and operationally central judgment for the general originality test*

## Facts in six lines

Eva-Maria Painer, an independent photographer, took portrait photographs of Natascha Kampusch at a kindergarten (background, pose, facial expression, capture and development all determined by her). After the abduction of Natascha K. in 1998, the Austrian security authorities used those photographs in a wanted notice. After Natascha K. escaped in 2006, five German and Austrian publishers published the photographs and a photo-fit created by computer montage, without attribution to Painer as author. Painer sued the publishers before the Handelsgericht Wien. The Oberste Gerichtshof had held in interim proceedings that portrait photographs enjoy only limited copyright protection due to "limited creative scope". The referring court asked thirteen questions for a preliminary ruling, of which the fourth is central for the originality test: can (portrait) photographs receive "weaker" or no copyright protection due to insufficient creative scope?

## Legal questions

The judgment addresses four main themes; only the second is relevant for the originality skill.

1. **Jurisdiction** (question 1, art. 6(1) Regulation 44/2001) — *not relevant for originality*
2. **Originality of portrait photographs** (question 4, art. 6 Directive 93/98 and art. 2(a) InfoSoc) — *core question for the skill*
3. **Public-security exception** (question 3, art. 5(3)(e) InfoSoc) — *not relevant for originality*
4. **Right of quotation** (question 2, art. 5(3)(d) InfoSoc) — *not relevant for originality*

## Operative part (question 4 — originality of photographs)

> Article 6 of Directive 93/98 must be interpreted as meaning that a portrait photograph can, under that provision, be protected by copyright if — which it is for the national court to determine in each case — such photograph is an intellectual creation of the author **reflecting his personality and expressing his free and creative choices in the production of that photograph**. Once it has been determined that the portrait photograph in question constitutes a work, it enjoys protection which is not inferior to that enjoyed by other works, including other photographic works.

## Key reasoning — originality

### Paragraph 87 — *extension of Infopaq to photographs*

> "As regards, first, the question whether realistic photographs, particularly portrait photographs, enjoy copyright protection under Article 6 of Directive 93/98, it is important to point out that the Court has already decided, in Case C-5/08 *Infopaq International* [2009] ECR I-6569, paragraph 35, that copyright is liable to apply only in relation to a subject-matter, such as a photograph, which is original in the sense that it is its author's own intellectual creation."

### Paragraph 88 — *reflecting personality*

> "As stated in recital 17 in the preamble to Directive 93/98, an intellectual creation is an author's own if it reflects the author's personality."

### Paragraph 89 — *introduction of the "free and creative choices" formula*

> "That is the case if the author was able to express his creative abilities in the production of the work by making free and creative choices (see, *a contrario*, Joined Cases C-403/08 and C-429/08 *Football Association Premier League and Others* [2011] ECR I-0000, paragraph 98)."

### Paragraph 90 — *three stages of free creative choices in portrait photography*

> "As regards a portrait photograph, the photographer can make free and creative choices in several ways and at various points in its production."

### Paragraph 91 — *concretisation of the three stages*

> "In the preparation phase, the photographer can choose the background, the subject's pose and the lighting. When taking a portrait photograph, he can choose the framing, the angle of view and the atmosphere created. Finally, when selecting the snapshot, the photographer may choose from a variety of developing techniques the one he wishes to adopt or, where appropriate, use computer software."

### Paragraph 92 — *"personal touch"*

> "By making those various choices, the author of a portrait photograph can stamp the work created with his 'personal touch'."

### Paragraph 93 — *creative freedom in portrait photographs not necessarily limited*

> "Consequently, as regards a portrait photograph, the freedom available to the author to exercise his creative abilities will not necessarily be minor or even non-existent."

### Paragraph 94 — *synthesis: operational test for portrait photographs*

> "In view of the foregoing, a portrait photograph can, under Article 6 of Directive 93/98, be protected by copyright if, which it is for the national court to determine in each case, such photograph is an intellectual creation of the author reflecting his personality and expressing his free and creative choices in the production of that photograph."

### Paragraph 97 — *no gradation of protection according to creative freedom*

> "Moreover, nothing in Directive 2001/29 or in any other directive applicable in this field supports the view that the extent of such protection should depend on possible differences in the degree of creative freedom in the production of various categories of works."

### Paragraph 98 — *portrait photo protection not inferior*

> "Therefore, as regards a portrait photograph, the protection conferred by Article 2(a) of Directive 2001/29 cannot be inferior to that enjoyed by other works, including other photographic works."

## Doctrinal significance

### The three new formulas

Painer introduces three operational formulas that refine the EU originality test and recur in all subsequent case-law:

1. **"Free and creative choices"** — paragraph 89. Operational substantiation of the Infopaq formula: a work reflects the author's personality if he was able to make free and creative choices in producing it. *A contrario* reference to Football Association Premier League (C-403/08 and C-429/08, paragraph 98), where the opposite situation arose: football matches, dictated by the rules of the game, leave no scope for creative freedom and do not qualify as works.
2. **"Reflecting the author's personality"** — paragraph 88. Bridge between the objective test (author's own intellectual creation) and the subjective criterion (personal expression). Derives from recital 17 of Directive 93/98.
3. **"Personal touch"** — paragraph 92. The stamp the author places on the work through his choices. Operational synonym for "personal stamp" in national legal traditions.

### An operational test for photographs: three stages of choice

In the most cited paragraph of the judgment (paragraph 91), the Court lists three stages in which the photographer of a portrait can make free creative choices:

- **Preparation stage**: staging, pose, lighting
- **Shooting stage**: camera setting, angle of view, atmosphere
- **Development stage**: techniques, software

This enumeration is not exhaustive but illustrative: it serves as a checklist for identifying creative choices in any photographic expression.

### No "weak" protection for photographs

The Court expressly rejects the "diminished" protection doctrine for portrait photographs that the Oberste Gerichtshof had applied:

- Paragraph 93: creative freedom in portrait photographs is "not necessarily minor or even non-existent"
- Paragraph 97: nothing in EU law supports a gradation of protection according to creative freedom
- Paragraph 98: portrait photographs do not receive less protection than other photographic works

Once the threshold is reached, the **scope of protection is uniform** for all categories of work. This is an important rejection of national doctrines that previously applied graduated protection to photographs.

### Vertical harmonisation through art. 6 Directive 93/98

Painer formally applies the Infopaq formula in the framework of art. 6 Directive 93/98 (and the codified version art. 6 Directive 2006/116). That article had already, before InfoSoc, set a harmonised originality test for photographs. Painer confirms that the Infopaq formula and the art. 6 test are identical: a single uniform EU originality concept for all categories of work.

### Threshold and counter-limit (FAPL reference)

The *a contrario* reference to Football Association Premier League (paragraph 89) introduces the counterpart: where there is no scope for free creative choices (sports events dictated by the rules of the game), there is no work. This implicit limit is later elaborated expressly in Brompton (C-833/18) for functionally dictated form.

## Argumentative consequences

### For those wishing to establish originality of a photograph (pro-rightholder)

- Walk through the three stages of paragraph 91 and identify the choices made in each:
  - **Preparation**: chosen background, pose, lighting, location, clothing, accessories, direction
  - **Shooting**: camera settings (aperture, shutter speed, ISO), lens, framing, angle, atmosphere created
  - **Post-processing**: development technique, retouching, software, colour treatment, conversion
- Show that alternatives were possible — show other photographs of the same subject making different choices
- Point to the "personal touch" (paragraph 92): recognisable stylistic elements distinguishing this photographer
- For doubt on portrait photographs: rely on paragraph 93 — freedom not necessarily limited — and paragraph 98 — protection not inferior
- For utilitarian / documentary photographs (passport, product photo): even there choices are possible; the threshold is low

### For those wishing to contest originality of a photograph (pro-alleged-infringer)

- Show that the photograph came about **automatically** or **technically dictated**: no human choice in any of the three stages
- *A contrario* reasoning from FAPL (paragraph 89): where there is no creative scope, there is no work
- Concrete categories where this may work:
  - Surveillance/CCTV photographs: no choice, automated
  - Speed-camera or identity photographs: standard procedure, no creative input
  - Reproduction photographs of 2D artworks: deliberately no creative input (the photographer seeks fidelity)
  - Production photographs on a white background according to a standard protocol
- Show that the choices made were trivial or conventional (all practitioners in the field would make the same choices)
- **DO NOT** argue on the basis of "weaker protection for portrait photographs" — Painer paragraphs 93/98 expressly exclude that

### Strategic pitfall for the defence

The Painer judgment actively excludes two well-known defence lines:

- "It's just a portrait photograph, limited creative scope" → paragraph 93 rejects this
- "Photographs have weaker protection anyway" → paragraphs 97-98 reject this

Both are still regularly heard in trial-court case-law; they are not legally tenable under EU law.

## Significance for the two-step test

Painer is a judgment on **step 2** (originality), not on step 1 (work concept in the sense of objective identifiability). The question whether the portrait photograph is objectively identifiable is implicitly affirmed without elaboration — a photograph is by definition objectively fixed. The work-concept step is first expressly elaborated in Levola Hengelo (C-310/17) and Cofemel (C-683/17).

## Follow-up case-law building on Painer

- **Football Association Premier League**, C-403/08 and C-429/08 — *a contrario* reference in Painer paragraph 89
- **Football Dataco**, C-604/10 — confirmation of the "free creative choices" formula for databases
- **Cofemel**, C-683/17 — confirmation that the EU originality test is uniform across all categories of work and that no additional aesthetic criterion may be applied
- **Brompton**, C-833/18 — elaboration of the function-dictated-form exclusion (the counterpart that Painer paragraph 89 invokes *a contrario*)
