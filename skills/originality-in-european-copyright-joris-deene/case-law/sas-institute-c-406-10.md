## Subject matter and category

- **Main category**: **scope of copyright software protection** — what is not a form of expression of a computer program?
- **Special contribution**:
  - **Functionality, programming language and file formats are not a form of expression** of a computer program under the Software Directive (art. 1(2) Directive 91/250)
  - Express **policy consideration against monopolisation of ideas** at the expense of technological progress (paragraph 40)
  - **Reverse engineering / black-box reproduction of functionality** is permitted where no source-code access or object-code decompilation takes place
  - Confirmation and extension of **BSA's two-track system**: programming language and file formats may be protected via InfoSoc if they are an author's own intellectual creation (paragraph 45)
  - **Manuals for software** are copyright-protected under InfoSoc, not the Software Directive; Infopaq formula applied (paragraphs 65-67)
- **Applicable legislation**:
  - Directive 91/250/EEC (Software Directive — since 23 April 2009 replaced by codifying Directive 2009/24/EC, with identical core provisions)
  - Directive 2001/29/EC (InfoSoc), art. 2(a)
- **Relevance for the skill**: ★★★★☆ — *key judgment for the software module; strengthens and operationalises the BSA doctrine; gives express legitimisation to reverse engineering*

## Facts in four lines

SAS Institute developed over 35 years an integrated suite of programs (the SAS system) which allows users to write scripts in the SAS Language. WPL developed "World Programming System" as alternative software capable of running the same scripts — by observing and testing the SAS system, **without access to the source code** and **without decompilation of the object code**. WPL had lawfully purchased copies of the "Learning Edition" (under a click-through licence limited to non-productive purposes). SAS Institute brought an infringement action based on copyrights in the SAS system and the manuals.

## Legal questions

Nine questions, grouped in three clusters:

1. **Questions 1-5**: do functionality, programming language and file formats constitute a "form of expression" of a computer program under art. 1(2) Directive 91/250?
2. **Questions 6-7**: may a licence holder under art. 5(3) Directive 91/250 observe, study and test the working of the program to discover its underlying ideas and principles — even where that falls outside the licensed purpose?
3. **Questions 8-9**: can the reproduction of elements from a software manual (keywords, syntax, commands, options) in another program or in the manual of that other program constitute copyright infringement on the manual under art. 2(a) Directive 2001/29?

## Operative part

> **Operative part 1**: Article 1(2) of Directive 91/250 must be interpreted as meaning that **neither the functionality of a computer program nor the programming language and the format of data files** used in a computer program in order to exploit certain of its functions **constitute a form of expression of that program** and, as such, are not protected by copyright in computer programs under that directive.
>
> **Operative part 2**: Article 5(3) of Directive 91/250 must be interpreted as meaning that a person who has obtained a copy of a computer program under licence **may, without the authorisation of the copyright holder, observe, study or test the functioning of that program** in order to determine the ideas and principles which underlie any element of the program, where that person carries out acts covered by the licence as well as acts of loading and running necessary for the use of the program, **provided that he does not infringe the exclusive rights** of the holder of the copyright in that program.
>
> **Operative part 3**: Article 2(a) of Directive 2001/29 must be interpreted as meaning that **the reproduction, in a computer program or in a user manual for that program, of certain elements described in the user manual of another copyright-protected computer program may constitute infringement of the copyright in the latter manual if that reproduction is the expression of the intellectual creation of the author** of the user manual of the copyright-protected computer program, which is a matter for the referring court to verify.

## Key reasoning

### Paragraph 31 — *expression versus idea*

> "Article 1(2) of Directive 91/250 extends that protection to the expression in any form of a computer program. That provision states, however, that **the ideas and principles which underlie any element of a computer program, including those which underlie its interfaces, are not protected by copyright under that directive**."

### Paragraph 32 — *logic, algorithms and programming languages = ideas*

> "The 14th recital in the preamble to Directive 91/250 confirms [...] that, in accordance with the principle that only the expression of a computer program is protected by copyright, **to the extent that logic, algorithms and programming languages comprise ideas and principles, those ideas and principles are not protected under that directive**."

### Paragraph 39 — *the core conclusion* ⭐

> "[I]t must be stated that [...] **neither the functionality of a computer program nor the programming language and the format of data files** used in a computer program in order to exploit certain of its functions constitute a form of expression of that program for the purposes of Article 1(2) of Directive 91/250."

### Paragraph 40 — *policy consideration: anti-monopolisation* ⭐

> "[...] to accept that the functionality of a computer program can be protected by copyright would amount to **making it possible to monopolise ideas, to the detriment of technological progress and industrial development**."

### Paragraph 41 — *legitimisation of similar or identical independent programs*

> "[T]he main advantage of protecting computer programs by copyright is that such protection covers only the individual expression of the work and thus **leaves other authors the desired latitude to create similar or even identical programs provided that they refrain from copying**."

### Paragraph 43 — *delimitation: partial reproduction through code-taking*

> "[I]f a third party were to procure the part of the source code or the object code relating to the programming language or to the format of data files used in a computer program, and if that party were to create, with the aid of that code, similar elements in its own computer program, **that conduct would be liable to constitute partial reproduction within the meaning of Article 4(a) of Directive 91/250**."

### Paragraph 45 — *two-track system confirmed: programming language can be a work under InfoSoc* ⭐

> "[T]he finding made in paragraph 39 of the present judgment cannot affect the possibility that **the SAS language and the format of SAS Institute's data files might be protected, as works, by copyright under Directive 2001/29 if they are their author's own intellectual creation** (see Bezpečnostní softwarová asociace, paragraphs 44 to 46)."

### Paragraph 51 — *anti-circumvention via licence*

> "Article 5(3) of Directive 91/250 seeks to ensure that the ideas and principles which underlie any element of a computer program are **not protected by the owner of the copyright by means of a licensing agreement**."

### Paragraph 61 — *the operational test for reverse engineering*

> "[T]he copyright in a computer program cannot be infringed where, as in the present case, the lawful acquirer of the licence **did not have access to the source code** of the computer program to which that licence relates, **but merely studied, observed and tested that program in order to reproduce its functionality in a second program**."

### Paragraphs 65-67 — *manuals under the Infopaq formula*

> (paragraph 67) — "It is only through the **choice, sequence and combination** of those words, figures or mathematical concepts that the author may express his creativity in an original manner and achieve a result, namely the user manual for the computer program, **which is an intellectual creation** (see, to that effect, Infopaq International, paragraph 45)."

## Doctrinal significance

### 1. What is not a form of expression of a computer program?

SAS Institute answers the crucial question on which BSA had not expressly ruled: are functionality, programming language and file formats "forms of expression" of a program under the Software Directive? Answer: **no**.

| Element | Protected under Software Directive (track 1)? | Protected under InfoSoc (track 2)? |
|---|---|---|
| Source code | ✅ yes (BSA paragraph 35) | n/a |
| Object code | ✅ yes (BSA paragraph 35) | n/a |
| Preparatory design material | ✅ yes (art. 1(1) + recital 7) | n/a |
| Graphical user interface (GUI) | ❌ no (BSA) | ✅ possible (BSA paragraph 46) |
| **Functionality** | ❌ **no** (SAS paragraph 39) | ❌ no (idea) |
| **Programming language** | ❌ **no** (SAS paragraph 39) | ✅ possible (SAS paragraph 45) |
| **File formats** | ❌ **no** (SAS paragraph 39) | ✅ possible (SAS paragraph 45) |
| Algorithms, logic | ❌ no (recital 14) | ❌ no (idea) |
| Manual | n/a | ✅ yes (SAS operative part 3) |

### 2. The idea/expression dichotomy expressly as an EU principle

SAS Institute confirms the **idea/expression dichotomy** as a general EU principle, with international anchoring:

- Art. 9(2) TRIPS: "expressions and not [...] ideas, procedures, methods of operation or mathematical concepts as such"
- Art. 2 WIPO Copyright Treaty: idem
- Recital 14 Directive 91/250: ideas and principles underlying logic, algorithms and programming languages are not protected
- Recital 15 Directive 91/250: the **expression** of those ideas and principles is protected

Paragraph 40 adds a **policy justification**: copyright protection of functionality would amount to the **monopolisation of ideas** to the detriment of technological progress and industrial development.

### 3. The two-track system for software confirmed and refined

SAS Institute confirms the two-track system introduced in BSA:

- **Track 1 — Software Directive 91/250/2009/24**: only forms of expression (source code, object code, preparatory design material). Specific rules (art. 4 exclusive rights, art. 5 exceptions, art. 6 decompilation).
- **Track 2 — InfoSoc Directive 2001/29**: all other elements qualifying as a work — GUIs (BSA), programming languages (SAS paragraph 45), file formats (SAS paragraph 45), manuals (SAS operative part 3). The general Infopaq test (author's own intellectual creation) applies.

Importantly: art. 1(2) InfoSoc states that the InfoSoc Directive "leaves intact and in no way affects existing provisions [...] on the legal protection of computer programs". Tracks 1 and 2 do not exclude each other; for elements that are not "a form of expression of a computer program" (and therefore fall outside track 1), track 2 remains available.

### 4. Legitimisation of reverse engineering and clean-room development

SAS Institute is an **operational legitimisation** of black-box reverse engineering. The practice is expressly permitted under the following conditions:

- **No access to the source code** (paragraph 61, operative part 2)
- **No decompilation of the object code** (paragraph 61, operative part 2)
- **Lawful licence** for the program (paragraphs 47-48)
- **Within the licensed acts** or acts necessary for use (loading and running, even if the purpose is outside the licence) (paragraphs 56-59)
- **No infringement of the exclusive rights** of the rightholder (paragraph 59)

Important nuance: contractual clauses prohibiting such observation are **void** (art. 9(1) + art. 5(3) + paragraph 53). A software producer cannot contractually shield itself against reverse engineering. This is a fundamental tenet of EU software protection.

### 5. Manuals under the general Infopaq formula

Manuals for software fall **outside the Software Directive** (they are not a program) but **within InfoSoc** as literary works. The Infopaq formula (author's own intellectual creation, with choice/sequence/combination as the operational test) applies.

Important refinement (paragraph 66): keywords, syntax, commands, options, defaults and iterations **on their own** (considered separately) are not an intellectual creation. Only through the **choice, sequence and combination** can originality arise. This is a direct application of the Infopaq doctrine on elements of works (Infopaq paragraphs 45-46).

Practical consequence: copying a **single keyword or a single syntactic rule** from a manual is not infringement. Copying a **substantial combination** in which original choices reside, may be (operative part 3, with the test left to the national court).

### 6. Relationship with BSA

SAS Institute expressly builds on BSA and applies the same reasoning to new elements:

| Aspect | BSA (2010) | SAS Institute (2012) |
|---|---|---|
| Source and object code = form of expression | ✅ paragraph 35 | ✅ paragraph 38 (cross-ref BSA) |
| GUI ≠ form of expression of software | ✅ paragraph 41 | confirmed in paragraph 38 |
| GUI may be a work under InfoSoc | ✅ paragraphs 44-46 | confirmed in paragraph 45 |
| Functionality ≠ form of expression of software | implicit | ✅ paragraph 39 express |
| Programming language ≠ form of expression of software | implicit (recital 14) | ✅ paragraph 39 express |
| File formats ≠ form of expression of software | n/a | ✅ paragraph 39 express |
| Programming language may be a work under InfoSoc | n/a | ✅ paragraph 45 new |

SAS Institute is thus a **complementary judgment** that further delimits track 1 and further broadens track 2.

### 7. Implications for the originality test

Although SAS Institute is primarily a scope judgment, it contains two originality confirmations:

- **Paragraph 45**: programming languages and file formats may be works if they are an **author's own intellectual creation**. This is not a new formula but an application to these elements.
- **Paragraphs 65-67**: the Infopaq formula (author's own intellectual creation through choice/sequence/combination) is applied to software manuals.

For the two-step test:
- **Step 1 (work concept)**: these elements must qualify as a work (Levola test)
- **Step 2 (originality)**: the general Infopaq/Painer/BSA/FAPL/Football Dataco test, particularly the summarising Football Dataco formula (paragraphs 37-39)

### 8. Relationship with later case-law

SAS Institute was confirmed and developed in later case-law:

- **SAS Institute II** (UK High Court, [2013] EWHC 69 (Ch)) — application of the preliminary answers to the main proceedings; final judgment confirmed that WPL had not infringed
- **Top System** (CJEU C-13/20, EU:C:2021:811) — further elaboration of decompilation under art. 5(1) Directive 2009/24 (lawful decompilation for error correction)

## Argumentative consequences

### For those wishing to establish copyright protection of software (pro-rightholder)

#### Protectable (track 1 — Software Directive)

- **Specific source code**: the actual program text — literally, with variable names, structuring, comments
- **Specific object code**: the compiled version
- **Preparatory design material**: design documents, flowcharts that can lead directly to the program
- **Specific structuring and organisation** of the code (where not dictated by function — BSA exclusion)

#### Protectable (track 2 — InfoSoc)

- **GUI** if author's own intellectual creation (BSA + SAS)
- **Programming language** as such (rare, but possible) if author's own intellectual creation (SAS paragraph 45)
- **File format** as such if author's own intellectual creation (SAS paragraph 45)
- **Manual**: choice, sequence and combination of keywords, commands, etc. (SAS operative part 3 + paragraph 67)

#### Not protectable (general)

- Functionality as such
- Algorithms, logic, method
- Idea, principle, concept
- Look & feel as concept (idea)

#### Strategic recommendation

- **Document source-code access**: could the opposing party have had it? If yes, the case tilts towards infringement (SAS paragraph 43)
- **Demonstrate decompilation**: if the opposing party decompiled, that falls outside the art. 5(3) exception and possibly within art. 6 with strict conditions
- **Split work elements**: claim separately for source code (track 1), GUI (track 2), manual (track 2)

### For those wishing to contest copyright protection of software (pro-alleged-infringer)

#### The SAS defence lines

1. **Functionality exclusion**: "what we reproduced is only the functionality of the program — no form of expression under the Software Directive (SAS operative part 1, paragraph 39)"
2. **Programming-language exclusion**: "use of the same programming language or file format is no infringement under track 1 (SAS operative part 1, paragraph 39)"
3. **Anti-monopolisation policy consideration**: "copyright protection of functionality would amount to monopolising ideas, contrary to SAS paragraph 40 and the aims of EU law"
4. **Reverse-engineering legitimisation**: "our client lawfully acquired the program under licence, merely observed/tested/studied, and had no access to source code nor decompiled (SAS paragraphs 50-61, operative part 2)"
5. **Similar or identical programs permitted**: "EU software protection expressly allows other authors to create similar or even identical programs, provided they refrain from plagiarism (SAS paragraph 41)"

#### Crucial evidentiary leverage for the defence

- **No source-code access**: show that the client had no source-code access (licence conditions, delivery documents)
- **No decompilation**: technicians' statement that no decompilation took place
- **Independent development**: documentation of the own development process, design documents, code history
- **Clean-room development**: evidence of separation between specification team (which observed) and implementation team (which programmed without source-code access)
- **Specifications based on published manuals**: shows that the information was publicly available

#### Limitation — when the SAS defence does not work

- **Literal source-code taking**: paragraph 43 — where **part of source or object code** is procured and used to create similar elements, there is partial reproduction under art. 4(a)
- **Substantial taking from manual**: operative part 3 — if the "choice, sequence and combination" from a protected manual is taken, the manual may be infringed (not the program itself, but the manual)
- **Decompilation outside art. 6**: where decompilation was undertaken for purposes other than interoperability (and not within the strict conditions of art. 6), this is infringing

### For the cumulative exclusion grounds in the skill

SAS Institute strengthens the general doctrine on exclusions in the software context:

| Exclusion ground | Source | Application in SAS |
|---|---|---|
| **Technical function dictates expression** | BSA paragraphs 48-49 | Not primary, but implicit (algorithms are ideas) |
| **Rules dictate outcome** | FAPL paragraph 98 | Not primary |
| **Effort without originality** | Football Dataco paragraph 42 | Not primary, but implicit (the quantity of work to develop SAS functionality does not count) |
| **Functionality as such** ⭐ | **SAS paragraph 39 + operative part 1** | Specific for software |
| **Idea/expression dichotomy** ⭐ | **SAS paragraphs 31-34** | General EU principle |

SAS Institute thus adds a **fourth exclusion ground** (functionality) and strengthens the general **idea/expression dichotomy** as an EU principle.
