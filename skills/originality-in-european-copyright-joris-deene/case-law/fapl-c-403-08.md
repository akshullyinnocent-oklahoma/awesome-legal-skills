## Subject matter and category

- **Main category**: **category boundary of the work concept** — what cannot be a work?
- **Special contribution**: introduction of the doctrine that events **dictated by rules of the game** (sports events) are not works, with the express *a contrario* formulation to which Painer (C-145/10, paragraph 89) refers
- **Applicable directives**: Directive 2001/29/EC (InfoSoc), art. 2(a) (and besides much non-relevant matter: free movement, competition, communication to the public)
- **Relevance for the skill**: ★★★★☆ — *key judgment for the general category boundary and the FAPL benchmark referenced by later judgments (Painer, Cofemel, Brompton)*

## Important delimitation: scope limitation

This judgment is procedurally **extensive** (8 operative-part items, addressing free movement of services, competition law, conditional access, communication to the public). For the originality skill, **only** the following is relevant:

- **Paragraphs 96-100 + 117 + operative part 3 (in part)**: sports matches are not works because rules of the game leave no creative freedom
- **Paragraphs 155-157 + operative part 5**: confirmation and elaboration of the Infopaq doctrine on parts/fragments of works

All other items (Conditional Access Directive, art. 56 TFEU free movement, art. 101 TFEU competition, art. 3 InfoSoc communication to the public, art. 5 InfoSoc exception to the reproduction right) fall outside the scope of this skill.

## Facts in four lines

FAPL manages the English Premier League and sells exclusive territorial licences for the broadcasting of matches. UK pubs used Greek satellite decoder cards (NOVA) to show Premier League matches — cheaper than the British BSkyB subscription. FAPL brought actions inter alia for copyright infringement; the British High Court referred 18 questions, including the fourth (question 8(b) in case C-403/08): can FAPL rely on copyright to defend the territorial exclusivity? The Court held that it cannot, because sports matches themselves are not works.

## Legal questions (only those relevant for originality)

1. **Are sports matches (here Premier League football matches) works within the meaning of EU law?** — question 8(b) C-403/08, in the context of the justification under art. 56 TFEU
2. **How is the Infopaq doctrine on parts of works to be applied to transient fragments in a satellite decoder and on a TV screen?** — question 4 C-403/08

## Operative part (only items relevant for originality)

> **Operative part 3 (relevant element)**: Although sporting events, as such, have a unique and, to that extent, original character which can transform them into subject-matter that is worthy of protection comparable to the protection of works, that protection is granted, where appropriate, by the various domestic legal orders. *[But:]* FAPL cannot claim copyright in the matches themselves.
>
> **Operative part 5**: Article 2(a) of Directive 2001/29 must be interpreted as meaning that the reproduction right extends to transient fragments of the works in the memory of a satellite decoder and on a television screen, **provided that those fragments contain elements which are the expression of the authors' own intellectual creation**, so that the unit composed of the simultaneously reproduced fragments must be examined to determine whether it contains such elements.

## Key reasoning — originality

### Paragraph 96 — *sports matches are not works*

> "FAPL cannot claim copyright in the Premier League matches themselves, as they cannot be classified as works."

### Paragraph 97 — *confirmation of the Infopaq formula*

> "To be so classified, the subject-matter concerned would have to be original in the sense that it is its author's own intellectual creation (see, to this effect, Case C-5/08 *Infopaq International* [2009] ECR I-6569, paragraph 37)."

### Paragraph 98 — *rules of the game exclude creative freedom* ⭐ *the Painer benchmark*

> "However, sporting events cannot be regarded as intellectual creations classifiable as works within the meaning of the Copyright Directive. That applies in particular to football matches, **which are subject to rules of the game, leaving no room for creative freedom for the purposes of copyright**."

### Paragraph 99 — *no protection under EU law*

> "Accordingly, those events cannot be protected under copyright. It is, moreover, undisputed that European Union law does not protect them on any other basis in the field of intellectual property."

### Paragraph 100 — *opening for national sui generis protection*

> "None the less, sporting events, as such, have a unique and, to that extent, original character which can transform them into subject-matter that is worthy of protection comparable to the protection of works, and that protection can be granted, where appropriate, by the various domestic legal orders."

### Paragraphs 149 + 152 — *the broadcast does contain protected works*

> (paragraph 149) — "First, authors can rely on the copyright which attaches to the works exploited within the framework of those broadcasts. In the main proceedings, it is common ground that FAPL can assert copyright in various works contained in the broadcasts, that is to say, in particular, the opening video sequence, the Premier League anthem, pre-recorded films showing highlights of recent Premier League matches, or various graphics."

### Paragraph 155 — *Infopaq formula confirmed for the reproduction right*

> "As regards its meaning, it has already been observed, in paragraph 97 of the present judgment, that copyright for the purposes of Article 2(a) of the Copyright Directive can apply only in relation to subject-matter which is its author's own intellectual creation (*Infopaq International*, paragraph 37)."

### Paragraph 156 — *parts of a work*

> "The Court has thus stated that the various parts of a work enjoy protection under that provision, provided that they contain elements which are the expression of the intellectual creation of the author of the work (*Infopaq International*, paragraph 39)."

### Paragraph 157 — *testing of composite fragments*

> "This means that the unit composed of the fragments reproduced simultaneously – and therefore existing at a given moment – should be examined in order to determine whether it contains such elements. If it does, it must be classified as partial reproduction for the purposes of Article 2(a) of the Copyright Directive."

## Doctrinal significance

### 1. The *a contrario* formula to which Painer expressly refers

Painer paragraph 89 expressly refers *a contrario* to FAPL paragraph 98. The reasoning runs as follows:

- **Painer** (positive formula): the author must have been able to make free and creative choices — then there is a work
- **FAPL** (negative formula, *a contrario*): where rules of the game or external rules leave no scope for creative freedom, there is no work

Both judgments are the same coin in two sides. FAPL provides the counterpart that delimits the Painer doctrine. An advocate who invokes the FAPL doctrine without addressing the connection with Painer, or vice versa, misses half the story.

### 2. Category boundary: rule-dictated events are not works

FAPL builds in a **general category boundary** for the work-concept test: events whose course is wholly dictated by external rules (here rules of the game) cannot produce an "author's own intellectual creation" because the author has no scope for creative freedom. This doctrine applies to:

- Sports matches (expressly: FAPL paragraph 98)
- Chess matches, card games (by analogy)
- Programmed or algorithmic output without human creative input (by analogy)
- Random natural events (by analogy — no human creator)

Important: this is not about all matches or events indiscriminately. **The course** of the match itself is not a work; the **production around it** (camera direction, edit, commentary, montage, graphic elements) can be (FAPL paragraph 149).

### 3. Two connected negative exclusion grounds in the skill

FAPL and BSA together provide the two principal **negative** exclusion grounds of the EU originality test:

| Judgment | What does it exclude? | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **BSA** (C-393/09, paragraphs 48-49) | Components whose expression is dictated by **technical function** | Idea/expression merger |
| **FAPL** (C-403/08, paragraph 98) | Events whose course is dictated by **rules of the game or other external rules** | No scope for creative freedom |

Both can be summarised as one general principle: **where expression is dictated by external factors without scope for free creative choices, there is no work**. This principle is later consolidated in Brompton (C-833/18) for useful articles.

### 4. Nuance: opening for national sui generis protection (paragraph 100)

Although sports matches are not works, the Court recognises in paragraph 100 their "unique and, to that extent, original" character. Member States may grant comparable protection through specific national arrangements (sui generis rights for organisers of sports events). That falls outside the scope of this skill.

### 5. Significance for the two-step test

FAPL is a judgment on **step 2** (originality, in particular the negative test: when is there no author's own intellectual creation?). The work concept in the sense of objective identifiability (step 1, Levola test) does not arise — a sports match is by definition objectively perceivable. The question is only whether there is an **intellectual creation** in it.

### 6. Confirmation of Infopaq for fragment applications (paragraphs 155-157)

The second half of FAPL confirms and applies the Infopaq doctrine to a specific context: transient fragments in the memory of a satellite decoder and on a TV screen. The relevant doctrine for the originality skill is:

- The composite whole of **simultaneously** reproduced fragments must be examined (not one isolated fragment)
- If that whole contains originality-bearing elements, there is reproduction within the meaning of art. 2(a) InfoSoc
- This confirms the Infopaq doctrine that the qualitative criterion (originality) and not the quantitative (length) is decisive

## Argumentative consequences

### For those wishing to establish originality of a sport/event-related object (pro-rightholder)

- **Split the object**: the sports event itself is not a work, but the production around it is
- Concrete protectable elements around a sports broadcast (FAPL paragraph 149):
  - Opening video, intro, jingle
  - Anthem, theme song, background music
  - Pre-recorded clips (highlights, intros, mood shots)
  - Graphic elements (scoreboards, logos, lower thirds)
  - Camera choices and direction (framing, angles, switching)
  - Commentary (language, phrasing, narrative choices)
  - Replay selection and edit
- Avoid claiming protection for works wholly or largely dictated by rules — that weakens the position

### For those wishing to contest originality of a sport/event-related object (pro-alleged-infringer)

- **FAPL attack line**: the material taken is a **recording of a rule-dictated event** without author's own intellectual creation
- Show that:
  - The event is dictated by rules of the game (or other external rules)
  - The recording itself has no creative direction (static camera, automatic recording, single-camera setup)
  - No added elements (commentary, edit, graphics) were taken
- Strong contexts:
  - Live sports matches (FAPL paragraph 98)
  - Chess or board-game output
  - Match results and statistics (facts, not works)
  - Automatic recordings of natural phenomena
  - Algorithmically generated output without human creative input

### General *a contrario* reasoning from Painer paragraph 89 / FAPL paragraph 98

For every category of work it can be examined whether there was scope for free creative choices. Where external rules (technical, functional, conventional, sporting, legal) remove that scope, there is no work. This is the general counterpart to the Painer doctrine.
