## Status within this skill — KEY JUDGMENT for derivative works

Calinescu is the **first CJEU judgment** to apply the two-step test to a **critical edition** of a public-domain work. The judgment opens a sectoral block on derivative works within the skill architecture (module `07-derivative-works.md`), in which critical editions and philological reconstructions form a distinct sub-genre. Future case-law on translations, adaptations and arrangements can attach to this module.

The judgment also formulates **a generally relevant anti-fragmentation doctrine (paragraph 64)** and a **public-domain safeguard (paragraph 68)** which operate cross-sectorally.

## Subject matter and category

- **Primary category**: text works / **derivative works on public-domain works** — first CJEU application of the two-step test to critical editions
- **Particular contributions** ⭐:
  - **Cofemel two-step formula reaffirmed** (paragraphs 49-50): no heightened threshold for derivative works; the same test as for primary works
  - **Operational core formula for critical editions** (paragraph 54): originality arises from **the choice, sequence and combination of words** (Infopaq paragraph 45 applied); grammatical, lexical, literary and stylistic choices — **"whereas the skill in creating that subject matter is not relevant in that regard"** (exclusion of sweat-of-the-brow for philological expertise)
  - **Anti-fragmentation doctrine** ⭐⭐⭐ (paragraph 64): "It is not necessary to draw a distinction between the parts corresponding to the original work [...] and the comments, critical notes or explanations accompanying them [...]. Such an approach would run the risk of breaking up a work which is only meaningful as a whole"
  - **Paragraph 56**: originality also assessed by reference to composition, structure, form and arrangement of the original text in relation to the comments and critical apparatus
  - **Paragraph 63**: a critical edition may, as a whole, appear to be subject matter identifiable with sufficient precision and objectivity
  - **Paragraph 66**: scope of protection does **not** depend on the degree of creative freedom (Cofemel cross-ref paragraph 35); derivative works are not "weakly" protected
  - **Paragraph 67** ⭐: partial reproduction may constitute infringement where the parts reproduced "contain elements which are the expression of the intellectual creation of the author of the work" (Infopaq cross-ref paragraph 39) — applied to the attempt to restore a partly lost work
  - **Paragraph 68 — public-domain safeguard** ⭐⭐: "the recognition of copyright in the critical edition of a literary work in the public domain does not bring that work into the private domain and cannot confer on the author of that critical edition an exclusive right over that pre-existing work"
- **Applicable legislation**: Article 2(a) Directive 2001/29/EC (InfoSoc); Article 5 Directive 2006/116/EC (critical and scientific publications — optional national protection); Article 2(3) Berne Convention (derivative works)
- **Significance for the skill**: ★★★★★ — *first CJEU judgment on critical editions; generally relevant anti-fragmentation doctrine; public-domain safeguard*

## Facts in four lines

Professor Dan Slușanschi prepared a critical edition of the Latin work *Incrementorum et decrementorum aulae othmanicae* by Prince Dimitrie Cantemir (early 18th century, public domain). Slușanschi used the manuscript discovered at Harvard University in 1984 (1999 facsimile + later photocopies) and, through corrections and additions, reconstructed the text in a complete, intelligible form which was "as close as possible" to Cantemir's intention. After Slușanschi's death (2013), his heirs concluded an agreement with the Călinescu Institute on the use of the transcriptions; in 2015 the National Foundation for Science and Art (NFSA) published a bilingual edition which reproduced the Slușanschi edition in full (with only a footnote acknowledgement). The heirs brought infringement proceedings; the Tribunalul București and Curtea de Apel București held that there was infringement and classified the Slușanschi edition as a derivative work; Călinescu/NFSA brought a cassation appeal before the Înalta Curte de Casaţie şi Justiţie, which referred the question to the Court.

## Legal question

> "Must Article 2(a) of [Directive 2001/29] be interpreted as meaning that a critical edition of a work, the purpose of which is to restore the text of an original work, by consulting the manuscript, accompanying it with comments and the requisite critical apparatus, may be regarded as a work protected by copyright?"

## Operative part

> Article 2(a) of Directive 2001/29/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council of 22 May 2001 on the harmonisation of certain aspects of copyright and related rights in the information society must be interpreted as meaning that **the critical edition of a work which is in the public domain, the purpose of which is to restore the text of that work with comments and the requisite critical apparatus, may be regarded as a work protected by copyright, within the meaning of that provision, provided that it is an intellectual creation reflecting the author's personality, as an expression of his or her free and creative choices, and that it can be identified with sufficient precision and objectivity**.

## Key reasoning

### Paragraph 47 — *autonomous concept of "work"* (Levola cross-ref)

> "Articles 2 to 4 of Directive 2001/29 state that the Member States are to provide for a set of exclusive rights relating, in the case of authors, to their 'works', while Article 5 thereof sets out a series of exceptions and limitations to those rights. That directive makes no express reference to the laws of the Member States for the purpose of determining the meaning and scope of the concept of a 'work'. Accordingly, in view of the need for a uniform application of EU law and the principle of equality, that concept must normally be given an autonomous and uniform interpretation throughout the European Union (judgment of 13 November 2018, Levola Hengelo, C‑310/17, EU:C:2018:899, paragraph 33 and the case-law cited)."

### Paragraph 49 — *Cofemel two-step formula reaffirmed*

> "In that regard, it must be borne in mind that the concept of a 'work' requires two cumulative conditions to be satisfied. First, that concept entails that there exist an original subject matter, in the sense of being the author's own intellectual creation. Second, classification as a work is reserved to the elements that are the expression of such creation (judgment of 12 September 2019, Cofemel, C‑683/17, EU:C:2019:721, paragraph 29 and the case-law cited)."

### Paragraph 50 — *Painer formula reaffirmed*

> "As regards, in the first place, the criterion of originality, it is apparent from the Court's case-law that, in order for a subject matter to be regarded as original, it is both necessary and sufficient that it reflects the personality of its author, as an expression of his or her free and creative choices. On the other hand, when the realisation of a subject matter has been dictated by technical considerations, rules or other constraints, which have left no room for creative freedom, that subject matter cannot be regarded as possessing the originality required for it to constitute a work (judgment of 12 September 2019, Cofemel, C‑683/17, EU:C:2019:721, paragraphs 30 and 31 and the case-law cited)."

### Paragraph 51 — *decisive factor*

> "Thus, the fact that the subject matter in question reflects the personality of its author, by expressing his or her free and creative choices, constitutes a decisive factor of the concept of 'originality' and, consequently, of the protection of that subject matter by copyright in EU law."

### Paragraph 52 — *press articles as works* (Infopaq cross-ref)

> "In that regard, the Court has already held that press articles may constitute 'works' protected by copyright, for which the author's own intellectual creation results from the way in which the subject is presented, as well as from the linguistic expression of that author (see, to that effect, judgment of 16 July 2009, Infopaq International, C‑5/08, EU:C:2009:465, paragraph 44)."

### Paragraph 53 — *negative examples: words and military reports* (Infopaq + Funke Medien cross-ref)

> "On the other hand, the Court has held that words as such cannot constitute 'works' (see, to that effect, judgment of 16 July 2009, Infopaq International, C‑5/08, EU:C:2009:465, paragraph 46) nor can military reports, the content of which is essentially determined by the information which they contain, so that such information and the expression of those reports become indissociable and that those reports are thus entirely characterised by their technical function, precluding all originality (see, to that effect, judgment of 29 July 2019, Funke Medien NRW, C‑469/17, EU:C:2019:623, paragraph 24 and the case-law cited)."

### Paragraph 54 — *the core formula for critical editions* ⭐⭐⭐

> "In order to determine whether a literary object such as the Slușanschi critical edition is original, it is necessary to ascertain, as the Advocate General observed in point 56 of his Opinion, **whether, when drafting it, its author was able to make free and creative choices capable of conveying to the reader the originality of that subject matter, the originality of which arises, not from the words themselves considered in isolation, but from the choice, sequence and combination of the words by which the author expressed his creativity in an original manner and achieved a result which is an intellectual creation, whereas the skill in creating that subject matter is not relevant in that regard** (see, to that effect, judgments of 16 July 2009, Infopaq International, C‑5/08, EU:C:2009:465, paragraphs 45 and 46, and of 29 July 2019, Funke Medien NRW, C‑469/17, EU:C:2019:623, paragraph 23)."

### Paragraph 55 — *expertise, knowledge and interpretation of the author's intention*

> "In that context, as the Advocate General observed in point 63 of his Opinion, **the grammatical, lexical, literary and stylistic choices made by the author of a critical edition may probably be dictated, or at the very least influenced, by his or her years of experience, philological expertise, knowledge and understanding of the period in which the original work concerned was drawn up and of the historical period covered by that original work, his or her knowledge of the author of that original work, style and linguistic expression, and by his or her interpretation of what he or she perceives as being that author's intention**."

### Paragraph 56 — *additional assessment factors*

> "Lastly, as the Advocate General also observed in point 65 of his Opinion, **the condition of originality may also be assessed in the light of the composition of the critical edition in question, the structuring of the edition concerned, the form given to it and the arrangement of the original text in relation to the comments and critical apparatus**."

### Paragraph 57 — *application: Slușanschi edition not a mere transcription*

> "In the present case, it is apparent from the request for a preliminary ruling that the Slușanschi critical edition is not a mere transcription of the Latin manuscript of Dimitrie Cantemir's work, nor is it a facsimile of that manuscript. The objective of the Slușanschi critical edition was to restore, by making corrections and additions, the text of the original work in a complete, comprehensible form that was **as close as possible** to the intention of its author, Dimitrie Cantemir."

### Paragraph 58 — *comments and critical apparatus also intellectual creation*

> "In addition, it is apparent from the order for reference that the comments and critical apparatus in the Slușanschi critical edition, relating to corrections, word replacements and additions which may be necessary to understand the manuscript of Dimitrie Cantemir's work, and to the various language versions or variants of words or expressions which have been discarded, **are also the intellectual creation** of Professor Slușanschi."

### Paragraph 59 — *satisfies originality unless dictated by technical considerations*

> "Consequently, unless the drafting of the Slușanschi critical edition was dictated by purely technical considerations, rules or constraints with no creative freedom, it seems that that critical edition satisfies the criterion of originality, **which it is, however, for the referring court to assess**."

### Paragraph 60 — *objective identifiability* (Cofemel cross-ref)

> "As regards, in the second place, the criterion relating to the identifiable nature of the subject matter at issue, the Court has stated that the concept of a 'work' necessarily entails the existence of a subject matter that is **identifiable with sufficient precision and objectivity**. That is because, first, the authorities responsible for ensuring that the exclusive rights inherent in copyright are protected must be able to identify, clearly and precisely, the subject matter so protected. So also must third parties against whom the protection claimed by the author of that subject matter may be asserted. Second, the need to exclude any element of subjectivity, which is detrimental to legal certainty, in the process of identifying that subject matter means that the latter must have been expressed in an objective manner (see, to that effect, judgment of 12 September 2019, Cofemel, C‑683/17, EU:C:2019:721, paragraphs 32 and 33 and the case-law cited)."

### Paragraph 62 — *expression + objectively identifiable* (Levola cross-ref)

> "In that regard, as has been recalled in paragraph 60 of the present judgment, the concept of a 'work' referred to in Directive 2001/29 necessarily implies the existence of an expression which makes it **identifiable with sufficient precision and objectivity** (see, to that effect, judgment of 13 November 2018, Levola Hengelo, C‑310/17, EU:C:2018:899, paragraph 40), in relation to the parts corresponding to the original work."

### Paragraph 63 — *critical edition as a whole*

> "As the Advocate General observed in point 70 of his Opinion, **the critical edition of an original work may, as a whole, appear to be subject matter identifiable with sufficient precision and objectivity**."

### Paragraph 64 — *the anti-fragmentation doctrine* ⭐⭐⭐

> "**It is not necessary to draw a distinction between the parts corresponding to the original work, which may have been the subject of textual amendments, and the comments, critical notes or explanations accompanying them in order, where appropriate, to identify those parts which are capable of falling within the scope of copyright protection. Such an approach would run the risk of breaking up a work which is only meaningful as a whole**, in particular where such comments, notes or explanations supplement or relate to a specific part of the text of the original work which they are commenting on or restoring."

### Paragraph 65 — *conditional classification as a work*

> "Accordingly, in the present case, subject to the checks to be carried out by the referring court, taking into account the specific nature of the Slușanschi critical edition, the purpose of which is to restore the partially lost text of the original work with comments and the requisite critical apparatus, that critical edition may be regarded as a 'work', within the meaning of Article 2(a) of Directive 2001/29, **provided that it is an intellectual creation reflecting the personality of its author, as an expression of his or her free and creative choices, and that it can be identified with sufficient precision and objectivity**."

### Paragraph 66 — *uniform scope of protection* (Cofemel cross-ref paragraph 35)

> "As regards, in the third place, the scope of the protection of the work, it should be borne in mind that, where a critical edition has the characteristics set out in paragraph 49 of the present judgment and therefore constitutes a work, it must, as such, qualify for copyright protection, in accordance with Directive 2001/29, and it must be added that **the extent of that protection does not depend on the degree of creative freedom exercised by its author, and that that protection is therefore not inferior to that to which any work falling within the scope of that directive is entitled** (judgment of 12 September 2019, Cofemel, C‑683/17, EU:C:2019:721, paragraph 35 and the case-law cited)."

### Paragraph 67 — *partial reproduction may constitute infringement* (Infopaq cross-ref paragraph 39)

> "It should be added, as the Advocate General observed in point 73 of his Opinion, that **the infringement of copyright need not consist in the complete reproduction of the work, but may consist in a partial reproduction**. The various parts of a work also enjoy protection under Article 2(a) of Directive 2001/29, **provided that they contain elements which are the expression of the intellectual creation of the author of the work** (see, to that effect, judgment of 16 July 2009, Infopaq International, C‑5/08, EU:C:2009:465, paragraph 39), which could be the case where an author seeks to restore a partially lost literary work in the form which he or she considers to be as close as possible to that drawn up by the author of the original work."

### Paragraph 68 — *public-domain safeguard* ⭐⭐

> "Finally, and irrespective of the foregoing considerations, **the recognition of copyright in the critical edition of a literary work in the public domain does not bring that work into the private domain and cannot confer on the author of that critical edition an exclusive right over that pre-existing work**."

## Doctrinal significance for this skill

### 1. First CJEU application of the two-step test to critical editions

Before Calinescu there was no CJEU case-law explicitly addressing critical editions, philological reconstructions or similar scientific publications. The judgment closes the work-concept debate on this genre by stating clearly: critical editions **can** be works, provided they satisfy the ordinary Cofemel two-step test. **No heightened threshold** applies to derivative works on public-domain works.

The judgment is consistent with Article 2(3) Berne Convention (translations, adaptations, arrangements and other alterations of a literary or artistic work are protected as original works without prejudice to the copyright in the original work) and with Article 5 Directive 2006/116 (optional national protection of 30 years for critical and scientific publications of public-domain works — referring to recital 19). Paragraphs 47-48 make clear that, alongside this optional national regime, the full copyright regime of Directive 2001/29 remains available.

### 2. The core formula for critical editions (paragraph 54) ⭐

The operational formula has three components:

| Component | Content |
|---|---|
| **(a) Free and creative choices** | The author must have had room for creative expression in drafting |
| **(b) "Not the words themselves, but the choice, sequence and combination"** | Infopaq paragraph 45 applied to critical editions — originality in **structure and composition**, not in isolated text fragments |
| **(c) "The skill is not relevant"** | Philological expertise, years of experience, knowledge of the period and the author are **evidence of the possibility of creative choices**, not an independent ground of originality (sweat-of-the-brow exclusion) |

Component (c) connects directly with Football Dataco (C-604/10) paragraphs 38-39 (sweat-of-the-brow excluded) and Funke Medien (C-469/17) paragraph 23 (same). It confirms that **academic expertise as such** does not imply originality — the creative choices must be identified in the expression itself.

### 3. Additional factors for critical editions (paragraph 56)

Paragraph 56 adds four additional assessment dimensions:

- **Composition** of the critical edition (which texts/variants are included)
- **Structure** of the work (chapter division, organisation of the material)
- **Form** given to it (typographical choices, presentation)
- **Arrangement** of the original text in relation to the comments and critical apparatus (parallel/footnotes/apparatus pages)

These factors come on top of the grammatical, lexical, literary and stylistic choices referred to in paragraph 55.

### 4. The anti-fragmentation doctrine (paragraph 64) ⭐⭐⭐ — generally relevant

This is a principled statement that is **not confined to critical editions**. The Court explicitly holds that the national court must not artificially break up a work into "original parts" and "public-domain parts" or into "creative parts" and "non-creative parts" where the work is "**only meaningful as a whole**".

Operational consequences:

| Type of work | Anti-fragmentation application |
|---|---|
| **Critical editions** | Original text + corrections + comments + apparatus = one work, not separately assessed |
| **Compilations** | Selection + arrangement + contributions may together form one work |
| **Multimedia works** | Text + image + sound + interactivity cannot be artificially split |
| **Applied art with functional elements** | Functional + aesthetic parts may together form one work (cross-ref Brompton paragraph 38, Cofemel paragraph 36) |
| **Software architectures** | Code + data + interface not always severable |

**Limitation**: the anti-fragmentation doctrine does **not** relieve the rightholder of the Cofemel two-step test for the whole. The work **as a whole** must be original and objectively identifiable (paragraph 65). Only once that is established does the need for internal splitting fall away.

Cross-reference: Infopaq paragraph 39 (components are protected provided they contain elements which are the expression of the intellectual creation) remains the basis for **partial reproduction** as infringement (paragraph 67). The anti-fragmentation doctrine governs the **qualification** as a work, not the infringement question.

### 5. Public-domain safeguard (paragraph 68) ⭐⭐

Paragraph 68 is a principled limitation on copyright in derivative works. **Recognising copyright in a critical edition does not deprive the public domain of its effect for the original work.**

Concrete consequences:

| Permitted | Not permitted |
|---|---|
| Making one's own critical edition of the same public-domain work | **Literally taking over** the critical edition of another |
| Reproducing the original work itself (Latin text of Cantemir) | Reproducing the **specific expression** of Slușanschi (his corrections, arrangement, comments, apparatus) |
| Making a new translation/adaptation/reconstruction | Copying the **delta** of Slușanschi's reconstruction without adding own creative choices |
| Reference to existing editions in own scholarly work | Substantial taking of Slușanschi's specific reconstruction |

For the pro-alleged-infringer, paragraph 68 provides a defence anchor: **where the reproduced parts correspond to the original work** (and thus belong to the public domain), there can be no infringement of the derivative-work protection. Infringement arises only where the **specific creative additions** of the editor have been taken.

Concrete distinction: in the main proceedings the NFSA edition reproduced "the text of the Slușanschi critical edition as published in 2001" in full, including **unpublished additions and corrections** by Slușanschi. It was this taking of Slușanschi's **specific creative additions** which constituted the infringement — not the taking of the Cantemir text as such.

### 6. Relationship with the Cofemel/Brompton/Mio-Konektra line

Calinescu is **not** an applied-art judgment and does not build on the Cofemel/Brompton/Mio-Konektra line on utilitarian objects. It does, however, apply the Cofemel two-step formula to a **new genre**: derivative works on public-domain works. The connection with Mio/Konektra lies in the shared holding that:

- **No heightened threshold** applies to specific types of work (Cofemel; Mio/Konektra operative part 1)
- The two-step test is uniform (Cofemel paragraph 29 restated as core formula)
- "Skill is not relevant" / "no presumption of creativity" — both judgments place the burden on the rightholder to positively identify creative choices (Calinescu paragraph 54; Mio/Konektra paragraph 65)

Difference: Mio/Konektra adds the "unique appearance" requirement as a specific exclusion ground for applied art (application-judgment-with-doctrinal-addition). Calinescu remains within the Cofemel formula and applies it to a new genre (application-judgment-with-anti-fragmentation-addition).

### 7. Relationship with the text-works line (Infopaq + Funke Medien)

| Judgment | Text-works contribution |
|---|---|
| **Infopaq** (C-5/08, 2009) | General formula + "choice, sequence and combination of words" (paragraph 45) + "even short fragments may be original" (paragraphs 47-48) |
| **Funke Medien** (C-469/17, 2019) | Three cumulative criteria for idea/expression merger in functional texts (paragraph 24); sweat-of-the-brow excluded (paragraph 23) |
| **Calinescu** (C-649/23, 2026) | First CJEU judgment on critical editions / derivative works on public-domain; "skill is not relevant" (paragraph 54); anti-fragmentation doctrine (paragraph 64); public-domain safeguard (paragraph 68) |

Calinescu fits seamlessly into this line and expands the text-works corpus to **derivative works**.

### 8. Distinction between Article 5 Directive 2006/116 and Article 2(a) Directive 2001/29

An important doctrinal distinction the judgment implicitly draws (see also recital 19 of Directive 2006/116):

| Regime | Basis | Classification | Term | Character |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Full copyright** | Art. 2(a) Dir 2001/29 + Berne Convention Art. 2(3) | "Work" — author's own intellectual creation; two-step test | Life + 70 years | Mandatory EU harmonisation |
| **Related right for critical/scientific publications** | Art. 5 Dir 2006/116 | No work-status required; lower regime | Max 30 years from publication | Optional national law |

In concrete terms: **a critical edition which does not satisfy the Cofemel two-step test** may nevertheless be protected under any national related right under Article 5 Directive 2006/116. This optional national protection falls outside the scope of Calinescu (and outside the scope of this skill — which strictly addresses the work concept).

## Argumentative consequences

### For those seeking to establish copyright in a critical edition (pro-rightholder)

Calinescu is **particularly favourable** to the rightholder because:

1. **No heightened threshold**: the ordinary Cofemel test suffices
2. **Anti-fragmentation doctrine**: the opposing party cannot artificially split the work to limit protection
3. **Broad originality factors**: choice-sequence-combination + grammatical/lexical/literary/stylistic + composition/structure/form/arrangement + knowledge of period/author/interpretation of intention
4. **Comments and apparatus count**: paragraph 58 explicit
5. **Uniform scope of protection**: paragraph 66 — derivative works are not "weakly" protected
6. **Partial reproduction suffices for infringement**: paragraph 67 + Infopaq paragraph 39

Points of caution:

- **"Skill is not relevant"** (paragraph 54): avoid arguments built heavily on philological expertise or academic reputation; focus on the **creative choices** in the actual end result
- **Not a mere transcription/facsimile**: a bare reproduction of the manuscript is not a work; show corrections, additions, reconstruction or substantial interpretative choices (Slușanschi edition as reference model, paragraph 57)
- **Technical constraints as limit**: an edition entirely dictated by scientific conventions (e.g. a mechanical diplomatic edition reproducing every character of the manuscript exactly) does not meet the threshold (paragraph 59)

### For those seeking to contest copyright in a critical edition (pro-alleged-infringer)

Calinescu is **challenging** for the defence — the threshold is low and the Court takes a broad view of critical editions. But there are lines of attack:

1. **"Skill is not relevant"** (paragraph 54): counter any argument resting on philological expertise or academic reputation
2. **Mere transcription or facsimile** (paragraph 57): show that the edition adds no substantial creative choice, but merely reproduces the manuscript in modern typography
3. **Technical conventions of philological editorial practice** (paragraph 59): show that the edition is dictated by scholarly standards (readings according to conventional criteria, classical editorial practice)
4. **Public-domain safeguard** (paragraph 68): where only the **parts corresponding to** the original work (Latin text of Cantemir) have been taken, **no infringement** of the derivative-work protection can be made out
5. **Own alternative reconstruction**: paragraph 68 does not preclude a third party from making a **different** critical edition of the same public-domain work
6. **Make the "creative addition" concrete**: force the rightholder to positively identify which specific corrections, additions, arrangements or comments represent creative choices (cross-ref Mio/Konektra paragraph 65: no presumption of creativity)

**Important limit on the anti-fragmentation doctrine**: paragraph 64 does not prevent the defence from showing that **certain parts of the work** are not original. It only prohibits the splitting of a work which is "**only meaningful as a whole**". For an edition whose parts have meaning independently of one another (e.g. an edition with a substantial independent introductory study + critical apparatus + text), the defence may continue to argue that individual components are not original on their own.
