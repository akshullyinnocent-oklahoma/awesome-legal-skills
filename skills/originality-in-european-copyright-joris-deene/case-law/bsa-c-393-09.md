- **Court**: CJEU (Third Chamber)
- **Reporting judge**: G. Arestis

- **Language of the case**: Czech
- **Referring court**: Nejvyšší správní soud (Czech Republic)
- **Short name**: BSA
- **EUR-Lex (EN)**: https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/HTML/?uri=CELEX:62009CJ0393

## Subject matter and category

- **Main category**: sector-specific doctrine for **software** and **graphical user interfaces (GUI)**
- **Special contribution**: introduction of the **technical-function exclusion** as a general implication of the EU originality test
- **Applicable directives**: Directive 91/250/EEC (Software Directive), art. 1; Directive 2001/29/EC (InfoSoc), art. 2(a) and art. 3(1)
- **Relevance for the skill**: ★★★★☆ — *key judgment on software/GUI and first clear formulation of the technical-function exclusion in EU originality doctrine (precursor to Brompton, C-833/18)*

## Facts in five lines

The Bezpečnostní softwarová asociace (Czech association for software protection) asked the Czech Ministry of Culture for a licence for the collective management of economic copyright on computer programs, arguing that the graphical user interface (GUI) also falls under that protection. The Ministry rejected the request on the ground that the Czech Copyright Act protects only object and source code, not the on-screen result. After several administrative proceedings the case reached the Nejvyšší správní soud, which referred two questions: (i) is a GUI a "form of expression" of a computer program within the meaning of art. 1(2) Directive 91/250? (ii) Is showing a GUI during a TV broadcast a communication to the public within the meaning of art. 3(1) InfoSoc?

## Legal questions

The judgment addresses two questions. Only the first is directly relevant for the originality skill.

1. **GUI under the Software Directive?** (art. 1(2) Directive 91/250) — answer: NO. But the reformulation of that question yields a doctrine of broad relevance on the general originality test (art. 2 InfoSoc + idea/expression and technical function).
2. **Showing GUI during TV broadcast = communication to the public?** (art. 3(1) InfoSoc) — answer: NO due to passive reception without interaction. *Not relevant for originality; outside the scope of this skill.*

## Operative part (question 1 — relevant for originality)

> A graphic user interface is not a form of expression of a computer program within the meaning of Article 1(2) of Directive 91/250/EEC (...) and is not protected by copyright as a computer program under that directive. **Nevertheless, such an interface can be protected by copyright as a work by Directive 2001/29 (...) if that interface is its author's own intellectual creation.**

## Key reasoning — originality

### Paragraph 38 — *expression of the program versus elements "around" the program*

> "As the Advocate General states in Point 61 of his Opinion, any form of expression of a computer program must be protected from the moment when its reproduction would engender the reproduction of the computer program itself, thus enabling the computer to perform its task."

### Paragraph 41 — *GUI does not enable reproduction of the program*

> "In those circumstances, the graphic user interface does not enable the reproduction of that computer program, but merely constitutes one element of that program by means of which users make use of the features of that program."

### Paragraph 42 — *rejection of Software Directive protection for GUI*

> "It follows that that interface does not constitute a form of expression of a computer program within the meaning of Article 1(2) of Directive 91/250 and that, consequently, it cannot be protected specifically by copyright in computer programs by virtue of that directive."

### Paragraph 45 — *confirmation of the Infopaq formula under InfoSoc*

> "The Court has held that copyright within the meaning of Directive 2001/29 is liable to apply only in relation to a subject-matter which is original in the sense that it is its author's own intellectual creation (see, to that effect, with regard to Article 2(a) of Directive 2001/29, *Infopaq International*, paragraphs 33 to 37)."

### Paragraph 46 — *GUI can be a work under InfoSoc*

> "Consequently, the graphic user interface can, as a work, be protected by copyright if it is its author's own intellectual creation."

### Paragraph 48 — *arrangement and configuration — core criterion*

> "When making that assessment, the national court must take account, inter alia, of the specific arrangement or configuration of all the components which form part of the graphic user interface in order to determine which meet the criterion of originality. In that regard, that criterion cannot be met by components of the graphic user interface which are differentiated only by their technical function."

### Paragraph 49 — *technical function and idea/expression merger*

> "As the Advocate General states in Points 75 and 76 of his Opinion, where the expression of those components is dictated by their technical function, the criterion of originality is not met, since the different methods of implementing an idea are so limited that the idea and the expression become indissociable."

### Paragraph 50 — *no scope for creative mind*

> "In such a situation, the components of a graphic user interface do not permit the author to express his creativity in an original manner and achieve a result which is an intellectual creation of that author."

### Paragraph 51 — *synthesis answer question 1*

> "In the light of the foregoing considerations, the answer to the first question referred is that a graphic user interface is not a form of expression of that program within the meaning of Article 1(2) of Directive 91/250 and thus is not protected by copyright as a computer program under that directive. Nevertheless, such an interface can be protected by copyright as a work by Directive 2001/29 if that interface is its author's own intellectual creation."

## Statutory basis — idea/expression dichotomy in the Software Directive

Art. 1(2) Directive 91/250 (codification: art. 1(2) Directive 2009/24) contains the express idea/expression dichotomy for software:

> "Protection in accordance with this Directive shall apply to the **expression in any form** of a computer program. **Ideas and principles** which underlie any element of a computer program, including those which underlie its interfaces, **are not protected by copyright under this Directive**."

BSA confirms and concretises this: the GUI is not a form of expression of the program (no reproduction of the program is possible via the GUI) and components characterised solely by their technical function do not meet the originality threshold.

## Doctrinal significance

### 1. Two-track protection for software expressions

BSA confirms a **two-track system**:

- **Track 1 — Software Directive (91/250 / 2009/24)**: protects only the form of expression of the program that **enables reproduction of the program itself** (source and object code, preparatory design material). Lex specialis with its own, lower originality threshold (art. 1(3) requires only "author's own creation"; no further aesthetic criterion).
- **Track 2 — InfoSoc Directive (2001/29)**: protects the general copyright on works within the meaning of art. 2(a), thus also elements that fall outside the core program but are an author's own intellectual creation. The Infopaq formula (author's own intellectual creation) applies.

What falls where?

| Element | Track |
|---|---|
| Source code | Software Directive (track 1) |
| Object code | Software Directive (track 1) |
| Preparatory design material (specs, design docs) | Software Directive (track 1), if it can result in a program |
| Graphical user interface (GUI) | InfoSoc (track 2), if author's own intellectual creation |
| Screenshots, layout, icons | InfoSoc (track 2), if original |
| Programming language as such | Neither — not a work (see SAS Institute, C-406/10) |
| File format (general) | Neither — idea, not expression (idem SAS) |
| Algorithm as such | Neither — idea/principle (art. 1(2) Directive 91/250) |

### 2. Technical-function exclusion (paragraphs 48-50)

This is the most **broadly applicable** part of BSA, with effects far beyond the software context. The Court here expressly formulates for the first time that **technically dictated elements** do not meet the EU originality threshold, because:

1. where the expression is dictated by the technical function (paragraph 49)
2. the different methods of implementing the underlying idea become so limited (paragraph 49)
3. that idea and expression become indissociable (paragraph 49)
4. as a result of which the author has no scope to express his creative mind (paragraph 50)

This is the EU formulation of what in the Anglo-American tradition is called the **merger doctrine** (idea and expression merge; no protection on the idea → no protection on the expression that necessarily follows from it).

### 3. Precursor to Brompton (C-833/18)

BSA paragraphs 48-50 are the direct precursor to the Brompton doctrine (C-833/18, 11 June 2020) on technically dictated form for **useful articles**. Brompton expressly builds on BSA and extends the doctrine beyond software/GUI to all categories of work, particularly applied art. Key passage in Brompton:

> "the criterion of originality cannot be met by the components of a subject matter which are differentiated only by their technical function." (Brompton, paragraph 27, consistent with BSA paragraph 48)

In other words: BSA provided the general doctrine, Brompton applied it to a three-dimensional object.

### 4. Confirmation of Infopaq for the general doctrine

BSA paragraph 45 expressly confirms that the Infopaq formula ("author's own intellectual creation") applies to the **whole** InfoSoc Directive and is not limited to press articles or text fragments. This is the first express extension of the Infopaq formula by the Court itself to another category of work.

### 5. Question 2 (communication to the public) — outside scope

The second question (passive showing of GUI on TV = no communication to the public for lack of interactivity) does not touch copyright at the level of the work concept or originality, but at the level of the exploitation right. That doctrine is further developed in *inter alia* SGAE (C-306/05), FAPL (C-403/08 and C-429/08), TV-Catchup (C-607/11) and GS Media (C-160/15). It is irrelevant for the originality skill.

## Argumentative consequences

### For those wishing to establish originality of software elements (pro-rightholder)

- **Source code / object code**: rely on track 1 (Directive 91/250 / 2009/24, art. 1). Low threshold: only "author's own creation". No aesthetic requirements.
- **GUI**: rely on track 2 (InfoSoc, art. 2(a)). Originality test via BSA paragraph 46 — author's own intellectual creation. Concretely show:
  - **Arrangement and specific configuration** of all GUI components (BSA paragraph 48)
  - For each component: choices not dictated by technical function (colour, placement, design, animations, layout, icon style)
  - Show that alternative implementations were possible — show other GUIs for the same function
- Apply Painer formulas (free creative choices, personal touch) to the non-functional elements of the GUI
- Where originality is contested: argue that **arrangement and composition** (= the BSA paragraph 48 + Infopaq paragraph 45 pattern) are not dictated by function, even if individual building blocks are

### For those wishing to contest originality of software elements (pro-alleged-infringer)

- **Strongest attack line: technical-function exclusion** (BSA paragraphs 48-50)
  - Show that each challenged GUI component is dictated by technical or functional necessity
  - Point to standard patterns (Microsoft Windows conventions, Material Design, iOS HIG): conventions that limit expression to a functional pattern
  - "Idea coincides with expression" — limited execution possibilities
- **Idea/expression merger argument**: art. 1(2) Directive 91/250 and BSA paragraph 49 — no protection on functionality, algorithms, look & feel as concept, interaction patterns as such
- **Building blocks vs. composition**: where GUI protection is contested it can be argued that only loose, conventional building blocks were taken, not the specific arrangement
- **Programming language / API / file format as such**: not a work (later expressly in SAS Institute, C-406/10) — not via BSA but closely connected

## Reformulation of the question — meta-procedural lesson

BSA paragraphs 43-44 illustrate that the Court does not let itself be restricted by the literal terms of the question referred when a useful answer requires a broader reading. The Court answers question 1 from *two* angles: first under Directive 91/250 (NO), then under Directive 2001/29 (POSSIBLY YES, if author's own intellectual creation). Procedurally: a national court ruling on GUI protection may — and must — likewise examine both bases.

## Significance for the two-step test

BSA is a judgment on **step 2** (originality, via the Infopaq formula), without expressly thematising the work-concept step (step 1). The work-concept step (objective identifiability) is implicitly addressed: a GUI is by definition objectively identifiable (there is a perceivable on-screen output). The explicit two-step test follows only in Levola Hengelo (C-310/17) and Cofemel (C-683/17).

## Follow-up case-law building on BSA

- **SAS Institute** (C-406/10) — confirms that functionality, programming language and file format are not works; builds on BSA's technical-function exclusion
- **Football Dataco** (C-604/10) — application of the "author's own intellectual creation" formula to databases; refers to BSA for the general doctrine
- **Cofemel** (C-683/17) — confirms the uniform originality test across all categories of work, with express reference to BSA
- **Brompton** (C-833/18) — elaboration of the technical-function exclusion for useful articles, building directly on BSA paragraphs 48-50
