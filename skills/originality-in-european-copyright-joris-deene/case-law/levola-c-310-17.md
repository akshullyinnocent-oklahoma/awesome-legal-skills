## Subject matter and category

- **Main category**: **work concept** — step 1 of the two-step test (can an object qualify as a "work" within the meaning of Directive 2001/29 at all?)
- **Special contribution**:
  - **Express formulation of the two-step test**: two cumulative conditions for qualification as a "work" (paragraph 35) ⭐
  - **Requirement of objective identifiability**: a work must be "identifiable with sufficient precision and objectivity" (paragraph 40) ⭐
  - **Non-permanent forms of expression permitted**: dance, performance art, theatre can be works even if not fixed (paragraph 40)
  - **Two underlying rationales**: (i) clear and precise identification by authorities and economic operators; (ii) legal certainty through exclusion of subjectivity (paragraph 41)
  - **Taste of a food product is not a work** (operative part + paragraph 44)
  - **Full EU harmonisation** of the work concept (paragraph 45, operative part)
- **Applicable legislation**: Directive 2001/29/EC, in particular art. 2(a) (reproduction right) and the general scope
- **Relevance for the skill**: ★★★★★ — *the judgment that definitively operationalises step 1 of the two-step test; before Levola the two-step test was implicit, since Levola explicit*

## Facts in four lines

"Heksenkaas" is a Dutch spreadable dip with cream cheese and fresh herbs, created in 2007 by a vegetable trader. Levola, which acquired the IP rights in the product, brought an action against Smilde over its "Witte Wievenkaas", contending that its taste was a reproduction of the copyright-protected taste of Heksenkaas. The Gerechtshof Arnhem-Leeuwarden referred the question on copyright protection of taste, in a context where divergent national approaches to copyright on sensory creations (taste, scent) had emerged.

## Legal questions

Two questions, of which only the first was answered:

1. **Does Directive 2001/29 preclude copyright protection of the taste of a food product?** Sub-questions:
   - (a) General question
   - (b) Does the wording of art. 2(1) Berne Convention ("whatever may be the mode or form of its expression") preclude copyright protection where the examples listed concern only sight- and/or sound-creations?
   - (c) Do the (possible) instability of a food product and/or the subjective character of taste experience preclude protection?
   - (d) Does the system of exclusive rights and limitations in art. 2-5 Directive 2001/29 preclude protection?
2. If the answer to 1(a) is negative: what requirements apply? (Not answered.)

## Operative part

> Directive 2001/29/EC [...] must be interpreted as precluding **the taste of a food product** from being protected by copyright under that directive and as precluding national legislation from being interpreted in such a way that it grants copyright protection to such taste.

## Key reasoning

### Paragraph 33 — *work concept = autonomous EU concept*

> "[...] The directive makes no express reference to the laws of the Member States for the purpose of determining the meaning and scope of the concept of a 'work'. Accordingly, in view of the need for a uniform application of EU law and the principle of equality, **that concept must normally be given an autonomous and uniform interpretation throughout the European Union**."

### Paragraph 35 — *two cumulative conditions for work qualification* ⭐

> "In that regard, **two cumulative conditions must be satisfied** for subject matter to be classified as a 'work' within the meaning of Directive 2001/29."

### Paragraph 36 — *condition 1: originality*

> "First, the subject matter concerned must be **original in the sense that it is the author's own intellectual creation** (judgment of 4 October 2011, Football Association Premier League and Others, [...] paragraph 97 and the case-law cited)."

### Paragraph 37 — *condition 2: expression of the intellectual creation*

> "Secondly, **only something which is the expression of the author's own intellectual creation** may be classified as a 'work' within the meaning of Directive 2001/29 (see, to that effect, judgments [...] Infopaq International, [...] paragraph 39, and [...] Football Association Premier League and Others, [...] paragraph 159)."

### Paragraph 39 — *idea/expression dichotomy via international law*

> "[...] in accordance with Article 2 of the WIPO Copyright Treaty and Article 9(2) of the [TRIPs Agreement] [...] **copyright protection may be granted to expressions, but not to ideas, procedures, methods of operation or mathematical concepts as such** (see, to that effect, judgment [...] SAS Institute, [...] paragraph 33)."

### Paragraph 40 — *the core formula: objective identifiability* ⭐

> "Accordingly, for there to be a 'work' as referred to in Directive 2001/29, **the subject matter protected by copyright must be expressed in a manner which makes it identifiable with sufficient precision and objectivity, even though that expression is not necessarily in permanent form**."

### Paragraph 41 — *the two underlying rationales*

> "That is because, first, **the authorities responsible for ensuring that the exclusive rights inherent in copyright are protected must be able to identify, clearly and precisely, the subject matter so protected**. The same is true for individuals, in particular economic operators, who must be able to identify, clearly and precisely, what is the subject matter of protection which third parties, especially competitors, enjoy. Secondly, **the need to ensure that there is no element of subjectivity –– given that it is detrimental to legal certainty –– in the process of identifying the protected subject matter** means that the latter must be capable of being expressed in a precise and objective manner."

### Paragraph 42 — *application to taste: subjective and variable*

> "The taste of a food product cannot, however, be pinned down with precision and objectivity. **Unlike, for example, a literary, pictorial, cinematographic or musical work, which is a precise and objective form of expression**, the taste of a food product will be identified essentially on the basis of taste sensations and experiences, **which are subjective and variable** since they depend, inter alia, on factors particular to the person tasting the product concerned, such as **age, food preferences and consumption habits, as well as on the environment or context** in which the product is consumed."

### Paragraph 43 — *technical state of development*

> "Moreover, **it is not possible in the current state of scientific development to achieve by technical means a precise and objective identification of the taste of a food product** which enables it to be distinguished from the taste of other products of the same kind."

### Paragraph 44 — *conclusion: taste is not a work*

> "[T]he taste of a food product cannot be classified as a 'work' within the meaning of Directive 2001/29."

### Paragraph 45 — *full EU harmonisation*

> "In view of the requirement [...] for a uniform interpretation of the concept of a 'work' throughout the European Union, it must also be concluded that **Directive 2001/29 prevents national legislation from being interpreted in such a way that it grants copyright protection to the taste of a food product**."

## Doctrinal significance

### 1. The explicit two-step test

Before Levola Hengelo the test was usually presented as a single whole ("author's own intellectual creation" with the Infopaq/Painer/BSA/FAPL/Football Dataco criteria). With Levola the test is expressly split into **two successive steps**:

| Step | Condition | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Work concept** | The subject matter must be objectively identifiable — a form of expression in which it can be identified with sufficient precision and objectivity | Levola paragraphs 35-44, particularly paragraph 40 |
| **2. Originality** | The subject matter must be an author's own intellectual creation (the Football Dataco formula, building on Infopaq/Painer/BSA/FAPL) | Levola paragraph 36 + all preceding case-law |

The two steps are **cumulative**. An object that is objectively identifiable but not original is no work (e.g., a banal alphabetical list). An object that might be original but is not objectively identifiable (taste, scent) is likewise no work.

### 2. The core formula (paragraph 40) as operational test for step 1

> "[T]he subject matter protected by copyright must be expressed in a manner which makes it **identifiable with sufficient precision and objectivity**, even though that expression **is not necessarily in permanent form**."

This formula combines two elements:

- **Positive requirement**: sufficiently precise and objectively identifiable
- **Negative requirement not introduced**: permanent fixation is **not** required

The non-permanence is important: dance, theatre, performance art, oral delivery and even improvisation can be works, even if not fixed. What counts is whether they are **at the moment of performance** objectively identifiable.

### 3. The two underlying rationales (paragraph 41)

The Court explains **why** objective identifiability is required:

**Rationale 1 — Identification by third parties**
- Authorities (judges, collective-management organisations, customs) must be able to determine what is protected
- Economic operators (competitors) must be able to know what they may not reproduce
- Individuals (users) must be able to know whether an act is infringing

**Rationale 2 — Legal certainty through exclusion of subjectivity**
- Copyright rests on the premise that the subject matter of protection can be objectively delineated
- Subjective assessment of what is protected would undermine legal certainty
- It is a policy choice that copyright applies only to subject matter that can be intersubjectively identified

### 4. What is and is not objectively identifiable?

The Court gives explicit examples (paragraph 42):

**Objectively identifiable** — can therefore be a work (if also original):
- Literary work (text)
- Painting
- Film
- Musical work
- (Implications — not express but following from the logic): sculpture, dance, choreography, theatre, performance, recitation

**Not objectively identifiable** — therefore cannot be a work:
- **Taste of a food product** (Levola operative part)
- **Scent** (implicitly, via the alignment of the Court with positions categorically rejecting copyright on sensory experiences)
- Sensory experiences dependent on age, preferences, habits, environment and context

### 5. Taste and scent — definitive EU position

Although the case formally concerned taste, the same reasoning applies to scent — both are sensory, subjective and variable, and at the current state of science not objectively identifiable. Levola operates as a knock-out at step 1 of the two-step test: where an object cannot be identified with sufficient precision and objectivity, the originality question never even arises.

### 6. Full EU harmonisation of the work concept

Levola paragraph 45 + operative part confirms that the work concept is **fully harmonised**. Member States cannot, through a broad national interpretation of their own copyright statute, grant copyright protection to subject matter that is not a work within the meaning of Directive 2001/29.

This has practical consequences for:

- Earlier national case-law granting broad protection to sensory creations — superseded since Levola
- Other sensory experiences (texture, feel) possibly protected in national case-law

### 7. Relationship with later case-law

Levola is directly incorporated into:

- **Cofemel** (C-683/17, EU:C:2019:721) — aligns with Levola for the two-step test; paragraph 32 expressly repeats the formula of Levola paragraph 40
- **Brompton** (C-833/18, EU:C:2020:461) — confirms Levola for useful articles
- **VG Bild-Kunst** (C-392/19, EU:C:2021:181) — confirms the autonomous work concept

### 8. Which works thereby possibly fall in a grey zone?

Levola has tightened the work concept. Objects whose copyright status is now less certain:

- **Scents** (perfumes, aromas): probably no longer protectable — Levola logic is directly transferable
- **Tastes** (recipes, "haute cuisine" dishes): expressly excluded (Levola operative part)
- **Textures** (such as the mouthfeel Levola pointed to): presumably excluded
- **Improvisational art** (jazz improvisation): can be a work if objectively identifiable at the moment of performance (paragraph 40 — not necessarily permanent)
- **Performance art and dance**: idem, if objectively identifiable at the moment of performance
- **AI output**: separate problem (requirement of "author's own intellectual creation" by a human author, not so much work-concept-step-1)
- **Taste sensors / chemical "fingerprints"**: not decisive — Levola paragraph 43 tests against the **current state of science**, even if chemical analyses exist that do not yet substitute for human taste experience

## Argumentative consequences

### For those wishing to show that an object is a work (pro-rightholder, step 1)

#### The three elements to show

1. **Form of expression**: the object must be expressed in a form (not exist only as an idea in someone's head)
2. **Sufficient precision**: the expression must be precise enough for the content of the work to be concretely identified
3. **Objectivity**: identification must not depend on subjective assessment

#### Strong examples where step 1 normally easily succeeds

- Writings, software, photographs, films, music, paintings, sculptures, drawings, maps
- Architectural plans, technical drawings
- Choreography if described (Labanotation) or filmed
- Theatre performance if filmed or via a developed script
- Performance art if objectively identifiable in performance

#### Strategic pitfalls to avoid

- **Do not describe the experiential quality of the work, but identify the work itself**: a musical work is identified through the score or the recording, not through "what it sounds like to the listener"
- **Do not rely on subjective descriptions**: a work that can only be described via "impression" or "experience" (without objective anchoring) is no work
- **For sensorially oriented products**: argue through the objective fixation (recipe, score, plan) — not through the sensory experience

#### Burden of proof and evidentiary means

- **Reproduction of the work itself**: produce the manuscript, the score, the code, the image, the object
- **Fixation in a medium**: tape, video, photograph, copy
- **Descriptive elements supporting objective identification**: technical specifications, dimensions, colour codes
- **Comparison with other works** in the same category: shows that this type of object can be objectively delimited by third parties

### For those wishing to show that an object is not a work (pro-alleged-infringer, step 1 attack)

#### The Levola attack

If the opposing party makes a claim on an object that is **not objectively identifiable**, the defence can cut off the claim at step 1, **before the originality question** even arises.

#### Strong examples of work-concept attack

- **Taste**: directly via Levola operative part
- **Scent, perfume**: Levola logic directly transferable
- **Visual "ambience" or "atmosphere"** without concrete form
- **Style as such** (genre, aesthetic): no objectively identifiable object
- **A "concept"** without concrete elaboration
- **A type of experience** (a kind of dance party, a kind of interactive event) without objectively fixed elements
- **AI output** without identifiable human author (separate problem — combination of step 1 and originality)
- **An "overall impression"** without concrete elements forming the expression

#### Operational test for the defence

Ask the opposing party to **concretely describe**:

1. What exactly is the work on which you rely?
2. How can a third party identify this work without subjective assessment?
3. What form of expression has this work taken?
4. How can authorities and competitors determine what is protected?

If the opposing party can only answer with terms such as "the taste of", "the scent of", "the atmosphere of", "the style of", "the overall impression of" without concrete anchoring, the step 1 test fails.

#### Quotations for the attack

- **Paragraph 40**: "identifiable with sufficient precision and objectivity"
- **Paragraph 41**: "able to identify, clearly and precisely, the subject matter so protected" + "legal certainty"
- **Paragraph 42**: subjective and variable — depending on age, preferences, habits, environment, context

### How step 1 relates to step 2

- **Step 1 (Levola)**: is there a work at all?
- **Step 2 (Football Dataco)**: is this work original?

In practice the argumentation runs:

1. **First step 1**: identify the concrete object and show its objective identifiability
2. **Then step 2**: show originality via the Football Dataco formula (free creative choices + personal touch; no exclusion ground such as technical function, rules, sweat of the brow or idea/functionality)

If step 1 fails, step 2 is not reached. The Levola attack is therefore **a knock-out attack**: one successful attack at step 1 makes the entire further argumentation of the opposing party superfluous.
