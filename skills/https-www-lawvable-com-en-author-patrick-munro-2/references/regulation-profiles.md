# EU Regulation Quick Reference Profiles

Concise profiles of the core EU digital regulations covered by this skill. Verify current text against the primary source before relying on any profile for binding decisions. Dates and thresholds cited here are accurate to the last verification date shown at the end of each profile but are not re-verified continuously.

---

## GDPR: General Data Protection Regulation

**Citation**: Regulation (EU) 2016/679
**Primary source**: https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2016/679/oj
**Status**: in force since 25 May 2018

### Scope

- **Who**: controllers and processors of personal data
- **What**: processing of personal data of EU data subjects
- **Where**: EU/EEA establishments; non-EU controllers targeting EU data subjects (Art. 3(2))
- **Exemptions**: purely personal or household activity; national security (limited); law enforcement (covered by LED)

### Key dates

- 25 May 2018: entered into application
- Ongoing: continuous compliance

### Major obligations

- Lawful basis for processing (Art. 6) and consent conditions (Art. 7)
- Transparency (Art. 12-14)
- Data subject rights: access, rectification, erasure, portability, objection, restriction (Art. 15-22)
- Data protection by design and by default (Art. 25)
- Security of processing (Art. 32)
- Breach notification: 72 hours to supervisory authority; notify individuals if high risk (Art. 33-34)
- DPIA for high-risk processing (Art. 35)
- DPO where required (Art. 37-39)
- International transfers (Chapter V)

### Penalties

Up to €20 million or 4% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher. Up to €10 million or 2% for lesser infringements.

### Common compliance challenges

Lawful basis determination, consent management for layered services, data subject rights at scale, international transfers post-Schrems II, vendor management.

## Data Act

**Citation**: Regulation (EU) 2023/2854
**Primary source**: https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2023/2854/oj
**Status**: in force since 11 January 2024; main obligations applied from 12 September 2025

### Scope

- **Who**: data holders (including cloud providers), manufacturers of connected products, users, and third parties authorized by users
- **What**: access to and sharing of data generated by connected products and related services
- **Where**: products placed on the EU market and related services offered in the EU
- **Exemptions**: certain micro and small enterprises for aspects of the product design obligations

### Key dates

- 12 September 2025: main obligations applied
- 12 September 2026: connected product design obligations apply to newly placed products
- 12 September 2027: cloud switching provisions apply

### Major obligations

- User data access (Art. 4): readily available, free of charge, structured machine-readable format
- Third-party sharing on user request (Art. 5)
- B2B data sharing for aftermarket services (Art. 5-7)
- Unfair contract terms prohibition (Art. 13)
- Public body access in exceptional circumstances (Chapter V)
- Cloud switching (Chapter VI)
- Interoperability (Chapter VIII)
- International transfer restrictions for non-personal data (Art. 32)

### Penalties

Set by Member States; must be effective, proportionate, and dissuasive. Personal data violations subject to GDPR penalty regime.

### Common compliance challenges

Distinguishing user data from provider IP and trade secrets; API design for real-time versus batch provision; international transfer handling for non-personal data; renegotiation of pre-existing cloud contracts.

## AI Act

**Citation**: Regulation (EU) 2024/1689
**Primary source**: https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj
**Status**: in force since 1 August 2024; phased application through 2027

### Scope

- **Who**: providers, deployers, importers, distributors, authorized representatives of AI systems
- **What**: AI systems placed on the EU market, put into service, or whose output is used in the EU
- **Where**: EU market; also non-EU providers where output is used in the EU
- **Exemptions**: exclusively military, defence, national security purposes; scientific research and development (conditional); certain open-source models (conditional, not applicable to GPAI with systemic risk)

### Key dates

- 2 February 2025: prohibited AI practices apply (Art. 5); AI literacy obligations (Art. 4)
- 2 August 2025: GPAI obligations, governance, penalties
- 2 August 2026: main high-risk AI system obligations; transparency obligations for certain AI systems (Art. 50)
- 2 August 2027: full application including high-risk AI in Annex I harmonisation legislation

### Major obligations

- **Prohibited practices (Art. 5)**: social scoring, subliminal manipulation, biometric categorisation by protected characteristics, emotion recognition in workplace/education, untargeted scraping for facial recognition databases, real-time remote biometric identification (with narrow law enforcement exceptions)
- **High-risk AI (Chapter III)**: risk management (Art. 9), data governance (Art. 10), technical documentation (Art. 11, Annex IV), record-keeping (Art. 12), transparency to deployers (Art. 13), human oversight (Art. 14), accuracy, robustness, cybersecurity (Art. 15), quality management (Art. 17), conformity assessment (Art. 43), CE marking (Art. 48), post-market monitoring (Art. 72), serious incident reporting (Art. 73)
- **GPAI (Chapter V)**: technical documentation, copyright policy, transparency; systemic risk models above 10^25 FLOPs have additional obligations
- **Transparency (Art. 50)**: natural persons interacting with AI systems must be informed; deepfakes and AI-generated content labelling
- **FRIA (Art. 27)**: fundamental rights impact assessment for public bodies and certain private entities deploying high-risk AI

### Penalties

Up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover for prohibited practices. Up to €15 million or 3% for other violations. Up to €7.5 million or 1% for supplying incorrect information. SME adjustments apply.

### Common compliance challenges

High-risk classification under Annex III with Art. 6(3) exception; GPAI vs. AI system distinction; conformity assessment path selection; FRIA scope; systemic risk thresholds; interaction with sectoral law.

## Cyber Resilience Act (CRA)

**Citation**: Regulation (EU) 2024/2847
**Primary source**: https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/2847/oj
**Status**: in force since 10 December 2024; main obligations apply from 11 December 2027

### Scope

- **Who**: manufacturers, importers, distributors of products with digital elements
- **What**: hardware and software products, including remote data processing solutions necessary for the product's function
- **Where**: products placed on the EU market
- **Exemptions**: products covered by sectoral legislation providing equivalent or higher cybersecurity (medical devices under MDR/IVDR, in-vitro diagnostic medical devices, motor vehicles under Regulation 2019/2144, certain civil aviation products); pure non-commercial open-source

### Key dates

- 11 September 2026: vulnerability reporting obligations apply
- 11 December 2027: full application

### Major obligations

- Essential cybersecurity requirements (Annex I): no known exploitable vulnerabilities, secure by default, secure update mechanisms, minimisation of attack surface, protection of confidentiality/integrity/availability
- Vulnerability handling (Art. 13, Annex I Part II): coordinated disclosure, security updates throughout support period
- Incident reporting (Art. 14): early warning within 24 hours, incident notification within 72 hours, final report within 14 days of mitigation
- Conformity assessment (Art. 32): self-assessment for default category; third-party for Important and Critical products
- CE marking (Art. 30)
- Technical documentation (Art. 31, Annex VII)
- Support period: as long as products are reasonably expected to be in use; minimum 5 years unless justified

### Penalties

Up to €15 million or 2.5% of global annual turnover for violation of essential requirements. Up to €10 million or 2% for other obligations. Up to €5 million or 1% for supplying incorrect information.

### Common compliance challenges

Product classification (default / Important / Critical); support period determination; SBOM generation; vulnerability disclosure programmes; open-source component governance.

## NIS2: Network and Information Security Directive

**Citation**: Directive (EU) 2022/2555
**Primary source**: https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/dir/2022/2555/oj
**Status**: in force since 16 January 2023; Member State transposition deadline 17 October 2024

### Scope

- **Who**: essential entities and important entities in designated sectors, based on size thresholds and sector criticality
- **What**: cybersecurity risk management and incident reporting
- **Where**: applies to entities providing services or carrying out activities in the EU

### Key dates

- 17 October 2024: Member State transposition deadline (several Member States missed, infringement proceedings followed)
- Ongoing: national implementation timelines vary

### Major obligations (Article 21(2))

The ten minimum cybersecurity risk management measures, mapped to the statutory sub-paragraphs:

- (a) Risk analysis and information system security policies
- (b) Incident handling
- (c) Business continuity, backup, disaster recovery, crisis management
- (d) Supply chain security
- (e) Security in network and information systems acquisition, development, maintenance
- (f) Policies and procedures to assess the effectiveness of cybersecurity risk management measures
- (g) Basic cyber hygiene practices and cybersecurity training
- (h) Policies and procedures regarding the use of cryptography and, where appropriate, encryption
- (i) Human resources security, access control policies, and asset management
- (j) Multi-factor or continuous authentication, secured voice/video/text communications, and secured emergency communication systems

### Incident reporting (Article 23)

- Early warning within 24 hours of becoming aware
- Incident notification within 72 hours
- Final report within one month

### Penalties (Article 34)

- Essential entities: up to €10 million or 2% of global annual turnover
- Important entities: up to €7 million or 1.4%
- Management body personal accountability provisions

### National implementations

National implementing laws vary. Verify the specific national statute applicable to the entity before relying on generic NIS2 framework analysis. Implementation status as of April 2026 varies across Member States: most have transposed; some remained in legislative process into early 2026; Germany enacted the NIS2UmsuCG with entry into force on 6 December 2025 and BSI registration portal opened 6 January 2026. Verify current national status with the relevant competent authority.

### Common compliance challenges

Entity classification (essential vs. important); scope of supply chain security; 24-hour early warning capability; cross-border incident coordination; management body training and accountability.

## DORA: Digital Operational Resilience Act

**Citation**: Regulation (EU) 2022/2554
**Primary source**: https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2022/2554/oj
**Status**: in force since 16 January 2023; applied from 17 January 2025

### Scope

- **Who**: approximately 20 categories of financial entities listed in Art. 2, plus ICT third-party service providers designated as critical (Art. 31)
- **What**: ICT risk management and operational resilience
- **Where**: EU financial sector

### Key dates

- 17 January 2025: main obligations applied
- Ongoing: critical ICT TPP designation process; regulatory technical standards (RTS) in phased adoption

### Major obligations for financial entities

- ICT risk management (Art. 5-16): governance, protection, detection, response, recovery
- ICT incident management and classification (Art. 17-23)
- Digital operational resilience testing (Art. 24-27) including threat-led penetration testing (TLPT) for significant entities
- ICT third-party risk (Art. 28-44): register of information, contractual requirements, oversight, exit strategies
- Information sharing arrangements (Art. 45)

### Major obligations for critical ICT TPPs

Oversight by Lead Overseer (ESA) with direct powers including recommendations and periodic penalty payments for non-compliance.

### Penalties

Up to 2% of total annual worldwide turnover for critical ICT TPPs (via periodic penalty payments). Administrative penalties for financial entities are set at national level within a harmonised framework; criminal sanctions where provided by national law.

### Common compliance challenges

Comprehensive ICT risk register; TLPT implementation for significant entities; renegotiating legacy ICT TPP contracts; exit strategy documentation; major incident classification consistency; critical TPP designation process for providers.

## DMA: Digital Markets Act

**Citation**: Regulation (EU) 2022/1925
**Primary source**: https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2022/1925/oj
**Status**: in force since 1 November 2022; applied from 2 May 2023; designated gatekeepers had compliance deadlines from 6 March 2024

### Scope

- **Who**: designated gatekeepers providing core platform services
- **What**: core platform services (online intermediation, search engines, social networking, video-sharing, N-IICS, operating systems, web browsers, virtual assistants, cloud computing, online advertising)
- **Where**: EU market

### Major obligations (Art. 5-7)

Approximately 22 substantive obligations including:

- Interoperability (messaging, app stores)
- Data portability and business user data access
- No self-preferencing in rankings
- No tying or bundling of core platform services
- No use of non-public data from business users to compete with them
- Restrictions on personal data combination across services
- Transparency for advertisers and publishers

### Penalties

Up to 10% of worldwide turnover; up to 20% for repeat infringements. Periodic penalty payments up to 5% of average daily worldwide turnover. Structural remedies available for systematic non-compliance.

### Common compliance challenges

Scope of "core platform service"; interoperability implementation; data combination controls; interaction with GDPR and DSA.

## DSA: Digital Services Act

**Citation**: Regulation (EU) 2022/2065
**Primary source**: https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2022/2065/oj
**Status**: in force since 16 November 2022; fully applied from 17 February 2024

### Scope

- **Who**: providers of intermediary services (mere conduit, caching, hosting, online platforms, very large online platforms (VLOPs), very large online search engines (VLOSEs))
- **What**: illegal content, systemic risks, transparency, user protection
- **Where**: services offered in the EU
- **Size thresholds**: VLOPs and VLOSEs designated based on 45 million average monthly EU users

### Key obligations scale with service type

- All intermediary services: transparency reporting, terms of service, contact points
- Hosting services: notice-and-action mechanisms, statement of reasons for content moderation
- Online platforms: complaint mechanisms, trusted flaggers, out-of-court dispute settlement, transparency for recommender systems and online advertising
- VLOPs/VLOSEs: risk assessment, risk mitigation, independent audits, data access for vetted researchers, crisis response mechanism

### Penalties

Up to 6% of global annual turnover. Periodic penalty payments up to 5% of average daily worldwide turnover.

### Common compliance challenges

Notice-and-action at scale; recommender system transparency; risk assessment methodology; researcher data access; interaction with DMA for gatekeepers.

## ePrivacy Directive

**Citation**: Directive 2002/58/EC (as amended)
**Primary source**: https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/dir/2002/58/oj
**Status**: in force; ePrivacy Regulation proposed to replace it remains in negotiation

### Scope

- **Who**: providers of electronic communications services and end-user device access
- **What**: confidentiality of electronic communications; storage and access to information on terminal equipment (cookies and similar)
- **Where**: EU; implementation via national transposition

### Key provisions

- Confidentiality of communications (Art. 5)
- Storage and access to terminal equipment information: prior consent required with narrow exceptions (Art. 5(3))
- Traffic data retention and processing (Art. 6)
- Location data (Art. 9)
- Unsolicited communications (Art. 13)

### Pending ePrivacy Regulation

Would replace the Directive with a regulation, broader scope, align more closely with GDPR. Legislative process has been ongoing; check current status before relying on either the Directive or proposed Regulation for planning purposes.

### Common compliance challenges

Cookie consent mechanics and compliance with emerging national practice; interaction with GDPR lawful basis regime; cross-border enforcement variation.

## Verification notes

Last verified: 23 April 2026. This reference material must be re-verified against EUR-Lex and the relevant national sources before being used in a binding context. The following areas change most frequently:

- **National transposition status** for directives (NIS2, ePrivacy) varies across Member States and evolves
- **Delegated and implementing acts** continue to be adopted under AI Act, Data Act, CRA, and DORA; their content affects compliance obligations materially
- **Harmonised standards** for conformity assessment under AI Act and CRA are published progressively
- **Enforcement guidance** from EDPB, EC, ENISA, ESAs, and national authorities evolves and changes what compliance looks like in practice

Where a profile above is material to a decision, verify current text against the primary source.
