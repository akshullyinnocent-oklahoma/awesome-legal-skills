# Common Regulation Interaction Patterns

Reference analysis of the most common overlaps among EU digital regulations. Each pattern gives the core provisions, the interaction analysis, and a compliance strategy. Verify current text against the primary source before relying on this in a binding context.

---

## GDPR and Data Act

### Data access rights

- **GDPR Art. 15**: right of access to personal data
- **Data Act Art. 4**: right to access data generated by connected products and related services

**Interaction**

- GDPR covers personal data only; Data Act covers all product-generated data
- Where product data includes personal data, both apply
- Data Act is broader in scope; GDPR is deeper on data subject rights specifically for personal data

**Strategy**: a single access mechanism built to Data Act specifications can satisfy GDPR access for personal data as a subset, provided GDPR-specific rights (rectification, erasure, objection) are also addressed through complementary mechanisms.

### Data portability

- **GDPR Art. 20**: right to data portability for personal data
- **Data Act Art. 4-5**: portability of all product data, including to third parties nominated by the user

**Interaction**

- Both require structured, commonly used, machine-readable formats
- Data Act extends beyond personal data and beyond controller-to-controller transfers
- Data Act includes user-to-user and user-to-authorized-third-party portability, which GDPR does not

**Strategy**: single technical solution (API plus export) serving both. Note the expansion of recipient categories under Data Act.

## AI Act and GDPR

### Automated decision-making

- **GDPR Art. 22**: right not to be subject to solely automated decisions with legal or similarly significant effect
- **AI Act Art. 50**: transparency obligations for certain AI systems interacting with natural persons
- **AI Act Art. 86**: right to explanation for decisions taken on the basis of high-risk AI

**Interaction**

- Complementary rather than overlapping: AI Act adds AI-specific transparency and explanation obligations on top of GDPR's automated decision-making regime
- GDPR attaches to personal data processing; AI Act attaches to AI system operation regardless of personal data
- Where both apply (high-risk AI processing personal data), both regimes operate cumulatively

**Strategy**: integrated transparency notice covering GDPR Art. 13-14 disclosures, AI Act transparency obligations, and Art. 22 safeguards where applicable.

### Data governance and design

- **GDPR Art. 25**: data protection by design and by default
- **AI Act Art. 9-15**: AI system requirements including risk management, data governance, accuracy, robustness, cybersecurity

**Interaction**

- Reinforcing at the design-stage level
- GDPR focuses on personal data protection; AI Act focuses on AI system reliability, safety, security
- Training data governance under AI Act Art. 10 has direct GDPR implications where personal data is involved

**Strategy**: unified design review integrating DPIA (GDPR Art. 35), fundamental rights impact assessment (AI Act Art. 27, where applicable), and technical documentation (AI Act Art. 11) into a single design-stage governance process.

## CRA and AI Act

### Product security and safety

- **CRA Annex I**: essential cybersecurity requirements for products with digital elements
- **AI Act Annex IV**: technical documentation requirements for high-risk AI
- **AI Act Art. 15**: accuracy, robustness, cybersecurity obligations

**Interaction**

- Separate conformity assessment procedures, each with its own CE marking path where applicable
- CRA focuses on cybersecurity of products with digital elements; AI Act focuses on AI-specific risks
- AI-enabled products fall under both where the product has digital elements and contains a high-risk AI system
- AI Act Art. 6(1) read with Annex I treats AI safety components in products covered by existing Union harmonisation legislation as high-risk AI; the mechanism routes their conformity assessment through the existing sectoral procedures rather than creating a parallel AI-only path. Implementation through delegated acts and harmonised standards is evolving.

**Strategy**: parallel compliance programmes with coordinated documentation. Watch for integrated procedures as they emerge through delegated acts and harmonised standards.

### Vulnerability handling

- **CRA Art. 13 and Annex I Part II**: manufacturer vulnerability handling obligations (coordinated vulnerability disclosure policy, software update delivery, single point of contact)
- **CRA Art. 14**: reporting actively exploited vulnerabilities and severe incidents to CSIRTs and ENISA (obligations applying from 11 September 2026)
- **AI Act Art. 73**: serious incidents involving high-risk AI systems reported to market surveillance authorities

**Interaction**

- Different triggers: CRA for actively exploited vulnerabilities and severe incidents; AI Act for serious incidents involving high-risk AI
- Different authorities: CRA to ENISA via national CSIRTs; AI Act to national market surveillance authorities
- Overlapping scope: a cybersecurity incident in a high-risk AI system may trigger both

**Strategy**: unified incident response playbook with branch logic that triggers both reporting paths where thresholds are independently met. Do not rely on one report satisfying both.

## NIS2 and DORA

### Incident reporting

- **NIS2 Art. 23**: incident notification (early warning within 24 hours, incident notification within 72 hours, final report within one month)
- **DORA Art. 17-23**: ICT-related incident reporting with initial, intermediate, and final reports

**Interaction**

- Financial entities fall under both by default
- DORA is lex specialis for financial sector ICT risk; DORA compliance substantially satisfies NIS2 for financial entities
- Different competent authorities: NIS2 to national CSIRT and competent authority; DORA to financial supervisors
- Reporting formats and timelines are aligned in direction but differ in detail

**Strategy**: DORA as primary framework for financial entities. Where the incident reaches NIS2 thresholds and NIS2 is not pre-empted for the specific issue, report both. Build reporting workflows that produce the superset of required data.

### Third-party ICT risk

- **NIS2 Art. 21**: cybersecurity risk management including supply chain security
- **DORA Art. 28-44**: ICT third-party risk management with detailed contractual, due diligence, monitoring, and exit obligations

**Interaction**

- DORA is substantially more stringent for financial sector
- DORA requires explicit exit strategies; NIS2 does not at the same level of specificity
- DORA designates critical ICT third-party providers with direct oversight by EU authorities

**Strategy**: DORA compliance programme generally exceeds NIS2 baseline. Non-financial entities under NIS2 should not assume NIS2 compliance satisfies DORA if they also deliver services to financial entities subject to DORA as critical ICT TPPs.

## GDPR and NIS2

### Security measures

- **GDPR Art. 32**: security of processing appropriate to the risk
- **NIS2 Art. 21**: cybersecurity risk management with a list of minimum measures

**Interaction**

- Reinforcing at the operational level
- NIS2 is more specific and prescriptive: named minimum measures including incident handling, business continuity, supply chain security, cryptography, MFA
- Entities under both must meet the stricter standard in areas of overlap

**Strategy**: NIS2 security framework generally satisfies GDPR Art. 32 for entities subject to both. Document the mapping explicitly to evidence compliance under both regimes.

### Breach and incident notification

- **GDPR Art. 33-34**: personal data breach notification (72 hours to supervisory authority; notify affected individuals if high risk)
- **NIS2 Art. 23**: significant incident notification (24 hours early warning)

**Interaction**

- Different thresholds: GDPR triggers on personal data breach; NIS2 on significant incidents
- NIS2 timeline is faster for the early warning
- Both may apply to the same event: a personal data breach that qualifies as a significant NIS2 incident

**Strategy**: NIS2-covered entities must be capable of 24-hour early warning regardless of whether the incident is personal-data-only. Build incident triage to classify against both frameworks simultaneously.

## Data Act and CRA

### Product data access and security

- **Data Act Art. 4**: user data access from connected products
- **CRA Annex I**: essential cybersecurity requirements
- **CRA Art. 31 and Annex VII**: technical documentation

**Interaction**

- Complementary: Data Act governs data sharing; CRA governs product security
- Data access mechanisms under Data Act must meet CRA security requirements
- APIs exposed for Data Act purposes are within CRA scope if the product is a product with digital elements

**Strategy**: secure-by-design data access. Data Act access APIs are CRA-regulated interfaces; authentication, authorization, and secure update mechanisms apply.

### Connected product requirements

- **Data Act Art. 3**: product design for user data access (from 12 September 2026 for newly placed products)
- **CRA**: products with digital elements; full application from 11 December 2027

**Interaction**

- Both apply to IoT and connected products
- Data Act product design obligations apply earlier than CRA full application
- Coordinated design process avoids compounding costs of separate redesign

**Strategy**: unified product design process addressing both regimes from the earliest applicable date.

## DSA and DMA

### Layered platform obligations

- **DSA**: content moderation, transparency, user protection across all intermediary services, scaling with size
- **DMA**: additional obligations for designated gatekeepers

**Interaction**

- Cumulative for gatekeepers: DSA baseline plus DMA gatekeeper obligations
- Different enforcement architecture: DSA primarily enforced by national Digital Services Coordinators with European Commission oversight for VLOPs; DMA enforced directly by European Commission

**Strategy**: layered compliance with clear internal ownership distinguishing DSA obligations from DMA obligations. Interoperability and data access obligations under DMA interact with GDPR in ways that require specific attention.

## AI Act and sectoral regulations

### Medical devices

- **AI Act**: horizontal framework for AI systems including high-risk medical AI
- **MDR / IVDR**: sectoral medical device regulations

**Interaction**

- Parallel compliance with different notified body procedures
- AI Act applies horizontally; MDR/IVDR sectorally
- Integration under AI Act Art. 6(1) read with Annex I may simplify future conformity assessment by routing AI safety components through existing sectoral procedures; current practice is parallel

**Strategy**: coordinate assessment timelines; ensure technical documentation covers both regimes; monitor for harmonised standards and delegated acts that reduce duplication.

### Vehicles and automotive AI

- **AI Act**: high-risk AI for safety-critical functions
- **Type approval regulations**: vehicle safety standards

**Interaction**

- Cumulative requirements for automotive AI
- AI Act Art. 6(1) read with Annex I routes high-risk AI safety components through existing type approval processes

**Strategy**: parallel compliance until integrated procedures are adopted; monitor regulatory developments.

### Financial services

- **AI Act**: horizontal obligations; high-risk AI in credit-worthiness assessment and insurance risk assessment explicitly named
- **Sectoral regulation**: CRD, CRR, Solvency II, DORA, and applicable consumer credit and insurance law

**Interaction**

- AI Act obligations layered on existing sectoral governance
- DORA governs ICT-specific operational resilience for the AI system as ICT
- Consumer-facing AI may additionally trigger consumer protection law

**Strategy**: integrated model risk management framework covering AI Act technical requirements, DORA ICT risk obligations, and sectoral governance expectations.

## Common cross-regulatory solutions

### Unified data governance framework

A single framework addressing:

- GDPR data protection principles and lawfulness
- Data Act data access, portability, and fair contract terms
- AI Act data governance for training, validation, and testing
- NIS2 and DORA data security measures

### Unified incident response plan

One process with branch logic covering:

- GDPR personal data breaches
- NIS2 significant incidents
- DORA ICT-related incidents
- CRA actively exploited vulnerabilities and severe incidents
- AI Act serious incidents for high-risk AI

Each branch includes its own classification criteria, timeline, authority, and documentation requirements. The front end of the triage is shared.

### Consolidated third-party risk programme

One programme covering:

- GDPR Art. 28 processor requirements
- NIS2 supply chain security
- DORA ICT third-party risk, including critical TPP designation
- CRA software supply chain transparency (SBOM)

### Integrated security-by-design

One design review covering:

- GDPR Art. 25 data protection by design and by default
- AI Act Art. 9-15 requirements for high-risk AI
- CRA Annex I essential cybersecurity requirements
- NIS2 Art. 21 and DORA security measures

### Common documentation architecture

One set of living documents mapping to:

- GDPR Art. 30 records of processing
- AI Act Art. 11 and Annex IV technical documentation
- CRA Art. 31 technical documentation
- DORA policy documentation

Design the documentation architecture to evidence compliance under multiple regimes without producing parallel bureaucracies.

## Conflict resolution hierarchy

When regulations appear to conflict, apply in order:

1. **Lex specialis**: specific regulation prevails
   *Example: DORA prevails over NIS2 for financial entities on ICT risk management.*

2. **Stricter standard**: meet the highest applicable requirement
   *Example: NIS2 24-hour early warning applies even for incidents that are also personal data breaches under GDPR's 72-hour rule.*

3. **Cumulative compliance**: satisfy both where neither is clearly specialis
   *Example: CRA and AI Act for AI-enabled connected products.*

4. **Grandfathering and transitions**: check for exemptions
   *Example: products placed on the market before the CRA application date may fall outside certain obligations.*

5. **Regulatory guidance**: consult official guidance
   *Example: EDPB opinions, European Commission FAQs, ENISA publications, national competent authority positions.*

6. **Formal legal opinion**: for novel or ambiguous cases
   *Example: where a product genuinely sits across multiple sectoral regimes with unclear precedence, document the analysis in writing.*
