# Conformity Assessment (Art. 43, Annex VI, Annex VII)

## Purpose

Article 43 determines how the provider demonstrates conformity before placing a high-risk AI system on the market or putting it into service.

For many Annex III systems, the practical route is **internal control / self-assessment** under Annex VI. In some cases, especially higher-scrutiny biometric scenarios, a **notified body** route under Annex VII may be required.

The key question is:

**Do we know which conformity assessment path applies, and are we building the right evidence pack for that path?**

---

## Practical assessment routes

### Route 1 - Internal control (Annex VI)
Typically relevant for many Annex III high-risk systems.

Practical meaning:
- the provider self-assesses conformity
- the provider must still maintain serious evidence
- self-assessment is not “light” compliance
- the technical file, risk management, QMS, testing, and declarations still need to be robust

### Route 2 - Notified body involvement (Annex VII)
More likely where the legal framework requires third-party assessment, including certain biometric or specially regulated cases.

Practical meaning:
- evidence expectations increase in rigor and discipline
- timelines lengthen
- document completeness becomes even more important
- early engagement strategy matters

---

## Simple decision tree

### Question 1
Is the system actually high-risk under Annex III after considering any Art. 6(3) exception argument?
- If no or unclear: resolve that first.
- If yes: continue.

### Question 2
Is the system in a category where internal control is generally available?
- If yes: prepare for Annex VI-style self-assessment.
- If no or unclear: assess whether notified body involvement is needed.

### Question 3
Is the system linked to biometric use cases or another route with higher assessment intensity?
- If yes: treat notified body analysis as a front-loaded workstream.
- If no: continue on internal control unless sector rules say otherwise.

### Question 4
Are harmonized standards/common specifications available and being relied on?
- If yes: reflect them explicitly in the evidence package.
- If no: the provider needs a clearer reasoning record for how conformity is demonstrated.

---

## Minimum evidence pack for internal control readiness

Even under Annex VI, expect to need:
- classification and scope memo
- intended purpose statement
- technical documentation / Annex IV file
- risk management documentation
- data governance evidence
- validation and testing evidence
- oversight and logging documentation
- cybersecurity/robustness evidence
- QMS documentation
- post-market monitoring plan
- declaration / registration readiness materials

If these do not exist, “self-assessment” is not really ready.

---

## When to escalate early

Escalate route analysis early if any of the following is true:
- biometric identification/categorization/sensitive use is involved
- sector-specific law adds assessment expectations
- internal teams disagree on classification or route
- the organization wants to launch on a compressed timeline
- supplier dependencies make evidence collection difficult
- there is no prior experience with regulated conformity processes

---

## Readiness scoring guide

### RED
- no confirmed assessment path
- teams assume self-assessment without legal/technical analysis
- evidence pack not assembled
- no owner for conformity timeline

### AMBER
- likely route identified, but not documented
- evidence exists in parts but not packaged
- route dependencies and timing not fully planned
- supplier evidence or external review needs unresolved

### GREEN
- route is documented and justified
- evidence pack requirements mapped and owned
- timing, dependencies, and review gates defined
- internal control or notified body preparation is underway in a disciplined way

---

## Common pitfalls

### 1. Treating self-assessment as casual
Internal control still requires serious documentation and lifecycle governance.

### 2. Deciding the route too late
If route analysis starts near launch, remediation time may disappear.

### 3. Ignoring external dependencies
Supplier evidence, validation data, and technical files often take longer than expected to obtain.

### 4. No launch gate
If registration/declaration/conformity milestones are not part of product release governance, the process is fragile.

---

## Practical next steps

### If RED
1. Produce a short route memo
2. Confirm whether Annex VI likely applies
3. Identify any trigger for notified body involvement
4. Appoint an owner and timeline
5. Build an evidence pack checklist

### If AMBER
1. Fill route memo gaps with legal/technical input
2. Stress-test evidence completeness
3. Create a launch-gate checklist
4. Plan supplier evidence collection and internal reviews
5. Decide whether a mock assessment is needed

---

## Notes on Annex VI vs Annex VII mindset

### Annex VI mindset
- self-assessment, but with strong internal discipline
- focus on completeness, consistency, traceability
- prove the provider can stand behind its own conformity position

### Annex VII mindset
- assume more formal scrutiny
- optimize for reviewability and evidentiary clarity
- reduce ambiguity in role definitions, controls, and test evidence

---

## What assessors or counsel will care about

They usually want to know:
- why this route applies
- what evidence supports it
- where the biggest route-related risks remain
- whether launch depends on unresolved issues

That is the operational core of Art. 43 readiness.
