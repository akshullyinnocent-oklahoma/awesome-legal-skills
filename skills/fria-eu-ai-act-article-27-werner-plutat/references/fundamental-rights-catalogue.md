# Fundamental Rights Catalogue for Article 27 FRIA

This reference supports the identification of **fundamental rights at stake** in a FRIA under Article 27 EU AI Act.

**Purpose:** A FRIA is not limited to privacy or data protection. It requires a broader analysis of how a high-risk AI deployment could affect persons and groups in practice. Use this catalogue to identify relevant rights, typical AI impact pathways, and context-specific questions.

## How to Use This Catalogue

For each right:
1. Ask whether the right is **realistically engaged** in the concrete use case.
2. Identify the **affected groups**.
3. Describe the **mechanism of impact** - how the AI system could interfere with, burden, or chill the right.
4. Assess **severity**, **likelihood**, and **reversibility**.
5. Link the right to actual safeguards, oversight, and remedies.

Do not list rights mechanically. A short, well-reasoned list is better than a generic catalogue dump.

## Primary Charter Rights Often Relevant in AI Deployments

### 1. Human dignity (Article 1 Charter)

**Why it matters:** AI systems can objectify persons, reduce them to risk scores, or treat them in ways that undermine dignity and respect.

**Typical impact pathways:**
- Dehumanising treatment in welfare, migration, policing, health, or employment contexts
- Automated profiling that labels people as risky, suspicious, or non-compliant
- Intrusive surveillance or emotion inference in sensitive settings

**Questions to ask:**
- Does the system reduce a person to a score or category in a way that shapes treatment?
- Would a reasonable person experience the process as humiliating, degrading, or depersonalising?
- Are vulnerable persons exposed to coercive or opaque decision environments?

### 2. Right to integrity of the person (Article 3 Charter)

**Why it matters:** Relevant where AI affects bodily or psychological integrity, especially in healthcare, education, workplace, policing, or biometric contexts.

**Typical impact pathways:**
- Medical triage or diagnostic support errors
- Biometric categorisation or emotion recognition impacting treatment
- Safety-critical failures causing physical harm

**Questions to ask:**
- Could the system contribute to physical or psychological harm?
- Does it influence treatment, access to care, or intervention decisions?
- Are safety controls and escalation pathways robust enough?

### 3. Respect for private and family life (Article 7 Charter)

**Why it matters:** AI can enable broad inference, surveillance, and behavioural analysis beyond what people expect.

**Typical impact pathways:**
- Monitoring communications or movement
- Inferring health, family, financial, or social circumstances
- Combining datasets to reveal intimate patterns

**Questions to ask:**
- Does the system expose or infer intimate aspects of life?
- Is the monitoring continuous, invisible, or difficult to avoid?
- Are intrusions limited to what is actually necessary?

### 4. Protection of personal data (Article 8 Charter)

**Why it matters:** Data quality, fairness, lawful basis, minimisation, accuracy, and governance are central where personal data is used.

**Typical impact pathways:**
- Processing inaccurate, outdated, or biased data
- Repurposing data beyond original expectations
- Poor transparency, retention, access control, or governance

**Questions to ask:**
- What personal data is processed? Is special-category data involved?
- Are training, testing, or input datasets representative and accurate enough?
- Do people have meaningful transparency and access rights?

### 5. Equality before the law (Article 20 Charter)

**Why it matters:** AI systems can reproduce unequal treatment by embedding historical, structural, or proxy-based bias.

**Typical impact pathways:**
- Unequal error rates across groups
- Unequal access to services, benefits, or opportunities
- Different treatment based on protected or proxy characteristics

**Questions to ask:**
- Does performance differ across groups?
- Could protected characteristics or proxies influence outcomes?
- Are some persons systematically disadvantaged in review or appeals?

### 6. Non-discrimination (Article 21 Charter)

**Why it matters:** One of the most frequent FRIA issues. High-risk AI can discriminate on grounds such as sex, racial or ethnic origin, religion or belief, disability, age, or sexual orientation.

**Typical impact pathways:**
- Biased classification or ranking
- Proxy discrimination through postcode, school, language, device, or behavioural data
- Discriminatory exclusion from services, insurance terms, hiring, credit, or public benefits

**Questions to ask:**
- What discrimination grounds may be engaged directly or indirectly?
- Are there known bias patterns in the provider documentation or validation?
- Do safeguards catch group-level disparities before harm occurs?

### 7. Cultural, religious and linguistic diversity (Article 22 Charter)

**Why it matters:** AI may systematically disadvantage persons whose language, accent, names, or behaviour differ from the dominant dataset patterns.

**Typical impact pathways:**
- Lower model performance for minority languages or dialects
- Misclassification of cultural or religious expression
- Inaccessible interfaces or review processes

**Questions to ask:**
- Is the system validated across relevant language/cultural groups?
- Could naming conventions, accents, or communication styles skew outcomes?
- Are appeals and explanations available in understandable form?

### 8. Equality between women and men (Article 23 Charter)

**Why it matters:** AI may reproduce gender bias in employment, finance, insurance, healthcare, or safety contexts.

**Typical impact pathways:**
- Biased recruitment or promotion support
- Gendered risk predictions in credit or insurance
- Clinical underperformance for sex- or gender-specific health profiles

**Questions to ask:**
- Are outcomes or false positives different by sex/gender?
- Does the system encode stereotypes or historical underrepresentation?
- Are intersectional effects considered?

### 9. Rights of the child (Article 24 Charter)

**Why it matters:** Children require special protection. Even when not the intended user group, they may be indirectly affected.

**Typical impact pathways:**
- Educational scoring, monitoring, or behavioural profiling
- AI in family, child-protection, healthcare, or social services contexts
- Systems that children cannot understand or challenge

**Questions to ask:**
- Could children be directly or indirectly affected?
- Has the best interests of the child been considered explicitly?
- Are explanations, oversight, and safeguards adapted to minors?

### 10. Rights of the elderly (Article 25 Charter)

**Why it matters:** Older persons may be disproportionately affected by healthcare, insurance, service-access, or fraud/risk systems.

**Typical impact pathways:**
- Exclusion through digital-only or opaque processes
- Age-based proxy discrimination
- Reduced ability to challenge adverse outcomes

**Questions to ask:**
- Are older persons likely to face access barriers or over-flagging?
- Is there assisted review or non-digital escalation?
- Are age proxies embedded in the model logic or data?

### 11. Integration of persons with disabilities (Article 26 Charter)

**Why it matters:** AI systems may fail to accommodate disability or may misread disability-related behaviour as suspicious, risky, or non-compliant.

**Typical impact pathways:**
- Accessibility failures in user interface or explanation layers
- Speech, movement, or behaviour misclassification
- Automated procedures without reasonable accommodation

**Questions to ask:**
- Is the interface accessible?
- Could disability-related behaviour be treated as anomalous or risky?
- Are reasonable accommodations built into review and remedy pathways?

### 12. Workers’ rights / fair and just working conditions (Article 31 Charter)

**Why it matters:** AI used in workforce management, evaluation, scheduling, or monitoring can directly affect autonomy, pressure, and working conditions.

**Typical impact pathways:**
- Performance scoring and automated management
- Behavioural monitoring and productivity surveillance
- Opaque escalation or disciplinary triggers

**Questions to ask:**
- Does the system materially shape workload, discipline, or promotion?
- Is there human review before adverse action?
- Are employees informed, consulted, and able to contest outcomes?

### 13. Freedom to conduct a business (Article 16 Charter)

**Why it matters:** Relevant where AI influences access to markets, contracting, banking, insurance, or licensing.

**Typical impact pathways:**
- Denial or restriction of business services
- Opaque commercial risk scoring
- Unequal treatment of SMEs or certain business profiles

**Questions to ask:**
- Could the system unjustifiably block access to essential services?
- Are business customers able to understand and challenge adverse outcomes?
- Are risk models evidence-based and proportionate?

### 14. Consumer protection (Article 38 Charter)

**Why it matters:** Consumers may be subject to opaque segmentation, unfair terms, or harmful denial/steering decisions.

**Typical impact pathways:**
- Manipulative personalisation or exclusion
- Insurance or banking decisions that consumers cannot understand
- Poor complaint handling after automated adverse outcomes

**Questions to ask:**
- Is the consumer exposed to opaque or unfair treatment?
- Does the process create practical barriers to redress?
- Are vulnerable consumers disproportionately affected?

### 15. Right to good administration (Article 41 Charter)

**Why it matters:** Particularly important for public bodies. Administrative decision-making must remain fair, reasoned, and reviewable.

**Typical impact pathways:**
- Administrative opacity
- Rubber-stamping of AI outputs by caseworkers
- Inability to provide reasons or exercise discretion properly

**Questions to ask:**
- Can the authority still explain and justify the decision?
- Is human discretion preserved where legally required?
- Are records sufficient for review and accountability?

### 16. Right of access to documents / transparency (Article 42 Charter)

**Why it matters:** Public-sector AI may affect disclosure, traceability, and transparency obligations.

**Typical impact pathways:**
- Inadequate documentation of model-assisted decisions
- Missing logs or unverifiable AI inputs
- Outsourced opacity caused by vendor secrecy

**Questions to ask:**
- Can the authority document what the AI influenced?
- Are procurement and governance records available?
- Can affected persons obtain enough information to understand the process?

### 17. Right to an effective remedy and to a fair trial (Article 47 Charter)

**Why it matters:** A central FRIA issue whenever AI contributes to adverse outcomes.

**Typical impact pathways:**
- People cannot tell AI was used
- Appeals lack meaningful review of AI outputs
- Decision trails are too opaque to contest effectively

**Questions to ask:**
- Can a person challenge the decision meaningfully?
- Does the reviewer have authority and competence to overturn the AI-influenced outcome?
- Are time limits, costs, or opacity practical barriers to remedy?

### 18. Presumption of innocence and rights of defence (Article 48 Charter)

**Why it matters:** Particularly relevant in law-enforcement or anti-fraud contexts where AI produces suspicion scores or risk flags.

**Typical impact pathways:**
- AI-generated suspicion influencing enforcement action
- Over-reliance on risk scores without evidentiary caution
- Stigmatising or irreversible false positives

**Questions to ask:**
- Does the system create suspicion without adequate human scrutiny?
- Are flagged individuals treated as risky before the facts are verified?
- Are defensive rights preserved in the process?

### 19. Freedom of expression and information (Article 11 Charter)

**Why it matters:** Monitoring or moderation systems can chill speech or suppress lawful expression.

**Typical impact pathways:**
- Over-removal or downranking
- Self-censorship due to monitoring
- Disproportionate treatment of certain communities or viewpoints

**Questions to ask:**
- Could people self-censor because of the AI system?
- Does the system mistake lawful speech for risky or harmful content?
- Are moderation/review processes balanced and accountable?

### 20. Freedom of assembly and association (Article 12 Charter)

**Why it matters:** AI surveillance or classification can chill participation in unions, protests, associations, or collective action.

**Typical impact pathways:**
- Monitoring attendance, affiliations, or social graphs
- Risk scoring tied to collective activities
- Workplace retaliation concerns

**Questions to ask:**
- Could the system discourage lawful association or collective action?
- Is organisational or union activity indirectly monitored?
- Are aggregation/inference functions overbroad?

### 21. Social security and social assistance (Article 34 Charter)

**Why it matters:** In public-sector or quasi-public service contexts, AI may affect access to essential welfare entitlements.

**Typical impact pathways:**
- Incorrect denial or delay of benefits
- Suspicion scoring in anti-fraud systems
- Burdensome review processes for vulnerable claimants

**Questions to ask:**
- Could errors jeopardise access to basic subsistence?
- Are claimants able to correct data or challenge outcomes quickly?
- Is the burden of proof shifted unfairly onto vulnerable persons?

### 22. Health care (Article 35 Charter)

**Why it matters:** Healthcare AI can directly affect diagnosis, triage, treatment, waiting lists, and access to care.

**Typical impact pathways:**
- Clinical underperformance for certain populations
- Unsafe recommendations or automation bias
- Unequal access to care prioritisation

**Questions to ask:**
- Could harm arise from inaccurate clinical support?
- Are clinicians able and willing to override system outputs?
- Has the system been validated in the relevant patient population?

## Additional German Constitutional Lens (Grundgesetz)

For Germany-based assessments, consider relevant **Grundrechte** of the **Grundgesetz** as an additional interpretive lens alongside the Charter, especially:
- Human dignity (Art. 1 GG)
- General right of personality / informational self-determination (Arts. 1 + 2 GG)
- Equality (Art. 3 GG)
- Freedom of occupation (Art. 12 GG)
- Freedom of expression (Art. 5 GG)
- Effective legal protection (Art. 19(4) GG)

This is especially useful in public-administration, labour, education, and healthcare settings.

## Practical Warning Signs

A right should usually be treated as materially engaged where one or more of the following are present:
- The system influences access to essential services or rights
- Errors are hard to detect or challenge
- Vulnerable or marginalised groups are disproportionately affected
- There is surveillance, scoring, ranking, or suspicion generation
- The human reviewer tends to rely heavily on the AI output
- Harms may be severe, cumulative, or difficult to reverse

## Good FRIA Practice

A strong FRIA does not merely say "privacy and discrimination may be affected." It explains:
- **Which right** is engaged
- **For whom**
- **Through what mechanism**
- **How serious** the impact could be
- **What safeguard** reduces the risk
- **Whether residual risk remains acceptable**
