# Step 1: Pursuit of a Legitimate Interest — Detailed Reference

## Legal Basis

**Art. 6(1)(f) GDPR** requires that processing be "necessary for the purposes of the legitimate interests pursued by the controller or by a third party."

**EDPB Guidelines 1/2024, paras. 14-27** elaborate on what constitutes a legitimate interest.

---

## The Concept of "Interest" vs. "Purpose"

An "interest" is the broader stake or benefit the controller has in engaging in a processing activity. A "purpose" is the specific reason why data are processed — the aim or intention.

**Example (EDPB Guidelines, para. 14):**
- Interest: promoting products
- Purpose: direct marketing (sending promotional emails)

The interest must be identified FIRST, then the processing purpose derives from it.

---

## Three Cumulative Criteria for "Legitimate"

### Criterion 1: Lawfulness

The interest must not be contrary to EU or Member State law (C-621/22 *KNLTB*, para. 49).

**This does NOT mean the interest must be enshrined in law** — rather, it must not violate applicable law (C-621/22, para. 40).

**Examples of UNLAWFUL interests:**
- Promoting products prohibited by EU law (EDPB Example 1: e-cigarette marketing banned under Tobacco Products Directive 2014/40/EU)
- Processing to facilitate illegal activities
- Interest in circumventing legal restrictions

**Key point:** A commercial interest CAN be legitimate if lawful (C-621/22, para. 49), but must be assessed case-by-case.

### Criterion 2: Clearly and Precisely Articulated

The interest must be defined with sufficient precision to enable meaningful balancing against data subject rights. Vague or generic statements fail this criterion.

**Failing examples:**
- "For the greater good of society" (EDPB Example 2)
- "Security purposes" without specification
- "Business improvement" without details
- "Combating fraud" as a generic catch-all (EDPB Guidelines, para. 106)

**Passing examples:**
- "Preventing unauthorized access to customer accounts through IP-based anomaly detection"
- "Assessing creditworthiness of loan applicants to manage default risk"
- "Sending promotional emails about similar products to existing customers"

### Criterion 3: Real and Present

The interest must be present and effective at the date of processing — not hypothetical or speculative (C-708/18 *Asociaţia de Proprietari*, para. 44).

**Failing example (EDPB Example 3):**
A newspaper creating a database of former subscribers for a possible future magazine launch — when no concrete plan exists.

**Passing examples:**
- Active fraud prevention for a running service
- Ongoing IT security monitoring for operational systems
- Direct marketing to current customer base

---

## Controller Interest vs. Third-Party Interest

### Controller's Own Interest

Must relate to the actual activities of the controller (C-252/21 *Meta*, para. 124).

**Failing example from CJEU:**
Meta arguing legitimate interest in sharing data with law enforcement — the Court found this unrelated to Meta's essentially economic and commercial activity.

### Third-Party Interest

Art. 6(1)(f) explicitly allows pursuing the interest of a third party. Same three criteria apply.

**Recognised third-party interest scenarios (EDPB Guidelines, paras. 21-25):**

1. **Legal claims** — Third party needs personal data to establish, exercise, or defend a claim (C-13/16 *Rīgas satiksme*: scooter owner needing taxi passenger identity)

2. **Transparency and accountability** — Disclosing executive salaries to shareholders/employees where not mandated by law

3. **Scientific research** — Historical or scientific research in third-party interest

4. **Investment fund data sharing** — Partners obtaining data about other partners for negotiations (C-17/22 *HTB Neunte Immobilien Portfolio*)

5. **Creditworthiness** — Credit agencies processing data in interest of lenders (C-26/22 *SCHUFA*)

**Important:** The controller and third party can have simultaneous legitimate interests (C-26/22, para. 83).

**Special rule:** Third-party interests should not be confused with general public interests. General public interests primarily fall under Art. 6(1)(c) or (e) (para. 25).

## GDPR Recital Guidance

**Recital 47:** Legitimate interest exists where there is a relevant and appropriate relationship (e.g., customer/service). Direct marketing and fraud prevention may be legitimate interests. Reasonable expectations matter.

**Recital 48:** Controllers in a group of undertakings may have a legitimate interest in transmitting data for internal administrative purposes, including processing of employees' and customers' data.

**Recital 49:** Network and information security processing — strictly necessary and proportionate — may constitute a legitimate interest.

---

## Compatibility with Further Processing (Art. 6(4))

If data initially collected under Art. 6(1)(f) is later processed for a different purpose (e.g., initially for service delivery, now for marketing), compatibility must be assessed under Art. 6(4) GDPR, considering:
- Link between original and new purpose
- Context of collection and relationship with data subject
- Nature of data (especially if special categories)
- Possible consequences for data subjects
- Existence of appropriate safeguards

This is especially relevant when processing shifts from controller's interest to third-party interest (EDPB Guidelines, para. 26).
