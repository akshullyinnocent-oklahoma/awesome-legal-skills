# Data Subject Rights under Art. 6(1)(f) Processing

## Overview

When processing is based on legitimate interest, specific data subject rights interactions apply. This reference covers the key interactions and practical guidance.

**Source:** EDPB Guidelines 1/2024, paras. 61-89; Chapter III GDPR

---

## 1. Transparency (Arts. 12-14)

### Requirements

| Obligation | What to Provide |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Legal basis disclosure (Art. 13(1)(c), 14(1)(c)) | Inform that processing is based on Art. 6(1)(f) |
| Legitimate interest specification (Art. 13(1)(d), 14(2)(b)) | Precisely identify and communicate the specific legitimate interest(s) |
| Balancing test availability | Make clear data subjects can request balancing test information (para. 68) |

### Practical Guidance

- Use layered privacy notices to avoid information fatigue
- Include LIA summary in the relevant layer
- The specific interest must be **precisely identified** — not just "legitimate interests of the controller"
- Transparency is linked to the fairness principle (Art. 5(1)(a))
- Mere transparency does NOT create reasonable expectations for Step 3 balancing (para. 53)

---

## 2. Right of Access (Art. 15)

### Scope

Art. 15(1) does not explicitly require providing the legal basis. However:

- **EDPB recommends** providing legal basis info or indicating where it can be found (para. 70)
- The right of access must enable the data subject to **verify lawfulness** (C-154/21 *Österreichische Post*)
- Without knowing the legal basis, data subjects cannot assess which rights they can exercise

### Practical Recommendation

Include in access responses:
- Confirmation that processing is based on Art. 6(1)(f)
- The specific legitimate interest(s) pursued
- A reference to where the full LIA can be obtained upon request

---

## 3. Right to Object (Art. 21(1))

### The Most Critical Right for Art. 6(1)(f) Processing

**Trigger:** Data subject objects "on grounds relating to his or her particular situation" at any time.

### Controller's Obligations After Objection

1. **Do not dismiss for insufficient detail** — limited elaboration on "particular situation" is not sufficient to reject (para. 71). If unclear, ask the data subject to further specify.

2. **Cease processing** unless compelling legitimate grounds demonstrated.

3. **Burden on controller** — under GDPR (unlike Directive 95/46/EC), the burden shifts to the controller to demonstrate compelling grounds (para. 72). Presumption favours the data subject.

### "Compelling Legitimate Grounds" Standard

This is a **HIGHER threshold** than the initial Art. 6(1)(f) assessment (para. 73):

| Element | Art. 6(1)(f) Initial Assessment | Art. 21(1) Post-Objection |
|---------|-------------------------------|--------------------------|
| Standard | Legitimate interest not overridden | **Compelling** legitimate grounds override |
| Burden | On controller (but balanced) | Shifted to controller, presumption for DS |
| Threshold | Proportionate | Must be **essential** to the controller |
| Scope | General assessment | Must account for **particular situation** of this specific data subject |

**What qualifies as "compelling" (para. 73):**
- Protecting against serious immediate harm to the organisation or systems
- Avoiding severe penalty that would seriously affect the business
- NOT merely "beneficial or advantageous" to the controller

**Assessment process:**
1. Identify compelling legitimate grounds
2. Weigh them against this particular data subject's situation
3. Consider the specific impact on THIS person
4. Document the outcome
5. If grounds not compelling → cease processing, inform of erasure rights

### Restriction During Assessment (Art. 18(1)(d))

While verifying whether compelling grounds exist:
- Data subject can request restriction of processing
- Controller may store the data but must cease other processing
- Once assessment complete: delete (if DS prevails) or lift restriction (if compelling grounds found)

## 4. Right to Object to Direct Marketing (Art. 21(2))

### Absolute Right — No Exceptions

- Data subject objects → processing for direct marketing **must cease**
- **No balancing test** — no "compelling legitimate grounds" defence
- Applies regardless of whether the initial LIA was valid
- Includes profiling to the extent related to direct marketing (Art. 21(3))

### Information Obligation (Art. 21(4))

The right to object to direct marketing must be:
- Explicitly brought to the data subject's attention
- Presented **clearly and separately** from other information
- At the latest at the time of first communication

---

## 5. Right to Erasure (Art. 17)

### Relevant Grounds Under Art. 6(1)(f)

| Ground | GDPR Reference | Application |
|--------|---------------|-------------|
| Data no longer necessary | Art. 17(1)(a) | When the legitimate interest has been fulfilled or ceased |
| Unlawful reliance on Art. 6(1)(f) | Art. 17(1)(d) | Processing was unlawful because LIA was flawed |
| Successful objection | Art. 17(1)(c) | Following successful Art. 21 objection |

### Link to Right to Object

Erasure and objection are closely linked (para. 77):
- Same criteria: "overriding legitimate grounds" test
- If objection succeeds → erasure request should also succeed (para. 78)
- If request is ambiguous (objection or erasure?), don't default to minimal compliance — assess what the data subject actually wants (para. 77)
- If in doubt about scope → ask the data subject to clarify

### Documentation

Controller must demonstrate that erasure was fully complied with (Art. 5(2) accountability; C-60/22 *UZ v. Bundesrepublik*).

---

## 6. Right to Rectification (Art. 16)

Can be invoked regardless of legal basis. Especially relevant when:
- Data not obtained from the data subject (more likely inaccurate)
- This is often the case in Art. 6(1)(f) processing (para. 84)
- Accuracy must be assessed in light of the processing purpose (C-434/16 *Nowak*, para. 53)
- Data subject has a legitimate interest in rectification
- But cannot use rectification to change subjective evaluations (e.g., exam answers) — C-434/16, para. 52

---

## 7. Automated Decision-Making (Art. 22)

### Key Rule

**Art. 6(1)(f) is NOT Union law authorising automated decision-making** under Art. 22(2)(b) (para. 81).

This means:
- Art. 22 prohibition applies even if Art. 6(1)(f) is a valid legal basis for the underlying processing
- Automated decisions with legal or similarly significant effects require separate authorisation (Art. 22(2))
- Member States cannot override the balancing test required by CJEU case law (C-634/21 *SCHUFA Scoring*, para. 70)

### Profiling Without Art. 22

Not all profiling triggers Art. 22. But the LIA balancing test must still consider (para. 82):
- Level of detail of the profile
- Comprehensiveness of the profile
- Impact of profiling on data subjects
- Possible future combination of profiles
- Safeguards for fairness, non-discrimination, and accuracy

---

## 8. Right to Data Portability (Art. 20)

Art. 20 applies where processing is based on consent (Art. 6(1)(a)) or contract (Art. 6(1)(b)) AND carried out by automated means. It does **not** apply to Art. 6(1)(f) processing by default.

**Mitigating measure opportunity:** Offering portability for Art. 6(1)(f) processing can serve as a mitigating measure in the balancing test (EDPB Guidelines, para. 57).

---

## Practical Rights Response Checklist

When receiving any data subject request in relation to Art. 6(1)(f) processing:

- [ ] Identify which right is being invoked (or if ambiguous, clarify)
- [ ] Respond within one month (Art. 12(3))
- [ ] Do not impose formality requirements the GDPR doesn't require
- [ ] Do not ask for the specific legal ground of the request (para. 77)
- [ ] If objection: assess compelling legitimate grounds (not just re-confirm initial LIA)
- [ ] If erasure after successful objection: comply
- [ ] Document the request, assessment, and outcome
- [ ] Inform the data subject of the action taken or reasons for not acting
