# Cross-Regulation Mapping - GDPR, AI Act, CRA, DGA Interaction

## Overview

The Data Act does not operate in isolation. It interacts with multiple EU data and digital regulations, creating **overlapping obligations** and **integration challenges**.

This reference maps the key interaction points and provides guidance on integrated compliance.

## GDPR Interaction

### Fundamental Principle: GDPR Remains Fully Applicable

**Article 1(4) Data Act:**
> "This Regulation is without prejudice to Union law on the protection of personal data, in particular Regulations (EU) 2016/679 [GDPR] and (EU) 2018/1725, and Directive 2002/58/EC [ePrivacy]."

**Practical implication:**
- Data Act obligations apply **in addition to** GDPR, not instead of
- Where data is personal data, **both regimes apply**
- More protective rule governs in case of conflict

### Data Access Rights Overlap (GDPR Article 15 vs Data Act Article 4)

| Aspect | GDPR Article 15 | Data Act Article 4 |
|--------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Scope** | Personal data concerning the data subject | Data generated by connected product use (personal or non-personal) |
| **Right holder** | Data subject (individual) | User (may be individual or legal entity) |
| **Free of charge** | Yes (first request; reasonable fees for repetitive/excessive) | Yes (except "frequent or complex" requests) |
| **Format** | Structured, commonly used, machine-readable | Structured, commonly used, machine-readable |
| **Timeline** | Within 1 month (extendable to 3 months) | "Without undue delay" (no specific timeline) |

**Integration strategy:**
- Where user = data subject and data includes personal data: **apply both rights**
- Use the **shorter timeline** (GDPR 1 month if applicable)
- **No fees** for GDPR-scope requests (stricter rule)
- Provide **combined response** covering both GDPR and Data Act access

### Third-Party Data Sharing (GDPR Article 6 Lawful Basis vs Data Act Article 5)

**Scenario:** User instructs data holder to share data with third-party data recipient.

**GDPR analysis:**
- If data is personal data, data holder is **controller**, third party is **controller or processor** (depending on relationship)
- **Lawful basis required:** likely **consent (Article 6(1)(a))** or **contract (Article 6(1)(b))** (if sharing is part of service user requested)
- Data holder and data recipient must have **GDPR-compliant data sharing agreement** (controller-controller or controller-processor)
- **Data minimization** applies - share only what is necessary for the third party's service

**Data Act analysis:**
- User has **right to instruct** data holder to share (Article 5)
- Data holder must comply unless refusal grounds apply (trade secrets, security, etc.)
- Data recipient may not use data beyond purpose agreed with user

**Integrated approach:**
1. **User consent/instruction** satisfies both Data Act user control and GDPR lawful basis for sharing
2. **Data sharing agreement** between data holder and recipient must cover GDPR (controller roles, security, breach notification) and Data Act (use limitations, trade secret protection)
3. **Transparency:** Inform user of GDPR implications (data recipient identity, purposes, retention, rights)

### B2G Data Sharing (GDPR Article 6(1)(c)-(e) vs Data Act Chapter V)

**GDPR lawful basis for public authority requests:**
- **Legal obligation (Article 6(1)(c))** - if national law implementing Data Act creates a legal obligation to provide data
- **Public interest task (Article 6(1)(e))** - public authority exercising official authority

**Special categories (Article 9):**
- Health data, biometric data, genetic data require **Article 9(2)** exception, typically:
  - **(i) Public health** (epidemiology, emergency response)
  - **(g) Substantial public interest** with Member State law basis

**Data subject rights:**
- **Article 23 GDPR** allows Member States to restrict data subject rights (access, erasure, etc.) where necessary for public interest tasks, including emergency response
- Data Act B2G requests will often fall under Article 23 restrictions

**Integration:**
- Public authority B2G request under Data Act **does not automatically satisfy GDPR**
- Data holder must verify that public authority has **GDPR-compliant legal basis**
- **DPIA required** for large-scale or sensitive personal data sharing (GDPR Article 35)

### Data Processing Service Providers (GDPR Article 28 vs Data Act Chapter VI)

**Cloud providers as data processors:**
- Under GDPR, cloud providers processing personal data on behalf of customers are **processors** (Article 28)
- **Data processing agreements (DPAs)** required, with specific mandatory clauses

**Data Act switching obligations:**
- Cloud providers must enable data export and switching
- Customer data includes personal data processed on customer's behalf

**Integration:**
- **DPAs must allow for data export** without restriction (GDPR Article 28(3)(h) - assistance with data portability)
- **GDPR Article 20 (data portability)** applies to personal data held by controllers, separate from but aligned with Data Act switching rights
- **Subprocessor changes:** Data Act switching rights interact with GDPR subprocessor approval requirements (customer must be notified, can object)

### Key Takeaway - GDPR Integration

**Checklist for combined compliance:**
- [ ] Identify whether data is personal, non-personal, or mixed
- [ ] Apply **both GDPR and Data Act** where personal data involved
- [ ] Use **most protective rule** (shorter timeline, no fees, stricter security)
- [ ] Ensure **lawful basis under GDPR** for any Data Act-triggered sharing
- [ ] **DPIAs** for high-risk personal data processing under Data Act obligations
- [ ] **Data subject rights** still apply unless Article 23 restriction justified

## AI Act Interaction

### Data for AI Training and Testing

**AI Act obligations on data quality (Article 10):**
- High-risk AI systems must use training, validation, and testing datasets that are:
  - **Relevant, representative, free of errors, complete**
  - Subject to appropriate **data governance and management practices**
  - Take into account **bias detection and mitigation**

**Data Act sourcing:**
- Data obtained via Data Act user access or B2G sharing may be used for AI training
- **Trade secret protection** (Data Act Article 5(4), 6(2)) may limit access to proprietary training data
- **Use limitation:** Data recipients must respect purpose limitations (Article 5(1)) - if data shared for service X, cannot unilaterally repurpose for AI training without user consent

**Integration strategy:**
- If **AI developer is data recipient** under Data Act: must comply with use limitations and GDPR
- If **AI system uses personal data** for training: GDPR Article 5(1)(b) (purpose limitation) and Article 6 (lawful basis) apply
- **Bias in training data:** AI Act Article 10 + GDPR Article 5(1)(b) (fairness) + Data Act non-discrimination considerations

### High-Risk AI Systems Using Connected Product Data

**Scenario:** Deployer uses data from connected products (e.g., medical devices, automotive sensors) as input to high-risk AI system.

**AI Act obligations:**
- **Article 9 (risk management)** - assess risks from data quality issues
- **Article 10 (data governance)** - ensure data is appropriate for the AI system's intended purpose
- **Article 12 (record-keeping)** - log data sources, transformations
- **Article 14 (human oversight)** - especially where data quality may be imperfect

**Data Act obligations:**
- **User access rights** (Article 4) - users can access data being fed into AI systems
- **Third-party sharing** (Article 5) - users may share data with alternative AI providers
- **Trade secret protection** - AI system logic may be protected, but user data access rights remain

**Integration:**
- **Transparency layering:** User must understand (1) what data is collected (Data Act transparency), (2) how AI system uses it (AI Act transparency), (3) GDPR automated decision-making rights (Article 22)
- **Data quality feedback loop:** User access to their data (Data Act) enables detection of data errors → feeds into AI Act risk management

### Data Sharing for AI System Improvement

**Scenario:** AI provider requests access to operational data from deployers to improve the model.

**AI Act considerations:**
- **Substantial modification** (Article 3(23)) - significant changes to training data may trigger re-assessment
- **Monitoring obligations** (Article 72) - providers must have post-market monitoring systems

**Data Act considerations:**
- If operational data is generated by **connected products**, users have rights to access and control sharing
- Deployers acting as **data holders** must respect user rights before sharing with AI provider

**Integration:**
- **User consent** required where data is personal (GDPR) and user has Data Act rights
- **B2B contract fairness** (Data Act Chapter IV) applies if deployer and provider are enterprises
- AI provider cannot impose **unfair terms** forcing deployers to share all operational data

### Key Takeaway - AI Act Integration

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Training data sourced via Data Act mechanisms must respect use limitations
- [ ] AI Act data quality obligations apply to Data Act-sourced data
- [ ] User transparency: combine Data Act (what data), AI Act (how it's used), GDPR (legal basis, rights)
- [ ] B2B contracts between AI providers and data holders subject to Data Act unfair terms rules

## Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) Interaction

### Connected Products with Digital Elements

**CRA scope (Regulation (EU) 2024/2847, Article 2(1)):**
- Products with digital elements (software, hardware with digital components)
- Includes IoT devices, smart products, connected equipment

**Overlap with Data Act:**
- Many **Data Act connected products** are also **CRA products with digital elements**
- Both regulations apply to manufacturers and economic operators

### Security of Data Access Interfaces

**CRA obligations:**
- **Article 13:** Products must be designed, developed, and produced to manage cybersecurity risks
- **Article 14:** Secure by default configuration
- **Article 15:** Protection against unauthorized access

**Data Act obligations:**
- **Article 4:** User data access must be **secure**
- **Article 5:** Third-party data sharing must be **secure**

**Integration challenge:**
- **Data access APIs are attack surfaces** - must be designed with security by design (CRA) while enabling access (Data Act)
- **Authentication and authorization:** Strong security controls required, but must not block legitimate Data Act access rights

**Best practice:**
- **OAuth2/OIDC** for third-party data access (standardized, secure, auditable)
- **Rate limiting** and anomaly detection to prevent abuse
- **Logging and monitoring** for both CRA incident detection and Data Act accountability
- **Vulnerability disclosure:** CRA requires coordinated disclosure (Article 14(2)) - data access APIs must be included in vulnerability management

### Incident Response and Reporting

**CRA incident reporting (Article 16):**
- Manufacturers must report **actively exploited vulnerabilities** and **severe incidents** to CSIRT (Computer Security Incident Response Team)

**Data Act implications:**
- **Breach of data access system** (e.g., unauthorized access via API) is both:
  - **CRA security incident** (report to CSIRT)
  - **GDPR personal data breach** if personal data affected (report to DPA)
  - **Data Act trade secret breach** if confidential data exposed (notify affected data holder/recipient)

**Coordinated response:**
- **Single incident, multiple reporting obligations**
- Establish **unified incident response plan** covering CRA, GDPR, Data Act, NIS2 (if applicable)

### Key Takeaway - CRA Integration

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Data access APIs designed with **security by design** (CRA Article 13)
- [ ] **Secure authentication** that does not block Data Act access rights
- [ ] **Incident response plan** covers CRA, GDPR, and Data Act breach scenarios
- [ ] **Vulnerability management** includes data access interfaces

## Data Governance Act (DGA) Interaction

### Data Intermediaries (DGA Articles 9-15)

**DGA scope:**
- **Data intermediaries** - services that facilitate data sharing between data holders and data recipients (data sharing platforms, data cooperatives, personal data spaces)

**Data Act interaction:**
- Data intermediaries often act as **data recipients** under Data Act (receive data from data holders on users' behalf)
- May also facilitate **B2B data sharing** subject to Data Act unfair terms rules

**Integration:**
- **DGA registration/notification** required for data intermediary services (Article 12)
- **DGA neutrality obligations** (Article 10) - intermediaries must not use data for own purposes → aligns with Data Act Article 5(1) use limitation
- **Data Act user rights** - data intermediaries must respect and facilitate user access and third-party sharing rights

### Data Altruism (DGA Articles 16-27)

**DGA scope:**
- Voluntary data sharing for public interest purposes (research, statistics, policymaking) without compensation

**Data Act interaction:**
- **B2G data sharing** (Data Act Chapter V) is **mandatory** in emergency/public interest cases
- **Data altruism** is **voluntary** and typically broader (not limited to emergencies)
- Organizations registered under DGA data altruism regime may also be subject to Data Act B2G requests

**Coordination:**
- Data altruism can **supplement** Data Act B2G - e.g., companies voluntarily share aggregated data for climate research, avoiding need for mandatory requests
- Public bodies should **prioritize voluntary mechanisms** before invoking mandatory Data Act B2G requests (proportionality)

### Key Takeaway - DGA Integration

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Data intermediaries comply with **both DGA registration/neutrality** and **Data Act user rights**
- [ ] Voluntary data sharing (DGA altruism) can reduce need for mandatory B2G requests
- [ ] Data cooperatives acting on behalf of users can exercise **collective Data Act rights**

## Digital Markets Act (DMA) Interaction

### Gatekeeper Data Access Obligations

**DMA Article 6(9):**
- Gatekeepers must provide **business users and third-party providers** access to data generated through use of gatekeeper's platform

**DMA Article 6(10):**
- Gatekeepers must provide **end users** with access to data they provide or generate through use of core platform services

**Data Act provisions:**
- **Recital 13:** Data Act obligations on gatekeepers are **modified or may not apply** where DMA already regulates data access
- **Article 2(3)(a):** Data Act does not apply to data access obligations already subject to DMA

**Practical implication:**
- **If you're a DMA gatekeeper:** Consult DMA obligations first; Data Act may not add new requirements
- **If you're a business user/end user of a gatekeeper:** DMA provides your primary data access rights
- **If you're a non-gatekeeper connected product manufacturer:** Full Data Act obligations apply

### Key Takeaway - DMA Integration

- DMA obligations take precedence for **gatekeeper platforms**
- Data Act applies to **non-gatekeeper entities** and **data not covered by DMA**

## Integrated Compliance Strategy

### Unified Governance Framework

**Recommended approach:**

1. **Single data access policy** covering:
   - GDPR data subject access requests
   - Data Act user access and third-party sharing
   - DMA data access (if applicable)
   - Sector-specific access obligations (e.g., PSD2, EHDS)

2. **Unified data classification:**
   - Personal vs non-personal
   - Trade secrets vs public
   - High-risk AI training data vs operational data
   - Connected product data vs service data

3. **Cross-functional compliance team:**
   - Legal (GDPR, Data Act, DMA)
   - Security (CRA, NIS2)
   - Product (access by design)
   - Data governance (quality, lineage, access controls)

4. **Integrated risk assessment:**
   - GDPR DPIA
   - AI Act risk management (Article 9)
   - Data Act trade secret protection
   - CRA security risk assessment
   - Combined into single assessment where overlapping

### Key Compliance Milestones

| Regulation | Key Date | Milestone |
|------------|----------|-----------|
| **GDPR** | 25 May 2018 | Already applies |
| **DGA** | 24 September 2023 | Already applies |
| **Data Act** | 12 September 2025 | Main provisions apply |
| **AI Act** | 2 August 2026 | High-risk system obligations apply |
| **Data Act** | 12 September 2026 | Access-by-design for new products |
| **CRA** | TBD (~2027) | Products with digital elements obligations |
| **Data Act** | 12 September 2027 | Cloud switching fee elimination for existing contracts |

### Common Pitfalls

1. **Siloed compliance** - treating each regulation independently
   - **Fix:** Establish cross-functional governance with unified policies

2. **Assuming Data Act overrides GDPR**
   - **Fix:** Apply both; use stricter rule where overlap

3. **Ignoring DMA for gatekeeper-related data**
   - **Fix:** Check DMA scope first if dealing with gatekeeper platforms

4. **Weak security in data access APIs**
   - **Fix:** Apply CRA security by design + Data Act secure access requirements jointly

5. **Forgetting AI Act when sharing data for model training**
   - **Fix:** Include AI Act data quality and purpose limitation checks in data sharing workflows

## Key Takeaways

1. **GDPR always applies** to personal data - no Data Act override
2. **AI Act and Data Act converge** on training data quality and transparency
3. **CRA security requirements** must be designed into Data Act access mechanisms
4. **DGA data intermediaries** facilitate Data Act rights exercise
5. **DMA takes precedence** for gatekeeper platforms
6. **Integrated compliance is essential** - these regulations are designed to work together, not in isolation
