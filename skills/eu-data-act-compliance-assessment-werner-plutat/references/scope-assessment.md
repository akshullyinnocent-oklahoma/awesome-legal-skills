# Scope Assessment - EU Data Act Roles and Definitions

## Overview

The Data Act creates obligations for different actors in the data ecosystem. Your compliance obligations depend on which role(s) your organization occupies.

## Key Roles

### 1. Manufacturer (Article 2(4))

**Definition:** Entity that designs and manufactures a connected product or related service, or has a connected product or related service designed or manufactured and markets it under its name or trademark.

**Scope includes:**
- Original equipment manufacturers (OEMs)
- Private label manufacturers
- Entities that commission manufacturing and market under their brand
- Service providers offering services integral to connected product functionality

**Scope excludes:**
- Pure distributors or retailers without design/branding control
- Repair services that don't modify product data functionality
- Resellers of used products

**Obligations:**
- Pre-sale transparency (Article 3(1))
- Access-by-design for products designed after 12 Sept 2026 (Article 3(2))
- Ensure data holder compliance where manufacturer is also data holder
- SME simplification (< 50 employees, < €10M turnover)

**Boundary cases:**
- **Software updates that change data generation:** If a manufacturer releases a software update that materially changes what data is generated or how it's accessible, this may trigger renewed transparency obligations even for existing products
- **White-label products:** The entity under whose brand the product is marketed is the manufacturer for Data Act purposes, not necessarily the physical production entity
- **Component suppliers:** Generally not manufacturers unless the component itself is a connected product marketed separately

### 2. Data Holder (Article 2(6))

**Definition:** Legal or natural person who has the right or obligation, in accordance with the Data Act, applicable Union law or national legislation implementing Union law, or in the case of non-personal data, control through publicly available terms of service, to make available certain data.

**Scope includes:**
- Manufacturers of connected products (for data generated by those products)
- Service providers operating digital services tied to connected products
- Platform operators holding user-generated product data
- Intermediaries with contractual control over data access
- Cloud/edge providers holding customer data (separate obligations under Ch. VI-VII)

**Obligations:**
- Enable user access to data (Article 4)
- Enable third-party data sharing on user request (Article 5-7)
- Respond to B2G data requests (Chapter V)
- Protect trade secrets proportionately (Article 5(4), 6)
- Charge only reasonable fees for frequent/complex access (Article 9)

**Boundary cases:**
- **Joint data holders:** Where multiple entities have control over the same data (e.g., product manufacturer + cloud platform), both may be data holders with coordinated obligations
- **Data processors vs data holders:** A pure data processor acting solely on behalf of a controller may not be a data holder if it has no independent right to make data available
- **Successor entities:** If a manufacturer sells a product line, the successor may become the data holder for ongoing data access obligations

### 3. User (Article 2(7))

**Definition:** Natural or legal person that owns, rents or leases a connected product or receives a related service.

**Scope includes:**
- Product owners (purchasers)
- Lessees and renters
- Service subscribers
- Both consumers (B2C) and businesses (B2B)

**Rights:**
- Access data generated by product use (Article 4)
- Instruct data holder to share data with third parties (Article 5)
- Receive pre-sale information (Article 3)
- Protection against unfair contract terms in B2B context (Chapter IV)

**Boundary cases:**
- **Employees using employer-owned products:** The employer is the user, not the individual employee, unless the product is assigned to the employee with ownership-like control
- **Shared ownership/leasing:** All co-owners or joint lessees are users with independent data access rights
- **Temporary possession (e.g., test drive, demo):** Temporary possession without ownership, rental, or lease relationship generally does not confer user status

### 4. Data Recipient (Article 2(8))

**Definition:** Legal or natural person, acting for purposes related to its trade, business, craft or profession, other than the user, to whom the data holder makes data available, including a third party following a request by the user and a data processing service provider.

**Scope includes:**
- Third-party service providers receiving data at user's instruction
- Aftermarket service providers
- Data analytics firms
- Competitors offering alternative or complementary services
- Data intermediaries (data cooperatives, data trusts - see DGA interaction)

**Obligations:**
- Use data only for the purpose agreed with the user (Article 5(1))
- Not use data to develop competing products that undermine data holder's market position (Article 5(2) - limited anti-competitive use restriction)
- Maintain confidentiality and security
- Comply with GDPR where personal data is processed

**Boundary cases:**
- **Subcontractors of data recipient:** If a data recipient engages a subcontractor (e.g., cloud provider for storage), the subcontractor is not an independent data recipient unless it uses the data for its own purposes
- **Data recipient becoming data holder:** If the data recipient gains control or generates new derivative data, it may itself become a data holder for that new data

### 5. Data Processing Service Provider (Article 2(11))

**Definition:** Provider of a commercial service of data processing, storage or other data processing support service that the customer does not have physical control over, including cloud computing, edge computing or similar services.

**Scope includes:**
- Infrastructure-as-a-Service (IaaS) providers
- Platform-as-a-Service (PaaS) providers
- Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) providers where data processing is the core service
- Edge computing providers
- Hybrid and multi-cloud orchestrators

**Obligations (Chapters VI-VII):**
- Facilitate customer switching (Article 23)
- Eliminate switching fees by 1 Jan 2027 (Article 25(3))
- Minimum 2-month termination notice period (Article 24(1))
- Enable data export in structured, machine-readable format (Article 23(1)(c))
- Support interoperability (Article 26)
- Apply contractual fairness (Chapter IV applies to B2B contracts)

**Boundary cases:**
- **On-premises software:** Not in scope - customer has physical control
- **Managed services with data processing as ancillary component:** If the core service is not data processing (e.g., marketing agency using cloud tools), the entity is not a data processing service provider under the Data Act, though its cloud vendors are
- **Open-source self-hosted solutions:** Not in scope unless offered as a commercial managed service

### 6. Public Body (Chapter V)

**Definition:** State, regional or local authorities, bodies governed by public law, or associations formed by one or more such authorities or bodies, acting in their official capacity.

**Includes:**
- National ministries and agencies
- Regional and municipal authorities
- Public universities and research institutions acting in official capacity
- Emergency response agencies (civil protection, health, environmental)
- Regulatory and supervisory bodies

**Rights:**
- Request data from data holders in exceptional need (emergency or public interest) (Articles 15, 17)

**Obligations:**
- Justify necessity, proportionality, and specificity
- Provide compensation for public interest requests (Article 20)
- Maintain confidentiality and use data only for stated purpose
- Apply safeguards for trade secrets (Article 15(3))

## SME Simplifications

**Micro and small enterprises** (< 50 employees AND < €10M annual turnover) as **manufacturers** benefit from:
- Simplified pre-sale information obligations (Article 3(3))
- Potential derogations or lighter implementation requirements

**Important:** SME status is assessed at the manufacturer level, not the user or data holder level. An SME manufacturer selling to large enterprise users still has obligations.

## Gatekeeper Exclusions

**Digital Markets Act (DMA) gatekeepers** have separate data access obligations under Article 6(9) and 6(10) DMA. Data Act obligations on gatekeepers are modified or may not apply in certain contexts (Recital 13).

**Practical implication:** If your organization is designated as a gatekeeper for a core platform service, consult both Data Act and DMA for applicable data sharing rules.

## Multiple Roles

Many organizations occupy **multiple roles** simultaneously:

**Example 1: Smart thermostat manufacturer**
- **Manufacturer** (designs the device)
- **Data holder** (controls access to temperature, usage data via cloud backend)
- **Data processing service provider** (if offering cloud storage to users)

**Example 2: Automotive OEM**
- **Manufacturer** (produces the vehicle)
- **Data holder** (vehicle telematics, diagnostics)
- **User** (for fleet vehicles the OEM operates itself)

**Example 3: Industrial equipment lessor**
- **User** (leases equipment from manufacturer)
- **Data holder** (for operational data generated during lease)
- **Data recipient** (receives maintenance data from manufacturer at user's request)

When occupying multiple roles, **obligations are cumulative** - you must comply with all applicable requirements for each role.

## Enforcement and Jurisdiction

**National market surveillance authorities** are responsible for enforcement (Article 38).

- In **Germany:** Bundeskartellamt expected as lead authority, with sector-specific roles for BNetzA (telecoms, energy)
- In **Austria:** Federal Competition Authority (Bundeswettbewerbsbehörde)
- **GDPR overlaps:** Data protection authorities (DSB, BfDI, Landesdatenschutzbehörden) for personal data aspects

**Cross-border cases:** If a manufacturer is established in one Member State but data holders or users are in others, the authority in the Member State where the manufacturer is established generally has primary jurisdiction, with cooperation mechanisms under Regulation (EU) 2019/1020.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Start with role identification** - all other obligations flow from which role(s) you occupy
2. **Roles are defined functionally** - based on what you do with the product/data, not your business label
3. **Multiple roles = cumulative obligations** - no safe harbor from being just one thing
4. **SME benefits are narrow** - apply only to manufacturers, and only if you're genuinely small
5. **DMA gatekeepers have a separate regime** - don't assume Data Act obligations apply as written
